Description:

This course explores how performers, artists, and activists interpreted the AIDS epidemic at the beginning of the HIV/AIDS crisis to revise the shame and stigma of an AIDS diagnosis and agitate for medical research, effective and available treatment, and social support services and compassionate responsive government leadership to end the epidemic. We will examine representations of the HIV/AIDS crisis in popular culture and contrast that with the work of writers, artists, filmmakers, and performers who participated in or aligned themselves with AIDS activism â€“the artists who resisted remaining silent so as to change the course of the epidemic to benefit People With AIDS (PWAs).

We will study works by artists such as: Ishmael Houston-Jones (dancer/choreographer), Group Material (artist collective); Felix Gonzales- Torres (visual art and installation); John Greyson (filmmaker); Bill T. Jones (dancer/choreographer);;  Karen Finley (performance and installation), Marlon Riggs (filmmaker), Essex Hemphill (poet/performer); Jean Carlomusto (video); Lola Flash (photography); Larry Kramer (playwright); David Wojnarowicz (writer and artist); Kia LaBeija (visual art and dance); Jeff Weiss (playwright and actor) and we will work with the ACT UP Oral History Project  and the John Bernd Archives in the Harvard Libraries. For a final project, course participants will curate a show for World AIDS Day, December 1, 2022, at Harvard.
