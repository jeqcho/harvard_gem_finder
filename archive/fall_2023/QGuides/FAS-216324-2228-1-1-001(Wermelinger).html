AC 207 is a superb way to fulfill the programming requirement. Even as someone who's taken multiple higher level CS classes (124, 121, 61 and others), I still got a lot out of this class. If you're like me and skipped CS 50, I highly recommend using AC 207 instead.

The course begins by diving into Git, which is a very important skill to have. It also goes over basic command line syntax that I did not know beforehand. After that, the course dives into Python and goes over an impressive amount of content in a single semester, including decorators, dunder methods, the interpreter itself, and much more. Fabian also teaches a C++ primer supplemental class (not part of grading, just purely for students to learn) which goes over the same concepts but in C++.

If you are already an expert on Git, the command line in Linux, and an advanced Python user, then perhaps this class will be review for you. But otherwise, you will likely get a lot out of this class.

My only criticism of the course are the online Canvas 4 quizzes, which I believe were a poor evaluation metric that unnecessarily brought people's grades down and in particular penalized people who took the quiz honestly and did not collaborate.

There are some things everyone should note before deciding to take this course:

1. Prerequs: When I took this course, the only prerequisite was CS50. This is 100% not true. I think this goes with a much larger theme of the course, which is that I think Fabian has some difficulty understanding what students do and don’t know. You should have a strong math background of linear algebra, which many CS50 people do not have. Also, while I guess it’s technically possible to do well with just CS50 (I’m sure there are students who fall in this camp) I would not recommend this course without more CS/programming background.
2. Quizzes: To put it bluntly, these quizzes were awful. It was the first year of them being introduced. Now imposter syndrome is real, but I’m a senior concentrating in Computer Science. I got 50% on all of the quizzes. I had friend who who did similarly. Apparently the averages were high, and many students got 100%, so maybe it was just me. But these quizzes seemed to be purposefully tricky, with a strict time limit and very, very few questions (12). I really hope Fabian rehauls how he does these. If not, be warned.
3. Participation: Part of participation is to be “active” on Ed. This is not my biggest issue with the course, but it was very annoying (especially when it was relative and based on all the other classmates, which I would say is unacceptable, but he changed it part way through the semester so it was absolute). It think it’s not a great philosophy to force students to post, it just creates unnecessary spam and stress. But I got full marks, so it’s not too terrible.
4. Lectures: I think Fabian still has some learning to do on how to write and perform a good lecture. Everything just seemed unmotivated, and you could never tell what he’s assuming you knew, what was background, and what was the actual meat of the class. It just always felt disjoint. He had good examples and obviously understands the material down to the bit, but I think his educational communicative skills could improve.
5. Psets: These were a huge range from “fine” to “annoying”. I understand that there is a tad of a language barrier, and for the most part it’s not an issue. However, in the instructions for each pset, there would be at least 1–2 errors that made it drastically more unclear what to do and how to proceed (when it was obvious that was not the intention).

Overall, I would only recommend this course for CS seniors/juniors who need an extra class. You will learn how to effectively use the terminal and git/github, which is probably my biggest takeaway. I really hope that Fabian takes another look at this course with what works, and what does not.
