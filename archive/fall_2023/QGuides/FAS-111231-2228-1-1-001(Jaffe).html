Physics 151 is a beautiful, mathematically rigorous introduction to theoretical physics. Professor Jaffe and Ahmed are amazing. You will get a lot out of this class!
This is more of a math class than a physics class. You must have a working knowledge of proofs based linear algebra to get anything out of this class––not just familiarity with basic concepts, but a deep conceptual understanding of vector spaces, groups, functionals, etc. Professor Jaffe is brilliant but often inaccessible and out of touch with the student being exposed to the material for the first time. If you've taken 15a or 16, go ahead and skip this class––many students enrolled were freshmen too advanced for them.
This was a great class, and I'd definitely recommend it.

First, I found the content of this course to be incredibly interesting. The focus is not really on solving problems per se but understanding classical mechanics as a whole. Professor Jaffe develops the material in a very rigorous way, and I really gained a lot of appreciation for physics more broadly. This was a chance to push classical mechanics to its limits. I think the course is well organized, with an emphasis on symmetries a common thread throughout. The last couple weeks we branched out and talked briefly on a number of different topics and closed the course in a transitionary way between classical mechanics and quantum mechanics.

As far as the lectures go, I found them to be quite good. It is very clear that Professor Jaffe is incredibly knowledgeable on this subject, and truly enjoys teaching. He will smile with glee as he derives an elegant result ('the matrix is Hermitian!'), and he starts every class by asking for questions – he wants the class to be engaged. He tries not to start without answering at least one question, which may be a tad awkward when nobody has any to offer, but it really is a great chance to make sure you're actively engaging with the material, and so if you take the class, come with questions! Quite honestly, I think this is a practice that should be done in every class.

Professor Jaffe wrote very extensive and rigorous lecture notes, that take the place of a textbook for this course. He reworks them every year (!), and it is clear he cares a lot about having a good set of notes. In the process of rewriting them, some typos do get through, but he often finds them quite quickly and is fast to resolve them. While at times a typo can be a little frustrating, for better or for worse they do serve as good checks, to make sure you really understand that material and aren't just skimming through it on a surface level. That notwithstanding, the notes are quite thorough and in–depth – a very useful resource indeed! His notes often go into more depth than in lecture (doing all derivations explicitly, etc.), or approach the problem in a different way, providing multiple approaches on a particular issue which can be quite useful. There was a topic or two for which I felt I'd benefit from additional outside reading, and David Tong's lecture notes have come highly recommended as a supplement to Prof. Jaffe's. But in general, I found his notes to be quite good, and the exercise of reading through them carefully (including derivations) to check my understanding was quite helpful.

Also, I like Professor Jaffe. He is quite approachable, and always happy to talk. He's very nice, passionate, and an incredible physicist. Go to his office hours!

Section was great, Ahmed was very thoughtful in what he went over, and it was a great opportunity to solidify ideas from lecture etc. And Ahmed was just very nice and helpful throughout the course, a great TF!

Problem sets were not weekly, but every few weeks or so. Keep that in mind when looking at the hours/week. I personally liked this arrangement; physics nights were a great chance to knock out a good chunk of the pset. They were generally reasonable, although some of the derivations got quite tedious. Doable, but at times a lot of algebra/calculus, not necessarily the most fun. The cancellation was very satisfying when it worked out, but it often took some 'bashing.' Tests were not too bad, although at times I felt a little unsure of what to study. Professor Jaffe was quite clear on the topics that would be on the test, but we went into such depth in class that it was hard to know what extent of the material we needed to be comfortable with. I have to say, I think the grade breakdown was very fair, and I am quite fond of it. Tests were weighted enough that one did genuinely care about them and study hard, but at the same time weighted lightly enough that one was not unduly stressed. I think Professor Jaffe really has found a very good balance in the grade breakdown. I also am a fan of the final projects. I think it was a great opportunity to actually learn through doing, rather than focusing simply on regurgitating material. I know I learned a lot in my project and solidified a lot of ideas from the course by really applying them rigorously.

Now, to talk about prerequisites. It's good to have some background with abstract linear algebra, but most things can be picked up along the way if needed. Also, basic vector calculus etc. As far as physics experience goes, I think it would be good to be at least familiar with Lagrangians, although not absolutely necessary. A lot of people were not exposed to them before and seem to have done well, yet I personally found it very helpful to have had some experience with them. More broadly, it's good to have had some classical mechanics background before this course, though not absolutely necessary. While the course may be doable without, I think one gets a lot more out of 151 with some such background than one would otherwise.

I'm very glad I took this course and would recommend it! You'll come out of this having fallen in love with the Hamiltonian :)
This course has interesting topics and would be a lot of fun to take pass/fail. For a letter grade though, it is very difficult because many of the students have advanced math skills (from classes such as Math 55) and are therefore ahead when it comes to the math in the course.
The treatment of the material in this course is extremely mathematical in nature, so only take this course if you have a strong background in multivariable calculus and linear algebra. Moreover, the lectures are usually hard to follow, the lecture notes are not very well written and there is no textbook assigned for the course, so you will need to put in a lot of effort outside of class to understand the material. Hence, make sure to attend the course sections since they will help consolidate your understanding of the course material. However, this course can be potentially very rewarding if you want to concentrate in theoretical physics.
This course is super interesting –– it is a great survey of classical mechanics, and Professor Jaffe also made sure to connect the topics to many fields of theoretical physics. I feel like I learned a lot, and also became much more comfortable with math, as the course and lectures were quite mathematical. Great class!
This class will develop your understanding of physics and ability to approach higher level physics. Ahmed is a fantastic TF and all the assignments and test were very reasonable. The lectures are a step up in difficulty from what is assigned. The readings may look difficult, but with some patience all of them are very digestible. The notes only look long because they contain a lot of "propositions' with their proofs. If one just reads the propositions and treats the subsequent proofs as footnotes the reading and class material becomes very straightforward.
This class is really interesting if you are interested in the mathematical aspects of physics. The material itself is beautiful, but every class was just mind–numbing algebra done on the blackboard. Jaffe expects everyone in the class to be a human calculator and do derivatives/matrix multiplication in their heads on the spot within 2 seconds, which can be mildly annoying at times. What's even more annoying is that he is extremely hung up on people asking questions before he begins every class, and more often than not that results in a long and awkward silence as he waits for people to speak up while people clearly do not want to. He is also not a particularly pleasant person at times, and tends to give off an air of spite when he answers questions, although I am really not sure whether he means it or not. Take this class if you are able to tolerate through all that, because what you get in the end is quite useful and more importantly very interesting. Ahmed was also an amazing TF, and he singlehandedly sustained my interest in the material all the way for an entire semester.
take this class if you want an intellectual challenge.
