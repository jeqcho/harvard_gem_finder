This class is amazing! Rachel is a wonderful preceptor whose knowledge and enthusiasm for labor sociology make it really easy to get interested in the class content! Her feedback is always super helpful, and if you take it in stride, your writing can become monumentally better. This class is definitely challenging, but this is all for the better! If you commit to the revision process and invest time into this class, you are bound to come out a better writer. Take deadlines seriously, and read assigned texts very carefully, as this will help you when you begin to write your essays. Do your best to submit high–quality drafts – doing so will make your revision process simpler and it easier to elevate your paper. If you are at all interested in sociology, this class is a great way to get an intro to it through writing!
One of the most interesting classes I've taken and with an incredibly passionate professor. Certainly, however, not an easy course. A lot of effort goes into understanding concepts of sociology that you have probably never been introduced to and then you have to write 7+ long page papers about it. Be prepared. It's a tradeoff really––harder material to write about, but the outcome of learning about something really deep and reflective of our society and lives as workers.
Rachel is truly a phenomenal instructor, no complaints. She will help you truly become a better writer with her amazing advice that you can always put into practice and because the entire class is structured based on the students direct needs. She is incredibly in–tune with students and knows how stressful college life is, and never makes class any harder than it needs to be. In fact, the major theme is that everything is designed to make writing papers as easy as possible. Every minute in class helps you form your questions and theses. The readings are reasonable and you have so much time to write papers. The class discussions will be among your most enlightening at Harvard, you will all learn from each other. You probably don't need it, but a decent interest in the topics the class description names will make your experience even better.
Be very intentional about what Expos course you chose because the content matters more than you might think. It is easier to write about content you are excited about.
If you do the readings (which are super interesting and aren't long), you will get a lot out of this course — both in terms of writing and argumentative skills and an introductory understanding of labor sociology.
GEM GEM GEM (provided you're interested in sociology or Marx).

Rachel is the nicest, best instructor on this planet, and will reduce your readings to the absolute minimum necessary for discussion while also giving you the option to read more if you're interested. Readings are mostly taken from labor sociology books and papers (Cech, Kunda, Braverman), which are fairly standard in difficulty, although the Marx section is a bit denser (but Rachel makes it easier and provides alternative sources in case you find it a bit too hard). You will be doing a non–trivial amount of close reading, so this is not the Expos section for you if you want to just cruise by while not doing the readings, but if you allow it to, it will definitely be a paradigm shift for you in all walks of life that contain work, from your future career to your view of headlines in the present and the near future.

There are three essays of increasing length, and Rachel does make grading stricter as the semester progresses, but the amount of feedback you receive makes it completely fine, and holds your hand to an appropriate amount to prep you for college writing. Keep an open mind about your writing, it's not as good or watertight as you remember it ;)

Discussions were honestly the best part of this course: this course attracts such a wide variety of people from all walks of life and all political opinions that seminars are both very broad and surprisingly deep, and can get you to extend your idea of work beyond what you know from your own experience. Definitely a contrast with other Expos section, where the same opinion is parroted ad infinitum and the 75–minute sections feel like they take up your whole morning.

Rachel is also super flexible and available outside of class time, and is more than willing to talk about things other than the course that you are interested in, whether that is out of sheer curiosity, to advance your paper, to figure out what you want to do in life, etc.

I'd be lying if there isn't a voice in my head that is telling me to switch my concentration to sociology.

TL;DR: If you're at all tangentially interested in political philosophy or sociology, TAKE THIS EXPOS. Non–trivial amounts of reading (maybe like 50 pages total per reading assignment, spread across 2 or 3 sources), but everything else makes it so, so worth it, and Rachel's incredible. Honestly the best class I took at Harvard this semester.
This class will teach you how to make your writing much more personal. Writing an essay will feel more like giving a speech or retorting in a live debate. It will challenge you to ask questions while you're writing and find ways to answer those questions.
