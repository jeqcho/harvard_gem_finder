I really enjoyed the class! We covered a lot of material from lots of different perspective aspects of HCI and specifically on how to write a UIST paper. Be sure to start your final project early enough so you can get the proper feedback. Personally, I found it super rewarding, but definitely be prepared to put in some work!
The course assignments were too much. Having 2 things due Monday, 3 things due Wednesday, and 1–2 things due Friday was so undoable and defeating. The tier 1 assignments were at first cool to get to learn about different softwares/languages. The above and beyond criteria for that was unfair given how much work had often went in to even get the MVP running. When the tier 1 assignments started overlapping with Tier 2 and tier 3 it was very hard to balance. Tier 2 assignments were okay but had so little instruction or guidance that they became more annoying than a cool exercise. Tier 3 was confusing for everyone really until the last 3 weeks of the class. It was an added stress having been mentioned so early in the course. The pair research assignment was maybe the most upsetting of all. I get the teacher wanting to learn what areas it looks like people need help on. But, forcing us to then submit assignments on what we learned and then grade them was very unfair. Finding 30 minutes to meet was already a challenge, and a lot of times people made of random things to get help with. It was all the more surprising that after this, people could then get 15/20 points for having met with someone but not describing what was learned to the teacher's satisfaction. All of these combined made the class feel like more like a punishment than learning experience; students felt like they were in middle school, so distrusted to do the assignments that every assignment then had to be graded. It felt like there was no rest in this class or time for reflection.
Take this class if you have a strong interest in HCI. This class goes very in depth for a lot of concepts very quickly.

If you're interested in design, you'll love this course! The paper selection that you'll read over the semester is awesome –– and students get to choose which papers will be read since each person presents one paper over the course of the semester. The entire class is reading papers before class and then listening to your fellow classmates present ~2 papers per class (30 min presentations). These presentations are encouraged to be interactive and engaging, so its all–around lots of fun :) The course staff gives great guidance for the final project, which is fulfilling and eye–opening. I really loved doing user interviews and then synthesizing results. I feel like I actually learned something and did "real science" (unlike some theory classes where the papers feel "made–up" or "fake").

The course will teach you ways to think about designing technology for humans (frameworks for analyzing whether a solution is actually solving a pertinent pain point, principles for AI–infused systems, principles for designing *good* and *necessary* features in software) and actual concrete applications of these principles (task–based tab manager, AI–powered VScode extensions, doctors' notetaking systems, AI brainstorming tools, better museum exhibits, Doodle usage statistics, chatbot best practices –– are just a few things we learned and discussed this semester).

The course staff is generally supportive, approachable, and extremely flexible. All deadlines were soft (but not so soft as to allow students to fall too far behind). Professor Glassman cares a lot about her students and it shows through the way she teaches the class, encourages presenters, and poses thoughtful questions.

My only gripe with the course was that some of the assignments were rough–around–the–edges. It's still a young course so I'm sure the course staff will act on the feedback we gave this semester to improve it.

This class assumes a very strong background in software engineering, but if you have the right background, you will be fine. It is very busywork heavy, and the first half of the semester required you to create 1–2 interfaces (todo lists, recreate doodle, when2meet) every week with new frameworks each time. Some of these frameworks are not very well used or documented either (Svelte?) so that wasnt fun. On top of this, I wrote about 1000 words of paper responses per class that followed a specific framework, and these were fairly useful in understanding HCI research but felt like a pain on top of other assignments. On top of that, we also had to do pair research every week, where we essentially got matched to other students on a website based on what we need help with that week, and help each other out for an hour before writing a response on what we learned. Once again, could be useful if it wasnt on top of so many other assignments for this class alone. I did enjoy learning about user studies in HCI research and it was fun to execute one throughout the semester and for our final project. If you like to create cool side projects with different frameworks, this class is definitely for you, since my final project just felt like testing out a cool side project. It really caters to people who independently like to develop cool, useful interfaces. Just beware of all the busywork that is required.
