This is certainly one of the most interesting and thought–provoking classes I have taken at Harvard. Jasanoff is one of those professors with a cult of personality around her and it's mostly for good reason; her insights cut through a lot of suggestions to reveal the decisions and assumptions we take for granted in moving through our world. Only rarely did I feel she was looking for a specific type of answer in lecture. Her questions always keep you on your toes and force you to prepare a defense of your thoughts before just presenting them as fact. Mauro's section was also amazing. He really curated an environment where students could discuss the information and readings they found relevant rather than trying to force students to follow a rigid agenda of analyzing the readings. Reading responses mostly forced me to think more critically about the readings and provide my own insights, which I appreciated. It definitely takes a few Jasanoff reading responses to figure out what the type of writing expected in this class was. The midterm was not amazing given that we were forced to take it in 55 minutes instead of 75 like most classes do, but the assignments overall were manageable. You should expect the readings to take quite a while if you actually do them all though, which is perhaps the biggest knock against this course. Some of the readings were simply too long and towards the end of the semester one takeaway became crystal–clear (science and society cannot be separated!) which is the only time Jasanoff really gets to brag about founding her field of "Science and Technology Studies" of which that is the thesis. Great course overall though for expanding your critical thinking abilities.
You'll learn a lot in this class, but primarily from the readings. You can certainly breeze through this class without doing the readings, but I would say that since almost all of what I learned was from the assignments and not lecture/section, I would recommend following the homework if you want to gain something. Prof Jasanoff spends a lot of time simply regurgitating her own work in "Science and Technology Studies", which can feel very narcissistic and ego–driven at points. She is a very smart and accomplished academic, but she is not a good lecturer. Ultimately, almost everyone who enrolled in this class took it because it is an ESPP concentration requirement. Between this class and ESPP 77, I would definitely recommend ESPP 78 for a more critical and social studies–approach to understanding environmentalism, but it is not a groundbreaking class. I would recommend with some reservations about the teaching.

If you're an ESPP concentrator, you should take this class early and get it out of the way. You'll learn the most if you're coming in fresh, but I found as a senior there was almost nothing new here other than specific case study examples. If you're not a concentrator, I would think hard before taking it. This is not an environmental politics class, it's an STS class, so think about if that makes sense for you. The internal power dynamics of the class are toxic. If you're happy to sit down and be quiet, then you'll get through the class just fine, although be aware that it's a ludicrous amount of reading and you really have to do all of it in some degree of depth for the midterm. If you're actually interested in engaging deeply with the content, questioning things, trying to truly complicate them, then this isn't the place for you. Professor Jasanoff is nice if she likes you and not if she doesn't.
