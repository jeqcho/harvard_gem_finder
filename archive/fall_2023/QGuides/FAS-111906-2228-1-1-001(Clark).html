This is an incredibly engaging course which, while not without its challenges, is definitely one where you get out what you put in.
While learning some economic frameworks and history is critical for weekly preparation, a lot of the course centers around the reading and critical engagement with political economy literature, pushing all to not only learn the material but to leave able to participate in debates in the field –– understanding the strengths and weaknesses of various models and how all the pieces fit together.

This is the type of course where week–to–week assignments are lighter, but you really need to stay on top of the reading for exams and papers –– plus, the stronger your background in the topic for a given class day, the more you'll get out of the discussion! I wish I had been able to put a little more time in than I did, but would definitely recommend.
great professor!
Regardless of your concentration, this class is worth taking. You learn an immeasurable amount about many of the political and economic issues that dominate current events, gain a historical and global perspective on economic growth and interconnectedness, especially as it pertains to the economic systems that have the potential to both improve and diminish quality of life around the world. As a child of immigrants, this was a detailed and immersive introduction into many of the topics I and myself family think and talk about often, and I feel more equipped to study and advocate having taken the course. You will also learn a tremendous amount about academic criticism and developing a bulletproof argument and research design, which is incredibly useful if you are interested in writing a thesis or pursuing academia as a profession.
One of my best university classes ever, highly recommended.
I strongly recommend gov concentrators should take this for IR subfield or elective credit and it is probably the best gov class I've taken yet at Harvard. Prior political economy experience (gov 1004) is helpful but definitely not necessary. It nominally is pro–trade but definitely discusses the pros and cons of liberalism and protectionism in a balanced and realistic manner, without the cult like worship of the free market seen in pure economics courses. It is probably the only gov course which emphasizes reading carefully instead of skimming and is one of relatively few which has us question the findings of every paper. For instance, we discuss the data methods and statistical findings of each paper instead of memorizing the one sentence summary.
This class is really great if you are looking for a challenging but rewarding GOV class that tackles the intersection of government and economy!
