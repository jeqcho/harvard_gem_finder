If this class seems unexpectedly stressful at first, know that you're not the only one who feels that way! The class covers a wide range of important biological concepts, and lectures are fairly interesting. However, the quizzes are often very specific and can throw you off. I would advise either attending all of the lectures or at least watching them all on your own time (this will help with quizzes and make p–sets a breeze), and make sure to go to all of the sections and pay attention (sections are actually pretty fun and helpful). The best part of this course is the Harvard Forest field trip, so definitely go to that!

The Harvard Forest trip is so much fun and the Charles River trip can also be fun, just buy into it!
This course was administratively a mess. While a lot of the lectures were well done and fascinating and my TF made my section experience incredible, the assignments of the course had me constantly asking how a course so old could be such a mess. The only assessments in the course were these 7 true or false quizzes which were often poorly written in such a way that they tested intuition, reading comprehension, and mind reading more than course material. Additionally, it was expressed to students that the quizzes were written so that a random Harvard student couldn't just guess the answers, a mindset which is inherently arrogant and focused on the pride of the instructor rather than the learning of the students.

Additionally the random assignments in this course distracted from the actual assignments. For example, students would receive an email at 4:00 pm telling them to do a task before midnight for the next day's lecture. The course also had three lectures were the students provided the material. These lectures often felt pointless to me with the exception of the first time where it was led by a guest and she was able to guess every plant. The following two student made lectures felt like an attempt to fill time to me as students were expected to lecture about the material rather than the instructor educating the students as happened in the first lecture. As a student, I frequently found myself asking why we didn't just cancel these lectures since it seemed like the course couldn't think of anything to do and just asked students to generate material for them instead of planning a lecture.

The problem sets seemed to sometimes be written more with the idea of being a discussion question rather than a graded question as sometimes TFs didn't even know how to grade some ambiguous questions. Also there would be sections on the psets that we wouldn't be allowed to collaborate on which was pointless since there was no way to prevent collaboration and restricting collaboration goes against the point of psets.
