Ryan is an awesome professor, super nice and knows a ton about the field. The readings are good, and the class discussions are fun. I think this is a no–brainer class if you have the time to do the problem sets
You get out of this class what you put in – if you spend some additional time, you'll be able to have a really great product (blog posts predicting the election) that you can have for a while. Professor Enos is great and the lectures are guests are as well. Expect to spend a good amount of time figuring out R and the modeling process though.
Take this class. There is no pressure to get your forecasts right, making this class relatively low stress even though it is higher workload. Enos and the teaching staff are highly receptive to student criticism and were willing to change aspects of the course to meet student feedback. The weekly blog posts allow you to pursue aspects of the election that you are passionate about and dive deep into your interests. There are weekly assignments that sometimes require a lot of work but these assignments are really fun to complete. So if you are passionate about this, you will definitely enjoy. It was awesome to not just reproduce current literature, but to produce your own ideas. Great class!
If you're interested in electoral politics and know some R, take this course if given the chance! Enos is an expert in the field himself, and he is a kind, enthusiastic, and compassionate professor. You will spend about 8 weeks building out an election model, finalize it, and then evaluate what went right and wrong. The classroom feels cooperative and inclusive, and you come away from the course with not only a sharpened analytical toolkit, but also a nice, presentable GitHub blog and deeper understanding of how and why elections go certain ways.

I wouldn't take this course again. If you are a statistics or statistics–adjacent concentrator, you will find it a chore. The actual work–content of the course consists of making a different regression model each week on essentially the same data. This got very boring/monotonous, and also encouraged really bad data science practices since we were encouraged to "improve" our model each week, which naturally led to tinkering around with the model/variables until the R^2 was highest. Also, since the course is designed for students who may not have been exposed to regression before, the statistical models covered are very basic and uninteresting. If you read a sophisticated election forecast (like FiveThirtyEight's, for example), you'll see how much interesting statistics/modeling goes into it. So to then turn around and fit one linear/logit regression and call it a day was pretty underwhelming. Finally, the readings were actually very interesting (delving into sophisticated mathematical models of voter behavior, rational choices given certain preferences and incomplete information, etc.), but the actual course content had nothing to do with any of them. We would read an interesting theoretical paper, and then just cover different linear regression models for two hours. So, if you are interested in the more advanced theoretical treatment of voters, this class will be disappointing.

If you don't mind that though and are just interested in an introductory treatment of election forecasting, and you are good with R, then you will like the class.
If you've always been obsessed with election prediction, this is certainly the course for you. Note, though, that it is very coding intensive: if you do not have a decent grasp of R or aren't willing to put in the time to improve it will not be a fun experience. The blog posts take a while but are generally very rewarding. You'll learn a lot about politics and become a much better programmer. Enos is great and will bring in some really cool guests. Ultimately it's a great and super fun course as long as you're willing to put in the time.
Take this class if you have R experience and interested in learning how professional election analysts make forecasts! Professor Enos and the course staff are incredibly invested in students success and dedicate a lot of time to supporting you throughout a very challenging process. Forecasting elections is really really hard and it is definitely not for someone who may get discouraged easily but if you are interested in the subject matter there is definitely a great pay off. In addition, the class developed real camaraderie as we tried to figure out best forecasting methods and we definitely bonded as a class. You will love the people and learn a lot if you are willing to take the challenge!
