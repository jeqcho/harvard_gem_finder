This class focuses EXTENSIVELY on limits, and then moves into derivatives and then into very basic integrals. If you feel comfortable with derivatives and basic integrals (up to u–substitution), this course is not for you; go to a higher math. You will get far too frustrated with how much time is spent learning limits and doing limits for things you know how to easily take the derivative of. If you do choose to take this course, keep up with your Psets and go to every class! Take advantage of MQC.
3 p–sets a week that each takes about 1.5 hours. Kind of self–taught by working through worksheets in groups.
This class requires a lot of work but it is not always hard work.
This is an intro calc class, but if you're going in expecting a high–school type of formal teaching, you won't get it. The structure of the class (in my experience) is that they pass out a worksheet with the day's lesson and the teacher tells you to start on it with a group of people. If you don't know the concept period, you're stuck unless another member of the group has taken calc before. You can ask questions, but in my section the teacher usually just told students to think about it more (???). There's no note taking in a formal math notebook unless you want to make more work for yourself; you just jot down whatever you need in the margins of the worksheet usually. Honestly, you might be at a disadvantage if you have zero calc background since they don't really teach, just give you worksheet problems to do. I also had to switch out of a different teacher's class (same math class just different time slot and teacher) because his voice was hard to understand and he just didn't clarify students' questions. The course CAs are good mostly, so just pray that you get nice ones. For homework, you pretty much have to go the the math question center (MQC) if you want to get full points on PSets because there will be just little subtleties in the question directions that if you don't get, you'll be marked off for. Hannah subbed for my class period once, and from that one class I wished I was in her section since she explained things more clearly, but ask around and see who liked their teacher the most. The tests are a lot like the practice exams, so that's fine.
Be prepared to work. The class can be moderately difficult for you, as opposed to a complete nightmare, as long as you put in the effort and stay on top of the psets.
This class is not something to really be afraid of. They have all of the resources available that you would need to succeed, you just have to be willing to use them. I actually placed into Math Ma, and was very worried when I decided to take Math 1a instead. However, through attending workshops, MQC, and office hours religiously, I was able to do very well. I really believe that the key to succeeding in really any class here is to go to those extra resources when you feel confused or underprepared.
Take advantage of the MQC and office hours
You will have to be very self–sufficient in this class in order to do well. There is little instruction given in class, so it is ultimately up to you to figure out how to solve problems.
If you have taken calculus and trigonometry in high school, I would advise you to take this class. Brush up on logs, exponentials, trig, and algebra before the class starts.
Challenging; large workload; challenging exams. However, the instructors are great, and there are a lot of resources to do well. I would recommend you take calculus once already before taking this class because it's more of a reinforcing–information–you–should–already–know sorta class.
This class is likely nothing like any of the math classes you have taken in high school and certainly not in middle school. You are expected to work on problems and concepts that you have never seen before with your peers, and then the concept is lightly touched on at the end of the class to wrap it up. The concepts are not really taught by the instructors, you will have to figure them out for yourself for the most part.

If you plan on doing well, you will definitely want a solid pre calc background, and some exposure to calculus prior to taking 1A. I don't think this class moves slow enough, or gives you enough reps with the basic concepts you're using, to build up the calculus foundation you need moving forward. Like, you probably take less than 30 integrals over the course of this entire class, which really isn't enough practice to make the math seem easy enough moving forward. That being said, if you've taken a calc class before, and you can crank out simple derivatives/integrals easily, I think this is a great class to introduce yourself to math at Harvard. You probably won't be learning any new concepts, but your depth of understanding will be expanded, and hey, you can think of it as an easier class for freshman fall.

It was easy for me, but I genuinely cannot imagine how anyone coming from PreCalc could survive in this class without put in more time than they have. This class isn't hard if you already took calculus. Expect to receive little to no instruction from your TF. You will be told what topic you are going to "learn" about, and then you will go straight into work groups and be expected to solve the problems without any instruction or guidance from the TFs. Expect to be frustrated a lot during class since you'll often find yourself unable to solve problems, because you were never taught how, and highly dependent on the knowledge your peers have on calculus. You will not be able to do well in this class if you work only by yourself. Find someone who knows calculus and work with them, because there is no lecture or instruction given by the TFs during the class. The assessments aren't too bad, and you are able to reassess for full credit. The two projects are completely pointless and irrelevant to calculus, but they're graded SAT/UNSAT so they're super easy.
I would tell them that this is a very rewarding class that gives you a solid background if you are going to go onto any further math courses at Harvard. If not, it is a manageable class that does take a lot of time, but is doable.
Though the constant stream of PSets may seem intimidating, their constant presence is really worth it since it prevents you from forgetting too much between classes while further hammering in the material while they're still fresh in your mind.
