This is a very well–run course. Professor Shair is clearly passionate about the material he teaches. His lectures are effective and engaging, and his slides are organized and useful tools for contemplating both the human stories and the rigorous science underlying drug development, past and present. You come away from this course with an appreciation for the intense and rigorous process of designing and testing novel drugs; the class really opens your eyes to this field, its challenges, and the impact that small molecules can have on patients' lives. The psets and take–home exam are very reasonable in terms of time and difficulty, although I think it would have been helpful to provide assignment keys in addition to just individual comments. Moreover, for me, there was a steep learning curve for the software employed in the course (MOE), but ample help is available through OH and email if you ask for it. Discussion sections were also a neat way to take a deep dive into a past scientific publication every week. Overall, I definitely recommend this course if you are interested in drug development and therapeutics, as it offers a nice introduction to this field.
Take the course if you are interested in small–molecule target therapy!
Class is a gem for the credits it gives. You'll learn how to use MOE, which is not too hard. But MOE itself is a somewhat unreliable/inconsistent software for molecular docking, which leaves you wondering how applicable the skills you learn in the class actually are.
This is a great course if you're interested in learning the design of pharmaceutical drugs. There's pretty minimal work (2 problem sets, a midterm, and a final project), allowing you to still engage with the material without stressing a lot about your grade. Prof Shair is a great lecturer and is clearly enthusiastic about the material, which also helps. Lectures can get a little boring, but the implications of the material as a whole are meaningful enough to keep you engaged.
You might be skeptical of MOE at first, but it is actually a very useful tool. The topics are really engaging with great stories to tell. The instructor and TFs are passionate and really excited about therapeutics.

This class is very much dependent on how much you invest into it, but nevertheless super interesting and illuminating in terms of understanding what is at the cutting edge of biomedicine.
This course provides a solid overview of the small molecule drug discovery, design, and development process. The problem sets can be a bit long, so make sure to get a head start on them. The workload of the course was manageable, especially relative to the amount of content you're taught.
This is a great class to learn about small molecule drug design!
This is a pretty low–stress, interesting and useful course to learn about why certain drugs work in the body, and how to make ones that work more effectively and efficiently. However, there are only two psets, and your entire performance in the course is pretty much contingent on your understanding of how to use the MOE (molecular operating environment). Hence, I cannot stress enough how important it is to pay attention in the early lectures at the beginning of the course when MOE is explained haha. Overall, though, this is a great course to take, and you'll get the opportunity to collaborate with others through the section component and the final grant proposal, which is also a group project. I would highly recommend taking it if you're looking for an interesting and useful (but not too stressful) chemistry or general science class, especially if you have previous biology and organic chemistry coursework under your belt.

Because of this, the class is more loosely–structured and allows more room for just genuine intellectual curiosity. All the work is essentially take–home and very manageable. Take this class if you want to explore more aspects of chemistry and other ways to apply this field outside of pre–med.
