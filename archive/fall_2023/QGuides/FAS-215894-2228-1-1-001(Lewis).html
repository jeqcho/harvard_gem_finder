This course was a pedagogical catastrophe. I write this not out of spite but out of sadness and concern, disappointment at the gap between what this course promised to be and what it was. And I write this as someone who cares deeply about education, immigration, and history: If this course is not changed dramatically, it will produce poor historians, writers, and activists.

A list of major problems with this course:

1) Ethics: Although this is supposed to be an ethics and civics gened, there were no ethical frameworks used or any meaningful ethics teaching. Every unit would go a little something like this: We would read about a border that contributed to some violence, the professor would imply heavily that borders = violence = bad, this would be reiterated in section discussion, and then wed move on. This used truisms borders exclude people! to avoid meaningful ethical discussions: Is exclusion inherently violent or immoral? What are the relationships between violence and immorality? Can we generate axioms or a standardized set of ethical considerations when evaluating a particular border? I came into this course with experience in immigration protest and a strong belief in the need to abolish or at least heavily curtail borders. I thought Id be asked to engage with other beliefs in order to test, bolster, and better communicate my own. In fact, the opposite happened. The courses very obvious but tacit anti–borders orientation leaves its students utterly unprepared to have substantive, difficult conversations with people who believe in borders because the class never in good faith entertained those beliefs, and the ethical reasoning behind them.
2) History: This course jumped around in both space and time in a way that was disorienting and created shallow coverage. We spent much of the time on Europe World War I and the Cold War and yet had almost zero historical context for those units. (This was related to siloed, out of context assignments, explained further below.) Without an ethical framework affording cohesion, the course needed historical momentum and narrative. Instead, it felt like we were randomly picking borders with next to no historical awareness the plebiscites, for example and the only unifying thread was Eurocentrism. I understand the impulse to start and end in the United States, but if this course wants to be global, it should be willing to spend more time afield the Koreas, Africa post–colonization, etc.!
3) Assignments: The assignments were poorly constructed. The midterm in particular asked students to make an argument from six lines in an excel column. It was framed as a map exercise, but the map was essentially irrelevant; all the data came from those six lines. I got a near perfect score simply by reiterating the lecture on plebiscites, because there was zero other historical material from the class, and we werent allowed to even try to understand the historical context using outside material. The assignment implied the way to succeed was 1) restate exactly what the professor says; and 2) imply causation from correlation in the numbers. These are the opposite of the lessons students especially young historians should be learning. Similarly, with the final project: This isnt 2015 anymore; podcasts are no longer new and creative. The course needs to either commit to having a creative final project that lets people draw on their creative skillsets, or have a final paper/exam. This in–between is frustrating its as much or more work than a paper, but not truly creative enough to let students think outside the box.
4) Technology: Almost half of the lectures were waylaid by technological failures that sucked up class time and impeded learning. Things like setting up subtitles on a video, using Google Earth, etc. would neutralize entire class activities and knock out 10–15 minutes of class time. Yes, technology can be finnicky, but not a single other professor Ive had at Harvard has struggled to this extent, especially after the Zoom year, which afforded most teachers a basic level of technological competence. There are basic technological skills all instructors should have mastered by now.
5) Fraught lectures: Sensitive, politically complex discussions should be led by experts: We should not be learning about issues as fraught as Israel–Palestine from someone unequipped to facilitate deep conversations. This unit alone was shockingly mishandled. First, we spent an entire class watching a documentary with no context a documentary that was flagrantly propagandistic, playing machine gun and bomb noises whenever Jewish sources spoke. After the documentary, we had time for exactly two questions: One student in the class asked how the Jews got their weapons, to which the professor responded I dont know. !!! Another student asked who made the movie, to which the professor also responded that she wasnt sure. Beyond the obvious pedagogical neglect inherent in showing a movie without knowing its sourcing, it was devastating to hear absolutely zero response to a basic question about the conflict (phrased in a way that definitely demanded unpacking!) The next lecture featured some ex post facto attempts to clean up the mess, which seemed shoehorned in, given how much the class ran over. Anyways, the damage was done. I think the unit was indicative of a bigger flaw in the current gened system, which allows professors who are experts in other fields to each tenuously related subjects as pet projects. The professor told us she taught this class because she felt she had to after Trump era border separation. Im sympathetic to this political starting point, but not the fact that it emboldened her to claim expertise she doesnt have on regions and issues that matter a great deal to a lot of people. Perhaps a co–professor would go a long way.
6) Resistance to feedback: Earlier in the course, I submitted versions of these complaints via the anonymous feedback form. At the start of the next class, the professor summarily entertained them and dismissed them, in a way that made clear the point of the form was not to modify the course at all, but to reactionarily defend its decisions. Similarly, in the last lecture, we were told to be nice in our Q guide reviews, lest we hinder the teaching careers of our TFs. Do you all not understand that this defeats the purpose of the Q guide? Not to mention how condescending and educationally bankrupt it is. Both the mid–semester feedback form and the Q–Guide recognize that students may have legitimate issues with the way a course is being taught. The correct response to this is not to either ignore that feedback or preemptively shape it. The correct response is to LISTEN to the students.
7) Attention: Many lectures started with or included a plea for students to not be distracted. Yes, technology and the pandemic have had a deleterious effect on attention spans and attitudes toward learning. But theres a reason why we were bored! The lectures lacked cohesion or a driving principle; they felt like a random dissemination of information with weak gestures towards analysis (see the ethics point.) Attention is earned. If more than half your class is either not showing up or doing other work during class, that should be some indication that the class needs to be doing more.

In sum, please do not take this course especially if you are a freshman. It will prepare you poorly for later history courses, ethics courses, and courses that require writing. Upperclassmen take this course only if you need a gem.
