The course is very much what you put into it—that being said, there is still something to gain even if you cannot read the entirety of every single book. The lectures and seminar discussions are highly productive and interesting, and the course is still doable, even if you're not in a place to give it your complete all.
tl;dr Definitely interesting but not remotely worthwhile.
PROS: The books and the lectures are the reason to take this course. One should intend to do all of the reading with at most one exception. This class is far too much of a commitment (there is an absurd amount of busy work) to take without an intention to read a large portion of the material. If you like hearing elucidating description and analysis of great works of literature (which you should; its fun) these lectures will be extremely enjoyable. If you are good at speeding through high school english paper style assignments (which, given your situation, you probably are), the value of the readings and lectures may outweigh the burden of the assignments.
CONS: The expos requirement of the course ruins its structure. The papers have no relation to the overall goal of understanding relationships between texts. The writing lab is extremely repetitive and aims to teach material using a set of texts which are not remotely suited to the pedagogical goals. The assignments are extremely labor intensive without any intellectual purpose. The paper feedback and grading focuses almost exclusively on structure and stylistic without any interest in the validity or quality of analysis. The goal is to write towards a secret yet firm rubric as opposed to saying something interesting. Instead of one semester of this, you are signing up for two. The need to prepare for the final exam is the only officially imposed incentive to seek understanding of the books. Of course, a 3h exam is inherently a surface–level evaluation, which is not necessarily a bad thing.
Another important con for me was the quality of the section.
