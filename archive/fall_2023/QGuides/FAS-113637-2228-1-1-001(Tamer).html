Ec 1126 is genuinely an amazing class. It will give you a dive under the hood of how statistical methods work, and prof Tamer is amazing. What I really really (really!) love about prof Tamer's teaching style is that he emphasizes an intuitive understanding of the concepts above all else. Of course, the equations are essential to being able to describe ideas in econometrics precisely, but I believe that he values being able to explain things in words just as much (if not more) than in equations. He's also hilarious. This class is not easy and will require a time commitment from you if you really want to understand the material, but I wholeheartedly recommend it.

My understanding is that he gives a lot of B+ so beware. Overall, took away an understanding of basic econometric methods. Also, if you're taking stat 110 concurrently, read the section in Blitzstein's textbook on conditional expectation. 1126 draws heavily on the concepts and you only cover it in stat at the very end of the semester (even though it is a basic concept), so look ahead at the material

This class will give you a good conceptual understanding of the different statistical methods used in empirical social science research (particularly in economics). You learn about what you can do with observational data (most of social sciences data, contrasted with experimental data in the natural sciences), and how to deal with many problems that emerge with data, including different methods like instrumental variables or fixed effects analysis (with panel data). The class tries to focus on the theory behind these things, but I think it doesn't do this perfectly. There is not enough focus on how to actually apply these concepts on datasets by coding, which is a shame. That being said, the lectures are AMAZING and your best resource. Lecture notes are very good, though not perfect. PSets can be tricky, so take them seriously, specially since graders are a bit nit–picky. Go to Section; it will help you so much. Be careful with the midterm, it can be tougher than expected and not give you enough time.
If you are thinking about taking this as an elective, I have heard STAT 139 might be better because of the focus on coding.

There were many strengths in the course. I think the main strength was associated with the content and how the information in the class was critical for people wanting to be economists or who are interested in quantitative social sciences. I appreciated not just learning how to do something, but also delving into the theory and the reasoning behind why economists do things the way they do (on topics relating to data), making explicit the assumptions that are made and the weaknesses behind given methods. However, I wished that at times, we focused more on the big picture (i.e. applications) and were less focused on the mathematics.

I also think the Professor was another strength of the course. I thought that he presented information in a clear and logical way (and encouraged questions), generated enthusiasm for the class, and was very approachable.
