Comments
To do well on the exams, comprehensively reviewing and actually finishing the daily worksheets you receive in class (along with the PSETS) is a must prior to the exam. There also is a couple opportunities to retest on the sections of previous exams in which you may have not received perfect credit – which is a huge advantage of this class.
This class is certainly worth taking. Phil is a great lecturer, and you can tell he genuinely cares about the course. The material is straightforward and the assignments are fair.
Lots of collaboration rather than actual teaching.
The content for most parts matches 21a, but there are less PSets and the Economics material towards the end is very interesting and easy to understand. The Exams are fair and the Reassessment material is very very useful
This course is a great way to build your skills both mathematically and personally. I really grew as a student as a result of this class, and I fell as though I have the skills to be successful in my concentration and beyond. I think you get out what you put into this course, so if you take the time, it will be a great experience.
Utilize your resources
If you are going into econ or any social science, take this class rather than 21a. The difference is only about 10% of the course but those concepts are quite useful.
If you need to take a multivariable calculus class and can take 18A, do it. End of semester material much friendlier than 21A.
I took it for the econ honors requirement and would recommend this over 21A for other econ concentrators. The class is well–taught and well–paced. The grading is very reasonable because they allow you to redo all exam questions except for the final assessment.
If you have not previously taken multivariable calculus, make sure that you are putting in the work to excel. The course can be challenging, but I am so glad that I pushed through. At the end of the day, I learned so much from Math 18a out of all the classes I have taken this semester.
Don't take it if you don't have to. The material is not extremely challenging, there is just an humongous lack of actual teaching and those who had already taken multivariable calc in the past did much better, because we never actually learned a lesson in class. Looking at the worksheet solutions was more efficient then wasting an hour and 15 in class and walking out confused.
This is a great class for those interested in Economics or three–dimensional calculus. Phil is very fair/accommodating and the course is well–structured.
This is an awesome class. If you've ever wondered "How will learning this help me in real life?," this class is for you. Although you may need to study a lot to master the calculus, the applications to optimization and economics make the class very interesting and pertinent. Big advice... watch the course videos!! They are super super helpful. Having a background in Calculus III will help, but it is by no means necessary. I took this class with AP Calc BC and I did very well!!
Math 18a seems like the better of two evils when looking for econ honors requirements. While both 18a and 21a are more physics–related math than anything else, at least 18a had some concepts that were useful for econ.
I'd recommend coming to the class with previous knowledge about multivariable calculus. This course goes over it very fast and not as in depth. Be prepared for lots of work together time and figuring things out alone after class.
I put off taking Math 18A freshman year and sophomore year (and finally took it junior year) because I was a bit scared of taking a math course as a non–STEM concentrator at Harvard. If you're feeling a bit nervous as I was, I would strongly recommend taking the plunge and taking Math 18A with Phil. The class is taught in a very accessible format, assignments—while frequent—are very manageable, and the assessments are very fair (I would strongly recommend going through every practice test posted online in preparation—I did this and found it to be very helpful). My biggest piece of advice would be to form a study group at the beginning of the semester and stick to it—it makes the weekly sets a lot more manageable and the class more fun in general. Phil has a good sense of humor and while I thought this would be my most annoying class of the semester, I genuinely enjoyed going to it every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday.
Multi gets a bad rap, and despite what many others might say, I think this course was great. I get that for some it might not be easy (especially when this is your first time taking the course), and sometimes Professor Wood seems too smart for you, but I do think it's set up really great. From the hands–on and intuitive approach to learning, to the reassessments it is designed for you to do well, and at the very least understand what's going on inherently. Professor Wood is a great guy, super nice, super smart and generally accessible. If you go to his OH or ask him questions during class, he can be very helpful. I genuinely believe he wants to help students out, which is always a great quality in a professor. In all, I would recommend for social studies people as I think Professor Wood will continue to refine the course to make it better and I personally think it's a better alternative to 21a if you can.
