This class will have you doing some very unique assignments, and the content is incredibly valuable for civilians understanding military orders.
Taking this class was definitely a unique academic experience. At first, the debate component of the course intrigued me. However, as the course continued, I realized this was a very compelling way to discuss subjects relevant to the class. Furthermore, having to write papers for each debate was also helpful, both for the debate preparation and for the understanding of the class topics. Finally, the professor and the TF are very helpful and always willing to help the students. Overall, a good course; just be prepared to do a lot of reading and debating in front of the class at least twice throughout the semester.
Didn’t really know what to expect going into this course but debating these ethical questions has resulted in some of the most interesting conversations I’ve had at Harvard. I don’t think I was necessarily the right type of student for the course in that my interests lean more quantitative but got something out of it regardless
I would highly recommend this course, especially to anyone interested in national security or war studies. Even anyone just slightly interested in the topic should take the class because Prof. Rosen is great! I think this course accomplishes a lot in teaching material and skills as the debate component of the course is great public speaking experience. The first half covers foundational material and it is important to be engaged in the readings and lectures here. In the second half, I would recommend being as engaged for all debates but you can get by without too much work on the weeks that you don't debate you will just have to do a little more by the time the finals comes around. I also thought the final exam was actually very effective at tying the course together.
The course teaches ethical theories of war and military history. It is very discussion heavy and most of the classes are actually structured debates between students. If you want to get more exposure to public speaking and debate, this is a good course for you. The workload is fairly light and consists of writing argumentative essays on military ethics.
This course very much forces you into a box in a way that forces intellectual growth; it is based on the fundamental assertion that war is a reality that will occur. and discusses how war can be conducted most ethically. Do not take this class if you want to discuss the ethicality of war itself, but if you want to discuss the real considerations of military and civilian leaders around how to deal with very real aspects of war, this is a good fit for you. It also is a great introduction to ethical reasoning and will build your debate skills.
This is an interesting class at times, but it is also extremely broad. Be prepared to do a lot of readings that can be not super intuitive at times. If you're a good debater, you will do better for sure. AVOID CROSS–EX as your role if possible; this is the worst role in the debate since you will not really be able to show off your argumentative capabilities.
This class is excellent; I found it engaging, and it forced me to challenge my beliefs. Conduct in war includes some decisions made under impossible circumstances, and this class asks you to think deeply about what is moral and what is not when weighing gut–wrenching tradeoffs. During this class, you may be asked to argue for a position with which you do not agree, but this is all the more opportunity to sharpen your thinking. The readings for this class are also interesting and inform the debates appropriately without being overwhelming. Of note: After the initial 4ish weeks of overview, the workload for this class varies, and you will have to put in significantly more hours in the 2 weeks you are debating and writing a paper than in the weeks you are just reading and listening to the debates.
The debate component might seem strange but it is actually very useful to your own learning and to really seeing how the characters in the reading were thinking and what led them to make the decisions they did.
