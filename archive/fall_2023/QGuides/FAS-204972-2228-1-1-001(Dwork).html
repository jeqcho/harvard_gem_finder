The course content itself is awesome and Professor Dwork is great. However, the problem sets can be quite a struggle to work through because it's mostly new material and there isn't a lot of guidance/ clarification on these new topics.
This was the worst–run class I have taken at Harvard. The only good part of this class was learning from Cynthia Dwork, who is clearly a true genius. However, all the other parts of this class (especially the EXTREMELY LAZY TFs) ruined it. I think the professor should have done something to keep her staff accountable after multiple students complained when it became clear that the TF and CA were being intentionally lazy and trying to avoid their responsibilities, by scheduling only 1 hour of office hours per week at 12pm on Wednesday/Thursday (which students couldn't even go to). Additionally, the materials for the course were very lacking; there are no course recordings and the lecture slides are not really readable since they have too little information on them. Finally, the problem sets were very hard (and TF/CA are inaccessible), and feedback took forever (more than 4 weeks in one case). Additionally, I felt like the law school joint classes were disappointing, maybe because I expected to learn something legal but instead just had to listen to law school students blab about their own personal experiences and opinions. Also, for some reason even though this class was at 9am which was bad enough, it was decided that the joint classes should start at 8:45am on Mondays... That being said, I thought this class did a pretty good job at exposing us to ethics in CS and those considerations, although not really the legal side of things.
Content and lectures are interesting but the workload is extremely high; I really loved the class but would find it difficult to recommend this unless you are taking a relatively light courseload otherwise.
This is the best class in the CS department in how it combines policy and theoretical CS. If you're interested in how privacy and security concerns actually manifest in terms of the mechanics and technical aspects, I highly recommend this class. It was really cool seeing theoretical CS connected so directly to real world implications.
The problem sets are hard!! And there are errors throughout them. Be prepared to put in a good amount of work and find study partners soon – but it's worthwhile for the intellectual payoff if you put in that effort.
Mindblowing concepts, terrible course logistics. Be prepared to learn amazing new ideas about algorithmic fairness and privacy but have to tread through lectures that make little effort at explaining things intuitively, TF's that don't seem to be very invested in the course at all, and an amazing professor whose forte might not be explaining things in normal English. Take this course if you want to learn about what "fairness" actually means, how we can achieve privacy by adding noise, and a sampling of interesting cryptosystems (zero knowledge proofs, semantic security, etc).
One of the best courses I have taken at the school. Difficult material but extremely worthwhile course. Absolutely recommend to those wanting to dive into theory of a new and emerging subject.
Aside from being one of the best courses on embedded ethics, it tied together theory and application seamlessly in a manner that I have not seen in other theoretical computer science classes. The fields of algorithmic fairness and differential privacy are relatively new (and spearheaded by Professor Dwork herself), so a lot of the ideas and proofs we worked with were recent discoveries. It was an extremely fast paced course, and I feel like we barely scraped the surface of all these topics. That being said, because they were so new, the topics and homework assignments were extremely difficult. The course is held jointly with the law school, so each week we'd have one discussion class with the law students and one CS class. The joint class was really interesting and I was nice to get that kind of experience of a discussion format on topics such as policy and whatnot. Because we only had one CS lecture each week, a lot of the homework problems involved introducing some new concept and then having us self learn it before doing a problem. For that reason, a lot of the problems felt impossible because we were basically reproducing proofs from recent papers from experts on the topic. However, working through this class was one of the most fulfilling experiences I've ever had! The material itself is incredible and sososo important and Professor Dwork obviously really cares that her students learn.
The class is definitely challenging but extremely rewarding. Even if you don't think you interested in theoretical CS, if the content/topic of this class is of enough interest, I definitely recommend taking it. The joint law school aspect is very unique and a cool experience. While the assignments are difficult and can be stressful to complete, the grading of this class is very fair. Overall highly recommend to everyone just because the content is so interesting and relevant to the real–world, but would recommend with reservations if you are not comfortable with theoretical rigor (of the level of CS124).
The CS track of the class is extremely interesting as it brings math and rigor to tackle fairness and privacy issues in a principled manner. It covers topics in Algorithmic Fairness, Differential Privacy, and Modern Cryptography. This is a LOT of material packed into one course, but it is worth it. The interaction with the law students is a very nice touch that makes this class even more pleasant and enriching.
Note that this class is very time consuming, and you will need to have a solid background in algorithmics to solve some of the problem sets if you take the CS track . Naturally, proofs also require a solid background in probability and statistics.
This course is great material–wise. It's quite cool (and increasingly important) to learn about the topics in this course, especially considering Prof. Dwork effectively created the field! However, do note that the psets are quite heavy on the math and the course can feel more like a math one than a CS one. Have pset buddies going into the course and make sure to go to the law/joint classes to actually get the most out of the class, especially when it comes to the application of the material to the real world!
