Do yourself a favor and TAKE THIS COURSE. Do not take math21b. This class is literally the most low–stress class I’ve ever taken at Harvard. Shlomo is a great professor. He explains everything clearly and goes through the material at a great pace. Has a great sense of humor too. We also only learn about interesting stuff without delving into too many math concepts that we won’t ever come across again. The psets are very reasonable both in length and in content. Each pset consists of 3 parts: hand calculation, conceptual questions and proofs, and computation (matlab) questions. The proof questions are not too hard and he gives you hints. The computation questions also come with clear instructions and it’s generally very satisfying to see the application of the concepts we learned in class. And did I mention this class is 80% PSETS 20% MIDTERMS (and both midterms are open notes)? It’s such a great class to learn interesting concepts without having to stress too much about grades/tests. As long as you understand the concepts, the midterms are very straightforward. He gives you hints for the harder questions, and you won’t need anything outside of what is taught in class (no need to read the textbook, but if you want to, that’s great). Just make sure you pay attention to when Sholmo goes over the pset questions in class because he doesn’t publish pset solutions and there are no practice exams. Overall, I really loved this class, loved the mix of concepts and application, loved the structure. If you have to fulfill a linear algebra requirement, this is the best class you could ever ask for.

I would recommend taking this course over the other linear algebra/multivariable calculus options. This class actually shows you applications of the often abstract–seeming topics covered in these subjects.

Shlomo is clearly passionate about the material he is presenting, but it can often be difficult to follow what he is saying as he frequently uses analogies that don't really make sense to the class: i.e. describing the set of subjective matrices as "santa claus" and the injective set of matrices as "a juice machine". The main downside of the class is that the classes are all lecture based, with the only "problem solving" during classes occurring for about only 1 minute every three classes, before Shlomo cuts it off for more lecture. The psets and class notes are extremely helpful though, and are really all that is necessary to do well in the course.

As for prerequisites for the class, I had never coded before this class. There was definitely a learning curve in MATLAB for the first 2–3 psets, but I quickly learned that if you go to at least 2 office hours a week, you should be fine.

There are three different linear algebra courses on campus (Math 22a, Math 21b, and AM22a). The reason why I took this course is because Psets are worth 80% of your grade and the rest goes to the two "midterms" (10% to one midterm, and 10% to the final). Unfortunately, you don't learn too much fundamentals of linear algebra since we didn't cover determinants and some other major aspects of linear algebra. If you want to really learn about linear algebra and the math behind it, I recommend Math 22a. If you want to learn linear algebra without the proofs, take Math 21b. This course is a bit more manageable than those two because there are less midterms, but be prepared to learn a lot on your own, review notes outside of class, and go to office hours.
