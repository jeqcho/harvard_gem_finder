Attend classes, don't fall behind on lectures, and don't worry if you find the material challenging. RL is hard to learn for students with so many notations and concepts. Seek help on assignments if you are struggling. I don't think Sham and Lucas expect their students to do everything on the assignment by themselves. Finally, Sham and Lucas are legends in their field. It's a great privilege to be taught by them. Definitely use the opportunity to get involved in research etc., if that comes.
This is a rigorous class, and I think the hours reported may be deceivingly low (it's a lot of work just not uniformly done, read: 15 hours one week but like 0 the next). It's very easy to space out lectures once the math and notation becomes heavy. It's hard to catch back up and it's unfortunately possible to do a decent amount of the problem set without really understanding what's happening if you are good at statistics/reading pseudocode. My advice would be to stay on top of lectures, utilize section/OH, and start psets early. You do get two weeks to do psets, which is why this might seem like a chill–ish class, but the psets are hard and the programming can especially take a long time if you get stuck on bugs. If you were to take this class and have only taken the really large and established classes, I'd be flexible with any bumps that may occur (e.g. lack of practice material, bugs in psets) just because this is a new class and the course staff does work really hard. For prep, I would definitely finish Stat 110 first (not concurrent) and CS181 will offer the best background (definitely can do without, but I would have had a much harder time).

Since this was the first iteration, I'm sure changes will be made. But overall if you are interested in RL, this will be a perfect class for you!

Overall, I love reinforcement learning but the course needs iterations.

For those looking for a class that's easy or implementation–heavy but theory–light, this is not the one for you. It's heavy on math and statistics—but honestly does a pretty good job building up the core RL fundamentals from scratch. There really isn't a ton of good RL material on the Internet; rather than learning things like MDP's and policy gradients from half–baked Medium articles, this class will teach you the fundamental concepts, rigorously demonstrate how they work, and motivate things from a first–principles perspective. You will emerge from this class capable of reading almost any RL paper.

With that being said, it's a bit of a mess. The teaching staff was disorganized, and the lecture slides often had notational errors (although this was usually Sham, not Lucas). In other CS classes with a singular focus (e.g. 121), you get this nice tapestry of ideas throughout the semester that build on one another and serve as core to the course. The same is true in the *material* of 184, but not the execution—as a student, you have to put in a lot of mileage discovering the intricate connections of things that really could just be said in lecture. The homeworks were generally fine, but I was never clear how they would be graded + the programming components could be much better.
