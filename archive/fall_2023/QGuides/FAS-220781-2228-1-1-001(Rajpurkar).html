Super amazing course with awesome instructor, you should take it if you're interested in AI research, and potentially even if you're curious about how AI research is accomplished. The course can be dense at times with content (esp. nitty AI details) but it's very helpful for learning how to be a better researcher or even computer science. Very fun way to get your requirements done.
Definitely take this course if you are interested in diving into deep learning research. Just be warned that the project is quite hefty and you should definitely start early on it!
Overall this class was pretty chill for the most part. Lectures were well organized and I learned a lot about Pytorch, python, AI paper layouts and presentations, different ML tools like weights&biases, hydra, etc... Looking back at my previous classes, this was by far the course where I gained the most hands on experience, and probably the most practical in terms of how the skills would transfer to industry. You definitely learn how to put together a research paper, and how to look for new ideas from existing papers. I think the greatest thing I got out of the class is being able to digest AI papers and understand the high level approaches, and now being able to go and build my own pytorch models.

The majority of the class was great, and the assignments didn't take too much time and allows you to regurgitate the material. The main pain point of the course is the final project, where we had to try and improve upon the performance of Pranav's chex x–ray classification model CheXZero. That was the main goal of the project and we had to come up with our own ideas and approaches. Overall it was a cool idea, but I think it was too broad and unscoped, and many of our initial attempts failed. This was especially annoying because running the code would take a very long time, so a lot of time would be spent just waiting to see if an idea could work. Additionally, CheXZero was recently published in Nature and it seems really hard to do something groundbreaking that would improve upon the model within a few weeks, especially when our AWS accounts were resource–limited so we did not have the compute power to even get a model that could be directly compared with the original. I think overall there was very little guidance for the project and we were fumbling around in the dark just trying things randomly in hope that something stuck. Out of the 5 groups, I think only 2 ended up with something that had some small improvement, but it still felt like everything was very hacky.

Overall it is a decent class, especially for being new. Pranav is super cool and has done a lot of amazing work. If you take this class you will definitely learn a lot, but be prepared to spend a lot of time on the group final project (groups of 3). It is a very back–heavy class as you probably spend at most 5 hours on each assignment, but the project will take a long time because of the time it takes to train and run the code.
Take this course! Pranav is super knowledgable and clearly a star in the field. This course will give you the best bang for your buck. Psets are not overwhelming at all while you'll learn a ton from the project and lectures. Really can't recommend more highly.
I highly recommend this course to anyone who is new to research / unsure if you want to do research. Unlike most classes I have taken before, this class is very hands–on. I learned about practical tools and methodologies that researchers in the AI communities uses. I also learned about how to write papers and give presentations. The final project is a big component of the class, it is structured in a way to leverage the tools and knowledge we learned throughout the class.
If you are at all interested in a structured research experience with cutting–edge ML research then this class is worth checking out. There is a lot of work that you need to do on your own to make a solid research project, but you get out what you put in. If you take the time, you will likely end up working towards creating a publishable research project.
My takeaways of CS 197 were as follows: (1) I became very well–versed, close to expert level, in zero–shot learning, which was a complete mystery to me before taking this course (2) I'd done many ML projects before, but I learned about Pranav's workflow when it comes to ML research and definitely had the cleanest codebase for a research project than I've ever had before (3) I completed a high–stakes research project that took a lot of time and energy (I estimated ~100–150 hrs of my time, and 200–300 hrs from my group mates) that ended up going pretty well and is probably the research I'm most proud of at Harvard so far. Take this class if you want to become an expert in zero–shot learning (or read the CLIP paper if you just want to understand it quickly), want to learn about best software engineering principles when doing ML research (I was surprised by the amount of tools I was unfamiliar with), and have the time to complete a large project in the last 6 weeks of the semester.
Would recommend this course as a more practical introduction to coding and to AI, aws, git, wandb, and how to read, understand and conduct research.
This class is good overall (especially for the first time that it was offered). A few wrinkles need to be smoothed out but the material and final project were interesting and enjoyable.
This is a good class to learn more about vision–language models and chest x–ray identification, but is not as broad about learning to do ML research as advertised. As the first offering of this course, this year had some bumps that I expect will be smoothed out in the future. The project is a cool opportunity if you're interested in ML applications to medicine but otherwise feels like a game to maximize output results, not acquire a deeper understanding of ML models.
