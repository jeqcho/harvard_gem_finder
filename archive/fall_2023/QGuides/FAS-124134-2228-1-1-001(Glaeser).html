What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Ed goes through the material **VERY** fast so if you have trouble following in lecture please know that it is not your problem and if you study the material after class you'll be fine.
This class is hard. It's really not for undergraduates, since the grades aren't meant to be focused on. But it's a great course in game theory and the modeling/price theory was good, too.
There is so much content and it comes so fast that you will invariably fall behind during lecture. Do not worry. The primary purpose of the midterm is to have you review for the midterm. I learned most of the content in the two weeks before the exam.

Do not try to write down the calculus with Ed. It is not possible. Instead, try to get the broad strokes of what steps he is taking and if you have time try it at home.

The verbals are really hard, and there is no right answer. The first purpose of the verbal is to set up the problem (who are the agents? what are they optimizing? what is in the budget set? etc.). The second purpose of the verbal is to get a model where you can say ~something~. Answer the question that your model can answer, even if it isn't exactly the question asked in the problem set. I wouldn't worry so much about making the model general, at least to start. Focus on tractability and then go from there.
Really excellent class. People complain that Ed focuses too much on grind algebra w/o intuition. Maybe a little, but far less so than in 1011a, which is itself still a worthwhile class. More conceptual than people tend to give it credit for, and game theory is a lot of fun.
The course is difficult, but going through problem sets and learning how to handle verbals seem like valuable skills that could be beneficial for any econ student.
