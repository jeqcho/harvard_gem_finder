This is a really interesting course, to say the least. Prof. Rothschild +Seow are great and really encourage you to explore. I think the hardest aspect is there is so much flexibility that sometimes it can be paralyzing to decide what you want to do your final project on. This may change with future iterations of the course, but it is definitely a class to take intellectual risks in. You will get out what you put in. Also, it attracts really interesting people!
Take this class! A paper every week might seem a little daunting, but the weekly assignments are completion–based: the low stakes and open–ended prompts are a great way to push your writing forward. Professor Seow and Professor Rothschild are both fantastic, as are the rest of the teaching staff. The class environment is really friendly and the discussions are engaging and productive. I came into this class as a science concentrator, with little to no background in history, but I still felt like my perspective was valued in the class. Regardless of your background, if you're passionate about climate and want to get better at writing about it, this is the class to take.
This is a fantastic class; the professors and course staff are knowledgeable and deeply passionate about helping students write about climate change on their own terms. The weekly responses are some of the most valuable pieces of writing I've ever put together for my own understanding of climate change. If you've ever felt hopeless about the world, and if you've ever wanted the freedom and direction to find a bit of hope – take this class.
The weekly writing assignments may seem a little daunting, but they are relatively low stakes, so it shouldn't be too stressful!
If you're interested in exploring different ways of writing, this class is a great opportunity to do so! The assignments provide a great opportunity to try different methods of reflecting and communicating on big topics, and the takeaways are definitely applicable in subject areas beyond climate change. The emphasis on different styles of writing was especially refreshing after three years of feeling boxed into a very formal and detached kind of academic writing, and the weekly readings gave very useful examples of different ways you could break out of that mold while still tackling important topics in a rigorous manner.
Take this class if you want a lot of opportunities to practice your writing and explore new styles and genres of writing. It's a lot of fun and very low stakes. The whole teaching staff really wants you to improve your writing skills and grow as a student, writer, and informer of climate change.
TAKE THIS CLASS!!! Yes two hours is a long time but every class the teaching staff brings in cookies or treats of some kind for the break we take after the first hour!

The way assignments worked for this class was having readings to do for each class on Wednesdays, then in class you discuss the readings in small and large groups, then you have a response paper (2–3 pages, although these are loose guidelines) due Monday night each week. You get one week that you can skip writing the response, but the responses are also graded on a completion basis! Then at the end of the semester you put together and revise four of your responses into a portfolio that serves as the final paper/project! Overall an absolutely INCREDIBLE class that every Harvard student should take. Emma and Victor are incredible professors that you can learn so much from and the rest of the teaching staff is just as good.

This course provided me with a completely different way of thinking about climate change, where previously my background had been more about the science/math behind it, this class had us looking at the effects of climate change on a lot of different scales and timeframes. The discussions were always really interesting and the weekly responses were really fun to do, especially because we were encouraged to explore whatever writing styles we could possibly come up with (short stories, personal narratives, twitter threads, literally anything you could think of). This was the kind of writing class where you were not in any way penalized for pushing yourself to be creative and step outside your comfort zone as a writer, which has been the only time I have ever been able to do this!
Take this class! The course staff is amazing, the content is wonderful, the workload is eminently manageable, and you will come out of this a better writer and climate change thinker.
Take this course!! Professors Rothschild and Seow are amazing! This course opens you up to different writing styles, how to map data, and implementing different means of storytelling ––– that is, telling the stories of different locales and how they are affected by climate change. The teaching staff is super supportive and is always available to help/answer questions. Whether you are a history concentrator or not, this course will change how you view portrayals of climate change and allows you to delve deeper into micro and macro histories of areas that were/are impacted by climate.
This was a great course, with a lot of room for creativity. The final portfolio assignment was especially fun to put together, and I loved the weekly writing assignments which allowed me to explore different genres without worrying too much about writing in an academic historical way.
I can't say enough positive things about this course. The professors were excellent and provided the class with interesting lectures; the teaching staff was easily accessible and willing to help. One of the best classes I've taken here –– you get out what you put into this class, and I recommend being as engaged as possible.
This class provides a wonderful opportunity to develop your writing skills on the topic of climate change. Weekly discussion sections accompanied by a brief lecture (often delivered by an interesting guest lecturer they bring in) provide background and context as well as the chance to analyze various writing styles on a particular theme. The weekly writing assignments allow you to explore that theme further and develop content for the end–of–semester portfolio. The teaching team is really fantastic and part of what contributes to the richness of the course is speaking with them about your writing and interests outside of class. Take advantage of office hours and engage with the staff on your writing!

They generate lots of enthusiasm for the subject and bring wonderful perspectives to the classroom. Discussions were engaging. Learning to write about climate change – specifically about micro histories of climate change – is very rewarding. Not too much work for a seminar. Really lovely course and could not recommend enough.
TAKE. THIS. CLASS. It was by far my favorite course at Harvard for its unique approach to writing about climate change. I left every day feeling better than when I got there. The response papers and final project were exciting and interesting, and Professor Rothschild and Seow offer unique lenses into the world of environmentalism, economics, and history.
