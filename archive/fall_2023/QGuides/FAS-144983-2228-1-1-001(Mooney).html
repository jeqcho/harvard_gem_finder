Your experience in this class heavily depends on what you make of it, your peers, and your willingness to try and learn new things. You'll have a terrible time if you pigeon–hole yourself into only believing you can contribute by drawing from things you've learned in your concentration classes. Be creative, work hard, and play nice with others and you'll have a great time!
Take this class in the spring, where the groups are smaller and more focused on potential engineering topics. Also, this is not an engineering class, but a research and ideation class. It is very likely that your client and project will not revolve around a topic that actually utilizes the skills you have learned or want to display
I truly cannot represent how awful this course is in a Q guide review. DO NOT take this course unless you have to. I highly doubt this response will be published, but this class was mentally taxing to an extreme. Twice every week, I went to the SEC for 165 minutes and left in a worse mood than I would be in pretty much any other time of the week. This class is not particularly time consuming until the last weeks when the project is (undoubtedly) barely going to get done on time. You will learn how to work in a team, and build great communication skills that might help you in a collaborative work environment, but there will be people you lose all professional respect for when they inevitably don't pull your weight, so choose your direct teammates carefully. The course is generally poorly managed, with little direction or constructive feedback given. Hopefully you get a better client that CAMHS with which to undertake an engineering project, and hopefully your class size isn't 20 people.
Do not take this class if you aren't an SB student! Save yourself! And if you are required to take this course, take it in the spring. From my conversations with other students, I learned that the spring sections have 8–12 students per section (compared to 20 this fall). The best thing you can do for this class and your peers is to communicate. Do not wait until the last minute to tell your group members that you can't get something done, because you could have saved them loads of stress if you just told them a few days earlier. Also, pick project managers who actually have experience managing and who are not varsity athletes in season during the term. Having good PMs can make or break your experience in this class! In my instance, it was the latter. The professors are also not very involved in the class (especially if it's something they don't know or don't have a background in)
Success of the class is very dependent on the nature of the client and if what they need is able to be solved with an engineering solution
Not to stress about it. Assignment turnover from Monday to Wednesday can be brutal. Same amount of work required between monday and wednesday as wednesday and monday.
This class honestly varies year to year, and everything really depends on the instructors, your peers, and your involvement in the project. This iteration did not have a STEM client, so our project wasn't as engineering as I wish it could've been, but when you're taking it you may have a different experience. Be prepared to experience so many new dynamics between your peers and teaching staff. Sometimes teaching staff wasn't as supportive as they should have been. Also, the environment created by certain peers may make the space feel not as open. You might be uncomfortable at times, I know I was. It's a mentally exhausting course more than anything. Final project came together very well but I didn't enjoy the work leading up to it. Good way to meet other engineers and complain together. I'm glad its over. Good luck tbh.
