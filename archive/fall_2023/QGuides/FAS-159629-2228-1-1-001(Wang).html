If you've enjoyed the series through 120b, you'll enjoy it now. The teachers, content, etc are amazing as usual. I did, however, start to get tired of the format, especially the emphasis on building sentences with fixed grammar patterns. It didn't feel conducive to increasing spoken fluency.
The group of students who choose to take Chinese is always such a special and diverse group, you will meet people you never even imagined in this course. Chinese has always been the highlight of my academic journey and the Chinese department at Harvard has made sure that it will stay that way. Love this course and department with all my heart! If you were wondering about whether you should take Chinese, do it. On the fence about it? Sign up for the class. Want a welcoming community of students & staff? TAKE CHINESE!
Chinese 130a is similar to the 120 series but different too. The way the course is run is exactly the same, and the quality of the teachers is just as wonderful. However the material gets super super hard, and a lot of the vocabulary starts sounding similar and meaning similar things. As someone who did really well in Chinese 120 (A, A–), Chinese 130a is not the same. Even the vocabulary starts becoming more niche and less useful, like "euthanasia" and "real estate" and "country bumpkin." The vocabulary is clearly very specifically designed for particular topics of discussion. If you want to use Chinese professionally in a humanities aspect, this course is great and will help you learn the vocabulary necessary for social studies conversation. However, if you don't want a citation and just want to learn more Mandarin, I would unfortunately recommend that you look elsewhere.
LOVE Chinese staff as always. Definitely more of a jump from 120b than I was expected, but once you get over the 2 week hump at the beginning, all is okay!
When I took this class, it was a friendly environment with knowledgeable, clear, and supportive instructors! Attendance policies are firm, which makes sense in a language class—the best way to learn is through practicing. Like most language classes (to my knowledge), Chinese 130a met five days a week, but the workload outside of class was quite reasonable (studying new material, completing one homework assignment per week, and working on a capstone project due at the end of the semester). Every week, we had a 20–minute one–on–one Zoom session with a grad student from Taiwan; this was a great way to practice speaking in a more natural environment, although it is an additional schedule commitment to be aware of. Compared to first– and second–year Chinese, there is much less emphasis on handwriting, although remembering how to read new characters is still important. If you hope to continue studying Mandarin in college, I definitely recommend this class!
Take this class! Hard but worth it
