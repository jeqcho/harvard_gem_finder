It is truly a fantastic class. Highly HIGHLY recommend!!
This is the very best french course I took at Harvard to fulfill my citation. It is incredibly interesting, and the topic is not something easily found in french courses (at Harvard or elsewhere). Grading is not harsh at all, and the essays throughout the semester all help you formulate your final paper so the workload is not horrible at all ! The community in this course was great, and Dr. Brisson was an amazing professor, always sweet and helpful and responsive to the needs of the class. One of my favorite memories is one day where class was hosted outdoors because it was a beautiful day ! You will definitely not regret taking this class.
Take it if you have a solid understanding of the French language (reading, writing, listening, speaking) and want to learn more about less prominent French dialects and communities, especially in the French North American diaspora. The class is really chill and is one of the easiest ways of knocking off a citation/ distributional requirement as well. If you have a good project idea, it can be really fulfilling to work on it throughout the entire semester, culminating in a ~12 page research paper in French. I was a little apprehensive of the idea of writing in French before taking this class and now I'm quite confident! If you're still on the edge, take it for Dre. Brisson! She's an amazing professor and very sensitive to different issues, which makes her a good fit to teach concerning the nuances of marginalized French North–American communities, among others. I also thought it was really cool how she mostly used the Canadian French accent to teach (or the accent of the community we were learning at any point) and by the end of the semester I had gained a real interest in and appreciation for other French accents. One misgiving: the class lacked a little bit of structure in that the schedule of material to be learned was really fluid (which can be a good or bad thing), but I think it will change in the future because this was the first rendition of the class. Take it!
