This class is a lot of work for a GenEd. The course had weekly film screenings, weekly discussion posts, about 2–3 weekly readings, and 3 moderately difficult exams throughout the semester. I don't think GenEd classes should all be Gems, but the amount of information I had to retain on a week–to–week basis just felt too intense for what I signed up for. I can't really say I learned particularly a lot either; at first, seeing how Nazis utilized film to create propaganda was interesting, but the subject becomes a bit dull after a while because it's such a niche subject that there's just not a lot left to say. Also, because you are viewing these films for their historical context as opposed to their artistic value, a lot of the films are not enjoyable to watch, which takes a lot of the fun away from getting to have a "movie" class. The last 2 weeks of the class also weirdly switch to discussions about modern–day fascist aesthetics, and although we learn some interesting stuff during this time, it doesn't really relate enough to everything else we've learned and it also is too broad of a topic to really feel like it was covered thoroughly. Ultimately, this class allows you to learn a lot about a very specific subject, but compared to the other GenEd courses I have taken, it is not as fun, not as easy, and not as effective in teaching you concepts that you can apply to other classes and subjects.
Take this class if you are willing to do a fairly significant amount of fairly mundane and often dense reading each week. If you don't already know how to skim, you'll definitely learn how to do so over the course of taking this class. The interesting and eye–opening perspectives offered from other peers in section make the class worth it, as Professor Rentschler is not the most engaging lecturer. Overall, this class served as a great blend between a film studies, history, and art analysis class, and if you take it you will gain quite a bit of knowledge in a variety of different fields.
If you are looking for a fun way to fulfill your GenEd requirement or just learn about Nazism and propaganda, I highly recommend taking this course. The films and readings are generally very interesting and worth the time you spend on them. Professor Rentschler gives great lectures and is always willing to talk to the students about the class materials. Section is always filled with great discussions, and the TFs are all very helpful. However, expect to put more effort into this class than you probably would with other GenEds. You must write a Canvas posting weekly, and there are three quizzes throughout the term. All of these assignments were very helpful in enhancing my comprehension of the course topics, but they can also demand a good amount of your time for preparation. Overall, taking this course was definitely one of the highlights of my Harvard experience, and I look forward to taking similar classes in the upcoming semesters.
