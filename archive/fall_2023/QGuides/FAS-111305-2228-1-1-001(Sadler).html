This is hands–down the most unique course at Harvard and the one you'll find yourself telling people about decades from now—you'll have class on the Red Line trains and a sailboat/whale–watching boat (definitely take this course if you're interested in sailing!), and the psets involve walking around Harvard Yard and Cambridge Common with a more observant eye than you've probably ever had to exercise before. I will admit that this class is a good deal of work (weekly psets, 3–hr lab, and a journal of observations you need to keep up throughout the semester), but you gain a sense of orientation and hyperawareness of your surroundings after the course that sticks with you even outside of nautical settings; in fact, this is cheesy, but I literally feel more in tune with the physical world around me after taking this course. Phil and Max are also such dears—they'll help you with take–home exams during office hours, and the course even concludes with a magnificent homemade luncheon at Phil's house!!
Go all in. You won't regret putting in the effort
I highly enjoyed learning with Phil Sadler and Max! They're both super understanding and willing to help explain concepts and teach, and Max in particular was so understanding of personal outside circumstances and is very willing to explain concepts further during class and office hours and just talk about how to expand on the material learned in class.
Take this it is a significant time requirement between labs and class but it is completely worth it. Phil and Max are the absolute best people to learn this skill from.
If you are on the fence about taking the course, take it! Hands–on learning from the best teaching staff in the world is a missed opportunity.

Usually with a class this amazing I'd write TAKE THIS CLASS in all caps, maybe copy & paste it a few dozen times, but be warned! This class is not for the faint of heart! Like, sure, you could take it pass/fail or do all your psets in a group with the kids who DO do the work or google everything all the time or something, and it *would* work, like you'd get a grade and everything, maybe a good one, I don't know, but that's not how this class is meant to be experienced at ALL. You're supposed to have questions and you're supposed to want to answer them and try and figure them out yourself. And that takes time! You've gotta collect all that data, which takes immense time and patience because the sky moves simultaneously too quickly and too slowly, and then you have to actually figure out how you want to represent that data in a way that you can actually draw conclusions from it and then you have to go and DO that and then you have to figure out how to adjust your model of the sky accordingly. My favorite part of the class was measuring things and being surprised and learning things for myself through experimentation and you just can't cram that kind of thing. This is not some easy QRD–requirement–meeting–class (I've heard PSY 1900 is the class for that), this is an journey! It's a whole adventure! It's amazing! I don't want to say "don't take this class if you don't have the time and patience to take it right!" because it's an absolutely STELLAR class either way (see what i did there?), but if you're gonna take this class, whatever your reasons maybe, PLEASE don't treat it as that one class you don't have to put any effort into. This is the class that rewards extra effort the MOST!

In a way, it's a class deeply antithetical to the mindset of most Harvard students. Is it a pre–req for something? Nope! Is it required for any concentrations? Not that I know of! Is it gonna look good on your resume? I don't have a clue! Is it a class you can coast through without putting in any work and still get an easy A? I don't actually know, because clearly, I was one of the students who went way overboard. Is it practical for your daily life? I mean, if your life is really really cool, then yeah, but statistically the answer is gonna be no. Are the discoveries you're making particularly groundbreaking? No, people in the middle ages had a firmer grasp on some of this stuff than you will and the field as a whole is considered by many to be obsolete. Is it the kind of class you take strictly because you're amazed and full of wonder and you just want to learn for learning's sake and pour countless hours into something you find fascinating, even though it's not going to advance your career or esteem or whatever else people are so caught up about? YES. And I think we need more of that, you know? Take a class because you think it's cool and for no other reason! Pour time into it just because you're curious to find out the answer for yourself, even though you could totally just figure it out immediately with a cursory google search. Experience wonder again. Take this class.
This class is different than most you will take at Harvard. If you are taking a lot of reading–heavy classes, the hands–on learning experience might balance out your schedule. By the end of the course, you will be pretty well–versed in skills most people have not even heard of, except an experienced sailor. It will also change the way you think about the sky. However, don’t go into Celestial Navigation expecting to breeze by with minimal effort. It does require a substantial amount of effort outside of the 5 hours of class time (including a lab every week from 7–10 pm) and the organizational issues of the class can be stressful.

One component of the class is the weekly journal. Because of the journal, you’ll probably become oddly obsessed with the weather forecast, especially if you procrastinated on gathering data and now have to scramble to get sights on a cloudy day. Try not to procrastinate on the journal and start earlier in the week. It can be a little stressful to have to constantly remind yourself to get data, but having a thorough journal earlier in the semester, means you won’t have to make corrections and continue collecting after the midterm. Another big thing is to make predictions every week and to test those models every week.

I would really recommend forming a P–set group and making friends in the class. P–Sets, especially in the first half of the semester, can take a lot of time. Sometimes the instructions can be unclear or vague, and since problems build on each other, making an earlier mistake impacts later questions. It can be really helpful to have people to work with and ask questions. Also, go to office hours!! Max is incredibly helpful if you are confused and will guide you through the material.
First of all, this is a good way to get your QRD if you're not a math or linguistics person. Minimal math required or used, and the "data" component of it isn't that intense.

This is all to say, there are some fair criticisms of the class, but overall, I really enjoyed it. I learned a ton about the night sky, and now I can identify stars and planets. A lot of trauma bonding.

3–4 hours for the weekly pset, 5.5 hours for class, 1.5 hours for journals, 2 hours for complaining and stressing about the class and how weird it is but also how much you love it, and 1 hour for looking up at the stars with newfound appreciation.

I would recommend it if you think you have the stamina to manage the workload effectively, on top of your other classes. You will have fun and learn a lot, but it might come with a lot of stress.
