One one hand, it teaches unique concepts on a topic that is not really talked about, conflict resolution. It really is eye–opening to a good extent. It is like putting words to ideas and behaviors in a manner similar to psychology in attempting to explain how negotiation and conflict resolution work. The professor, Dan, is extremely eloquent and a very effective communicator, but almost too effective so that we become used to it. The teaching team is extremely dedicated as well, taking the time to review every assignment and plan everything very intentionally. They also take feedback seriously but almost to the point where it is too much input, as entire lessons are tried new even though their effectiveness is not clear.

On the other hand, the course sometimes feels dry in the lectures in that it is a 3 hour class and difficult to go on for a long time just seated. Activities sometimes help but even two hours is challenging at times, especially given the 3–5:45pm class time. Grading is also somewhat slow, taking a few weeks for each assignment, though immediate feedback is not always necessary. Additionally, many of the activities felt like there was not sufficient buy–in. The reason why conflict resolution is so difficult is because it is impossible to separate one's own perspective from the rational and emotional considerations. In any of these exercises, one could simply decide that the "so what" to losing or not doing anything is nothing, and so there is not sufficient effectiveness to some of them. It may be said that you get what you put in, but in these cases, maybe part of it is realizing not all conflicts are worth my time and assigning artificially inflated value to them is counterproductive. The course also suffers a bit from a self–aware sense of being one of the most desired and highly regarded courses, which makes it get an intangible vibe of being more important than it actually is and making sure every student treats it extremely seriously.
