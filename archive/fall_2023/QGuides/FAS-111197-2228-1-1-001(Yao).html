Overall: Physics 16 was by far the most definitive and transformative course I took this semester. It is (per the title) a course in mechanics and special relativity, and it rightfully lacks the title of “introductory.” Physics 16 takes concepts you have seen before in, say, AP and not only expands on its generality in the form of solving for general equations of motion but also pushes you to truly appreciate the deeper intuition and mathematical rigor behind these concepts. Further, it takes you beyond your beloved F=ma and reveals the entirely different formalisms of physics (think “approaches to physical problem solving”) that are Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics. This line from last year’s Q Guide continues to hold: “This class is not about learning physics. It's about learning how to think about physics.” Above all, 16 was where I met the most talented, creative, and enthusiastic peers. Never before have I felt so connected (trauma bonded) to so many people who share my passion for physics.

Professor: Professor Yao (“Norm”) does a brilliant job of keeping lectures exciting and bringing the class together. I am proud to have been a part of Professor Yao’s first year teaching at Harvard. With the relatively small class size, you are blessed with close connections with the world’s youngest tenured professor and with the brightest physics students of your age. Thursday lunches after class and after a long night of pseting were the best. At the expense of the physics department budget, I say: please keep up the class culture! Norm appreciates your community–building :)

Textbook: Norm’s approach to much of the content differs from Morin. Nonetheless, Morin provides an excellent supplement to the material from a slightly different perspective that greatly facilitates solidity of understanding. Especially important for material that you have not seen before.
David Morin’s Introduction to Classical Mechanics

Topics covered: See My.Harvard. With the restructuring of the class, the topics for next year will be more accurate there.

Course structure notes: Weekly lectures Tuesday and Thursday. Psets due Thursdsay in class. Physics nights on Wednesday. Sections (subject to change) weekly to supplement lectures. Don’t procrastinate.

That being said, I did figure out a balance for my time––I managed my other classes, extracurriculars, and social life, so it's not impossible to take this class.

First of all, the physics we do in this class is, for the most part, very beautiful. The problem set questions are carefully picked out, and they explored various intriguing topics. For example, we applied Calculus of Variations (concepts used in Lagrangian Mechanics) to analyze the stock market and understanding how to "short" a stock (big fan of this since I like econ); another example is when we figured out how a rotating ball that bounces against the underside of the table returns to its original position given certain initial conditions (trust me this is more interesting than it sounds).

Secondly, the peer community in this class was amazing this year; since there were only sixteen people in the class, the whole class was one big pset group, with all of us bonding with each other every week. Every physics night, most of the class stays till past midnight, eating pizza together while chatting and finishing up the problem set. Last but not least, the teaching staff in this class was incredible this year. Professor Yao is a great lecturer and a down to earth mentor; he's very supportive, kind, and grabbing lunch/going to his office hours with him is a blast. The TFs and CAs in this class are also very helpful and dedicated to assisting with your learning, and by the end of the semester they become friends that you would like to keep up with.
