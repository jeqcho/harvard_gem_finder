What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
This class is really stimulating, but you don't have to fully understand every concept to do well. Some of the topics were pretty heady or mathy, and it was tough to understand all of what was going on. Reading (and re–reading) some of the papers helped, but honestly, it helps to accept that you don't have to know every detail, just the greater themes, and reasons. For your final project, it is really helpful to pick a method that you did actually fully grasp since you have to use it and justify it. Also, don't be afraid to be wrong, ask "stupid" questions, or challenge some of the things you hear. This class was pretty two–way and it seemed like Scott was genuinely interested in what we thought about various methods in comparative genomics, and their strengths and limitations.
Take the course if you think you might use phylogenetics in your work later!
Every reading in this class feels like it was some researcher's personal bugaboo for years before they finally wrote the paper to expose it. This alone makes the class very fun. Reading is required to understand the material, and it is definitely work.
This course provides a great introduction to the field of phylogenetics and the major methods that are used in the field. If you are working with phylogenetic data or cross–species comparative analysis, this course is a must take! There are guest lectures from leaders in the field, like Joe Felsenstein, one of the fathers of phylogenetics.
