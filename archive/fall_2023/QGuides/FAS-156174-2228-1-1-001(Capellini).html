Take this class only if you are ready to put a lot of work into it. A lot of graduate students take this course and if you don't feel very experienced in biology classes and research yet (which as a sophomore, I did feel like it) be prepared for a difficult class. But the course did teach me a lot, and I recommend taking it if you are interested in doing research in the future. The final project might be one of the most difficult things that I have ever done in college, as I had no idea how to do my own research. The professor is very nice and helpful, and the topics are very interesting, and if you work on the class hard enough, you will not do bad on the exams as it doesn't ask you anything you haven't learned. Just study very hard if you want to take this class!
Take this class if you would like an opportunity to expose yourself to research in the evolutionary developmental genetics field
I can't recommend taking this course enough. The final project is incredibly rewarding and you're allowed free rein to explore whatever subject interests you. There's a moderate amount of work, but Professor Capellini trusts every student in the class to do it on their own terms, and it's all graded very fairly. If you've ever wanted to know how your bones came to be (and trust me, it's cooler than you think), or how a string of nucleotides actually turns out a human body, take this class.
This course is really worthwhile and sufficient background information on the genetic mechanisms is provided (don't be intimidated if you don't come from a genetics background).

This course is incredibly interesting and the flipped format offers a lot of freedom to learn at your on pace and on your one time. The final encourages you to consider the evolutionary implications of an anatomical/physiological feature and substantially improves your research and write–up skills.
Such a great class! Dr. Capellini is awesome and put together such a unique and engaging class. I learned so much the first half of the semester with the lectures and labs, and the final research project was a lot of work but rewarding to see what we could find. I highly recommend!
Topic–wise, this is a pretty great course. You'll learn about bone development, one of the weeks talks about embryo development, and you'll also get the chance to take a closer look at how genetics plays a role in all of this. However, don't take for granted the apparent easiness of take–home exams, in–class quizzes, and a final paper (but no exam). The quizzes are pretty tough, and the take–home exams are long and strenuous. Even if you have your notes in front of you, it will take hours to fill out all the short– and long–answer questions (because again, they're kind of tough or require a decent amount of detail). Plus, the final presentation and paper are no joke: even with a group to work together, it is not at all easy to get all the points on the presentation or the paper. Overall, I would say that it is quite tough to get a really high grade in this class just based on the grading scheme, so if that's your priority, I would most likely steer away from this class. If not, the teaching staff is personable and nice independent of the grading, and you'll likely enjoy learning about the concepts.
