For other fellow PhD students interested in taking this [209 version of the] course while actively engaging in research:
This course can be taken in two very different ways. You can do the bare minimum and pass, or you can try to squeeze the most value out of it. The former might be encouraged for most graduate students, for whom coursework is seen as a distraction. I opted to take the course seriously and try to make the most out of it, and that is far beyond Pareto optimal. When taken seriously, this course, especially the 200–level version, can take up 25–50% of your weekly time. That said, the reward can be great. This course teaches widely applicable tools that may include exactly what your project needs and frequent assessment holds you accountable. On the other hand, the course involves 5–7 weekly engagements: two lectures, one lab, one weekly section, one biweekly advanced section, and a pair of homework assignments that are always due on the same day. The bare minimum can still be a lot. Lastly, the theory aspect of the course is not particularly strong. Methods are highlighted on the basis of empirical performance rather than theoretical expectations (akin to how it's done in practice) but don't expect to come out ready to come up with a new type of neural network.

I'm writing from the perspective of a graduate student.

I would advise against taking this class if possible, as there are several significant issues that detract from the overall quality of the course. One issue is that the course covers a lot of material at a surface level, which makes it difficult for students to gain a deep understanding of the subject matter. As a result, students may leave the class with only a basic understanding of topics such as Neural Networks and Bayesian statistics, which may not be sufficient for them to use these skills effectively in a research or industry setting. This could make it challenging for students to pass job interviews or succeed in their future careers.

Another issue with the course is that the materials presented by Pavlos need significant refinement. While Pavlos is a likable person, his attempts to inject humor and coolness into his lectures can be distracting and take away from the substance of the material being presented. His reliance on memes and other "humorous" content can make it difficult for students to engage with the material in a meaningful way, and can detract from their ability to learn effectively.

Overall, I think the amount of time you will have to dedicate into this course is simply not worth it. You're better off spending 8–10 hours a week in another course where you will take more away from.
