reading the case studies every week prepares you to write your own at the end of the course so the final essay is not so daunting!
Do all the readings, engage in discussion during plenary and section, and you will have a wonderful experience with an incredible course staff. Professor Eck is so incredibly knowledgeable about the material and is so excited and willing to engage with students. Take advantage of it!
Professor Eck's style of teaching is captivating and engaging! Every class, we dove into a different case study that examined religious conflict in a specific community in the United States. The only real preparation for the course, daily, is reading the case beforehand, an endeavor that is quite enjoyable because the cases read like narratives.
This is probably one of the more relaxed but engaging ethics and civics GenEd classes. The weekly workload is fairly light. The background readings are interesting and informative. However, if you are swamped for one week and don't have time, you will survive without them. The two papers are fun. Write a case study! (it isn't as long and is more fun) The case study method is a cool way of tangibly learning about religion in America. The classes themselves felt a little circular at times cause it was an echo chamber of agreement. However, I loved my section. Speak up in your section and challenge one another! it is honestly the only way to really get a lot out of this class. Elam is also fantastic if he is TFing again.
It's a great class that really allows you to explore other faith communities as they engage in American civil culture. Honestly connected with my section more than I have for other classes. And the workload is fully manageable – like 1 case study a week!
This course was a really fun way to learn more about the religious landscape of America and how religion continues to play a part in public life. Rather than sitting through lectures each class session is just an opportunity to share thoughts and views on the relevant case study for that week, and section is mostly led by your fellow students which keeps things interesting and brings variety to how each section is conducted. The case studies are very manageable to read and Professor Eck is lovely, would recommend this course for anyone looking to fulfill their gened requirement or who just wants a survey course on religion in public life.
This is a really underrated class! The case studies are really topical and fun to read, and the focus on Asian religions is not something you typically see in many other courses. The discussion–based nature of the course also keeps class interesting. The weekly workload is very lightjust readings and one short discussion post. Students take turns leading section, which makes each one unique and exciting. The midterm short paper and final case study are also very manageable and give you the option to write about whatever interests you. The Ethics + Civics distinction can sometimes be daunting, but this class is really accessible and inclusive to students from all backgrounds. Definitely take this gem of a class!
TAKE THIS COURSE. There are so many elements of this course that would justify taking it on their own: a discussion–based focus, engagement with national issues on a local scale, opportunities to focus your research on areas that you're interested in, a fantastic section experience, or just the opportunity to take a class with the incomparable Diana Eck. When all of those and more exist in a course, it is absolutely worth taking. It's not an exaggeration to say that section for this class was the highlight of my week. Take Pluralism, you won't regret it.
Class is fine. Not a complete gem because she cold calls and you need to lead section, but if you are looking for an easier gened take it. Genuinely really interesting to learn about religious conflicts that I didn't know existed.
If you're interested in a fairly relaxed course with a unique format, this course is a good option. Be mindful, though, that you might have a couple of uncomfortable moments.
