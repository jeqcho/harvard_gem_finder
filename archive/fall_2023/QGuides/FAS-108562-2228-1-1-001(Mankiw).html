What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Really recommend it if you're looking for a discussion–based class where the prof is hands–off, and you're happy to do a lot of reading. It's not learning in the traditional sense and feels a lot like a book club at times; whether you end up enjoying the class as much as a lot of people do depends on if you enjoy this style of class. Workload fluctuates a lot based on the week (i.e. if there is a book assigned/if your essay is due).
Do the readings, take notes in class, take notes on readings and bring them to discussions. Try to talk as much as possible. Engage and become friends with your section as much as possible. Buy into the experience.
I absolutely LOVED this course. I've heard a lot of mixed reviews about freshman seminars, but I have nothing but positive experiences with this one. Importantly (for my class), we had 12 students who were all engaged with the material and the in class discussions, whereas other seminars can sometimes see less engagement. There is a relatively higher workload for this seminar, but most of time comes from reading rather than writing. Importantly, I think Mankiw is quite deliberate about the books he chooses and they all tend to provide fairly interesting perspectives. If you're someone who likes to talk with friends about politics/policy issues, this is an amazing class to take!
Take it!!
Take this class if you are passionate about economics or passionate about learning more about economics! This is truly a course that can change the trajectory of your study of economics. Throughout this course, you will gain an understanding of foundational economic thought and will be able to debate hot–button issues with a diverse range of people. This is a seminar that is not an echo chamber of the same views making it that much more valuable. The books were great, albeit sometimes long or a little dry. The debates about each of these books were always a pleasure. Mankiw was also quite insightful throughout the class and teaches you new information while also serving as a sort of debate moderator. If you take this class, you will also find a great group of economics–interested friends!
Professor Mankiw is a star –– but downright approachable. Greg is excellent at guiding our conversations , and I feel that what I have to say is genuinely valued by everyone in the class.
This class is not just about economics. It is economics, and history, and government, and it is relevant to not just policy thinking but so much more beyond. I am proud of the amount of reading I've done because of this class, and even prouder of how much I've grown both intellectually and personally. . It makes me want to put in my best effort (and I probably could have given it even more).
In other words, this seminar is a refreshing must among all the other large lecture–based intro classes you have to take freshman fall. Please rank it first choice. I beg you.
Be ready to engage in class discussions and do all the readings, if you do this you will maximize your experience in the class!
This course is the pinnacle of what a freshman seminar should be. It consists of lively discussion with people who will think differently from you, a top–notch syllabus (7 books that range from surveys of economic history to arguments for economic schools of thought to applications of economic analysis to issues like immigration and the gender pay gap), and the experience of getting to learn from an expert in the field. Writing an op–ed that will be edited by Greg Mankiw and discussed by the class for an hour is slightly terrifying but a great learning experience. It's also a great opportunity to bond with very interesting classmates, especially over lunch, which should fall right after the section. If you're considering this seminar, put it as your first choice and put genuine effort into the application.
It is an awesome experience! I highly recommend it, as long as you are the type of person who enjoys sharing their viewpoints and having discussions with others.
