The homework really helped me learn the material and Joe is a fun lecturer. For grad students –– I would be aware that this class is very much run like an undergraduate course (with 10 psets, a midterm, and a final) so I would make sure that works with your schedule before taking it.
This is a good class if you like stats and probability. The ideas presented are unique in the sense that it is hard to look at the topics presented in this perspective in another class. Like when Joe presents a one–line proof for something that took other people an entire paper, you begin to realize that there are hidden ways to relate seemingly different things. This class trains you to look for those relations in the context of probability. The material can get to be tough, but as long as you read the textbook before or after lecture and try to do the pencil problems, then it should be manageable.
Make sure you find the right OH that gives homework proof sketches if you don't want to be spending a lot of time on coursework. Also read the book, its can be more useful than lecture.
If you liked Stat 110 (especially thinking about different distributions and how they relate to one another), you'll probably also like Stat 210. It's a really interesting continuation of the topics that Stat 110 covers but in greater depth and rigor. Reasoning by representation (e.g, proving results by connecting different distributions) is really powerful and interesting, and the measure theoretic foundation that Stat 210 provides makes probability (at least in my opinion) more intuitive but also more rigorous. Problem sets are fair and interesting, and exams are reasonable (the midterm was fair, and the final felt generous I know it's been a bit harder in previous years though).

Overall, the course feels more like an upper–level undergraduate class compared to a graduate class both in content and in enrollment. From what I know, for better or for worse, the course differs a bit from other schools' graduate probability classes: it's less focused on measure theory (e.g., spends less time on rigorous constructions of parts of probability) and more focused on providing intuitive results and explanations. It also at times awkwardly balances between doing analysis (e.g., when proving the CLT) and not doing analysis (e.g., handwaving parts of measure theory and MGFs). I'd definitely still recommend taking some form of analysis before this class the class is definitely doable without it, but Math 25/55/112 or equivalent makes the course much more understandable (especially when it comes to topics like CLTs and LLNs). Also, taking Math 114 at the same time as this course is underrated it makes the measure theory parts of this course so much more understandable, and depending on who teaches Math 114, it'll make the end of that class a whole lot easier too.

Last bit because I would've liked to have a review like this when I was looking at the QGuide: I self–studied Stat 110 over the summer by watching the Youtube lectures, doing the psets, and reading the textbook in the end, I felt really well–prepared for Stat 210, and I think that it was a good move to take Stat 210. I came from a Math 25 background, and even though Stat 210 can be/sound a little intimidating, it's definitely doable, and if you already know some probability and like analysis, it's a jump worth considering. On the flip side, if your Stat 110 knowledge isn't fairly solid or you're not too confident in basic probability, Stat 210 will be a bit challenging. After a few lectures on measure theory, the class dives into reasoning by representation; if you're shaky on probability distributions, this (large) section and foundation of the course won't be very fun.

Not a traditional probability theory class by any means. This class is at the advanced undergrad level, and while it covers a lot of useful practical tools, it essentially doesn't address many of the fundamental questions in measure theoretic probability at all. The math and arguments in the book are also strangely informal at the same time, in the sense that the book at times tries to present some deeper concepts about the measure theory, but then treats it very un–rigorously, which begs the question as to why it should be mentioned in the first place. It makes the exposition more confusing, and I definitely would have preferred using a more standard grad probability textbook. Seems kind of weird that Harvard seems to lack a formal grad–level probability course, all in all.

As any example of the lack of rigor in the class, the treatment of the conditional expectation is extremely loose. The conditional expectation (and conditioning on sigma algebras) is one of the most fundamental concepts in probability, but wasn't even discussed in the book (the book only talks about conditioning on random variables in the undergrad sense). I thought it was odd that a grad level probability class would omit such fundamental concepts like this (and others), and overwhelmingly takes the flavor as an undergrad class that presents some useful tools than one that covers the basic theory.

That said, Joe seems very enthusiastic about teaching and responds to Ed posts extremely fast. Some of the tools in the class are quite useful, practically speaking, but I wouldn't consider this a "grad level probability class" by any means.
