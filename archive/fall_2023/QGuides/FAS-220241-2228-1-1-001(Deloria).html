What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
THIS COURSE IS AMAZING!!! In this course you are given the opportunity to engage with critical questions that face Harvard right now, as they continue to contend with the legacy of their (continued) presence on Native lands that has taken many forms since the school's founding. It is a special course to get to do original scholarship and help to develop a knowledge base about this topic. The professors are amazing, supportive, and are SO excited to be engaged in this process with you.
Take this class!! Professor Deloria is a wealth of knowledge, and his passion for the subject is infectious! It's also so rewarding to learn about the history of Native people from the lands you go to school at, and the things you learn about Harvard's history will change the way you think about and interact with the institution. I really loved this course! The only thing I disliked was the quantity of reading, which was pretty overwhelming in the beginning and middle of the course. However, I loved everything else! I especially loved the instructors' efforts to get speakers to come in and enhance our learning. I also loved how we were encouraged to use all the resources Harvard had to offer to enhance our research.
Simply put: Take this class! Expect to put in the work (seminars are only productive if people prepare for them) and be rewarded with fulfilling discussions that will change the way you see the world, past and present. The final project makes you feel like you are making a difference at Harvard, albeit small, and like you are laying the groundwork for future class iterations.
If youre interested in contributing to a meaningful, large–scale research project and interrogating Harvards harmful legacies, take this course!
Prof. Deloria and Dr. Niles are an incredible teaching duo. Both are very available during office hours and are deeply invested in seeing students succeed in the course. After all, the course is designed to provide the basic research to justify a larger university investigation into Harvard and native lands, meaning that it's in the teaching staff's interest to help students produce as substantial final projects as possible. I feel like I learned a ton about native history and historical research in this class, and I'm lucky to have had the chance to take it.
That this course (and what will hopefully be its many future iterations) is an incredible opportunity to do original research collectively to better understand the colonial history of this area, this institution, and our positionality within both of these today. It's a class that I think every single Harvard student could benefit from.
Take this class!!! The professors are fantastic, and you will learn so much about the history of Harvard college and native people. It is really an amazing class.
If you want to learn about Native dispossession in colonial New England –– which is something everyone living on this land should know –– and you are excited about doing original research to contribute to that body of knowledge & focus on Harvard's role, TAKE THIS CLASS! This class was the most academically empowering and impactful class I've ever taken, and it's work I think every Harvard student should be doing –– we are complicit in this institution's history too. Professors Niles and Deloria created a classroom setting that was welcoming and accessible but also rigorous and dedicated: it was clear from the beginning of the semester that we were doing important work and would be expected to take it seriously. The level of student engagement and participation was thus very high; don't take this class if you'd want to coast through it. It's definitely a LOT of work, at least in this iteration of the class (I imagine things will look very different in future semesters): the first half of the semester, we had heavy amounts of readings each week, and the second half of the semester was dedicated to our final projects, which required a huge amount of work and research and thought. In short, this class is hard, and a big commitment, but so so so worth it.
