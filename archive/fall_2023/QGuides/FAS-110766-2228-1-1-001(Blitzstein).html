The Good:

Blitzstein's a good lecturer, he's funny, likes to joke with the class, and very human. Very generous extension, drop, and grading policy, and he does have his heart in the right place. The material is also interesting and has many connections (too bad literally none of them are explored lmao).

The Bad:

This course wants to be an intro course while teaching material that is not intro–level, and definitely not at an intro–level pace. Do not listen to the requirements, if you don't have multivariable calculus or some notion of linear algebra you will not be able to fully understand and appreciate the material, especially in the latter half of the semester, where it's all calculus and Markov chains are entirely linear algebra. It also sits at a crossroads between rigorous and unrigorous maths; proofs are required in PSETS and exams but it is never explained how one should get to such proofs, some results are entirely opaque and not even the TFs understand them, and story proofs have a special place in hell.

The textbook is terrible and proof–laden, which is incredibly counterproductive and does not serve students. Practice problems are useless since you don't have the solutions to them. All the material pre–midterm does not require actual logic but rather rote memorization of the examples in lecture and the textbook, which artificially deflates grades and makes the course a lot harder than it needs to be.

The midterm is not useful in any way for a thorough understanding of the material and relies solely on being lucky about what topics are drawn. The class is also completely different pre– and post–midterm in terms of intensity and PSET difficulty, messing up students' plans and greatly increasing the amount of time needed. For evidence of this, look to 80% of the TFs, whom all say they did terribly on the midterm and then great on the final. Also, a standard deviation of over 20 points is insane.

Lectures are useless. Blitzstein is a good lecturer and can get his points across, but there's no point in going if everything he says is on the textbook anyway, literally word–for–word the same.

PSETS have little to do with what is actually being taught that week and are always a weird mix, especially post–midterm, of stuff you did the week prior and stuff you're covering the week of the PSET. They also vary wildly in terms of time commitment, with in most recent memory, PSETs 9 and 10 being day and night, respectively.

The course is structured such that there is no natural connection between topics even though there can definitely be one, which makes the material uselessly frustrating to wrap one's head around.

Section is not useful in the slightest, and there is so little publicity for it anyway that one can go the entire course without going and it would not make a difference. Office Hours also suffer from the same issues, with TFs not being much help and the bulk of the benefit of OHs being finding a PSET group, because even though this class is 800 people you might as well be the only one taking it due to the lack of communication between students.
Go to office hours if you want to get all the answers for the psets.
