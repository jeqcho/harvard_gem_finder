There is not much to say––organic chem is organic chem. It is a fairly tough subject, but how well you do is a direct output of how much time and practice you can put in. To be forward, I scheduled my whole semester around orgo so I can focus most of my time on this class. Do the psets early and as much on your own as you can and then go to office hours to check them. Keep a questions/concepts list of things you keep forgetting, getting wrong, and small concepts that you hear in office hours or on psets which are helpful. This was very important for me and I asked all the built up questions in the office hours and review sessions before exams.

It is EXTREMELY important to build an intuition for mechanisms. Know FMO theory and the importance of atomic orbitals, HOMO/LUMOs, sterics, and electrophilicity/nucleophilicity VERY well. Having a solid understanding of them, how they are important in reactions (and how they can be manipulated to proceed in mechanisms) will take you far. It is important to notice trends and patterns. For example, if you see pH=5 as part of the reagents, then you KNOW that it will proceed through a zwitter ion intermediate and involves iminium formation most likely. Half of mechanism problems are basically just a series of protonation and deprotonation to make things more/less reactive. Another big thing was to always be aware of if you are in basic or acidic conditions. That will give you 70% of what to expect for that mechanism. It is very helpful to identify and circle functional groups in the reactant since that can give you a sense of what will be reacting with what. For example, a hemiacetal in unit 1 likely mean you will open/reform the carbon ring somehow.

BTW COUNT HOW MANY CARBONS ARE IN THE CHAIN, IT IS VERY HELPFUL IS REALIZING WHAT WILL REACT WITH WHAT IN REACTIONS.

I did not spend much time memorizing intermediate names, reaction names, signature products of reactions (although this would have been helpful), but the intuition and memory I built from a lot of practice carried me through. I developed a sense for what is/is not allowed, what we have/have not seen yet. For the first 2 units that took me as far as I needed. HOWEVER unit 3 does involve a lottt of memorization before intuition can be built. MAKE SURE to know sn1/2 and e1/2 and which is most likely to happen like you know your name. One small thing about unit 3 is that often when the reaction has basic conditions for step 1, and acidic conditions for step 2, whatever is the leaving group will likely deprotonate something and become neutral BEFORE you move to acidic conditions. Then the acid is used to reprotonate at the end (just write these tips down if you see this and have to take this course).
