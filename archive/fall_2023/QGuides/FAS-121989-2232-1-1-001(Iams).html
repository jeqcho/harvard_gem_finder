This was nontrivially ALOT of work. Pros are that you know exactly what grade you're going to get because Sarah lists out the categories for each assignment and the level of accuracy needed for it. Cons are that this class has at least 1 hour of pre class work before classes on MWF, a weekly pset (granted this is graded on "completion" that takes on average 6 hours. two exams, an end of term project (that took 10+ hours that week), and a final project (with very little help provided by the teaching staff). This class is graded fairly and equitably and you know how you are going to do if you're going to put in the effort to hit the requirements for your grade category you're aiming for. However, it is just A LOT of work. Sarah is not the most flexible professor but she is fair and will make allowances.
TLDR: this is a better class than AM105, but probably not as good as AM104 (offered in fall). If you want to take a diffy class in the spring, take this one over AM105 as someone who took both. BUT, be prepared to work very hard and do a lot of busy work that isn't necessarily the easiest for an A.
This class is a lot of work but you definitely learn a lot. Stay on top of the course material in the first half of the semester and you'll be fine.
THE MATERIAL:
– It’s very cool and has many applications.
– The problem sets can be really hard, but I found working through the problems to be so worthwhile. Some problems were simply astonishing, in the best way possible.
– I became much more comfortable with Mathematica, algebra, and plotting.
– We even got to learn about good practices in presenting and in public speaking. :)

SUPPORT YOU’LL RECEIVE AS YOU LEARN THE MATERIAL:
– Everyone on the course staff is fantastic.
– Dr. Iams is very accessible via Ed, and posting my questions on Ed and reading her answers helped me a lot.
– Office hours are very useful to attend. Dr. Iams struck the perfect balance re: giving me the gentle nudges I needed to get out of the ruts I was stuck in but still creating opportunities for me to think deeply about and internalize the intuition, steps, etc. around solving a given problem.

CLASSROOM COMMUNITY, COLLABORATION, ETC.:
– The classroom community was the strongest one I’ve been a part of at Harvard. I got to know a substantial number of my classmates and made some new friends.
– It’s no surprise that the classroom community was strong; we all worked on problems together three days a week, and the atmosphere in office hours (where I’d encounter many of the same people every week) was quite pleasant.
– The group work component of the class was effectively organized. I liked working at the whiteboards and meeting new people. (The group configurations changed fairly regularly!)

OTHER REMARKS:
– I learned a LOT of math.
– This class and members of its teaching staff helped me become a more flexible and creative problem solver.
– Dr. Iams knows a lot about how people learn. From her, I learned a lot about learning.
– Taking this class is a big commitment. But it’s a useful one.

This course is very challenging and a lot of work. Since it is flipped classroom, you are required to do pre–class assignments (~60 mins of lecture videos, a discussion post, and a quiz) before each class, a skill–check quiz at the beginning of each class, and a weekly problem set that often took north of 10 hours. If you fall behind at all, it is extremely difficult to catch up. On a more positive note, many of the assignments are completion–based, and the flipped classroom format allowed me to get to know my classmates well and work through lots of practice problems to better grasp the material. Nonlinear dynamical systems are very interesting and have a lot of real–world applications, particularly in the sciences – I think that this class does a good job of ensuring that you have a solid understanding of the material, but be prepared for a heavy workload.
