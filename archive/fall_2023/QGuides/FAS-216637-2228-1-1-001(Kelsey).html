Take this class! It is by no means an easy A, but the challenge to examine a new form of the humanities every week with eleven of your peers should excite you!
If you can fit it in to your schedule, there's really no reason not to take it. Very reasonable work load, the professors are AMAZING and will leave you awestruck... truly a life–changer of a class. Also, in my experience, the grade I got on the weekly assignment correlated pretty perfectly with how much time I had to put into it on any given week, so definitely worth it to approach the assignment over the course of multiple days rather than just the night before.
YOU HAVE TO TAKE THIS COURSE. PLEASE PLEASE PLEASE I'M BEGGING YOU. Seriously no matter what you're concentrating in HUM 90 will make a better thinker, learner, conversationalist, and appreciator. You will get to know really cool and thoughtful people and learn so much from your classmates, from the instructors, and from leading world experts. I feel like HUM 90 has literally changed my life in that I feel like I can now deeply engage with any type of art: whether writing or film or theater or architecture or even trees. It's taught me to pay very very close attention (which is totally a skill!) and to see interconnections between really different fields. As much as I like to complain about Harvard, HUM 90 is seriously the kind of "only at Harvard" course that will make you feel like all the ~struggle~ is worth it. I've been excited for every single class, and am so jealous for future students to get to experience this for the first time. (P.S don't sweat it about the assignments it's just a way to help you grow your writing!)
Hum90 feels like an essential class to take your sophomore year. At a time when you're probably unsure about what you are going to study or do with your life, getting to learn from some of the best professors at Harvard about their field is incredibly valuable and rewarding. On top of that, Dean Kelsey and Professor Kaminsky make this class what it is. They are truly two of the most insightful, kind, and brilliant instructors, and really make Hum90 a liberating learning environment. Cherish each Friday session and each Monday discussion. Make the most out of the time you spend with the other sophomores and professors in this class. It's a class that really is a defining college experience.
Engage with the material and in discussion as much as you can, and think about lingering questions or ideas outside of class to enrich your experience both in and out of the classroom.
What is the humanities? What does it mean to be a humanist? How is technology transforming the way we interact with art? These are a few of hundreds of questions you’ll dissect in Humanities 90. This is a challenging, rewarding course that will teach you how integral different art forms are to your lifestyle and how to approach them. If you’re willing to dedicate the time and though, take this class for a true liberal arts experience. Grading is challenging, so be aware, especially if you don't come from a private school or rigorous humanities background. Feedback at times can be subjective.
Take this course if you can – this is an extremely rare and rewarding opportunity, and represents the best of what Harvard has to offer academically. Each week you will engage with a different medium, and you will be taught by an amazing professor of that field. The preparation required outside of class is similar to other courses, but unlike a gened it is critical that you actually do it, since you will get so much out of the discussions and the opportunity to hear from these professors. Approach every topic with open–mindedness and enthusiasm, even and especially the ones you think you have little interest in/prior exposure to – there are no wrong answers here, and this course is an incredible intellectual sandbox to engage with interesting content and ideas, surrounded by some of the brightest and most engaged classmates you will find in any discussion–based class. As for the writing assignments, while they are only 500 words do not be deceived into thinking they are easy or should be taken lightly – while they may not require as much time as longer papers, they require a similar if not higher level of engagement and effort to write well.
This course is a small seminar with two amazing professors and a new, interesting guest lecture every week. You definitely foster a course community, and have some highly interesting discussions pertaining to each week's topic. That being said, if you are interested in a course with guest lectures, I would recommend taking Hist–Lit 10 instead, because it has a lot of the same guests and a much more focused topic. Where this course fell short was in its lack of direction. I don't feel like I got much out of this because each week was like its own mini TED talk and talk–back with no connective tissue tying it all together. Professors Kaminsky and Kelsey are amazing facilitators, and I loved being their students, but the course in general could stand to define itself a little better. As someone who loves the humanities, this course felt disjointed and confusing, trying to touch on so many different fields of study. I presume that if you are more accustomed to STEM classes, this would be a fun way to dip your toe into different humanities fields, but for me, it just wasn't enough of each thing, and a serious lack of thematic cohesion. This course would be greatly improved if it chose a question to explore for each iteration, and then had each lecture tie back to that central question using each humanities discipline. I also think this course should have a writing workshop component if it continues with the 9–essays–per–semester structure. That way, you don't just spin your wheels trying to write a completely different essay each week and can get some tips and advice real–time. Your enjoyment of the course also greatly depends on your cohort, so keep that in mind when you sign up. I had a good time in this class, but I'm still trying to figure out if I got anything tangible out of it.
