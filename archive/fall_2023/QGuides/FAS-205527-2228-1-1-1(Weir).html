Have you ever been to an opera? Personally, I haven't, but after having taken Complit 153, I think I have an idea of what it must be like. In an opera, a singer comes on stage and performs some piece of music with tremendous passion, and although you have no idea what they were saying, you still nod to your friends, say it's good, and applaud because you appreciate the art of it. Similarly, in Complit 153, Professor Weir enters the lecture hall, talks in highly elevated diction for an hour and a half about some sophisticated–sounding topics, and although you have no idea what he's saying, you still try to jot down notes and nod to your friends because you appreciate the art of it. But what exactly is this art? It is the art of quoting from other scholars: When he explicates an interpretation, he makes sure to include as many sources of Nabokov scholarship as he can. He often always throws in a dash of Russian as well, just to add some interlingual insight. It is the art of improvisation: Arrive early enough and you will get a sneak peek of Complit 153 behind the scenes. Fifteen minutes to 6:45, Prof. Weir enters the premises. Five minutes to 6:45, Prof. Weir walks in and flips frantically through all 60 pages of his notes. At 6:45, Professor Weir begins his performance. It is the art of speed: Prof. Weir always has many more notes than he has minutes in class, but they don't call him the "master chef" for no reason. He always cooks up an incredible stew, expertly blending together scholarly ideas, usually relying on the last 10 minutes as a speedround to "popcorn" from person to person as they collectively crowdsource just about every insight to be had. It is the art of abstraction: His handwriting is illegible. But most importantly, it is the art of the simple: Behind the facade of the multilined model with thousands of different facets, there's always a really simple Nabokovian principle. Some of my favorites include: all art is flawed art, unless it's Pushkin's or Nabokov's; Freud is awful and any character who agrees with him is awful; nature (especially the butterfly) is beautiful; and of course the hermeneutics of perversion. Comparative Literature can be so wonderful. This was my first comparative literature class at Harvard, but after taking it, I became confused as to why people call comparative literature majors "losers," unless a loser is just someone who wrestles with a text long enough to extract an insight about the world.

In all honesty, this class has topics that are really interesting, but they are often presented in a way that blurs the fine detail and focuses on the big themes instead. I am a fan of the post–lecture 1–hour class discussion where we consider the artistic and political aspects of the text and this part is usually pretty well–done, even though the conclusion will be something obvious (funny). Lectures themselves often move too quickly as a result of the large number of literary buzzwords that are not explained in much detail (and time taken up by the class discussion). It is unfortunate that there is no textbook because there is no way to explore the material presented in class and get a better understanding except by combing through the books Prof. Weir quotes from, which are usually behind a paywall. Essays are pretty good both as a learning tool and in terms of content (they usually remind me of something from popular culture like Queen's Gambit or Stanley Kubrick that we then compare with the novel), although I wish the page length on them was just a little more high. I feel uncomfortable when I take notes for an essay and I am able to fit my notes on only 3 lines (with the paper in landscape orientation). In–class pop quizzes seem unexpected at first, but they actually happen just about every week an essay is due, so it's hard to be unpleasantly surprised. The weekly workload varies wildly, since Professor Weir assigns either most of a 200–page novel in addition to a two hour movie or just a couple pages' worth of selected excerpts to reread. He put the weekly homework on the course page weeks before we had to do this and this was actually what we did, leading many to be very happy. As a result work is pretty predictable, so don't be too sad if you don't have time to get it all done one week, chances are you can catch up in the week following.

If you want to take this class, make sure you know a bit about Nabokov, make sure you know your Russian early–20th–century politics and Russian idioms, and that's basically it. It's quite accessible really, once you actually find it in the course catalog.
