Math 25a is a fantastic proof–based linear algebra class. The class primarily follows Sheldon Axler's textbook called "Linear Algebra Done Right." This. Class. Is. Hard. Proofs will not come easy, but if you put in the work, find a good study group, and attend office hours each week, you will get a good grade in the class. I am not a math concentrator (nor even a STEM concentrator for that matter), but this class truly tapped into my love for pure math and proof. Wes is the best math teacher I have ever had, and his lectures are clear and easy to follow. NOTE: I would recommend taking 25 even if it is not your first time being exposed to linear algebra.

If you are deciding between 25a and 55a: 55 probably moves at twice the speed as 25. If you think you have a significant proof background and have already taken proof–based linear algebra and group theory, then you undoubtedly COULD do 55. 25, on the other hand, probably only requires a background in calculus and SOME exposure to proofs. However, I would recommend trying out 55 and dropping down after the first several psets if you think it is too much. Also, 25 notoriously has stricter grading that 55. Whereas 55 TAs do not require you to be super precise with your language, 25 graders will take off points for even the smallest logical leaps or incorrect explanations. That being said, a typical 55 problem requires around a page of proof, whereas a typical 25 problem is only a paragraph.

If you are deciding between 25a and 22a: 25 is a much MUCH better survey of linear algebra. Rather than talking about matrices and matrix operations, 25 is fundamentally the study of linear transformations. 25 also covers singular value decomposition and Jordan Normal Form, both of which are essential concepts in Linear Algebra that 22 lacks. The proofs in 22 are also SIGNIFICANTLY easier than the proofs in 25. 25, however, has a kind of camaraderie that 22 lacks, simply because 25 is such a small class. I would recommend 25 over 22 if you are prepared to spend upwards of 15 hours per week on the problem set solving tough problems that require creativity and insight. If, on the other hand, you are looking for a more introductory proof class, then take 22.
If you're considering 22A, 25A, and 55A, then 25A might not be the Goldilocks sweet spot in the middle if you want an engaging math class that will augment your mathematical maturity. Wes is self–aware about his strictness, and this may very well push the difficulty of this course near 55A in terms of accessing support structures and ensuring you have full comprehension of the course's content. If you seek to take a collaborative, problem–solving approach to your first math class here that will boost your critical thinking and sufficiently challenge you while still assuring you that you have the support of classmates, course assistants, and the course head without tension if you come up on a noticeably difficult problem on a pset, Math 22A or 55A are very likely better options (the former since the content rigor support structures are far more accessible, not to mention opportunities for improvement and growth in the form of corrections, the latter if you've had exposure to formal proofs and a bit of group theory and abstract algebra and want a trial–by–fire bonding experience collaborating on the problem sets). If you still insist on 25A, please watch the welcome video on the course site before considering this class, especially if you plan to do one or two extracurriculars while taking 25A. With a lack of sections and very early lectures, your experience might be very autodidactic beginning around the middle of the semester, which may or may not be a plus to you.

Overall: Math 25a is a rigorous, fast–paced, proof–based course on the linear algebra of (predominantly) real and complex vector spaces. It focuses on the construction of the field of linear algebra from the ground up. I hope you love matrices. If you have not taken a proof–based math course before, the learning curve is steep but certainly not insurmountable. The first week or so is dedicated to teaching the foundations of proof, and there is a proof techniques quiz towards the end of week 2. Also, even if you have taken lin alg in high school, I assure you this course is much deeper and much more extensive. I had taken a lin alg course at a local college, and although I had seen a little over half of the material before at a more basic level, I enjoyed the additional rigor, generality, and motives behind the content while deriving an appreciation for the power of linear algebra.

Professor: Wes is an amazing lecturer. He provides motivation behind the mathematical concepts at hand, approaches the material with clear reasoning, and most importantly, he writes large and legibly on the board. Very nice and approachable.

Textbooks:
1) Axler: the main textbook for this course. Does not always align with Wes on definitions and subsequent construction of lin alg, but is an extremely useful source. The biggest difference is that Axler staunchly refuses to use determinants; you won’t need to use his workarounds in this class. Helps a ton on psets; you can often cite Axler. Relatively smooth read, very straightforward.
2) Treil: we used a chapter or two, mostly while Axler was off doing anything but determinants.
3) Hammack: If you haven’t written proofs before, Hammack will be your best friend for two weeks and then never again. Shout out to Texas State HSMC for letting me skip this.
4) Abbott: We had a glimpse or two out of this, that was it.

S. Axler, Linear Algebra Done Right, 3rd Ed., Springer, New York, 2015.
S. Treil, Linear Algebra Done Wrong, https://www.math.brown.edu/streil/papers/LADW/LADW.html
R. Hammack, Book of Proof, https://www.people.vcu.edu/~rhammack/BookOfProof/
S. Abbott, Understanding Analysis, 2nd Ed., Springer, New York, 2015.

Topics covered: Gaussian elimination, RREF, relations/equivalence relations/functions, number systems/fields, vector spaces/subspaces and isomorphism, sums/direct sums, basis/dimension, linear independence, linear transformations, kernel/image/rank–nullity, change of basis, constructing the notion of a determinant, Eigenvalues/vectors, diagonalizability, systems of DEs/discrete–time systems via lin alg, Norms/inner products, orthogonality/projections, orthonormal bases, Gram–Schmidt/Riesz Thms, self–adjoint/normal/positive operators, spectral thm, isometries, singular value decomposition, generalized Eigenvectors, nilpotent operators, minimal polynomials, Jordan normal form

Course structure notes: 3 lectures a week; weekly psets due Wednesday afternoons. You will learn to typeset your psets in LaTeX, it’s really not that bad once you get used to it. Numerous TA OHs spread throughout the week. Do ask TAs to look over your work. Math nights on Mondays. Don’t procrastinate.
