Comments
Definitely apply and put your best foot forward in the application because this is a class that you want to take. Once a student in the class, my advice is to do as much reading for every book as possible and contribute as much as possible because you will never be able to replicate this environment with a world–renowned economist and 12 of Harvard's brightest minds debating macro econ theory.
This seminar will change your life. The books we read and the discussions we had as a class amounted to the most enriching academic opportunity I have ever experienced.
I am sure that many of my peers will write other sterling recommendations for this class, so I will give some advice for people looking to take this class. The real thing that makes or breaks this class is how good your class conversations are. If you don't feel comfortable speaking in a 12–person group or sharing and defending your opinion, you might get a little bit less out of the class (although it will still be great). If you are open to participating, this will be the best class you have ever taken. It was for me.
This class helps you form more well founded and carefully thought through opinions on a wide variety of economic topics. Discussions are extremely engaging, and you gain a solid background in the thinking of some of today's most prominent economists. The class is very debate oriented and not for everyone, but engaging with respectful disagreement from peers on a wide variety of issues is extremely helpful to clarify your own perspective.
If you are looking for the best freshman seminar, this is almost certainly the one you should take. The class is structured such that each week (sometimes every other week) students read a book discussing a different aspect of economics. Here is the list of books that we read this past semester:
– The Worldly Philosophers (Robert Heilbroner)
– Capitalism and Freedom (Milton Friedman)
– Equality and Efficiency: The Big Tradeoff (Arthur Okun)
– Leave Me Alone and Ill Make You Rich (Dierdre McClosky and Art Carden)
– A Brief History of Equality (Thomas Piketty)
– Open Borders: The Science and Ethics of Immigration (Bryan Kaplan)
– Career and Family (Claudia Goldin)

In addition, each week a different student writes a NYT–style op ed about an economic area of choice. These op eds are incredibly fun to write, read, and edit, and often produce very heated contestation from both sides of the aisle.

Honestly, even if you don't like economics, Professor Mankiw makes this class lively and intellectually engaging. He does a great job moderating the discussion, and he is very fun to debate, especially if you do not agree with his political leanings. If you do take this course, you will get out of it whatever you choose to put in. It is very possible to do well in the class simply by reading the introductions and conclusions of each book and quickly typing up an op ed. If, on the other hand, you instead put in the work to read each book cover–to–cover, craft an excellent op ed, and participate fully in each discussion, I can guarantee that by the end of the course you will have a much more developed and articulate understanding of the many economic challenges that currently plague our society.

Finally, if you do decide that you want to take this course, you must rank it at the top of your list, otherwise Professor Mankiw won't even evaluate your application. Also, he picks six left–leaning students and six conservative students in each section in order to increase political diversity. Make of that what you will.
Take this class! It's not as revolutionary as past students have described it to be, but Mankiw is great. It's one of the more time–intensive freshman seminars (we read a book a week), but it's really rewarding.
