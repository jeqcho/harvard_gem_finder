This course did a very good job balancing both theoretical and computational perspectives on linear algebra, and I came away from it feeling like I understood the concepts of linear algebra well. However, the course also had some issues, particularly in the gap in difficulty between the lectures and the problem sets. The course consisted of three lectures per week that reference material from two textbooks. Dusty assigned a chapter or two of reading per lecture, but these often felt unnecessary, since the lectures covered the same material. There was no section for the course. Problem sets were due weekly, and they consisted of a computational portion and a proof–based portion. Dusty is a very good lecturer, and I often came out of lecture feeling like I entirely understood the material. However, the problem sets felt very difficult relative to the lecture material. Be prepared to attend office hours regularly and collaborate on problem sets. Thankfully, Dusty is very easily accessible via email and eager to help students. He also created a nice community around the class, and I met many of my closest friends through this course. There were four midterms throughout the semester, which felt unrelenting at times, but the ability to submit corrections for our mistakes for half–credit was very generous and an excellent learning opportunity. There was no final exam, but instead there was a final group project paper to explore a topic of interest from a linear algebra perspective. I really enjoyed this format, and although it probably took just as long as studying for an exam, I preferred working on a topic of my own choosing. Having been a strong math student in high school, I was not expecting to struggle as much as I did in this course. It is not overly difficult, but I would advise interested students not to write this course off as a walk in the park by any means. Overall, Dusty did a nice job preparing students for continuing in pure math, applied math, or another subject area that uses proof–based linear algebra. If you want an extremely rigorous introduction to proof–based math, or if you are mostly interested in computation, you may consider a different math course. However, if you don’t want to devote 20+ hours per week on math and want to cover both proof–based and computational methods, this is the right course for you.
