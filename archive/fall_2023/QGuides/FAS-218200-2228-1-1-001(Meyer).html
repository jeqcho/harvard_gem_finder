Take this course! Especially if you're at all interested in the subject. No exams, just three papers (ranging from 4 to 6 pages) spread out across the semester. Start the papers early and go to office hours and you will do great.
This is a great course, but you definitely get what you put in. The reading selections are very interesting, and the classes serve to outline the readings and how they connect to the theme. Thus, if you don't actually do the readings every week, you may as well not take the class (the readings take 30–75 minutes a week). The papers are a little tricky because you really have to produce a novel idea, but that's not inherently bad. The grading thus far has been slightly harsh in my opinion, especially since a majority of the class has never taken a sociology class before (of course haven't gotten my final grade back). Rachel Meyer (prof) is very passionate about the topic and her enthusiasm is infectious. She definitely is left–leaning and is not afraid to express it. She's less available outside of class than I'd like, but she made up for it by having Rachel Kim as her TF, who is probably my favorite TF of all time. If Rachel Kim is TFing the class, then it's a no–brainer to take, because Rachel's sections were so thoughtful and really made the class for me. If it's an average TF, this class is more of a toss–up, it depends how much you want to put into the class.
TAKE THIS COURSE
gem!! professor meyer knows how to keep her class engaged. workload is minimal: 3 units, weekly readings (that are honestly pretty short bc professor does her best to cut them down), 2 analytical questions for each unit (literally a question about a week's reading), and a paper for each unit
section was also super fun but still learned a lot!!
this is coming from someone who took the class w/ no prior sociology knowledge at all! can get a lil confusing in lectures sometimes but nothing you can't pick up on
This course is, regardless of what your career plans or hopes are, 100% relevant to everyone. The course gives you a great opportunity to learn about work cultures and relationships.
This is one of my favorite classes I have taken at Harvard. No matter who you are, this course feels relevant to all Harvard students, particularly those who end up in a very classic career path post grad (consulting, finance, SWE, etc.) or those who have at one point questioned whether they should go down that path.
Without a sociology background this class can make your head spin at times. I often found that my interpretations of the readings did not align with the takeaways discussed in lecture, which makes following and contributing to in–class discussion difficult.
pay attention in class and do the readings – it makes everything so much more interesting and you take away so much since work and culture are literally the backbone of what we do every day.
The topics and materials used for this class are really interesting and lead to great discussions. However, almost all of your grade will come from three essays, even thought participation and attendance are mandatory. It definitely felt like the weekly experience of the class is not reflected in grading and so the essays end up feeling like a lot of work and pressure.
Course readings are super interesting and provide very insightful perspectives. Unfortunately, as a non–sociology concentrator, lectures sometimes felt more meta than I might have enjoyed (hard to understand). Would recommend trying out the lecture style for a little bit (maybe you'll like it) but definitely overall a great course.
Highly recommend this course! Rachel Meyer is a fantastic instructor and the course touches on several fascinating issues related to work.
I loved learning more about work culture and the different theories behind the American work system. I think this course brought more clarity to the type of employee or worker I want to be. It also gave me more of a critical lens on how the educational system is portrayed right now and the funneling of individuals into finance, consulting, etc. that is so heavily romanticized at Harvard. At the same time, I definitely have more understanding regarding the lure and attraction to go into these occupations. I also have a much greater appreciation for workers in general and feel like I understand management systems and also organizing movements a lot more. I just personally really loved this course and think it will carry with me beyond this year, and my lifetime.
