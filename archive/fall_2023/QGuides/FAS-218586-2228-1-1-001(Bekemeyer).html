This is a very interesting class! Be warned though that it focuses more on social and cultural history than political. Regardless, it is still incredibly interesting and fills the knowledge gap that many of us have of American history after WWII. The readings are doable for the most part, but start them in advance if possible.
Take this course! Super interesting and not at all difficult
This course allowed me to learn modern American history up to the present, which I had not previously covered. The readings were usually interesting though they could be long. Additionally, the section component of this course was great and the discussions were always interesting.
Mid class. Aaron is kind and structures lectures well, but that's the only good thing. The readings are long and dry. There's a mostly case studies about housing, which gets pretty boring if you're interested in culture. Lecture and section feel like two different classes. The TF (Sannoy) was unpleasant. He's snobbish, unclear with expectations, and puts you on the spot. He makes section feel like forever because he asks tangentially related questions to the readings that have little to do with lecture. Expectations for the final exam and paper are pretty unclear, and the syllabus doesn't have the right information. Take something else.
This is the perfect class to take for a broad understanding of US history from WWII up until present day. I found the lecture component of this course to be very interesting, however, the assignments could be a bit annoying. There are two exams with written essay components in this course, and I am not sure that the grading was flexible enough given the task to write an essay in a timed exam format. That being said, overall not too difficult of a course that gave me a lot of life knowledge as a citizen of the US.
This is a great intro to American history. The grading is a bit tougher than one would expect for a survey class, but I felt like I learned a lot about post–war history on the other end.
Take the class. Aaron is cool. There is a lot of opportunity to learn various aspects of a recent history that you did not really get in high school.
This is a really excellent U.S. history class. The lectures are interesting, the course is framed around concrete trends, and the section discussions are engaging. This course made me consider concentrating in history, which I had not considered before taking the course. The paper prompts were also really interesting to write about.
TAKE THIS CLASS! It's a lovely history lecture that doesn't take up too much time in your schedule and still feels very tangible and intellectually rewarding. Even if you feel like you're familiar with this time period in US history, Professor Bekemeyer does a fabulous job applying some historical frameworks and theories that make you view the same historical events through an entirely different lens. For those of you who feel like your high school US history class skimmed over post World War II history, I cannot recommend this class enough to fill in the gaps and enrich your understanding of how the last 80 years have shaped our country.
Take this class!! It's great because it gives you an in–depth review of post–1945 America with great material. Professor and TF staff are great and super passionate about the course. Super engaging class!
This class is a very general overview reminiscent of AP US History in exam structure, but is taken to higher levels in section and includes great interdisciplinary ties to social politics and sciences. You really get into the mindset and priorities of citizens around the country at various times, and since it is so close to present some of the laws and actions we talk about have extremely direct consequences on our current situation. It is a great chance to learn about how our present has fit into a greater arc of history set in motion after WW2's end––for example, you learn about how the Vietnam War is tied to the Oklahoma City Bombing, and how that in turn is tied to Trump's Jan 6 rebellion, all through the lens of the white power movement. It's a cool approach that allows you to make lots of your own conclusions about how all these different terms connect.
Future students shouldn't be afraid of this course because they already have a lot of familiarity with American politics and history. You'll learn facts you didn't know, but it's not really about that. It's about new analytical lenses through which to view periods of history about which you have cursory or moderate knowledge. It's a good course, and section is particularly helpful.
This class was definitely *not bad*, but I would think critically about if you really want to take it. If you have already taken AP US History or have a general understanding of US History, then this really will not further your knowledge more than a few Wikipedia articles. Professor Bekemeyer is undoubtedly an excellent lecturer––he almost reminds me of John Green when he's speaking, and definitely highlighted key laws and case studies in class. However, the readings, while very interesting (most were from books published for a general audience), felt disconnected and had too many pages assigned, so many people in section didn't do them, and honestly they did not really contribute much for the exams or writing assignments. Additionally, the grading is rougher than you'd expect for this class––each essay and exam is weighted heavily, and there are no small assignments to get feedback on or try to improve your grade, which makes these assignments incredibly important. On top of this, no rubrics were provided, and the only grading guideline was "it will be graded holistically." While the class did lack in actual substance (unless maybe you knew very little about modern US history), it could have made up for in primary source analysis or historical reasoning, but it really didn't, especially because there were two essays. Besides the lack of clarity for grading, there was no *major* issue with this class; I think it lacked in overall substance and meaning, and is neither a chill history gem or an engaging, fascinating course.
This course offers a great survey of later American history with a lot of excellent readings and very effective lectures. The workload is very manageable and worthwhile. The course includes a diverse set of perspectives in the readings and is very thoughtful about its focus on those perspectives in the weeks they are discussed.
This is a great introduction to both historical thinking and American history! Highly recommend, even if you are not a history/social sciences person.
