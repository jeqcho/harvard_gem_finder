So you want to take 143b...

Once upon a time, I was also a bright–eyed, bushy–tailed student who really enjoyed 143a (taught by Louis Deslauriers) and absolutely loved quantum mechanics, so naturally, I wanted to take 143b! However, be warned – unlike 143a, this class was an extremely frustrating and stressful experience. Although this course enhanced my understanding of the material from 143a and gave me a broader picture of quantum mechanics as a field, it unfortunately came at the cost of a lot of undue stress, sleep deprivation, time, as well as detriments to my mental and physical health and my performance in other classes. The course content is certainly challenging and requires substantial time and effort to understand (which I would have been happy to commit to), but what made the course especially frustrating (and not worth it, in my opinion), was the course design, which I will elaborate on.

Lectures: **TL;DR: Professor Randall is a brilliant physicist but struggles to convey information clearly in lecture and take different student backgrounds into account.** Professor Randall is, without a doubt, a brilliant physicist and researcher (probably one of the best of this generation), and I was (and still am) starstruck every time I see her. However, being a brilliant researcher does not inherently translate to being a good educator. I often found the lectures to be confusing and hard to follow, since Professor Randall often copied derivations off the lecture notes without elaborating on conceptual takeaways or putting the derivation in context. It also seemed like Professor Randall often assumed we had prior knowledge in many aspects of quantum mechanics that we did not have (though this varies depending on who you took 143a with), and she struggled to gauge what level of understanding the students have. If you plan on taking this class, I highly suggest coming up with a list of topics you honestly are/aren't familiar with to share with her, so she can get a better sense of where students are starting at.

Problem sets: **TL;DR: Psets are super time–consuming not because they are very difficult iterations of the lectures, but because they cover material or assume knowledge that is NOT covered in lecture, section, or any official pre–req.** This semester, problem sets were almost completely written by undergraduate CAs. A majority of the questions were interesting and fairly reasonable, with applications to various subfields of physics. However, on many problem sets, there was at least one unreasonably difficult/obscure question that basically took the entire week and multiple office hours to figure out. These questions required background knowledge that was not part of any pre–requisite to this course and was not mentioned at any point in lecture; in many cases, I needed to refer to random textbooks and notes online to even understand what the question was asking. What made things worse was that there were only 2 office hours each week (granted, they were usually much longer than 1 hour due to the kindness of the CAs), and they were hosted the day before and the day of the pset deadline. This meant that I often would start the pset as soon as possible, bang my head against a brick wall and struggle with the questions for a few days, then go to office hours a day before the deadline and realize that there was no way for me to understand the problem without a deep understanding of several graduate–level physics and math classes. It also meant that I had no time to actually think about the problem and its results to gain a deeper conceptual understanding/enlightenment for the topic, which is the whole point of psets.

Exams: **TL;DR: Exams are extremely challenging and will test you on material that was never covered in psets. Seriously one of the most stressful and unpleasant experiences I've had at this school. ** This semester, our first midterm was in–person, while our second midterm and final exam was take–home. The in–person exam was challenging (similar to many other physics classes), but at least our suffering was limited to an hour and 15 minutes. If you get take–home exams in this class, be prepared to spend every waking moment in the allotted time window thinking about it or working on it. The second midterm was designed to take 3–5 hours, and almost everyone I knew took at least 10–15 hours to do it (keep in mind that we were also going to other lectures and doing work for other classes, so this meant that a lot of people pulled all–nighters). We also had a pset due the same day :( The final exam was even worse; I'm pretty sure I worked on it for upwards of 20 hours. There were a few rare bright moments where I figured something out and got a sense of accomplishment from actually learning something from the class, but an overwhelming majority of the time I felt absolute despair, so much so that I actually felt physically ill and couldn't sleep (this has NEVER happened to me in another class). Exams will definitely cover material that you have never seen on a problem set and glossed over quickly in lecture, so nothing is safe – we had a problem on the final about path integrals without ever doing a path integral problem in a pset (and were not given any practice problems from a book that could've helped). If you can, take this class pass/fail so at least you don't have to worry about your grades.

In conclusion, be EXTREMELY wary before taking this class. I won't tell you not to take this class (many people advised me against this class and I ended up taking it anyway, so I understand lol), but be prepared to spend long hours and deal with a lot of frustration. Talk to your concentration advisor, Professor Morin, SPS, or any physics upperclassman to make the right decision for you. Here's my best advice if you do end up taking this class:
– have a good pset buddy/group to work with and emotionally support you in this class
– take it pass/fail if you can
– be honest with yourself and the teaching staff about your level of background and communicate it with them for the good of you and your peers (physics students tend to want to exaggerate how much they know – myself included – but being honest will serve you better in the long run)
– ask clarifying questions during/after lecture and read about the topic in Griffiths
– study chapters 1–6 in Griffiths before taking this class if you didn't cover it all in 143a
– take 151/153/264 if you can, or have friends who have taken those classes
Best of luck to you! I hope your semester goes better than mine did.
