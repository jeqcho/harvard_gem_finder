This is an incredible course that you will absolutely love if you're interested in privacy and/or technology. The content is fascinating and the course is very applicable to the real world; you will see things in a new light and challenge your current perception of privacy. Due to the discussion–based nature of the class, you will also get to learn a lot from your peers and their experiences. The work is very manageable and is interesting.
I would enthusiastically recommend CS105 to anyone, from any academic background, who finds these issues interesting! (It can be a tough lottery to get through, so if you're on the fence, give it a shot to potentially help your chances in future years!)

Professor Waldo brings a depth of knowledge, incredible dry wit, and supportive nature to every class. This course is unique among undergrad classes, offering a technical view into issues of privacy enriched by considering the issue from all perspectives (legal, philosophical, etc). Depending on your background, you can lean more heavily into technical or policy–analytic work, and will have ample opportunity to collaborate with others to make the most of the varied skillsets in the room.

Each individual assignment is absolutely manageable, but it is worth coming in with the understanding that you'll have a few small deadlines in a given week rather than rarer spikes of work.

This is a good class if you want an introduction to privacy/security and don't want too much work. You have to do weekly readings and ask/answer questions, but the way they set it up is fairly annoying where you can't really frontload any work. There are biweekly–ish papers that ask you to recommend a privacy policy to a public figure based on their previous stances or explain a technology, which was interesting, but somewhat less applied than desired. The classes and prep materials overlap heavily, and as a result the classes can feel redundant. Jim Waldo is an amazing professor with deep technical and cultural expertise about privacy and technology. This is a very "you–get–what–you–put–in" class, so if you want to take it for Jim, make sure you go to the office hours, do all the readings, and pay attention in class. If you don't care about that, you can get away with doing almost none of that.
Take this class! Professor Waldo is excellent with a great teaching style and industry experience, the lectures will have you thinking hard long after they're finished, and the workload is very reasonable. No coding experience required, and no final which is great. One of my favorite classes I've ever taken here.

When I took this course, it was at least 50% non–CS majors I believe. This means the classes rarely get extremely technical, and rather lay on the surface level of what should be legally allowed without much reference to the tech aspect. This comes with pros and cons
1) Grading is wack: It's done by undergraduate TFs despite the fact that most assignments (due Sundays!!!) are essay–type assignments. You'll get a heart attack on your first paper when you get a 2.5 out of 5 before Waldo explains that 2.5 is what he expects!
2) You really don't have to do all the readings to understand the class; since Waldo expanded the class to 48 people, you spend a lot of the time trudging through boring explanations of technology that the Gov concentrators don't understand (no joke, someone once asked if going into Incognito stops your ISP from tracking you).
3) Waldo is great! He is here to make sure you have fun, but some assignments are annoying because it's really just Waldo flexing how much he knows.
Please take this class! But only take it if you are interested in the subject and want to contribute to class discussion. I learned so much from not just Professor Waldo but also my peers. It's such an incredible and interactive experience.

There's no required coding (just optionals technical versions of assignments) which makes it very approachable.
