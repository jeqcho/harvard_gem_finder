An amazing, well–designed class. Each lecture teaches you so much information about a certain component of modern China, and is backed with so much interesting research that it leaves you stunned by the end of class. You cover so much breath (and depth) with the course material––it's clear that David Yang has spent a long time not only designing, but also practicing, the content he wants to teach. He also places special emphasis on 1) domestic Chinese politics (something that truly fuels China but is not as often discussed in political economy) and 2) experimental design (how some really interesting findings came to be, especially given the lack of public data on the CCP).

The sections are also very engaging and there are only 3 response papers, written on virtually any topic you're interested in. The class starts out with a question on China's future––will it continue to grow, or will it inevitably blunder?––and it really leaves you with so much to say for both sides. Probably my favorite class this semester, and my favorite econ/gov class overall. Highly recommend!

STOP SCROLLING AND TAKE THIS COURSE. HANDS DOWN THE BEST COURSE I HAVE TAKEN AT HARVARD. WHY? Keep reading.

Let's chat content. If you take this course, you will be able to read any (yes, ANY) article about China and actually understand how things work in China. That is a WILD skill that few have –– and it is important to understand China no matter what you do after college. There was not one week where I wasn't stimulated by the material; some of my favorite topics were discussions on how China's political system works, the ethnic tensions we keep hearing about, discussions about AI in China, and Hong Kong and Taiwan. I usually hate school and classes in general and let me tell you... I was actually so excited to go to class each day because I knew I was learning super interesting things that would change the way I view China.

Let's chat professor. David Yang. Is. The. Best. I tend to have a pretty critical view towards China as I imagine most people in the U.S. do –– and I was really nervous that the class might praise China. It did not. David really taught as neutral of a course as you will ever find. It forced each of us to make our own views. What surprised me is that my own views towards China were challenged and sometimes affirmed. Also, David actually cares about his students. He makes so much time for us in office hours and he does pizza lunches which you should definitely go to.

Ok, if that hasn't convinced you already, let's talk about grading/assignments. I hate (HATE) writing. I'm an AM concentrator and I have somehow managed to make all my other classes non–writing. BUT these essays are not hard to write, trust me. For each essay, you take one current events article and do more research on it. It would take up a weekend but that was it. And it's only 3 essays. Outside of that, though I did do the readings, you don't have to... in fact, none of my friends did and they all also crushed the final (the final only tests the lecture material). THAT IS AMAZING FOR THE VALUE YOU ARE GETTING OUT OF IT.

I cannot recommend this course more. I truly cannot.
