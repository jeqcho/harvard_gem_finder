The course material, for what it's worth, was solid and quite comprehensive, providing a theoretical treatment to linear and generalized linear models, and supplemented by a good set of lecture notes (although I think including all the R code is extraneous). However, the administration of the course itself has left a lot to be desired, in my opinion. As Prof. Glickman would put it, a potpourri of problems:
– The pacing of this course could be improved. The first few lectures were all linear algebra review. More technically challenging material or interesting content, such as quasi–likelihood or model selection, by comparison, was jammed into the last lecture. Some nontrivial technical details would also sometimes be skimmed over, creating a mirage effect in which you think you know what's going on until you don't, and when that happens, you really don't know what's going on.
– It's a bit ridiculous in my opinion to have 50% of the course based on two and a half hours (two 75–minute exams). 75 minutes isn't nearly enough time to provide a comprehensive evaluation of the course material. Combine this with harsh grading and I think the marks you get aren't very representative of your command of the class content. Also, having a problem set, final project, and exam due in one week—the week before exams when all other final assignments are due—doesn't give a good balance. This is an issue that's been brought up in past reviews.
– Homework assignments seemed more focused on proving technical details, lemmas, or special cases from the lecture notes, which wasn't a great way to review and consolidate the material.
– Some of the course staff had a lowkey holier–than–thou vibe, and were not very accessible.
In summary, while the actual material of this course was good, your mileage may vary. Will you learn stuff by taking this course? Probably. But will you have enjoyed learning it? In my view, that's debatable.

The course material is useful, but the pedagogy of this class was really bad.

In lectures, Dr. Glickman will often explain extremely basic concepts, like what a derivative is (a real example), how a Taylor approximation works, why the expected score derivatives are zero, etc. He wastes time for several minutes, and then suddenly he moves on and speedruns the explanation for some nontrivial algebraic manipulations, and you're completely lost. Sometimes it feels like he explains really basic knowledge because it's easier to explain than the more complicated stuff, but this is unhelpful because what you truly need help understanding is what kind of random variable the quasi likelihood model is actually supposed to represent and why you can’t write it down as a probability distribution (is it because the PDF doesn't integrate to 1 and just needs a normalizing constant? We still don't know!).

The exams are only one hour long, which is truly not enough time to do any problem solving. You also can't bring a theorem sheet. As a result, they ignore all but roughly two lectures’ worth of material, and only test how fast you can take a derivative, do tricks with matrices, or recall the specific wording of definitions. There is less than 10 points of variance between the min and max project grades, and project grades are capped at 95, since higher scores are reserved for “publication worthy” papers, even though the project is supposed to be a literature review and nowhere do the guidelines mention expecting novel results. Thus, the class grade is basically completely determined by the two *one–hour* exams.

Furthermore, the TFs were unhelpful. The review sessions were not intelligible because they were drawing on a Zoom whiteboard with a laptop touchpad. They were extremely stingy with pset grading, and took off points for typos and "poor choice of wording," even if the ideas are clear. It is also quite insulting that one TF expressed the belief the students taking the class this year were just a "dumb" group of students, hence why they thought Stat 244 was better as a 20–student class because the material "didn't get any harder" from last year and seemed to not understand why everyone can't “just do better on the midterm." Mind you, the course material is not rocket science. If the students in the course, who are generally graduate students in statistics–related fields and competent undergraduates who succeeded in stat 210/211, can’t figure out the basics of generalized linear models, it might not be because they are “dumb.”

I am not usually so annoyed by teaching styles, and this is the first time in several semesters at Harvard that I have expressed such a vehement level of distaste in a Q guide review. Stat 244 epitomizes all the bad things about taking classes: learning techniques that aren't particularly novel (and might not lead to interesting research directions), but in a convoluted way, and banging your head against the wall to complete pset problems that are all just quite random linear algebra tricks, and being tested on how fast you can reason about the four fundamental spaces of a matrix like a juvenile high schooler. This class was such a bad experience that it has made me jaded about continuing to take more classes that could turn out to be like this one and has motivated me to take time off from school.
