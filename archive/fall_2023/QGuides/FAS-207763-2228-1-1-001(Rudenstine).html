What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you want to take a class with a famous figure who genuinely cares for students and treats them as grandchildren, take this class! Professor Rudenstine is the former president of Harvard, though he is one of the most generous teachers I have met. He brought us out to Toscano's twice (an expensive Italian restaurant in Harvard Square), where we had a private room and were allowed and encouraged to order as much as we want free of charge. Although the class was technically 2 hours and 45 minutes, we had a 30–45 minute lunchbreak about an hour into class, which was super nice both for our physical wellbeing, as well as being a means to get to know the other students. To this day, I am good friends with many of the people in the freshman seminar, and I have to say that, despite reading similar comments about this class in prior years, I was not expecting this to happen. As for the academic aspect of this class, it is really no work, as one needs to read the poems before class, which can take as much or as little time as one would want. There are essays, but the stakes are low since it is a pass/fail class, and Professor Rudenstine will clearly pass everyone who takes the class. As a result, a very nurturing and pleasant environment results, where students come to discuss material without the worry of grades in the least bit.
This seminar provides a fantastic introduction to many famous and influential poets in English/American history, all within a small and tight–knit environment. Professor Rudenstine is very kind and knowledgeable and cares about each of his students. The workload is relatively light, which allows you to devote a lot of time to thinking about the poems for each class. However, do get started on the essays earlier rather than later.
This course is lovely. Professor Rudenstine knows an incredible amount about the poetry itself. It is so clear that he has lived with these lines—and his insights are precious. There were times in class when he explained a line or stanza so well that I could do nothing more than just smile.

The workload is quite manageable. Most weeks, the homework is just to read a set of poems. This usually took me a few hours. If you take this course, I encourage you to put in an honest effort toward the poems. It'll be difficult to meaningfully contribute in discussion if you don't really understand the poems. There are a few essays, but those two are enjoyable. We wrote ours on "Filling Station" (Elizabeth Bishop) and "Midterm Break" (Seamus Heaney), and both were poems that I'll remember for a while.

There were also a few dinners outside of class that we shared together, and those were thoroughly enjoyable. Aside from the clear benefit of being able to enjoy a meal outside of the dining halls, it was also fun to get to see everyone outside of a classroom setting. I think these dinners left me with the feeling that I knew the people in this seminar better.

I encourage you to take this class—provided that you are interested in poetry (but if you're not, this should never have been an option). The poems in this class are truly superb.
