You sprinkle some rigor on the ideas that you learned in real analysis. Neither useful nor fun.
The material from Math 114 is essential for any mathematics major. However, if the course is taught like it was this semester, it will be an unfortunate experience. The homework assignments are straight out of the textbook (where I did all of my learning for the semester), while the exams are poorly written and cover completely differential material. This is the second class that you should take in real analysis, but hopefully it is taught better in the future.
I would recommend this class, but only for math concentrators and only because the material is important if you're interested in math. When you do take the class, be warned that it's not great. The lectures are pretty dry. Each lecture just feels like a list of theorems with little motivation. And the proofs of the theorems in lecture are the kind of proof that doesn't follow a natural train of thought that you could think of yourself, instead seeming like someone just magically figured out that it works. Thankfully, the homeworks, while sometimes difficult, aren't too bad, so if you put in effort then you should do fine.

There were many complaints about this class, which you'll probably see in the other comments, and they're all fair complaints. But there were some bright sides as well. Although most of the material is dry, the basic underlying concepts of measure theory and Lebesgue integration are very interesting. Also, Prof Yau was very lenient. He only assigned 8 homeworks, and whenever a homework was too long or a particular problem was too difficult, we asked the CAs to ask him to shorten the homework, extend the deadline, or remove the problem, and he always listened. He could have explained some things better and been more engaging, but ultimately I think the material itself is just pretty dry, so I don't blame him in particular for the class being dry.
If you are a math concentrator who wants to gain a base level understanding of the concepts in measure theory you will achieve that through this course.
I went into this class to learn functional analysis, and while I did learn quite a reasonable amount, I would say I came out knowing more about Prof. Yau's life and personal beliefs more than I know analysis. I would say that I go to every class in the hopes that he decides to talk about how weird of a person von Neumann was during his time at Princeton. He is a very intelligent person, but he is so intelligent that he doesn't really know how to hold our hands through proofs, but instead skips a million steps, and jumps around all over the place when he presents results in class. It's quite a challenge to keep up with him, and I would even go so far as to say that if you are able to keep up with him, then you might be truly something special. I absolutely hated the last part of the class, felt more like I was taking stat than math. Take this class if you are a genius but don't expect anything to be smooth sailing.
HTYau likes to give a lot of life advice. His first few units were also quite clear, although the Fourier and Probability units were quite disjointed and difficult to follow.

The textbooks changed around a lot. This was quite confusing.

Also, the PSets vary an insane amount in the difficulty. Some weeks Psets will take you o(1) hours, some weeks Psets will take you O(10) hours. Be prepared.
This is my third semester at Harvard, and this is easily the worst class I've taken here. The professor did a poor job explaining the material, and I felt that I learned very little due to the poor quality of instruction. It didn't help that the organization was super unclear, so we were moving back and forth between different topics. Course logistics were super disorganized too, and it was routine for us to renegotiate problem set deadlines and the problems of the problem sets. The midterm and finals were not cumulative and were focused on what we did in class just the week and a half before. If you're a math concentrator you'll probably take this class because it is the only rigorous undergrad analysis class offered here, which is a real shame. But just go in with low expectations, and you'll probably have to learn analysis on your own if you ever want to understand it.
Avoid this class if possible! This was poorly taught, the psets/final were horrendous, and everything was altogether very disorganized and hard to follow. You will probably learn more measure theory and functional analysis if you teach it to yourself (say by self–studying Papa Rudin). However, I will at least give Yau credit for being pretty funny and entertaining, if a bad lecturer.
The material is difficult and a lot of the work will be done outside of class on your own time. So make sure you're able to commit enough time to it, because otherwise it's very easy to fall behind. PSETs aren't long but are tricky, but the CAs were extremely helpful so it wasn't too much of an issue. In general, I think it's a very important class to take if you're interested in pursuing higher math but it is definitely among the harder 100–level math courses.
If you believe infinity does not exist and it belongs to God, Yau's class is for you!
If you don't really like analysis, this class might be a good one for your analysis distribution requirement. The class was a little disorganized, but I still felt like I learned a lot, and it wasn't the most overwhelming (not that I didn't have to put a lot of time into it), and the course staff was pretty flexible and the CAs in particular were very nice and helpful. Lectures were not super helpful in my experience, but I'm still glad I didn't skip lecture since I got some stuff through osmosis.
This class depends almost entirely on the prof and how they choose to run it, take at your own risk. The material is interesting though

The content of this course is wide–ranging and can be very interesting in its own right. But, overall, it does matter how motivated you are to work with analysis later on.
Math 114 this year was a bit disappointing. While it's clear that Yau cares a lot about the content he teaches and that he's really experienced in research related to functional analysis/measure theory, it sometimes felt like the class didn't have much direction, lectures were often confusing, and problem sets/exams had various flaws. Changes were made to the take–home final exam up until a few days before the due date, and there was a ton of confusion over various questions.

The content itself is pretty interesting (e.g., Lp spaces, linear operators, measure theory, Lebesgue integration, Fourier analysis), but I'm not sure that the class stoked much interest in the subject by itself. Content–wise, be prepared to spend a lot of time doing Fourier analysis if Yau teaches the course – we probably spent at least a month or more doing various bits of Fourier theory. And while I'm glad that I now have a solid grasp on Fourier theory and various Fourier transforms (e.g., discrete Fourier transform, FFT, Fourier transforms/inversions), I think that some of the content could have been condensed, and I wish we had spent more time on other function space related topics.

The CAs were kind of the saving grace of the course – definitely go to office hours for some problems that don't seem very doable without help.
This is bad. Just take another class which satisfies the analysis requirement. Definitely don’t recommend. Yau is cool though.
