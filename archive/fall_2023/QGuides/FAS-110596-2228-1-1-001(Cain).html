If you take this class, make sure that you are ready to put in the required effort needed to have a worthwhile takeaway that can be applied to other aspects of your college career, or maybe even your professional career in the future. The concepts covered in this class are interesting, but can be really difficult to grasp at first. Although I didn't do it most of the times, I think that actually looking over lecture notes before the lecture happens would be really beneficial to you so that you can participate in discussions and have an understanding about what you're ultimately going to learn from the lecture at the end, because sometimes it's hard to understand the bigger picture when just learning about new formulas, examples, or methods to solving an ODE. Attending office hours are pretty much essential to being able to fully understand how to solve the psets, and the CAs are very helpful in answering questions you have about the psets, course material, and course–related topics in general. But, it requires effort on your part to make sure you go to those office hours weekly, or have a set group to do psets with, and also that you actually review the things that you learn in class frequently. It's still a difficult course regardless, but you can learn a lot of applicable ideas like interpreting biological literature and understanding different types of models, even at some basic level. Wes is very enthusiastic about the things he teaches in this course and although it can be intimidating at first, if you dedicate a good amount of time to the psets and the final project, that effort won't go unnoticed.
The two broad sentiments behind Math 19a is that you love it or you don't. I personally really loved the class.
Material: A large emphasis is on ecological modeling, but if you're into medicine or biochemistry, the topics will also flow throughout the course. There are also concepts that come from human anatomy (either in papers like epidermal wounds, leukemia modeling, cardiac electrophysiology) that are introduced through a mathematical lens and changed my way of viewing processes like heartbeats, healing, and medication.
Lecture: Honestly it is so useful when it comes to reviewing the steps you need to approach in solving a problem set question. Go to every class and write down everything Wes puts on the board, even if it takes a minute to understand. It might click as you work through problems.
Problem sets: They're only once a week, which is so much better than Math 21a and 21b. If you're between this class and Math 22a or 22b, I'd select 19a if you don't have experience with proofs. Also office hours were helpful because the TFs are patient and will guide you as you approach questions. Show up even if you barely started.
Exams: It's nice that there are only two. It's scary that they cover so much content. One thing that caught me off guard is that your understanding of the readings is also asked. It's nothing tricky or too specific. If you take notes while Todd presents the key points in the article and later review his lecture slides (which contain more notes on Canvas) then you'll be ready. As for the math portion, make sure you know the rationale behind what certain topics are and not just the steps, because some questions will not be so simple as to just memorize the steps and write them down.
Final Project: Start early!! Your proposal is due sometime in mid–October. If you're learning about a certain concept in LS1a, LPS A, or another life sciences adjacent class, pick your favorite and find some math behind it. This way, it feels more interesting as you learn more and study for your other class. As long as you begin graphing models (at least one graph) before Thanksgiving break and continue before/throughout reading period, you'll be fine.
Always reach out to Wes! He's a great person to talk to and genuinely is rooting for everyone's success. If his office hours don't work for you, reach out for an appointment! He will be more than happy to do so.

This is an incredibly cool class and probably the most interesting learning experience I've had. If you're curious about applications of advanced math in life science, this class does that extremely well. It's really amazing to learn to be able to model the processes you're learning about in LS1A or LPSA with differential equations. You cover such a wide range of topics in math and science, and Wes is SO passionate about what he's teaching and so perfectly clear in his explanations that you really feel like you're immersed in the material and learning every day in lecture. That being said, it was very, very hard! Don't take this class if you're not a math person! I have always loved math and considered myself very good at it, and I still strongly considered dropping this class at some points. It's a very small, honestly sort of experimental course with fewer resources than classes like 1b, 21a, and 22a that many first semester first years take, and I felt like there was a disproportionate number of legitimate math geniuses that really make you feel like you have no idea what you're doing. Maybe I just came from a slightly humbler math background at a non–feeder public school, but this class moved fast and did not really wait for you to catch up. You definitely need to pay attention during class and write everything down and then probably follow up at office hours too. A good TF and good pset buddies really saved me, but expect to spend hours and hours seemingly making no progress. It's a little bit of trauma bonding. But the sense of accomplishment is insane—for the most part, you're solving concrete problems and you can see the potential implications of your math. Midterms are hard, and you really have to do and understand the readings to prepare, but there's a final project rather than an exam. Get started on this fairly soon and suffer a little bit to get it done, but it's a great opportunity and you'll come away with a more or less original contribution to science via math. It's very cool.
