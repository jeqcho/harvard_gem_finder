Honestly, I took this class only because it was the only interesting one that could fit into my schedule. The essays/assignment due dates kind of interfered with several of my midterms, so just be wary of that. Also, don't think you're just going to get an easy A for this class— you'll actually have to put in quite a decent amount of work for each of your essays. Are there other classes that I kind of wish I'd taken? Yes. The material content for this class is great: reading about con artists or watching movies & documentaries about famous scams is comparatively enjoyable "homework!" That being said, the class won't be about how well you know these con artists. It's focused heavily on what you think a profile/journalistic piece about a con artist "suggests" about something greater (for instance American society's get rich quick phenomenon, etc.). Personally, I found this transition into speaking about what the text itself suggests and not about the author's intended effect tricky to navigate. Distinguishing that writing gets increasingly challenging after the first paper when you move on from one specific profile (on Anna Delvey) to having to grapple with comparing two texts for the next paper (on the Talented Mr. Ripley).

But, I most enjoyed the creative freedom with the topics we could write about within these texts (and in the final paper which can kinda just be about anything you want). The content is very interesting, which makes the writing/newly learned writing strategies all the more interesting to apply to your essays, even though it might be challenging! Learned a LOT in this class though: analytical questions, bridging concepts, Gaipa strategies, being encouraged to write very shitty first drafts, etc. are all concepts you will be familiarized with coming out of it. All in all, I'd recommend taking this class — it's very rewarding!
