pasting the above, because I think it reflects my thoughts:
I have nonstop talked about this course this whole term. I believe that everyone would gain something deeply valuable, because all of our lives are mediated through technology, whether its advanced, like our phones or air conditioning, or more rudimentary like the clothes that we wear. Through this course, I reconceptualized my definition of technology to understand that it has coevolved with humanity, and that we should consider how technology is no longer a means to an end, but has subsumed our culture to the point at which it is the end in and of itself. I see the way people move through campus with their heads down, consumed by their phones, and see ramifications of this culture absolutely everywhere. It has impassioned me to further explore this rich topic because I really believe that it is changing the phenomenological experience of being a human, and my worry is that it might not be for the better. I want to study it so that I might participate in a cultural pushback against this collective dependence on advanced technology.

The ideas that this class purports to cover are very interesting and the readings are well chosen (except for the Circle) but the class just feels like an expression of the professor's ego. The premise of the class is good and the questions are interesting but once you get a couple lectures in, it feels like every lecture is the same despite covering different readings. The professor doesn't really ground his lectures on the readings. He touches on them but goes into little depth about the texts, instead preferring to espouse his own (unsupported, no evidence provided) thoughts and talk a LOT about etymologies. The lectures don't help me understand the texts at all and every claim feels like an oversimplification with little nuance and again, no evidence backing it. Everything is put neatly into a dichotomy or everything has to do with a random etymology. It's a weird in–between between a philosophy class, which would have much more logical and reasoned arguments, and an English class, which would have textual support for every claim made. The TFs for the class were much more helpful for understanding the texts before having to write papers on them. The professor's lecturing style might seem engaging at first, but once you realize there's no substance behind the material, it's completely unsatisfying. There's so much potential for this topic and this class completely falls flat.
