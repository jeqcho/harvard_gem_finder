it's a higher math to coding ratio than advertised
When I was making my course schedule, I was debating between taking this course and CS50, but now I do not think they are equivalent courses. CS50 provides a broad and fast–paced introduction to computer science in general through several coding languages, whereas AM10 provides a slow and much more thorough introduction to coding with Python. I am less knowledgeable about computer science than my friends who took CS50, but I know how to do many more things than they can in Python. Additionally, the course load for AM10 is significantly less than for CS50. I had no prior coding experience, and I was able to grasp nearly everything we did in class throughout the semester. Additionally, this course has no tests or quizzes, and all assignments are collaborative, which is a plus.

Don't take this course concurrently with Math 1b if you don't have to. This is an applied math course. There will be math. You will have to apply it. Applying math to a coding/engineering problem generally requires *understanding* the math you are applying. Unless you're a rock star at math up through multivariable calculus and linear algebra, some of the math will seem a little hand–wavy, but you can still get a lot out of the course if you work to understand what's going on. For this course, prior math knowledge >>> prior coding knowledge.

It's a great alternative to CS–50 for all my engineering friends looking to fulfill that CS requirement who don't want to participate in the experience that is CS–50.

The pace of the course does pick up quite a bit so don't be fooled by the first couple of weeks (especially if you know how to code already). That being said, if you work to follow along and actually understand the lecture, lab, and review section, you will be able to copy–paste–edit large chunks of code on the problem sets without too much trouble.

WORK WITH OTHER PEOPLE: go to the help rooms (Logan is very helpful as are the CAs), talk to people at your table and exchange phone numbers, make group chats and meet–up times around the midterm and final projects so that you are not alone, etc.

No midterm or final exam is a HUGE plus. It fits the learning style and material of the course, and it saves your life during midterm and final season (especially if you're in STEM and have lots of lovely 3–hr exams).

The teaching in this course is medium to good. Logan's lecturing is quite good. The entire teaching team is friendly and approachable and they will make time to help you if you're confused.

Go to the review section if you can. It's directly applicable to the problem sets.
