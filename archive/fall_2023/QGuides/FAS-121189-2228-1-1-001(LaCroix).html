Coming to Harvard, I could never have imagined giving a course a 1/5 on the Q guide, but LS1a truly deserves it. It is very hard to put into words how much I hated this class. I came into this course with a very strong background in biology and chemistry and a passion for life science, but this course completely turned me away from ever taking another science class again.

Here is what makes this class just so bad.

Lectures: On the first day of class, the preceptors and professors introduced the “reverse classroom” approach, which soon became an excuse for not teaching. Most of the lectures consisted of the preceptors or professors just asking questions, rather than explaining the scientific concepts. At the start, this approach was ok because the material was easy, but as the concepts became harder, the lectures became a waste of time because I skipped most of the lectures and still got very high scores on the assignments and midterms.

Homework: This may be the worst part of the class. Each week, the problem set just becomes a challenge to see how long it will take for a TF to give you the answers at office hours. Most of the problems can’t be answered completely correctly using the textbook and the learning pod videos, so you have to go to office hours if you want to do well. Additionally, YOUR ANSWERS HAVE TO MATCH THE ANSWER KEY ALMOST EXACTLY TO GET CREDIT. This was incredibly frustrating during the semester because you can understand everything and have an almost perfect explanation written, but then have to rewrite the answer entirely because the answer key uses a couple of different words. My advice is to find TFs that basically read the answer key to you and go to SSN because some of the fellow students will just show you the answer key. Also, always check why you lost points on the PSETS because often it is just a grading error that you should not lose points for. Utilize regrades.

Tests and PIES: The assessment method in this course is just ridiculous. In fall 2022, there were 6 quizzes (PIEs), 2 midterms, and a final, all in 12 weeks. It gets to the point where there are no new concepts to even assess, so the preceptors just create PIE questions that focus on the most obscure details. My advice is don’t study for the PIEs a lot, just familiarize yourself with the textbook so you know what page to go to when the PIE is happening. The midterms also focus on tiny details, so don’t expect to do well because there will inevitably be some small things that you miss.

Grading: Unlike other classes, which reward you for understanding, this course just rewards you for being better than your fellow classmates and friends. From day 1, it becomes a competition for who can get an A because only the top 15% or so get one, even if that means you need a 96 or 97 to qualify. On problem sets, I repeatedly found myself asking myself if I should help others or if doing so would penalize me in the end.

The one redeeming factor of LS1a is the friends that you will make. But, this is not enough of a reason to take this class. I highly recommend TAKING LPSA INSTEAD. LPSA is actually taught. The structure of the class is also much better and the grading is more forgiving. Good luck if you decide to take LS1a anyway.

Office Hours – Go to Sien and Rebecca's office hours. They care so much about making sure you actually know the material and your wellbeing. Going to their office hours once will help clarify everything (I mean it) that you are confused about. On weeks you have lab assignments, you can often knock out the lab assignment and Pset before the weekend.

Learning Pods – Read the textbook and stay ON TOP OF IT (taking notes) throughout the entire semester. I think this was the biggest differentiator between people who did relatively well and people who were stressing every time the finals rolled around. Learning pod videos are essentially a duplicate of the textbook material and so is lecture so you don't really need to pay attention. Lectures are meant to be supplementary (not where you learn the material) and just skip through the learning pod videos to the quiz unless you are confused by something in the textbook. Then it's nice to go back and watch the videos, but ONLY IF YOU ARE CONFUSED. Otherwise, it's a waste of time.

Lectures – Lectures are often repeats of the textbook/learning pods but sometimes its nice to pay attention when they give out Pset help. Otherwise, do something more productive during lecture.

PIEs – You have to learn how to take PIEs. Each question is trying to test you on a certain topic and if you can figure it out you'll be able to do it (aka do not overthink the problems). They are lowkey stressful because they are biweekly and they can be a lot when you have two of them sandwiched with a midterm. Do the practice PIE questions and take good notes because they are open notes. Also, in the grand scheme of things, each PIE is not worth that much (2%) and you do get a drop. Make sure you are careful and take it with a calm mind.

Midterms – Midterms are very fair. There aren't any curveball questions and it feels like a longer PIE. The best studying you can do is to make a good cheat sheet by reviewing the labs, problem sets, section handouts, and your notes (they don't give practice midterms which is weird). Be aware though that they are pretty brutal with partial credit. It's also good to make your midterm cheat sheets online so that for future midterms your life is much easier.

Final – They give you a practice final and I thought it was similar to the midterms in terms of fairness and what they expect you to know. Many questions are similar to the practice final.

Section – If you read the textbook, then section might not be the most helpful but it's helpful to have a section leader who is good with grading because they are the ones who grade all of your assignments. Additionally, the handouts are useful because they often provide concise summaries. The most important thing with this course is to not drown in the details and section handouts help distill the core information and the broader patterns that LS1A wants you to know. It's super important to develop a sense of what these broader patterns that they are testing you on are.

A dark side of LS1A is that they curve and sometimes this can mean down. In the past when there were no midterms and only open note PIEs, the cutoff for an A was between a 95 and 97 depending on who you ask. I don't know how midterms will factor into this (overall the midterm grades helped even out the distribution and I think will help the curve) but if you want a good grade, just follow the above advice and you'll be able to get an A. Just figure out how the course works and you will do well. Overall, I do think it is a good intro to bio and chem, it's just a lot
