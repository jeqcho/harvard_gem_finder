This class teaches introductory mechanics, but it is taught with the expectation that you have already taken a course on classical mechanics. So the emphasis is not on building an intuition for the subject, but rather to develop a strong problem–solving foundation for Newtonian physics by tackling problems more complicated than the AP Physics C curriculum. I would recommend reviewing khan academy's physics library as preparation for this course. Reviewing Calc 1 is not a bad idea either, but physics fundamentals are more important for this course in my opinion.

That said, if you have a physics background and you don't want to be up all night in Physics 16, I would reccommed 19 over 15a. There would be much more new material.

I entered this class having taken C Mechanics and having tutored students in physics throughout high school. With this background, I was able to cruise through the first couple months of the course with low effort (and low lecture attendance). That being said, I had to put in much more effort after we got to rotation and angular momentum. The problem sets are nontrivial but not impossible. I spent 3–4 hours every Wednesday night getting through them. A good pset group is necessary. Section and lab are both boring, lab especially. Professor Morin is lovely and extremely enthusiastic about physics. He is approachable for questions and willing to help. While I learned how to solve mechanics and relativity problems in this class, I felt like I was missing a lot of theoretical background. We generally didn't prove what we learned in class, and at times it felt like we just learned how to apply formulas. All told, if you're premed, this course might be too hard. If you want to concentrate in physics, you might be better off taking 16 or 19.
This course can be what you make of it. You could do the entire pset during physics night (guilty) and get a surface level appreciation of the material, or really dive in during the week and gain a solid understanding of the concepts. I didn't have the time to do the latter, but can see how it would have made the course more enjoyable. I would have appreciated more derivations during lecture, as a lot of the time Morin would just state a theorem or formula and leave it up to us to seek out the proof. If you do the homeworks, the exams (at least the free response problems) are basically free––they're usually exact copies of or very similar to the hw problems. Overall, a standard and relatively challenging mechanics course.
