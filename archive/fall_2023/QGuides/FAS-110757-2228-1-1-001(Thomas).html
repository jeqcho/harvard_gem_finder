This is a high level Latin course in pretty much every sense. Workload is considerably high with a lot of translation for each class, weekly quizzes, a presentation, a midterm, a final paper, a recitation, and a final exam (the last three occurring in a week–long window). The subject matter of Vergil's pastorals, while superb poetry, may bore the unenthused. Much of the information covered in class discusses connections to other authors, so without a significant understanding of these less famous writers, this information is not easily understood. You need to have a high level of understanding of Latin elegy coming in to get a lot out of the course. Unfortunately, it seemed that more time could have been spent discussing the poem itself, but this would prove a challenge even if it were the sole focus due to the rapid pace of the assignments. With that said, Professor Thomas is clearly an expert on the subject and puts a lot of passion into this course. He is also very accessible and seems to care about the success of his students. It's a unique opportunity to learn from the world expert on this renowned poem, but be wary that the effort required might not match the reward.
This is a really wonderful class with fantastic, understanding, and supportive instructors. You'll read a lot of Latin and learn so much about the Eclogues and Georgics, not just what the text itself says, but also what questions it raises and what we still don't know about them. Also, this class is relatively low–stress up until finals week since there aren't many quizzes, but even though the class has numerous assignments during finals week, they're all different formats so you get to practice various skills such as speaking and writing alongside reading. Even though this is a large Latin class, it still feels like you can get to know the professor and TF well individually, too. Overall, this class is a great mix of challenging and fun.
This class is a really great overview of the two lesser known works of Virgil, and if you're a Classics concentrator or even just someone who took Latin in high school, I would highly recommend it! Readings can be heavy at times, but the workload is manageable and Professor Thomas is very understanding. He's also the best in the field, and there's no better to teach the course than him. The class is very well organized, informative, and enjoyable.
Take this course and know that your patience will be rewarded. It might not be until you are reviewing the readings ahead of the final that you are able to appreciate the scope and delicacy of Virgil's poetic practice. And if you are lucky enough to take this course with either Dr. Thomas or Phoebe Lakin, you will have as guides two inspired readers whose enthusiasm, far from applying undue pressure, is positively infectious. If you retain even a small portion of their expertise, you will can truly yourself among the most fortunate readers of Virgil.
This is not a class to take lightly, because the material is really precious and important—it should be read with utmost care if it is to be rewarding at all. Otherwise, the Georgics and Eclogues fall very flat.
Such a great class! Professor Thomas is so kind, and Phoebe as a TF was the most supportive. I really learned to love the Eclogues and the Georgics, and I feel like I want to read them again to keep understanding them better. The reading is probably standard for an 100–level Classics course, and it was very daunting for me at first since it was my first 100–level Classics class, but it truly did become so much more manageable even as the number of lines increased. The instructors really focus on understanding and appreciate the material first and foremost, so grades are a secondary matter; I really appreciated this as someone also taking high–pressure STEM courses.
Professor Thomas and Phoebe were amazing, and I highly recommend the course! Virgil's Eclogues and Georgics are so rich, and it's cool to read both of them in the same class to get to know more about continuity/themes in Virgil. Be prepared for a *lot* of reading, but the course staff are there to support you and I learned a lot.
Wonderful class! The opportunity to learn from Professor Thomas is obviously amazing. Class covers both a run–through of translations and intertexts, which are incredibly interesting: Virgil as an author uses extensive reference (e.g., to Theocritus, Callimachus, himself, etc.) — and it is a joy to discover each. There is a ~6–8 page paper at the end, where you can dive deep into a specific area, which is always interesting to do: I would encourage spending some time (whether in reviewing for class or exams or in the paper) to further analyze the texts and make connections between those themes discussed in class; there is always more to Virgil :) Also, it's always fun to search for scholarly articles on a part of the text one is interested in and find those from one's own professor.
Virgil is brilliant, Professor Thomas is brilliant, Phoebe is brilliant. I enjoyed this class so much, especially because of the material (I really didn't expect to like the nature poetry––it sounded dull but definitely proved otherwise once I gave it some time). Might be a little frustrating at first given how many words you'd have to look up if you haven't read nature–y Latin before, but it's a pretty quick adjustment and well worth it. Throughout the semester, I learned to read must faster while understanding a lot more, so if that's a goal for you I'd definitely recommend it. Basically, if you're in doubt, just take the class. Professor Thomas is literally the guy who wrote the book on the book, and this man's knowledge of intertext will leave you feeling inspired to look into other authors too.
This class was extremely difficult. There was a ton of homework, the material is very boring, and the classes are long lectures discussing largely irrelevant topics. There is also an excessive amount of exams/assessments. In addition to a quiz each week that needs to be studied for and hours of homework, there is a presentation, a midterm, a comprehensive final, a recitation, and a long final paper. The class may have been more interesting had we been able to dive deeper into the actual material. But because we had to cover so much material each class it was hard to truly appreciate the Eclogues and Georgics. The one positive is that both Prof Thomas and Phoebe were incredibly kind and accessible. Ultimately, unless you have a deep interest in plants and/or were born in ancient Rome, be wary taking this course.
This is a very special experience of reading beautiful poetry with a maestro. It's relatively easy and gives you a lot to appreciate and think about.
This is an incredible class. Reading the Eclogues and Georgics with Dr. Thomas is a privilege—his knowledge of these texts and intellectual passion for them are what make this course.
