Good class if you like Gov. Levitsky is an amazing lecture and truly one of a kind, but the readings can be a bit much if you're not that interested in the topic.
This class will touch on crucial ideas in comparative politics: why some countries have democratized and others haven't, why some authoritarian nations remain so durable, and why we may (or may not) be in a global democratic recession. What I like about all of these focuses is that they are incredibly timely; learning about what led to past changes and events helps inform one's current understanding of world affairs in a major way. There is a lot of reading, which requires good time management and note–taking skills to utilize effectively, but these are skills that this course will absolutely strengthen. You can't get a better lecturer than Professor Levitsky: energetic, passionate, detailed, with the ability to turn the most convoluted cases and ideas into something that makes sense. He is committed to his students, accessible, and approachable, who you will undoubtedly learn a lot from. A standout for me was the case memos in this class, where you select a country to study throughout the semester, using the readings as entry points. Be aware of a tough, memorization–based final exam, but I do not regret the sheer amount of content I have learned from across the globe, which will undoubtedly factor into how I look at the state of our world (and nation) today.

GOV 1290 is an intermediate–level course in the gov department that really dives deep into theories and cases for democratization and authoritarianism. Overall, I don't regret taking this course, but I feel like there were a few issues with it. You will be given a lot of reading each week (up to 200 pages), but you don't really have to do all of it (which is about true for any reading–intensive course). Lecture is really interesting because of Prof. Levitsky (who's probably the best gov prof. at Harvard), but go really deep into specific cases and histories. If you really are into comparative gov't and theories, then this course and its content is for you. Else, it might be a bit of a bore to dive into kind of obscure theories and case histories and can take up a bit of work to fully appreciate. You also only have about 7 assignments total in the class (5 memos, 1 midterm paper, and a final), so each one is weighted quite a bit even if they might not seem that rigorous –– the grading is not transparent at all (memos for example are grading on a check minus, check, or check plus scale –– but they never say how that translates to numeric or letter grades). Overall, Levitsky is awesome, but this course is not for everyone (and may not be for the eagle–eyed freshman like myself).
