This is a required course for HistSci concentrators, so you can't avoid it. But I will say that this course made me reconsider being a HistSci concentrator; if it weren't required, I would not recommend it to any student. Not because it is hard (it wasn't, especially if you start brainstorming topics and working on your paper early), but because there are so many better structured and organized courses that would teach you the same skills (and more) in writing an extensive paper. There is value in putting together your final 25–page research project, especially if you take the time to create something you are proud of. I think I'll look back on my paper fondly for a long time! That being said, there is so little concrete support from the class itself to put together this paper; the only thing I felt like this class gave me was the actual reason to write the paper.

Plenary is not beneficial. It is unclear what the attendance policy is, but if it is/was optional, I would say you could never attend plenary and still produce the exact same finished product! The utility of section is highly HIGHLY dependent on your TF, and I would recommend that you switch TF's/sections at the beginning of the semester if you get the sense that your TF is not going to give you useful feedback on your intermediate assignment if that is what you want.

Like I said, this is a required course, so nothing I say will change your mind on whether to take this course. But I hope a History of Science faculty or staff member reads this and realizes that some aspects of this course's structure and contents need to change. I have spoken with several concentrators who have said that this course actively made them decide not to write a senior thesis, which is a loss for the department. I also say all of these things as a student who has never actively disliked a class in my time at Harvard. This class was just not it.
This class is not easy! Pick a topic early in September to make your life easier (check with TFs). Also, something I didn't realize before the end of this class is that you write your 25 page research paper at a thesis–pace, meaning history of science thesis writers also submitted a 25–page chapter in December like you. That being said, it's fast and a grind at the end, so stay on top of your sources and get help early if you can.
This class is necessary for HistSci– make sure you pick a topic that is actually interesting. Pick it in the summer too, and get going on your draft earlier than the deadlines if you don't want to have tons to do at the end.
start thinking and preparing for the writing process early
Don't procrastinate.

The plenary often felt useless but the section component was where you really learned the skills to write your final paper. If you have choice, try to get Liv as your TF, she's the best!!
This paper may seem daunting. But the course requires many different smaller assignments throughout the semester that are helpful and that can keep students on track with the progress and thinking process of their essays! TF encouragement and assistance is vital. Also, it is important to not force the pressure on yourself to find the “best research question ever.” I think the best research questions are those you are actually interested in. And you will develop this question as the semester progresses.

This class is definitely more about the process than the teaching itself. You might very well not learn anything new in this class, but the process of writing a 25–page paper will make you learn how to do historical research. Like most things, you get what you put into this class, but even that is at the individual paper–writing level. Course faculty and staff are by in large very accessible and great, but the real value of the HistSci Department comes with its electives, not these required courses.
This course is a great opportunity to delve into designing and executing your own research project. However, plenary and sections were not incredibly useful and should be more structured. Additionally, most of the real work of the course (writing the paper) gets crammed into the last month, which seems like poor planning.
