If you're looking for a solid introduction to linear algebra, I really recommend this course. My section TF, Tyler, was extremely helpful in making sure we absorbed all of the concepts and definitions and made sure to connect each lesson so that the course made sense as a whole. The 3 psets a week might seem intimidating, but each pset is generally not too long and they really helped me with reviewing concepts and maintaining the course material. I personally didn't make it to each section, but I highly recommend that you do; it helps with keeping yourself accountable so that you don't fall behind. I don't think 21b is as proof heavy as the other course equivalents, but the concepts are still taught well in depth. Lastly, I really recommend that you start studying for the midterms and final exams at least a few days before; the material in this course is cumulative, so a lot of time is required to review it all.
Pretty boring but if you need to take it then you need to take it. Study for the exams by taking the practice exams, by far the best way. Class gets particularly ehhh towards the end.
Oh, Linear.
This class is indeed intense; conceptually, as the topics are abstract and the math is nothing like calculus, and time–wise, as the three–pset–per–week grind is constant, unending, and will require care and dedication. This class was like learning a language, or cultivating a plant requiring lots of daily attention. It's your child for the semester. Good time management skills are crucial to prevent late nights, but you will probably still have some late nights, and it does take a village––GO TO MQC!! Discussing questions with the CAs is extremely helpful because it helps iron out nuances, and that's very important.
The class takes a nice practical focus and is both computationally and conceptually oriented, so if you come into it mentally prepared for the grind and prepared to give it love, you will certainly do well. It **is definitely doable if you just give it the time it requires**, it's not the type of class that leaves you still lost if you're starting the psets the day they're released, and blocking out time to really digest the material and review and study in addition to doing the psets. I can imagine that if a person did this, they'd be just fine. Especially with how there are in fact a lot of supports for your grade, it's actually very flexible and supportive. If you bomb your first midterm because you thought doing the psets was enough to understand the concepts and didn't study much, (AKA you need to study outside of class), there is a grade–weighting option. Don't get to a pset until very late at night because of life happenings and/or other work, after MQC has closed? You can drop it, six of them in fact.
But a word of caution: DON'T fall behind early. DON'T use these pset drops early. Don't lean heavily on these supports, otherwise you will pay for it later and throughout the course because the material builds on itself!!!! This is NOT a class you can cram for, it's about building skills and knowledgeable intuition and *thoughtful* muscle memory. And did I mention the material builds on itself? It's like a nesting doll with three or four layers and you need each, in the right order! The first portion of the course will feel pretty doable––there are some nuances, and it's not bad. But if I can provide any one piece of advice from my experience, it's that you really, really need to have a strong understanding of the concepts that comprise all the material leading up to the first midterm (and doing well on the first midterm will save you a lot of anxiety). This will make the rest of the course go infinitely smoother, and not leave you with a sense that you're on a ship and there are a million holes leaking water everywhere that you have to keep patching up (speaking from experience here). Build the hull the right way the first time.
If you do these things, you will do well! Yes, it's fast–paced. Yes, the course really can't come second to others and be left behind or you will be left behind. But if you do this, you'll learn about an absolutely amazing realm of math that has just AWESOME implications for the world. It's genuinely incredibly powerful, with fascinating nuances and fascinating applications, including to differential equations (which was really cool to see through an explicit lens). You'll learn so, so much, and it's a very profound sense of satisfaction to have looking back.
Absolutely loved this class. Well taught, great structure. You learn so much and its such a useful class for everything. My favorite math class at Harvard.
