This class is a great experience. You learn a lot from a large variety of topics, some of which I had some background on before coming to college (cell cycle, genetics) and some of which I had never heard of (stat mech), but all of which we learned interesting new ways of thinking about.

I was worried that I would not have enough math experience coming in, as I had only done up to AP calculus (while many I talked to had taken multi or linear algebra), but this was not the case. While almost every unit has a math based approach, most of the math is well weaved into the other topics, and I did not need more than my knowledge of integrals/derivatives from BC calc (they sometimes use more complicated stuff in lectures so that we can know where their values came from, but you typically don't need to know how to replicate them for assignments). Prior experience with Python or a similar coding language is helpful, but not necessary as they will review everything you need to know and there will inevitably be someone in your class who knows how to code and can help you. I was also worried because I did not have any research experience coming in, but this wasn't a big problem either. Most people in the class have lab experience, which made me feel inexperienced, but they talked us through everything we needed to know in lab, so it wasn't a big deal. In terms of the bio/chem/physics content, it is mostly bio, and you should be fine if you did well in high school AP bio/chem.

The PSETs are difficult and you should start them early in the week and go to CA office hours, but they are not so long that you physically cannot finish them in the day or two before (it will just be a terrible experience), and there is only 1 a week (as opposed to the 4 a week you'd have between Math21a and LS1a). Organize study groups early in the year to make friends and stay on top of your work. LS50 is not the guaranteed bonding experience some of the Qreports make it out to be, but if you put in some effort to go to study sessions and talk to people in the class, you'll definitely meet some good friends. The midterms (our year at least) are easier than the PSETs, so if you do well on them and the practice exam, you are probably well prepared. If you are worried about workload and settling into, you could take a GenEd and a Freshman Seminar (like I did) with LS50, but honestly, it was not as much work as I thought it would be and I wish I'd taken a harder 3rd class.

The professors, TFs, and CAs were all amazing our year (Andrew Murray was on sabbatical, so I can't say for him, but Michael, Emma, Ben, and Kara were fantastic). They all have regular office hours (though most fell in times I couldn't attend, so I suppose I can't speak to their quality) and give engaging lectures with helpful slides (well.... Michael's can be a bit hard to follow, but you the classes are recorded so you can just rewatch parts). The CAs and TFs also office hours/PSET sessions which are very helpful

I can't say much about the labs since they change every year, but getting to do original research for the first time was very cool (even if I don't want to look at another ant for the rest of my life), but three straight hours every tuesday on top of an hour of LS50 and potentially an hour of another class is A LOT, but it was doable once we got through the initial lecture based lab sessions. If you are in a lab section right after class, make sure to eat some lunch. The cafe on the first floor of Northwest has some decent food and accepts Board Plus.

LS50 will NOT take over your life. It will be a heavy time commitment, and you will have to study a lot, but it does not have to consume your every waking moment. I had plenty of time to join clubs, make friends, and relax (except maybe in the day or two right before a midterm). I highly recommend this class because your first semester can feel strange and overwhelming, and it's grounding to have a regular class that meets every weekday and is a similar size to high school classes. Overall, this class is not easy, but I don't think it is much harder than taking LS1a and a math course, and instead of two large, generic classes, you get a more unique and personal experience from the small class size and original research.

There are more than enough resources to do well in this class if you care enough to do so.
If you are considering LS1a vs. LS50 AND you are passionate about science/research (even a little bit), please take LS50. It has everything that you would want in a class: a great community of 30–40 students, extensive time to get to know your teaching staff, incredible scientific topics that you will not learn in any other intro life science class, and just so much fun. I was in a similar boat of almost choosing LS1a if it weren't for an upperclassman who told me about this class, and to this day, I have 0 regrets.
First of all, you will get to know everyone in the class and the teaching staff make an effort to get to know you on first name basis. Even though this class is challenging, you will like you have a safe environment to ask anyone anything. I've had pretty deep conversations with course faculty about life, scientific approaches, finding research opportunities, problem sets, concepts, etc. You don't find this sort of environment and level of engagement in any Harvard class or any college class.
Second, your classmates in LS50 will make everything better. When things get difficult and overwhelming (which oh–boy, they definitely will), you will work through the semester WITH your classmates. 2am pset sessions at the Inn, eating lunch together almost everyday after class, pep–talking each other, holding small get–togethers after a long pset night, Lamonstering every night, attending research fairs/events together, and so so much more. When you're having a difficult time, there will be at least 5 people in your vicinity who are willing to spend their own time working with you and helping you out.
Third, the science taught in this class. I came in with a pretty broad range of science background and felt like I enjoyed all of them, so this class was perfect in combining those interests into meshed version. It's all types of life sciences applied with a perspective of statistics, math, chemistry, physics, etc. that make it so interesting. A true "Harvard–ian" experience. This class made my semester.
If you're excited about the life sciences, this is a phenomenal, truly unique class. It really lives up to the "integrated science" name, covering everything from statistics to chemistry to developmental biology. After taking the class, I am much more excited about studying biology and, surprisingly, math. Time commitment is definitely real, and that should be a major consideration. The work is challenging and involved, but the support system is extremely accessible and helpful. Another cool aspect of the course I enjoyed was the lab's focus on original research. Instead of running through tried–and–true biology textbook experiments, you work in a lab and conduct new research. It's extremely rewarding, but also disappointing at times when results don't pan out. The experience is definitely unique, though! Overall, this class does teach you to "think like a scientist". Concretely, that means conducting back–of–envelope calculations to assess plausible hypotheses, being confident deriving things from first principles, and designing experiments in many different fields of biology.
If you study triple sciences and math before, I would highly recommend this course! You get to see how everything merges to form new understanding. The TFs and CAs and instructors are all very friendly, and each of the instructor has their unique profession and background in relevance to their teaching. For example, Michael handles most of the physics and maths while Emma is knowledgeable in chemistry. There were times when I doubted my ability, but this course encouraged me to continue studying life science.

LAB: Personally, I feel unmotivated in the lab but I think it's because of the topic this year is about ants (I am neither interested in zoology nor evolutionary biology and these two formed the core of the lab this year), but I appreciate it because it gives me mmore understanding how research works and the symposium at the end of semester is so COOL.

PYTHON: CS in LS50 is actually pretty basic (maybe because I took CS50) and you can search up most of the stuffs online. How the CS component is different from CS50 is that it focusses on the graphical and mathematical application, a.k.a. you will use Python to draw graphs and simulate dynamical systems, which is pretty cool because you never get to do this in CS50.

EXAMS: we have 3 mid–terms and 1 final and before each exam we will have a mock paper and their difficulty differs a lot. For example, the mock paper for the first mid–term is harder than the first mid–term, but the mock paper for the second mid–term is easier than the second mid–term (I think the average score for second mid–term was 78 if I remember it correctly, so don't be discouraged). You get to know the average score across the class and I think that is useful so that you know where you stand in the class and use that as a motivation.
This class is incredibly challenging and requires a great amount of time and effort to learn the materials. But at the end of the day, what you learned and gained are unbelievably rewarding. There is no other freshmen life science course that exposes you to the breadth and depth of content that this does, alongside with the strong support system that makes you feel so so welcomed. I do not regret my choice taking LS50 because of the AMAZING sense of community and the new ways I approach science now. I am wholeheartedly grateful for all my experiences, whether painful or joyful, and for all the people I met along the way.
This course is a lot of work, but is also one of the best choices I made for my freshman year. You will be thinking about LS50 all the time, you will grow close with your classmates (I have made some great friends through this course), you will be frustrated at times, but you will learn a TON. To say that is is worth the challenge is an understatement. There is so much fascinating material presented and the course moves so quickly that the class is a continuous learning experience. The Problem Sets are challenging, as are Exams, but there is so much support from the teaching staff that nothing every feels unreasonable. Additionally, the small class size is perfect for fostering a collaborative environment. If you are thinking about taking this course, I would definitely recommend it. It really is special in its ability to bring together some great peers and push its students to learn at the best of their ability.
