What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
The capstone research essay was the highlight of the course for me, but you have less time for it than you'll think you do. Try thinking about what source you'd like to investigate earlier on so you can spend all the actual assignment time on really sinking your teeth into your source and your research on it!
If you are not interested in literary or film analysis and that is not the type of writing you will be doing in college, do not take this class!
You should tamper your expectations if you're expecting to enjoy it a lot based purely off of the subject matter, as the course is more about learning to write than learning stuff to write about.
This is one of the more english–y expos courses –– it is about interpretive analysis, so you will interpret the real meanings of different pieces of media. If you want to do STEM maybe pick a different one. But it's not a bad expos
The material of this class was interesting, but I struggled with the lack of academic discussion as well as the expectations toward the end. If you want straightforward instruction on how to compose an essay, this is a fine class, but not necessarily if you're looking to extend your writing/style.
Start writing your drafts and revised essays early
Before you choose this course, pay attention to the fact that it is literary analysis. Whereas many expos classes write about real–life events and topics, this course is entirely writing about literature and media which can be annoying depending on your writing style. I found the subject really interesting, and the final project allows you to write about any queer nostalgia media/literature of your choice which is exciting.
I'd honestly steer you away from this course. The course was an overall frustrating experience for several reasons. First, class time was very boring, and about a half an hour of class each day was just devoted to the Preceptor reading slides off the board word–for–word. Second, feedback was provided in a very slow manner. We would often only receive the feedback from our papers one day before our next paper or draft was due. This made any application of said feedback super challenging. Third, the feedback that we received wasn't very helpful or direct. I was frustrated by the feedback that I received as it was vague and sparse. Fourth, the feedback was not very tailored to my actual needs. One of my most frustrating experiences in this course was reading my feedback side by side with a peer in the course, only to notice that our feedback was nearly identical. Fifth, the materials for the course were not all very interesting, especially given the theme. Some of our materials didn't even mention queerness at all, and seemed to be reused from a past course on Nostalgia. Finally, getting help in this course was very angering. The preceptor was very reluctant to help students during or outside of class. When approached during class with a question the Preceptor often refused to answer, or did so with hesitation. We were allowed to send one email asking about one very specific aspect of our paper, and were occasionally allowed one 20 minute meeting to discuss our draft. For a course designed to teach students a new skill the overall lack of help along the way seemed like a poorly guided decision. I will say that I learned a lot about interpretive analysis in this class, but that is what I expected of this course. The process of said learning, however, was far more challenging because of the and Preceptor than it was the content.
