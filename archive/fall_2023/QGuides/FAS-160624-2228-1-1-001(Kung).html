Super cool high level class. Kung is unclear teacher at times, but its fine
Just don't take this class. It's not worth it. While Kung is a brilliant and important computer scientist, he is the worst, most confusing, and most boring lecturer I have every encountered in my life. The material is scattered, the programming assignments are easy but pointless, and the final project guidance is wacky. There are so many wonderful courses in the department. If you need a 24x or 26x for systems, take 263 or 262.
242 covers a lot of very interesting and very relevant ML topics. It was way less hardware–oriented than I expected (given HT's background), instead focusing more heavily on novel software/paradigms for efficient ML. That said, you can really learn a lot if you put in the effort. There's lots you can do with the final project, and the course staff are quite invested in helping you pick/develop ideas and research. HT is also just a baller –– quite a funny guy (in his own way), and really encourages you to be a great researcher.
Fun class if you're interested in AI and related content! It was a lot less "systems and networking" than I expected, and a lot more "recent advancements in AI". The quizzes–per–lecture can sound intimidating, but they test on relevant, fundamental details of the content that are actually very useful to know, and I found them a great learning resource. The final project can be a bit intimidating since the expectations are quite high, but you also get a lot of time to work on them in the later half of the semester.
Be prepared to read a lot outside of class and do a lot of independent catch–up
Take this course if you are interesting in learning novel approaches to making machine learning more efficient and are okay with being self–guided for most of the time. It's hard to recommend this course based on my experience with it but I'm sure that this course does appeal to some select few. The lectures are dense, often run over time, and not very engaging if you aren't 100% invested in the material. The homeworks are fairly interesting but I didn't love how everything was structured around a group that you form towards the beginning of the semester. 30% of your grade comes from a final project on which I feel we didn't get enough support other than regular check–ins where we were rubberstamped through with the aforementioned group. Take this class if you want to do research at a graduate level in the subject matter (which is the cutting edge of machine learning research, not "Computing at Scale"), but not with a teaching staff who are incredibly knowledgable, but be aware that this is not the most engaging course.
It was very interesting; the reading can be a bit dense but it was a thoroughly enjoyable course! There are quizzes every course though, and the readings + slides are super helpful
This course is kinda disorganized at times, but has some very cool topics and overall does a good job of providing you with the support you need. Take a look at the lecture material to see if you are interested.
There are in–class quizzes for the first half of the semester –– beware that these are somewhat difficult to study for and are worth 25% of the final grade. In terms of the final project, much of your grade seems to hinge on whether Kung likes it or not, so make sure to contact him often regarding your topic. Other than that, I found class material to be very interesting!
Great survey of machine learning techniques with a focus on performance and being aware of how the hardware works, but much broader in scope than the class description makes it seem. This is a class that includes discussion of hardware at a level appropriate for people with a software background, it's not a hardware class.
I learnt a new way of thinking and I feel equipped wit a bag of tools that are very relevant in the current state of ML. It was a great survey of ML current research topics and I feel that it gave me a very useful overview. I also appreciate that the course staff were Kung's PhD students who actually knew the material by heart –– they were super knowledgeable and helpful. (Unfortunately, other CS classes hire students for TFs who don't actually know that material, and that is really annoying.)
