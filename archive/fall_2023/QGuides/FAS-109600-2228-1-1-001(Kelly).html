If you have any interest in the big questions of existence–– purpose, fate, what it means to be a human being, who am I–– take this class!! Professor Kelly is a talented lecturer who is approachable and kind to his students and is knowledgeable enough to give lectures without relying on slideshow presentations. He is very open to students' questions during lectures, which does sometimes mean class will take a few diversions away from the bullet points on the lecture handouts. I also really appreciated the teaching staff, and I was able to build a good relationship with my TF, who helped me understand the specific expectations of writing philosophy papers.

This class attempts to give students a broad introduction a variety of existentialist thought. We covered theists and atheists–– from Sartre to Kiekegaard–– and philosophers who differed on just how much they believed humans were free to choose–– from de Beauvoir to Fanon to Camus. Additionally, we spend about a month reading The Brothers Karamazov by Dostoyevsky. At times, it was a tedious and dense novel, but I can understand why it is considered such an important work of literature. All this is to say, if you take this class, be prepared to do a lot of reading (60–200 pages a week). You get out of this course exactly what you put in. You can skate by without doing much reading and rely on lectures, but if you want to get the most out of this class, you need to keep up with the readings. No one will force you to do this (until you get to the two essays and the final and realize you need an understanding of each text), but it is something you will have to learn to do to get a transformative experience out of Harvard's humanities classes.
