This course is, at its core, a philosophy course that is targeted at the issue of racial justice. It looks at many specific places where anti–discriminatory policies can be developed, such as policing, hate speech, affirmative action, prisons, etc. However, the core of this class is a theoretical discussion on the morality of these issues and not specific to policy. If you have an even moderate interest in philosophy or racial justice, this class is interesting and worth taking. If you want to learn the best policies to promote racial inequality, it is not.
TAKE THIS CLASS! Do be advised that it is more philosophy than you might anticipate, but the philosophy does all tie back into the topics of race and justice and provides a foundational understanding of the concepts. You'll get the most value out of this class by doing the readings; all of them are carefully selected and highlight distinct aspects of race and racism. If you're looking for a class that discusses racial justice movements and modern social advocacy, this is definitely not the course for you. However, the class will build your foundational understanding of race in theory and philosophy in a way that is invaluable to anyone looking to be a better anti–racist. With only three manageable essays in the semester, this class is also very reasonable workload–wise.
