EC 1011A is a good course, but some of the things go against my philosophy of how classes should be. First, I will praise the course:
1) The class covers so much material. You will learn a ton, and it is a very rewarding experience. It is definitely worth the effort if you are interesting in how to model people, firms, agents etc. using math.
2) Giacomo was a good lecturer in my opinion. I really liked the policy discussions because it made me think about a lot of real world problems and how they relate to the class. He is also funny, but humor is subjective.
3) A lot of concepts you learn are very neat. I liked how we taught about heterogeneity using distributions, signaling using hidden types, moral hazard, game theory, etc. Ideas of equilibrium are very nice.
Now, I will talk about the downsides
1) You basically do not receive any feedback on modeling projects. NONE. we got the feedback for the second modeling project at the final exam, even though it was due like 6 weeks ago. Given that the most time you spend on this class is on modeling projects, this is a huge problem.
2) Psets can get very mechanical and instead of teaching you real modeling skills, it tries to teach you how to do computational math. I know this might be an important skill to have as an economist, but I doubt that anyone is taking this class to get better at algebra. I would appreciate more if the psets were directed more towards the learning goals of students.
3) I do not understand what they are trying to accomplish by making the exams extremely hard. The midterm median was around 34, which was an A–. Above 50 was an A, I believe. Lowest grade was a 10, which was a B– I believe. The exam was out of 100. A lot of students didn't even attempt the last problem because of time constraints, which defeats the purpose of putting the problem in the exam.
Don't take this class if you are not mentally prepared to get low scores on almost everything, and devote a BUNCH of time to this class. A lot of the content and work is so difficult that when you search the internet for help, you'll see lectures and slides and papers written for grad student classes. Collaborate with your peers often. Discussing economics with others is the most fun way to do it, and you'll learn more this way. I went into this class knowing that it was going to be difficult but unaware that it was going to be impossible. It is definitely the most difficult class I have taken at Harvard, and the most difficult class I hope I will take. I am proud of myself for sticking through it but would not recommend anyone take it. Here's why:

Lectures: Every single class, I would sit down in lecture, hoping that would be the day I actually understood something, and that was never the case. Not only do slides go fast, but there is no logical order to them, making it impossible to look away for one second without getting lost. Lectures then, became insufferable, as it was an hour and fifteen minutes of me feeling stupid. Halfway through the semester, I stopped paying attention altogether. I wish I had stopped going, because me being there did not help at all. The math is also overwhelming; if you have only done 21a and 21b, you will not understand most of what is going on. What they say is true: the class will be far easier for those comfortable with math than those comfortable with economics.

PSETs: PSETs were incredibly overwhelming at the beginning. I spent upwards of 10 hours on the first one and I'm sure it was completely wrong, so by the end I would waste no more than 4 hours on each one and still get the 100 (since they are completion based). Although it's a learning curve for everyone, I recommend not fretting too much over them; just do them quickly, and instead spend the additional time going over solutions once they're posted.

Modeling Projects: Modeling projects could be the best part of this course but they are somehow the worst. Only the TF's and the head CA had solutions, and few actually gave you guidance so it was impossible to know if you were on the right track or not. It felt like we were being set up for failure. However, go to office hours!! It is the only way you will get a semblance of a correct answer, so make sure to go early and try to go through every single part with the group. Alone, you will not be able to do it. Grading on this was also incredibly late, and we got them back on the day of the exam which I felt was cruel.

Exams: Exams are tough but fair. You just need to write fast and try to write something down for every point. You will not know what you're doing but the scattershot approach is the best way to ~optimize~ your points. I don't think you can prepare a lot for them but just try to have a general idea of what you'd do for every type of question (e.g. game theory, externalities, aggregate demand and supply) so that you don't blank completely on a question.

Staff/CA's: CA's are the saving grace of this course. They know what you're going through and will try to help you as much as possible. I know I was able to stick through the course because of them, especially the head CA. However, the class is so tough that sometimes they don't even know what we're doing. Also, staff doesn't help them out too much because they don't get solutions to modeling projects. TF's on the other hand I often found unhelpful, and their answers just confused and frustrated me more. Professor Ponzetto is amusing in lectures but a bit distant and unapproachable. The class felt intimidating and I don't think it was an environment conductive to learning; it was more of a performance where smart people got to show off how much they understood.

Overall, the class taught me a lot about resilience, dealing with failure, and problem solving in general. However, it also tarnished my self–esteem in academic terms. I left the midterm in tears because I felt so stupid, and every lecture, every PSET, and every modeling project just added to the impostor syndrome. I wish I could say this at least came with a lot of learning, but I don't think I actually learned any economics. To this day, I can not tell you what we covered in class, and therefore think I would have gotten much more out of 1010A.

I'm very proud I took this class but don't think it's worth it. If I could go back and enroll in 1010A, I would. If you end up enrolling, it will be crazy experience and you'll come out stronger at the end of it. But please, think twice before doing it and maybe save yourself the trouble.

Also, taking STAT110 concurrently showed me what a difficult but well–structured class can look like. I don't think the material here is that much more difficult but the way it is taught makes it very difficult to learn. The class needs to be restructured.
