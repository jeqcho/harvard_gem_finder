I think the course has a lot of value and I learned a lot about causal inference. I came away with more intuition and confidence. But I definitely I had frustration throughout, mostly in terms of the structure of the course, which I found made learning a lot harder. The pedagogical patience of the professor and TFs made things bearable.

The main issues IMO:
– The flipped classroom structure was not good in my opinion; we covered new material in recorded lectures AND in–person lectures. In other words, in–person lectures ended up being regular lectures with maybe fifteen minutes of working on exercises afterwards. It felt like we had 3 lectures per week. The course tries to cover too much material.
– Midterm and final were very long and difficult; we were given 24 hours, and it took me around 15 hours both times.
– You can drop modules (i.e. work for certain weeks), which definitely came back to bite people once it was exam time. I recommend doing all of the modules' exercises. However, it's also difficult because there's so much material covered in each module.
– We had exercises due 3 days after learning the material (eg. Wednesday lecture ––> Saturday exercise) was unnecessarily challenging; it didn't feel like there was enough time to understand the material before trying the exercises.
– Warning to anyone who isn't fluent in tidyverse: There was a lot of tidyverse in the coding solutions for the exercises.
– Lecture slides were hard to follow, and I found Kosuke to be hard to follow as a lecturer (but super helpful when asking clarification or going to office hours).
Stat 286 is a very, very time intensive course. It takes a lot of time and energy to not only learn the very challenging material, but also generally navigate the course's structure, as it's not the most organized or conducive to a general understanding. It feels that the course tries to accomplish a lot with limited time/course staff/resources, and you'll probably feel generally overwhelmed. There are three total assignments that can be submitted each week, with Gov 2003 students required to do the first two and Stat 286 students required to do the latter two. Exercises and review questions can feel arduous and a bit disconnected from material at times. You'll need to be very self–motivated to stay on top of the material, as most of it is taught through a flipped classroom format. There is also some flexibility in terms of what assignments you can turn in, but this can be dangerous as it's easy to "drop" quite a few modules without consequence to your day–to–day grade.
Understanding each week's covered material basically requires you to spend hours independently researching and reading papers on the concepts, and even then, not much can really prepare you for the exams, which essentially request that you conduct some sort of unreplicated novel research. Additionally, understanding the weekly assignments will not be enough for you to succeed on the exams either, since they often cover vastly different topics.
A difficult class that assumes a good amount of comfortability with STAT 110/111/139 material beforehand, as well as general familiarity with mathematical derivations especially in the first half of the course. However, causal inference is SO interesting and Kosuke does a phenomenal job motivating the material with his background in the field and I learned a ton from the class. Not the most well–run class overall (sections and HW felt a little disorganized), but if you are self–motivated you will learn a lot.
