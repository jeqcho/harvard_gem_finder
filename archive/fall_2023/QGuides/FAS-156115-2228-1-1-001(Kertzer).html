You can tell he truly cares about students' wellbeing and wants the class to be as stress free as possible.

In terms of class content, the readings can vary a lot in density. This is code for "open the readings WHEN THEY'RE ASSIGNED to see how long they are even if you don't plan on finishing them right away". Even though all of the information is super helpful, some readings are intrinsically much more difficult to work through than others and can take hours. The reading/study guides can help a lot if you are short on time and need to skim an assignment instead of reading a full 40 page analysis.

The most challenging part of this course is for sure the 10 page essay, as it is was quite difficult for me to get a grasp on what exactly to do having never written political analysis before. I would recommend getting started on this assignment as soon as it is given and making good use of the office hours offered if you are beginner. The exams are relatively straightforward with no tricks, although the grading system (for both the exams and paper) is a bit murky. I'm not really sure what the grade cutoffs are for this class, how the curves work, or what makes a grade a 95 instead of a 94 (just an example, not my actual grades haha). However, the grading system as a whole feels fair enough and I don't think anyone has to worry about failing or getting blindsided by a grade as long as they put the work in.

This course is a great one, but BEWARE. It is NOT a gem and it is NOT easy. You have to put in a lot (a LOT) of work to get in the A range, and even then, putting in a lot of work will only take you so far. Kertzer is an amazing lecturer and is extremely knowledgeable. The teaching staff is great. Section was also great (for me). The exams and paper were somewhat painful, but not horrible. This class covers a LOT of content, and you sometimes have to take strides on your own time to connect the dots from one topic to the next. This course is very pro–America (obviously), but it might be something you want to keep in mind.

The exams in this course are entirely writing–based. Anything Kertzer says and anything in the readings is fair game – and I mean it! Lots of definition–style questions (fill in the blanks), short–answer questions (paragraphs), and essays. The short–answer questions are graded very harshly (from my experience). You have to write in the way they want! If you're a mind–reader, this shouldn't be too difficult for you. For us lowly folk, it can be hard to know what to say. Giving the correct answer isn't enough. That will only get you ~1/2 the points. The essay questions are usually pretty broad and not too hard to do well on. With that said, it can be really hard to score in the top range (say 16–18/18 possible points).

The readings for class are LONG and PLENTIFUL. I spent hours and hours trying to understand the readings. If you get behind, it can be very difficult to catch up. You have to put in a lot of effort to memorize the readings. It was sometimes annoying that the reading I spent a lot of time studying wouldn't even show up on the exam. But you can't leave one out – if you do, you know that will be the one that shows up. The reading responses due each week on slack were horribly annoying, I won't lie.

The guest speakers for this class were amazing. Kertzer is also amazing. Attending lecture is usually a positive experience, although attendance isn't always required as far as an attendance grade. With that said, there is a LOT of government vocabulary you have to learn and be able to use. For that reason, I only recommend this course for people who are serious about pursuing a gov concentration. I thought that was me at the beginning of the year, but I've now settled into the English path.

I put my entire heart and soul into my policy paper to get an A. Although it wasn't perfect, I probably spent around 50 hours on the paper over the course of a month (more like 2.5 weeks because I slacked off at first). I recommend writing about something you are truly interested in. If this means you propose a new prompt, then so be it. You will be spending a LOT of time with this paper – make sure you care about it. When all was said and done, I was very proud of my paper and was able to display it to my friends and family; however, this was not without strenuous effort and grief. I didn't see the sun for 3 days leading up to the paper submission deadline.

Above all, try to make friends – with the teaching staff and with other classmates. It made the work much more bearable. I really enjoyed working with my friends leading up to exams and will miss it next semester. Good luck with Gov 1790!
