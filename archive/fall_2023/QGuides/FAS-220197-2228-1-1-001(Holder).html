This is a great seminar course! I will say though that learning the software for this course is a steep learning curve and it helps to have a windows operating device but not necessary.
Though the class is incredibly engaging and insightful, be aware that its workload is significant.
Be aware that this is not a gem. It's rewarding but sometimes painful and is certainly more work than your traditional freshman seminar. That said, you'll get to interact with architects at the top of their game and understand how they go about creating the future of architecture. Although this is likely the end of my architecture journey, it was well worth the effort. I become proficient with modeling software and learned quite a bit about the theory of how structures are created. Since our work was eventually reviewed by faculty, that added another element of coolness and seriousness to the nature of our work. Take this if you want to learn how to use modeling software quickly and dip your toe into theory that while dense, is worth parsing through.
Great class! You learn about theory and history behind architecture along with practical skills using modeling software (Rhino). Just make sure to start your modeling assignments early because they will take longer than you expect. Otherwise, it was awesome to learn from a GSD professor and experience the architecture design process with pinups and final reviews. Seeing your work all come together by the end of the semester was so rewarding too!
For a seminar, the workload is immense. I expected it to be much less intense than it was.
This class is a lot more work than you would expect for a Freshman Seminar. Class discussions are interesting and you'll find yourself engaged, but they don't necessarily prepare you in a technical manner for the assignment, meaning you will have to self–learn a lot of Rhino. The in–class demonstrations are helpful with introducing the basics, but you basically have to choose between following along and committing to memory or taking notes, as lectures aren't usually recorded. There aren't any written notes to refer back to, so it's very important to pay attention during class. Beyond the basics, it's up to you to learn the rest of Rhino.

Rhino itself can definitely be finicky. If you don't have a Windows desktop, either find a computer on campus at a lab to use (they are free of charge) or get one for yourself. You will have many breakdowns trying to get Rhino to work if you aren't on a device with enough computing power and compatibility with Rhino.

Finally, expect that you might take a lot of time working on some assignments or completing some readings, but they won't ever be reviewed or discussed. This isn't common, but it happened for the one and only essay of the year, which I was led to believe was a big milestone for the course.

However, you will definitely come out of this course more familiar with design, geometry, abstraction, and the fine arts. You won't necessarily come out of it with more technical skill though, but I think you get just about what the course is advertised as.
I would highly recommend taking this course if you have an interest in architecture and if you think you would enjoy the process of designing and revising your designs
