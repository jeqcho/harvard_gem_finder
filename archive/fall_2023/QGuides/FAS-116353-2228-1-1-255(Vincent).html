Take if you care about criminal justice reform. It will help you understand the issues with our current system in many ways.
I really enjoyed the content of this class. As an Engaged Scholarship course, it was structured a bit differently from other expos classes my friends were in and I think that was one of its best traits. The speaker series were an excellent lens into prison reform and abolition work in the area and in the modern day. The scholars we read are excellent examples of great writing on top of their innumerable contributions to the field of prison abolition. I struggled a bit in this course with the weight of the two main essays due to my lack of confidence in my ability to get good grades on them despite hard work and hours of effort. This course certainly has a lot of content that requires intense critical thinking and working out a personal position rather than restating someone elses, which is exciting but also daunting. Overall, this course and the instructor were phenomenal but the class was still difficult for me.

It will definitely strengthen your writing skills, but also, the topic is so relevant and important that you'll learn a lot more than just writing. Hudson is a great teacher; he's very supportive and cares a lot about the topic. The guest lectures are incredible and are definitely the most engaging experience I've had in class this year.

This is a great Expos class to take. Hudson makes class discussions consistently interesting and engaging. Expectations for assignments are clear and the homework is definitely manageable. Being able to meet with community activists and create the capstone project helped translate our classroom learning to the real world, making the coursework feel useful.
Take this class! The grading is kind of hard at times, but you can get an A on papers. This is one of the more interesting expos courses and it was well worth the hours spent stressing. Hudson genuinely wants to see you succeed.
This Expos course is, as you might expect, heavy in subject matter. It is also academically difficult, but I think that that is appropriate for the topic and for the course as a whole: it teaches you so much about writing and about why writing even matters. And Hudson is incredibly supportive and caring throughout the way. He is thoughtful and intentional with how he communicates with students and in helping us build community. He challenges you to write more and better than you have in the past, but he is also your greatest supporter through that learning process.

The engaged part of this course is phenomenal. Having visits from community members who are actively involved in finding solutions to the issues of mass incarceration is so valuable. You get to meet incredible people, many of whom are formerly incarcerated, who share their stories and their drive for enacting change.

This course inspired me to stop turning away from hard issues and instead look for the people who are affected, the people who are trying to find solutions. It has given me confidence that I can learn about and research incredibly complex issues, and then think critically about them and actually have something new to add to the conversation. If you are placed in this course, I hope you get as much out of it as I have.
Look no one really likes Expos 20 as a whole, but Hudson is a really nice and enthusiastic preceptor. The topic is really interesting and Hudson brings in some great speakers which really makes the course.
Due to the gravity of the subject this course centers on (mass incarceration in the United States), it is easy to feel intimidated by the task of writing an essay that features one's own ideas along with the arguments of experts. Future students should put forth their best efforts to write compelling and unique arguments despite these feelings. This course is a safe space for growing as a writer. Feedback from Hudson (and classmates) will always be helpful and kind. Come to class prepared every time and you may surprise yourself with how much you can develop your writing/communication skills. This is the best Expos 20 course offered.
This course is difficult. The material is likely new for most of us. If you are not humanities trained, be prepared to deal with reading 30–page long essays and analysing them (which was very painful for me as a STEM student).

The preceptor is one of the most positive and motivating people you will ever meet. But that means encouraging comments but harsh grades (which can be very jarring).

The social engagement part will really change or inform your view about prisons in the US. And the course can truly make you empathise with a group of people that we don't really think about (people in prisons).

Take this course if you are up for a challenge and want to embark on a difficult but fruitful journey. There are no easy As and every A you get will feel so sweet.
This class will be difficult but it is worth it. The professor has an amazing curriculum that enables you to explore the Prison System at different levels. You learn the metrics of writing and how to elevate your work.
