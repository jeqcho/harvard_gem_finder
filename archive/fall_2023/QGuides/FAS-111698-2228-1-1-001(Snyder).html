Gov 1300 is exactly what it says it is – a course analyzing how Congress functions. If this is something you're interested in, great! Professor Snyder knows an incredible amount about the topic, Noah (if he is still TFing) is great, and the course raises some interesting questions without being a ton of work. That said, if you're *not* genuinely interested in Congress, do not take this course – you will be bored. The p–sets are fair (if you are shaky in terms of R or Stata ability, go to office hours early – they release the p–sets early enough that you're able to do this), and the final paper is straightforward and not a ton of work. The readings are mostly from textbooks and ngl are kinda dull – skim them to get the general idea. Lectures can be somewhat hard to follow, sometimes – Professor Snyder is prone to going off on tangents or introducing questions and then not completely answering them – but he's also very open to discussions and clarifying questions in class. Overall a very solid class, definitely recommend if the topic is something you want to learn more about
take this class! It is a very easy way to explore gov, especially if you are more of a STEM person. As for Gov concentrators, this class is a nice break from heavy reading and writing classes. The workload is minimal and the content is interesting. Noah is the best TF ever and is very helpful and understanding. There is only one paper for this class, where you analyze your own rep in Congress for a final paper. There's 6 psets throughout the semester that have light coding and are very interesting. The psets are also graded very generously. Fantastic course.
Overall this class was fine. It is a useful course if you want to learn a little about how to use R, especially for Gov, without being all about coding. Lectures can be a bit boring but if you make the active effort to attend and pay attention you'll leave the class with a bit more knowledge about Congress.
The course, despite being data analysis–based, dealing with R/STATA, provides no proper instruction in either software, or how to code, in general. Also, lecture is always all over the place, and the professor tends to go on tangents, and it is very easy to get lost with what he is talking about because of his lecture style. In addition, the course lecture does not help with the analysis portion of the assignments in the course, so I would shift lecture to be more helpful, content–wise, with the assignments and what we are graded on in the course. A lot of the time, it almost felt as though what we were learning in the course, and what we graded on, had nothing to do with each other. Lecture helped with learning about the topics in section, but not with the actual assignments. I still do not know how to analyze or interpret what I code. I would not recommend this course.
The final paper is the best part of this course. Also, the sections were really helpful for the assignments in R and the final paper. Overall, recommend taking this class to learn more about Congress through data observation and research.
