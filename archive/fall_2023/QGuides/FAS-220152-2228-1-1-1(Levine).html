This is a really emotionally heavy class, but I think that everyone should learn these things and engage with these issues, especially if you're not queer and don't think you have any personal connections/ties to the AIDS epidemic.
'AIDS was not just an epidemic. AIDS is a metaphor,' we learn.

This class, to me, was the contending with the aesthetic, the plurality, the normativity, the magnitude, the performativity, and the logics of this metaphor.

This class teaches you about what it means to exist as a queer sexual being in a world that has been radically remade—by the AIDS epidemic—to fear, disclaim, and repel the existence of queer sexual beings. It exposes you to the politics of bodies, the art (that sometimes doesn't read as art at all), the sensuality of humor and the humor of sensuality, the satire, the desire, and so so so much death that makes you grateful to be alive.

And Debra is just the most incredible human being and professor. She is such a kind–hearted, witty, eloquent role model and advocate. The speakers (her friends) that come in to share with our class have lived such grueling, brilliant lives and carry such potent stories with them.

They say at college if you're lucky you'll take one class that profoundly changes you –– for me it is this class. I'm incredibly fortunate to learn about the queer people that came before me, not told through their deaths, but through their poetry, their song, their fashion, their film, their protest, their artifacts they left behind.

(TL;DR take this class!!)
This course provides a lot of interesting information about life during the AIDS epidemic. The art pieces and films that we looked at helped to understand how artists responded to the epidemic. Overall this course was enjoyable and would be a good class to take if you have an interesting in art and politics.
