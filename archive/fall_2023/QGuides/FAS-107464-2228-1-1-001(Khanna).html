Take it. Best GENED at Harvard. Hands–down.
A life–changing opportunity to take an HBS case–study style class, catch a glimpse of the brilliant world of social entrepreneurship, and practice creative problem–solving with real potential direct impact on people you care about. The curriculum surveys interventions from myriad approaches of technology, art, design, and more, and lays a foundation for how to start thinking about this whole field and future paths in the field. This is taught through 2 types of content: case study readings that are supplemented by insightful discussions during class, and a pretty in–depth business proposal developed with a team throughout the semester. Can't recommend this course enough, especially if you put in effort to meet other people in the class and bond with your team over the project. Agree with all other comments that state that every student should [be forced to] take this course.
This is an amazing and unique course! There are five different professors from different departments which provide different perspectives on issues in developing countries. The professors have lots of experience in entrepreneurship and are open to talk to you about your own projects. The student body is also really diverse, including many international students. Definitely recommend if you're interested in technology, business, development, or issues in countries like India and China. If you find a good project group, you get to work on an awesome business plan that you are passionate about and make good friendships with other students. You can even try to turn the business plan into a real company after the course! Be prepared to spend significant time on the readings and the business plan assignments.

It's a great class to take as an intro to entrepreneurship!
So in some ways I felt like I got catfished by the course. Professor Khanna and Balsari are incredible and they teach the first couple of weeks and I loved it. The case method was a lot of fun and the readings were incredible. Then came what can unfortunately only be described as a waste of time. Lecturers on thinking like a planner, scientist and especially artist provided no additional benefit to the creation of any one of our assignments or our final projects. They were boring, unengaging (with 15 minutes of dull silence at the end of lectures waiting for "do you have any questions"), and while at times interesting, added no additional benefit to our project. At the point that I had figured this out it was too late to drop and so I stuck with it. Don't get me wrong I'm glad I stuck with it but I felt catfished.

In terms of the homework, heed the wisdom of the other comments, the team that you're in will define this course. Try your best to befriend grad students and go with them because they often have significantly more experience doing a business plan than you and you can piggyback on that knowledge. You can do that or work with your friends but where possible try come into this course with an idea first and a team later as this prevents teams wanting to split up (as was pretty common).

All in all I'm glad I took the course but I wish I had know this all before I took it.
