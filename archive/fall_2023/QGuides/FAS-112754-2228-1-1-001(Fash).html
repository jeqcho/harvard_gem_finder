Great class! Definitely take it if you have any interest in learning about Mexico or if you identify as Mexican yourself! Professor Carrasco and Professor Fash are truly amazing, very passionate, and very knowledgable.
This class is a very easy Gen Ed, workload is very light and your grade rides on around a 40% mid–term and 50% final. However, these are take–home where you just have to write two short essays and some short answers. You get a week to do them, and the TFs usually grade nicely. As long as you hand in something that makes sense, you can probably leave this class with at least an A–. (They even have extra credit projects that you can do.) Also, there are a bunch of weekly readings, but I stopped doing them midway into the semester because it wasn't really necessary. You can get by by skimming them considering that we didn't have any assignments on them. Also, lecture is fine, and it's great when Dr. Carrasco is the one lecturing, but if not then it's just a way to kill time. They take attendance which is a bit annoying.

Main problem with this class is that it can definitely be BORING. This might just depend on section, but mine was incredibly dry and I hated having to walk there each morning just to sit for over an hour and not get much done. It made me uninterested in the material for sure, which is why I wouldn't call this class a gem. Otherwise, if you're just trying to have a lighter workload, then this class is for you.
Take this class! You will not regret it one bit!
Try to do the reading as a group. There is a lot, so splitting the readings among members of the section can really help reduce the burden
Is the class a gem? In most regards, yes. There are copious amounts of reading but you do not have to do them, at least not in depth. There are only two assignments, a take home midterm and a final, which are supposed to be 6–7 pages but realistically are 20 pages (albeit a relatively easy 20 pages to write, defining terms and 2 short essays). Lectures are interesting — very unorganized, at times impossible to follow, but the professors are certainly passionate, many lectures feeling like sermons. Great, its a gem, but do you walk away with anything? Maybe. I was very lucky to have Leonardo as my section leader who organized the often times scrambled readings and lectures into coherent lessons. I learned interesting stories about Aztec times and am grateful for a deeper understanding of Mexico’s cultural past, from the colonial days to revolution to present day Mexico, I have gained an understanding of the forces that have shaped today’s Mexico. I’ve walked away with a few interesting anthropological concepts that I doubt I will use in the future but I am grateful to have understood. In short, lecture did very little, the readings ocassionally helped (granted I only skimmed), and section was interesting and informative. Writing the final and the midterm forced me to draw connections and while I at times felt like I was making things up, I think was a valuable exercise (although wayyy too long). Do not take this class if you want a great interesting class — it is not that. If you are looking for a gem, take it. The workload is light and you will walk away having learned a handful of things.

The challenge is the two exams, because you will have to spend a lot of time skimming readings to find sources for your essays. Because of that, I felt that the class was very low workload for the majority of the semester, but an exceptional amount of work during the exams. Those two weeks were rough, and the exams weren't graded super easily, but I think it was worth it in the grand scheme of the semester. Just plan to be swamped by the exams.
In terms of class material, I found the class extremely interesting. Many days I was expecting to work on other homework during lecture, but I would get distracted by Professor Carrasco because the content is genuinely intriguing. If you have any interest in how Mesoamerican cultures shaped todays world, definitely take this class. You have the agency to decide how much work to put into the class week to week, and you will definitely get out what you put in.
