What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This class is not specifically about virtue ethics. It is about virtue theory, and covers three broad topics: virtue/vice theory (analyzing virtue/vice terms and their meaning with no context), virtue ethics, and virtue epistemology (basically the analog to virtue ethics in questions of knowledge). However, while these are the broad topics discussed, it does not feel like that. It feels like an overview course in which every week you read a different selection, and readings are not related too closely. If you are looking for a broader theory about virtue or vice, this course will present you many such theories, but will not help to decide how or which to pick. You will gain familiarity with many new ideas, and you'll probably end up feeling more disoriented than you started. That can be a good thing. Just keep in mind it'll be the start, not the end, of your journey into this topic.
I enjoyed this course a lot because of the exposure to distinct authors in philosophy from a variety of backgrounds, with narratives that are not, typically, brought up in philosophical discourse. The course is not as discussion–based as I expected, or would have liked it to be, but there is still so much rich content in this course, and required reads that this course has to offer. I recommend this course to anyone who is seeking a more exciting, relevant approach to ethics, touching on topics that one would not even expect.
Seth does a good job of creating an environment that encourages exploration and inclusivity, and he certainly wants his students to succeed and does his best to enable that. I think it's an ideal class for someone who has never taken a 100–level philosophy course before. The conference paper assignment is especially enjoyable, since it gives you the opportunity to work through one idea throughout the semester and collaborate with peers. It's important to note that this class is much more lecture–based than it is a seminar; there will be a short group discussion (around 5–10 minutes) at the beginning of class, but the rest is almost all lecture.
Readings are really good, and Seth is great at breaking them down!
Understanding the themes of the course not only sharpens your academic and critical thinking skills but also makes you think twice about your behavior as you move through the real world.
Fantastic course. Professor Robertson is just the nicest guy, and he’s really super knowledgeable about philosophy. You can ask him questions about literally anything and he will have good answers. The lectures are long and sometimes hard to follow
This is a great course! Dr. Robertson is one of the most engaging and passionate faculty members I've met at Harvard, and the readings and lectures discuss interesting philosophical theories that you won't see in most other philosophy classes. I don't know if Dr. Robertson selects for this or if the course content naturally draws certain types of people, but the people in the class were students with a variety of different concentrations and backgrounds, all of whom seemed to be primarily interested in thinking (both academically and personally) about how to be better people (Dr. Robertson is also exceptionally kind). In my experience, it is unfortunately rare to encounter such a positive and warm environment in Harvard so, even if you have no academic reason to take the course, I recommend this class as a way of meeting some incredibly kind people and think about how you can grow as a person.
