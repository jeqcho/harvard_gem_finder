This isn't a class you should take only if you find it interesting, it should be if you're serious about having an education in phonology
If you like phonetics or phonology and want to see if one or both is truly for you, this is the class to take. The two–hour–long lectures, very difficult assignments, and 3,000–word final paper will be a delight if you really do love phonetics and phonology. If it turns out phonetics and phonology aren’t for you, you’ll think, “I’m so glad I took this class, but I’ve had my fill now.” I fall into the latter category and can say that either way, you’ll win. So grateful I got to take this class and to learn from Professor Franich and can say it was definitely worth taking.

With that, under Professor Franich (a phonetician), the course is pretty phonetics heavy. We had two clear–cut phonetics assignments, one articulatory phonology assignment (which really just felt like a phonetics assignment), two Optimality Theory assignments, one rules–based phonology assignment, and one perceptual phonology assignment. You’ll use fairly specialized software for the entirety of the last two assignments (which you should DEFINITELY start early so that you can debug, find participants, etc. as needed). TaDa is very cool, and it’s always great to pick up some new technical skills. I didn’t realize just how rich of a discipline phonetics was, and I’m glad Professor Franich opened my eyes to that.

I liked the format of the homework. It’s usually two or three really hard questions that require substantial amounts of time and thought to answer. It was great to think through problems on such a deep level! I had a lot of fun working through them. And yes, they can be a lot to tackle, but Professor Franich is VERY helpful and patient over email and in office hours.

So really, you’ll get to spend your semester tackling conceptually rich problems that required thorough, flexible, creative, and critical thinking. This class will make you a much stronger problem solver and thinker.

Also, Professor Franich knows a TON about experimental design, laboratory work, field work, etc. and can discuss them with you in very accessible terms. And she has so much wisdom and experience to share re: the design of constraints, conventions around writing in linguistics, etc. (She’s also a phonologist!) Definitely go to her office hours, talk with her after class, etc.

The final squib was the hardest paper I’ve written since the spring of ‘21. But that made finishing it that much more empowering and encouraging.

Professor Franich would have us get up at the board and draw tableau, write rules, etc., which I loved.

In summary, LING 115 is the hardest linguistics class I’ve taken, and I’m glad I took it because it’s fun and interactive and made me a better problem solver!

Fellow undergraduates: DO NOT take graduate–level classes lightly. Only take one at a time. The final squibs are a beast — and one alone will be doable but a lot to manage when you have other finals to worry about. Treat this class as one of your tough ones when building your schedule.
