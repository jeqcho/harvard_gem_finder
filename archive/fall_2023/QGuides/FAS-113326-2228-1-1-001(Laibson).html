My background: had never taken an econ class before; international student; more interested in theory (Social Studies) than in more quantitative social sciences (not econ concentrator)
– Pro (different engagement levels): During the semester, you don't have to put too much effort if you're not 100% interested in the class. You have to separate time every week to do the PSETs (which are not very long, but the lecture doesn't prepare you to tackle the questions I felt, so it requires a bit of struggling to find out how to do it), and do some other fairly easy assignments such as 500–word essays, small debate, etc. You do have to take time to study for the midterm and final, though, since it's a lot of content to cover. But apart from that, you can dedicate as much as you want, basically. Some people go to every in–person lecture, PSET collab section, in–person review sessions, special events, office hours, etc. I wasn't this connected with course, but you can make your way in it as you prefer. You can make it a relaxed course and then cram for 4 days for the final. It works.
– Pro/grow (connection with the content): Sometimes the content seemed boring/superficial to me, but after the end of the semester (and studying for the final exam), being able to look back in perspective, I feel like I learned interesting and useful things. It's less of a critical space than I wanted to investigate the assumptions, though, but taking it in my first semester was helpful to have this view towards my other classes and allow me to have more perspective into my interest in econ.
– Grow (environment): Personally the environment wasn't very intellectually stimulating for me. People are not actively present in sections, you hear everything in the whole econ major stereotype such as advocacy for legal organ selling, etc. If these are your type of people you can perhaps feel more connected than I did. It's good though that it's a big first–year class, so there's all the adrenaline of entering Sanders for the first lecture, etc.
– Pro (material): There's a lot of material to study from. It's very self–explanatory. There's also lots of help. The course is very well structured.
Overall, I don't regret taking at all! If you're more interested in it than I was, you might love it, since I already liked it a lot!

I will write from the perspective of a person with no background in economics.

The class is good. You learn a lot of cool things at a **descriptive** level. This means that you mostly don't learn the math behind things, except at a really basic level. If you care about math and other technicalities, you should not take Econ 10a.

You will be required to purchase a textbook. Even if you have it already as a hard copy or as an ebook, you will still be required to purchase it and there is no way around it.

The problem sets are short and don't require a lot of time. The course staff organizes collab sessions for problem sets, but you won't need these, it's usually enough to ask your TF.

The class has a lot of readings, but you can mostly get away without doing any of them. Some people found the exercises in the textbook to help with problem sets and/or reinforcing concepts. Indeed, a subset of questions on problem sets seems to be drawn from the pool of textbook exercises.

The class has many, many, many different types of assignments. You will have to do problem sets, pre–lecture activities, two short essays, a debate, Unit Review Programs, Economics in Action reflections, and two exams. Most of these are short, but they are still there, and they can get annoying. Be prepared for this.

The exams are not easy, regardless of your background. Both the midterm and the final are long, multiple–choice exams, where questions test understanding of subtle topics. You really need an in–depth understanding to do well. Make sure to do all practice exams when studying; the types of questions are recycled. The midterm is a bit harder than the final because each questions is weighted more; make 3 mistakes on the midterm and you're down to 90% already. A cheat sheet is allowed for both exams, but be cautious about how useful the cheat sheet actually is. It's probably a good idea to put some information on it, but for most questions it might not be that helpful.

Overall, even though the material is very easy, it's also easy to slip up and not get an A, especially because the exams carry a lot of weight. Make sure you understand the grade risk before taking Econ 10a and do NOT underestimate it.

Finally, I think it's good to clarify what Econ 10a is about. You will NOT learn: what to do with money, how finance works, what should policy do. You WILL learn: why it is not clear what the tax rate should be, why increasing the minimum wage could either increase or decrease unemployment, why carbon tax is always a good thing. If these things sound appealing and you are willing to deal with the assignments, then take Econ 10a.

If you had a strong high school economics course, this class will probably feel very easy. From what I have gathered from peers, if you do not come in with this background, it can still be easy or it can be a bit of a challenge. If in the latter category, take advantage of the resources offered, including office hours and actually reading the textbook, and collaborate on psets. The workload is very manageable––mostly just the weekly psets, usually half multiple choice and half written, and the occasional essay (2) or debate preparation (1). When it comes to exams, be sure to attend the section class dedicated to reviewing for it, as well as the doing practice exams and attending office hours. Finally, use the Unit Review Program to gauge your understanding. It is a really effective way to find gaps in your knowledge as you are still learning the material, rather than right before exams. Take it seriously, rather than just trying to get credit for completion, and ask the tutors questions (for reference, the Unit Review Program is where you go and take a unit practice exam of sorts, pass/fail, that is graded and reviewed with you by an upperclassmen tutor). I would not say the class is always super engaging, but there are definitely things that even non–econ concentrators can find interesting (especially the Economics in Action seminars where other Harvard faculty share their research).
