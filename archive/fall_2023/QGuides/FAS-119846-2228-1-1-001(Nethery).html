What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This class was like an R coding class. Homework required lots of R coding of simulations and functions and did not add help advance understanding of didactic lectures much. Much time was spent, mostly to learn how to code in R, but little statistical knowledge was gained. The lectures did not go in–depth regarding mathematical proof but more on hand–wave expressions. Background in BST 222 is strongly required, if not a must.
Highly recommend this course, rigorously covers topics in linear/regression modelling (both theory and applications/practice). Experience with R and some previous exposure to regression modelling theory is helpful (maybe not necessary but workload increases heavily if you're also learning R on the fly).
This course is the crown jewel of the Biostatistics department. A lot of work, but provides incredible coverage of many statistical methods including linear models, computational methods, heteroscedasticity, binomial proportions, and GLMs for binary and count data. It was more theoretical than I anticipated, which was a happy surprise. The homework is highly relevant to both statistical research and practice. And to top it all off, Rachel is one of the kindest and most friendly professors I have met – I could not say enough good things about her!
Make sure you hav e a base knowledge of stats and probability, but otherwise you should be good.
Fast–paced introduction to linear models, Lasso/Ridge, Boostrap/Permutation, Likelihood theory, GLMs.
If you are thinking about a master of passing in Biostat for PhD students, it would be a good course to take to fulfill the requirement
this is a great class that is really comprehensive. be prepared to work hard but you will get a lot out of it.
This is an excellent course for anyone interested in understanding linear models and GLMs and looking to build a foundation for modeling data.
Make sure you understand every step of what is taught in lecture, from the first lecture to the last.
