This was overall a good course, and a great way to fulfill the relevant gened requirements, even if it's not as "gemmy" as other classes. Lectures were fairly interesting, and it was cool how Profs Flad and Ur (who are clearly both experts in the field) used examples from their own research. Section was by far the highlight –– you get to do fun experiments (making pottery, making stone tools, etc.) to get some hands–on experience with the archaeological ideas that were discussed in lecture. The TFs were also great. The workload wasn't too bad; reading quizzes make sure you actually do the reading, but it was rarely more than ~30–45 minutes worth per lecture. The weekly assignments (a 500–1000 word reflection on section) were a bit hit–or–miss in terms of topic but not the end of the world in the grand scheme of things. Overall, I'm quite glad that I took this course, and would recommend it strongly to anyone who is interested in learning more about archaeology and/or needs another gened. I think that this class does a great job achieving the goals of the gened system, as it caused me to engage with an interesting subject that I had no prior exposure to and that has important ramifications for society.

A few caveats:
• The course does not communicate expectations very well. The syllabus refers to nebulous "section assignments," without really specifying that this includes a not–insignificant writing assignment every week. If you're going to assign weekly work, make it clear in the syllabus so that people know what sort of workload to expect. Additionally, the expectations for the final project were extremely unclear. The assignment page was multiple pages long, with a very complicated rubric. It shouldn't require that much effort just to decipher what we're being asked to write. A clearer assignment (along with examples of what a successful project looks like) would go a long way.
• There is an extremely strong ideological bent, particularly in Prof. Ur's lectures. While I'm not going to pretend to be surprised that a professor at Harvard is liberal, at times the evangelism was so strong that the class could have been a skit made by Tucker Carlson making fun of what he thinks Harvard is like. During many of the "debates" it was clear that there was one right side and one wrong side, and that any perspectives other than the party line would not have been tolerated. I imagine that this doesn't bother most people, but if you're someone who gets bothered by one–sided preaching you might not like that aspect of the class.
• The professors did these annoying "breakouts" during lecture, where a question would be asked and people would have to discuss with their neighbors. After the 2020–21 year, I didn't think anything could be more annoying and awkward than zoom breakout rooms, but the in–person analogue was even worse. I would strongly encourage the professors to change this for next year.
