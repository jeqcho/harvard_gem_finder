As a student outside of the economics department, I would recommend this course to students who are not required to do so if they're interested in building a very core foundation of understanding the principles of econometrics as the course title says while also getting a reasonable idea of how this connects to actual empirical analysis.
It may feel very overwhelming and stressful at the beginning but with determination you’ll get there. You can reap what you sow in this class.
This is a difficult course, and the language of the course is very different from other quantitative classes so be prepared to learn a different framework for statistical arguments that you may have seen before.
This class is well–structured and a great basis for understanding econometrics. If you are an undergrad, consider taking it if you would like to understand more deeply the foundations behind the metrics you have already learned, but don't take this to learn additional applied methods per se. I think this class is a particularly excellent complement to MIT 14.32
Challenging course at times but worth it. Don't get too hung up on details of the notes, and if you do, ask the TFs.
This is a reasonable, comprehensive class on theoretical econometrics. Making the connection to using this work in producing papers is something you'll have to do on your own, though––there isn't much data/practical work here.
You can probably grind out the problem sets in an afternoon with your group. You might get more out of them if you try them on your own first.

I found it useful to read the lecture notes in advance of lecture.
For undergrads interested in taking this class: almost all theory and very little actual empirical work, so be aware. Otherwise a great theoretical treatment of metrics, you'll come away with a much deeper understanding of what's actually going on in different empirical estimation contexts.
It is very hard, it is very theoretical, but you'll learn a TON.
This is a great class, if a bit theoretical at times. If you spend the time to understand what is actually happening with the various formulas and derivations, the concepts are not too complicated and there is a lot to take away. All of the lectures tie together very nicely, and you can develop a nice understanding of the basics of econometrics.
Great course if you want to understand econometrics "properly", which is exactly what I was hoping to do as a first year. If you are a practitioner who cars mainly about learning fancy methods to apply, it does not go much beyond what is introduced in undergrad courses.
Helpful course to understand most basic econometric concepts
This is a course based on Gary Chamberlain's legendary notes, whose writings are notoriously dense, yet there is a reason why this course is one of the foundational Harvard Econ experiences, since it establishes a rigorous framework to then be able to follow discussions in contemporary econometrics. For undergrads considering the course, I would say that it is a great survey of 'serious' econometrics, but it requires very good familiarity with empirical research such that you can motivate on your own why these methods would be useful. It's a tough course but Elie is a very understanding and dedicated teacher. Also, Chris Walker was the best TF I've seen at Harvard in my 4 years.
Perfect course, highly recommeded!
