What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This course was incredibly interesting and Professor Georgi is personable and engaging, but the content of this seminar is advanced to the point of near incomprehension. I would recommend this course if you are interested in beautiful physics phenomena and have a strong foundation in mathematical and physical concepts, but I would hesitate to recommend it without this strong background.
If this class interests you, take it! Professor Georgi is so insanely knowledgable, but above all, he is the sweetest person ever. He really gets to know his students, and engages with us in the seminar format. As a life sciences–focused student, I had a slightly different perspective than other students, but I never felt like I was being left behind. On the contrary, because Professor Georgi knew what topics in science were particularly interesting to each of us, he would target questions for us to answer, and begin conversations so that we could all be involved. This was one of the absolute highlights of my semester, and I would recommend this course to anyone interested in physics.
It's almost impossible to fail this class (as long as you show up to class). You need a background in multivariable calculus and higher level physics in order to actually understand the concepts taught in this class. If you're thinking of pursuing physics (especially particle physics) this class is great. If not, don't expect to understand everything being taught, but it's still great to meet Georgi.
It's a great lecture series and there's no work outside of class. You should know linear algebra or higher to get the most out of it though.
This class is very interesting if you are good at math or think you will become very good at math within the semester. It highlights some important ways that a physicist thinks and explores very cool topics that might make you think differently about your experience of the physical world. Again though, you need significant mathematical understanding to get a lot out of this course. Georgi is a wonderful professor and their is not much work at all.
I would not recommend taking this class. It advertises itself as a replacement for 15c, but there's just not enough time to really understand the material. It's also rather uninspiring, and the class atmosphere was very dull. If you're interested in a physics seminar/freshman class with a similar "beautiful" advertisement, I think Physics, Math, and Puzzles or Physics 19 are both great options.
There is a student who wants to spend a lot of time working through Georgi's Waves textbook (certainly look at it before enrolling) and is willing to show up and ask lots of questions at Physics night. Perhaps some deep understanding might come out of the course for this student. If one cannot sustain this level of commitment for a large part of the semester, it might be wise to choose a seminar with more accessible material. The basic problem is that if you can't follow the material, you really can't follow the material. I could not follow the material after approx. week 3–4 (+a great and accessible lecture about rainbows)!
Prof Georgi's lectures are fascinatinggiven a hard concept, he will always provide some intuition, along with animations to help with visualization. This class will teach you to become more curious about the world around you, and give you a taste of how to begin analyzing complicated, coupled systems such as waves from the back of a boat. Sometimes, the material is quite heavy, and in my opinion requires quite a lot of physics and math background to fully understand at the pace it is taught. However, if you put in the effort of pre–reading material (like skimming through some 15c content or MIT OCW waves) and doing the optional problems, you will gain a lot from the lectures. Prof. Georgi also gives fun stories about his research and his interactions with other physicists including Feynman. Overall, I would highly recommend this classyou gain deeper insight into how physicists think, learn about oscillating systems/symmetry/the wave equation, and participate in a few fun hands–on demos as well.
