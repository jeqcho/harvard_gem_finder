Take this class! It's honestly been one of my favorite courses I've taken at Harvard (I'm a senior). Dr Whited is so enthusiastic and is a wonderful person –– she conveys her excitement for regeneration so well, and all the discussions were fascinating. I learned so much about regeneration. It's really interesting to think about the role of the immune system and nerve cells, as well as signaling proteins in inducing regeneration. I also liked that the course encompassed a fair amount of biodiversity (planarians, axolotls, newts, lizards, stick insects, frogs, mice, zebrafish...), and discussed different strategies of regeneration that these organisms have, as well as similarities across distantly related species. I found the discussions to be really thought–provoking and I've been recommending this class to all my friends.
Take this class! Dr. Whited and the TFs are very passionate about the material and care about the students. They take feedback seriously and I think the workload is very reasonable. The final project is a fresh take on science communication and I appreciate that it is a creative endeavor (versus a routine final exam).
This course is advertised as friendly to bio and non–bio concentrators alike. As a non–bio concentrator, here are some qualifying statements to that:

– In terms of the actual assignments, this is somewhat correct. While the quizzes are more difficult for non–bio concentrators, the grading is such that someone who has developed some level of experimental intuition from lecture can answer questions without all of the specific terms and get credit. The paper summaries also have a learning curve, but they aren't graded too harshly and two of them are dropped to account for the learning curve.
– As far as class goes, it seems like a lot of knowledge is assumed. There were often times when specific bio terms were used with little explanation that were only known by those who have taken an intro SCRB course. This made it seem at times like this was a course more for students with the implied SCRB prerequisites, and non–bio students were just allowed to be sitting in.

All in all, a non–biology student can definitely get through this class with a good grade, but their experience in class discussions will likely be limited by not having a certain level of background knowledge.
TAKE THIS CLASS! Especially if you want to learn more about regeneration pathways and potential modulations! Class is basically a cool journal club where your SCRB 10 and 50 skills can be applied!

Wouldn't recommend taking if you're not of a stem background – most of the quizzes deal with experimental methods and systems (like Cre/lox) and you will be lost if you can't create a rapid amount of experiments in a limited time period. There was a pure CS kid in our class and every quiz was like one of those dreams where you're naked in front of a firing squad for him, so would be wary.
Seniors, take it! Jessica is a great instructor, understanding that things come up in life, and teaches really interesting material. The course is a GEM of an upper level SCRB course. If you're a SCRB concentrator, you will breeze through this. If you haven't taken a bio course since high school, maybe choose another course LOL? You will have to put in a lot more time than the SCRB people.

However, this course may be a bit frustrating for SCRB concentrators. Because Jessica makes this course open to everyone, regardless of their bio backgrounds, the SCRB people often had to sit through really basic material while the others learned things for the first time. This made a lot of the SCRB seniors, myself included, really just check out during large portions of the course.

Also, every Thursday is not Jessica teaching but student presentations of papers. It's nice that students get to practice presenting scientific information, but a real drag for everyone in the audience. Why? If you're not interested in how experiments work, don't like hearing your peers stumble through half–prepared presentations, or just would prefer to hear the professor teaching material, you will be extremely bored every Thursday. That means Thursdays are often email catchup days, as there really isn't much to be gleaned from student paper presentations unless you are actually interested in going to grad school for regeneration (care about experimental design).

Beside this, the course was fun. Highly recommend.
Can be a lot of work but worth it
All of the coursework and assessments are fair and not too difficult, but don't expect this class to be an easy A. It was quite a bit of work, taking into account the 6 paper synopses, 3 quizzes, 1 full class length presentation, and final video project. It was a class that was supposed to be meant for all concentrators, but quizzes were definitely geared toward students who had previously taken SCRB 10 (they mostly tested knowledge of methods such as Cre/Lox and lineage tracing, and not too related to the actual material covered during lecture). I'm also not too sure that I solidly learned concepts about regeneration, as the lectures were sort of confusing. Also, the TFs this year were both pretty harsh graders, and had harsh comments during class discussions. The best part of the class was the final video project and being able to work at the Bok Center, as well as getting to know Professor Whited. She has such enthusiasm for the subject and is very willing to work with students to help them succeed.
