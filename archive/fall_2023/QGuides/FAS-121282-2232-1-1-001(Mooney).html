Easier than other classes in SEAS if you are required to take them, but you won't learn much and will spend some time doing busywork. The slides were very out of date and not didactic. This course was incredibly disappointing, especially given how prolific in the field Dave is, it was so poorly organized and so low effort and I left this class having learned nothing.
This course is a lot of busy work and not very rewarding. The premise sounds much more exciting than it actually is. The class is mostly Prof. Mooney reading off of old slides and lots of time outside of class reviewing papers, in the lab, or writing a proposal. I was pretty disappointed. If you are looking for a broad overview of tissue engineering it will definitely do that, but don't expect to be able to solve any problems in tissue engineering after the course.
This class is great as an introduction to the events happening in tissue engineering today, how to analyze scientific readings, and how to work with mammalian cells in lab. I highly recommend doing the lab component of the class as the independent project is really exciting and the team is incredibly supportive. There's a good amount of hours dedicated in lab, but its not nearly as bad as most make it sound. Lectures are difficult and sometimes unhelpful, doing the reading ahead of time is useful for understanding terminology. The exams are difficult and if they cannot find TFs this year I would suggest being very wary of the mathematical formulas used and making sure you understand their purpose, because they will rarely be explained more than once.
This was a good class overall. As for the class lectures, I personally did not feel like I gained a deep understanding of tissue engineering. Lectures sometimes felt dry and only briefly covered various aspects of the field without going into depth into any of them. However, I did enjoy the weekly journal club assignments, since they forced us to not just read landmark papers in the field but also think critically about them. Professor Mooney also really cares about the class and takes student feedback into account.

I will say that the lab was definitely the highlight of this class: Melissa and Avery really want you to succeed and create an environment where you feel safe asking questions and learning important/employable wet lab skills. If you have limited wet lab experience, I highly recommend doing the lab you learn the very basics (filtering media, culturing/passaging cells) as well as more advanced techniques (3D cell culture and modeling angiogenesis). Melissa and Avery also mentor you one–on–one every week throughout the entire scientific process, from developing a good scientific question/hypothesis to running your experiments to analyzing your data. Be prepared to spend up to 10 hours in the lab each week if you really want to get the most out of this class, but it's a rewarding experience like no other!
not many exam practice problems
Definitely a must–take for bioengineering students. On top of being exposed to a broad variety of tissue engineering techniques and gaining good insight into the field, you will learn how to read papers well and learn valuable wet lab skills (if you take that component of the course) or learn how to write a good research proposal if you opt that way. Professor Mooney is incredibly knowledgeable, but lectures move very very fast and can be overwhelming with how much information is presented. I would suggest being at least a junior with a few years of bio experience and exposure to bioengineering techniques before taking this course. If you opt for the lab component, this class will take time. There are usually 2 homework assignments every week which take around 2 hours each to complete. Lab is about 3 hours. There are 2 open note midterms as well, which are hard but definitely doable.
Super interesting class! Content was somewhat disorganized but it was all fascinating and gave a great overview to the field. I enjoyed doing the proposal; I felt it prepared me for grad school.
Do the sample exams and any old exams.
This class is definitely easier than BE 121. 100% take this over BE 121. It is still a difficult class but there is not as much work. Lab offers a lot of support and is very helpful with the learning curve. When working with your team you will likely only have to come in once a week outside of your scheduled lab time for 1/2 hours to work on your cells or project. Start analysis and the presentation early as almost all of our groups were scrambling last minute to finish things up. Also pick group members you trust as lab will be difficult otherwise because cells can get easily contaminated with bad lab hygiene. The journal club reviews are very lowkey but prepare to get no feedback on literally anything. It is helpful to team up with other members of the class to collaborate on notes for the midterms as they are open note and open book. Forming a study group and pooling your resources is the best approach.
Read the journal club assignments diligently. Study for exams with friends. Be incredibly communicative and work ahead of time for the final projects. Take this course because you will learn so much but keep in mind that you will have to put in the work. This class was not easy even though I thought I had some knowledge and experience in tissue engineering from the past.
