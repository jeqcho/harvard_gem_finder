This is a good class if you are interested in an introduction to earth science. It's not a particularly hard class at all. The problem sets and labs are not long, and the TFs are more than willing to help you do well. Sometimes the class is very disorganized, which is frustrating, but not the end of the world by any means. Lectures are interesting, though not mind blowing or super engaging.
definitely worth taking if you want exposure to these topics –– you leave with a decent understanding of how global level earth systems work ... however, be prepared for disorganized lectures and take the initiative to ask lots of questions during section (very helpful TFs)
This course covered a broad span of climate and ocean processes as promised; I feel like I have a good awareness of the major processes that facilitate climate balance, and also an awareness of the Earth's climatic past, with an emphasis on the causes and driving forces of our climate's history. As someone studying physics, I enjoyed how the physical models that represent climate mechanisms were introduced, characteristic of EPS studies. (If you aren't super into physics, by all means don't worry! I think we literally solved one equation and it was for chemistry––what I mean here is that we think about physical models and their behavior, takes a bit of imagination and it's quite fun). There was a lot of cool information to pick up and learn that informed how I see the world now. I really feel like I learned a lot and now have a broad understanding of the processes that mediate the behavior of our climate and its interaction with oceans. I would highly recommend!
This course has little to no deviations from what it's advertised as. You learn a lot about the Fluid Earth. However, I also learned that personally, the Fluid Earth just isn't something that I am particularly interested in (not necessarily a reflection on the course!). Don't come in as someone with a love for the outdoors and nature and conservation and ecology and expect to love this course at all.

Lecture feels longer than it actually is. The professor is great, clearly a genius in her field, and can explain concepts really well one–on–one, but her lectures just weren't interactive enough to keep me fully engaged. However, if you have a genuine interest in most of the material, you might have a different experience.

Sometimes, it seemed as if content was still a little unclear. Stephen, my TF, was a lifesaver in this regard (and in the course as a whole). Stephen is really great at breaking down complicated topics, is patient enough to explain material step by step, would go above and beyond in answering emails, staying after lab to answer questions, hosting office hours, would always provide helpful encouragement, and generally wanted the best for everyone. Countless times I've found myself understanding a topic I thought I'd never understand thanks to Stephen.

In general, reach out for help, stay after lecture (the professor is better at explaining material then), start on PSETs early to make the most out of section and office hours, and you will be just fine. If this isn't a concentration requirement and you just happen to love conservation/broader material on climate change, take another course.
It's a fun class that will introduce you to a lot of the more intricate details of weather, climate, currents, and a bunch of other concepts with plenty of detail. The labs and problem sets are genuinely useful for explaining more about the course and the lectures are effective at conveying information. If the subject matter is at all interesting, you should definitely take this course! I never took EPS 10 and found I did fine without it.
I took this class because I was interested in the topic being taught the fluid Earth, ocean dynamics, and atmospheric science. Although some concepts were interesting, this class truly taught me that the professor makes or breaks the experience. The class was not taught well by the professor, so my overall experience of this class was negative. I wouldn't recommend it, but if you are to take it, be prepared to teach a lot of things to yourselves and leave lectures confused almost daily. Even the TFs would seem confused during lectures and would sometimes have to correct the professor.
Really interesting material, TF Stephen is amazing.
If you're interested in the scientific side of how the Earth/climate/environment works, take this class! Keep in mind that this class IS science–based, so be prepared to apply physics, chemistry, and biology concepts. The class is definitely a challenge (not a GEM at all), so don't fall behind. Be attentive to lectures and sections, review what you learn in class very frequently, and don't fall behind.
This is an excellent class with some incredibly interesting topics and information. Lecturing was a little rough but the content is too cool for it to matter. The work load is very reasonable either a lab or PSET per week. Labs are very chill, PSETS are generally doable on your own in a few hours or less. Make sure to really study for the midterms and this class will be your favorite.
TAKE THIS COURSE! This is my favorite course so far at Harvard. The content was well organized, the lectures fascinating, the labs were well designed to teach the related concepts like the jet stream and ocean circulation, the problem sets were well–written such that I learned so much while doing them. I love Professor Linz and the TFs Stephen and Nathanael were amazing teachers. If you have any interest in the Earth, in climate change, in the science behind storms like El Nino, in understanding the ocean, or just the basics of anthropogenic emissions, then this course is for you. I came in with no background, but now will be studying EPS at Harvard extensively after this. Best experience ever!!
