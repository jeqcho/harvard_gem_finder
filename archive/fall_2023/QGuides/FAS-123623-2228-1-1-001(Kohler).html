Imagine you are dropped into a maze: a very large and convoluted maze that you have to get out of. Not only that, but it is pitch–black and you can't see anything in front of you. Thus is the environment of the CS61 pset.

Now, there are a couple of things people can do to get out of this maze. The most obvious approach is to run around and stumble into walls, while jotting down some notes with pen and paper along the way. After a lot of extremely tiring work and feverishly scribbling down notes on your sheet of paper, you may get out of the maze – if you're lucky. These maze runners symbolize the people who try to do cs61 psets on their own, or with friends who are equally as confused as them. For your sake, don't be this type of person. If you don't have friends to take this class with you, don't do it.

Now many of you may be asking "why don't you just go to lecture and sections? Then you can understand what's going on and this will help you do the psets." This is a great suggestion, but you must not overestimate its utility. Watching lectures and understanding material is like entering this maze with a nice flashlight. You'll see further than people without one, for sure, but you still have a lot of work to do. This is because the psets are structured in such a way that the lecture material is way too high–level for it. Just knowing the concepts will not get you much progress in the pset.

Then there are people who enter the maze with a complete map of it stowed in their pocket. All they have to do is examine the map for a bit and walk the correct path. These maze runners symbolize the students who know the correct TFs to go to who can guide them in the pset; like guide them a lot. Or they know other students who know the right TFs and have gone to all the office hours so that they know what to do and can help you out. You want to be this kind of person. You want to have a map of the maze.

If you are able to do the psets well, however, that doesn't mean that you will do well on the exams. Because of how hard the psets are, many students are incentivized to "get the map" so to speak, however this will harm your education as a whole, and you won't really know what to do when the exam comes around. Thus, my suggestion for students is to first really understand the lecture content, then try your hand on the psets before asking for help. Don't spend too much time, however, as it is almost certain that you will need help from a TF or a friend who has finished. At that point, you have to go get the map.

Overall: CS 61 is an introduction to systems programming. It primarily uses C, the language used to write most of Linux, to dive into how operating systems, file io, data caching, threading, etc. are implemented on the x86–64 architecture. There’s a fair proportion of freshmen here, but predominantly upperclassmen. If you’re an incoming freshman, it is quite a jump from AP CS, so hopefully you have programming experience beyond that level. Prior experience in C will help a lot; you do not want to be learning the language/syntax on the fly while Professor Kohler plows ahead. That being said, it is one of the few (and one of the hardest) actual coding classes here at Harvard before you get buried in theory. See past course websites; cs61.seas.harvard.edu/site/2022 for my year, and also the 2018 site has good notes. Pset themes may vary from year to year, but the content should be roughly the same.

Professor: Professor Kohler (Eddy) is an absolute menace at what they do. They enjoy showing us all the quirks of C and make lecture presentations fun and often themed. Eddy is also very responsive to feedback and gives a lot of guidance in class and on the Ed board (see course structure notes below).

Textbook: Did not use it as much as I probably should have, but it provides a good supplement to the theory behind many of the course topics.
Computer Systems: A Programmer’s Perspective, Third Edition by Randal E. Bryant and David R. O’Hallaron

Topics covered: Data representation (size, alignment, memory allocation, argument passing, segments, undefined behavior), Assembly (registers, calling convention, control flow), Kernel (process isolation, robustness, protected control transfer, system calls, virtual memory, page tables), Storage (memory hierarchy, caching, files, stdio, cache optimization/coherence, eviction policies), Process Control (fork, execvp, child/parent/zombie processes, pipes, shells, race conditions), Synchronization (atomicity, threads, data races, mutexes, deadlock, condition variables, blocking/polling, databases)

Course structure notes: Ed Discussion (the Ed board) is the forum site for CS61, with feedback from students, TAs, and Eddy. The initial setup is relatively long and generates the first batch of Ed board posts (mostly git/Docker; they guide you through the setup on the website, but it helps if you have used both before). Weekly mandatory lectures Monday and Wednesday in the SEC*; Friday lectures are usually supplemental, for questions/review/advanced topics. 6 psets, in the form of large multi–step incremental programming assignments, one every two weeks. There’s also these beautiful things called late hours that I got to know very well. Submitted through pushing to git and viewing on the grading server. Weekly sections; usually the first section gives us a boost into the pset and the second goes through exercise problems. Don’t procrastinate.
