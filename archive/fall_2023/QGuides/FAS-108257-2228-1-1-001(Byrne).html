What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you have an interest in neuroscience and how it relates to various other fields (e.g. linguistics, math, computer science, philosophy, economics), this is the PERFECT freshman seminar for you. The conversations I've had in this class were incredibly insightful, and never failed to make me think more deeply and broadly about the way the human mind works, both from a biological perspective and a more philosophical viewpoint. Professor Byrne is an incredibly caring and thoughtful professor who made this class such an enjoyable and comfortable space for these discussions to take place. Workload is incredibly manageable, largely compromised of a few readings/videos, a presentation, and a short final paper. The course is less biology–focused than one might expect – we do learn about the biological basis of some neural pathways, but it's not the key focus at all. Dr. Byrne really just wants you to think about how the brain is a mindblowing thing, and how it has shaped our thinking and the world we live in. An incredibly interdisciplinary seminar on the whole – would highly recommend to students of ANY discipline, as long as they have interest in the mentioned topics!
This class is fascinating, but you really get out of it what you put into it. There is a lot of reading that we never really discussed in class but was interesting nonetheless. It feels a lot like an open intellectual discussion between peers, and bridges the gap between neuroscience and philosophy in many ways. I would recommend knowing a bit of neuro, but it's not necessary at all.
This seminar, like many have found in past years, is simply amazing. The enthusiasm and wisdom that Dr. Byrne brings to the class is something that I've barely ever seen. He is extremely knowledgeable in the field, and makes his expertise available to you. He encourages you to meet with him, ask good questions in class, and explore intellectually in both technical matters of neuroscience and medicine and in broader philosophical issues. The workload is very light, but the content is absolutely fascinating (if not the most detailed and technical, but that's not a bad thing!).
I would tell future students that this class is very rewarding, but it is also more philosophical than one might expect. If you enjoy philosophical discussions, you will enjoy this class. If not, perhaps this is not the class for you.
This class is very rewarding – Dr. Byrne is an amazing person to get to know (you may even get to meet his wife at their home for a Thanksgiving dinner!), and he will teach you not only about the brain, but the implications behind studying the brain, contentious ideas, how to get started with research at Harvard, and his career journey. I loved the final paper towards the end of class (which is due before Fall Break, so it doesn't clash with reading/finals period), especially because I got to research about a topic regarding the brain I was interested in and had the opportunity to hear about what other students were interested in as well.
You really get what you put into this class. The class doesn't require much work, but you have to make an effort to engage and meet with the Dr. Byrne outside of class. Contributing to class discussions are a huge part of this class (and you will be called out on it) but it's often judgement free and everyone is very open to new ideas.
Apply for the class if you want to, and if you get in, TAKE IT! It was the best experience of my freshman fall because of our amazing discussions about neuroscience and philosophy, which lie close to my heart.
