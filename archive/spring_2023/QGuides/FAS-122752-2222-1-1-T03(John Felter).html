What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Professor Feldman is a great professor. He gives feedback that is very thorough and in–depth. There are a lot of readings but I feel I learned a lot from being in this class. While this class was difficult, I would consider it a gem.
This economics tutorial is phenomenal and is a gem within the sophomore tutorial selections. There are a lot of readings and writing assignments, but such is the case with most other tutorials. If you are on the fence about the law, or just want to take a class to learn a lot about economics and the law, I would highly recommend this class. Every reading was selected with care for the content. Professor Felter provides ample feedback to help you improve as a writer and critical thinker. The discussions every week were interesting. You do not need to be pre–law to take this class, and in my experience, most of the people were on the fence about the law. Professor Felter is an amazing professor, and if you take this class, you will leave the class knowing comprehensively what economics is and what the law is, and what an economic analysis of the law entails.
Professor Felter is a gem of a human being. Very responsive to meeting outside of class to get help on the final paper and has such industry knowledge of the law that hearing his anecdotes in class made the experience. Genuinely cares about his students. Actually took both his sections out to dinner this semester. Easily one of the best classes I've taken at Harvard.
Highly recommend if interested in law. Felter is great.
I was begrudgingly taking a sophomore tutorial my junior year and did not have high expectations based on what I had heard from friends. I forgot to submit rankings in time and randomly received this class. What good luck! This was a truly excellent class taught by an expert in the field in an intimate setting. This would be an appropriate course for students of many levels in each of law and economics – much to learn for all and great readings. My main complaint is the problem sets. I was familiar with R from past courses and found them to be easy, if entirely unrelated from the class itself. Others who did not know R or STATA struggled, as neither is taught in class. This is a broader problem with the economics departments conception of the sophomore tutorial, as they somehow seem to expect students to just pick up quantitative methods in a sophomore tutorial by osmosis and many of the instructors are not comfortable teaching econometrics and programming. I would advise taking an introduction to R course in the government department freshman or sophomore year before taking this class or any other economics course which pretends to teach empirical methods but then requires students to attend a separate section on R taught by whichever grad student needed a job that term.
If you are interested in law, behavioral economics and generally economics, TAKE 970 with PROF. FELTER! This is the best class I have taken at Harvard so far, and the class I have been waiting for. I wanted to be in a small class with engaging discussions, interesting readings and a very interesting professor. And this class has it all. It is a lot of work, and if you didn't come to work it's not for you. But this class challenges you to think and to consider different theories, to engage with difficult texts and to really learn.
Felter's Law class was incredibly interesting and he is great, but it is definitely more work than the other tutorials. I wouldn't even say that you need to be interested in Law at all to take it, just willing to read and write a lot consistently.
