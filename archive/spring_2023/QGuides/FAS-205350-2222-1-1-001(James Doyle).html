What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Reading Plato is always a blast. Go do it!
Doyle knows the dialogue really well and there's a complexity to the Socrates of the Gorgias that makes the dialogue eternally relevant
This course is a great dive into the Gorgias and Ancient Philosophy in general. Taking this class taught me a lot about the Socratic style of questioning, the Ancient Greek Virtues, and other related topics that would be impossible for me to glean from reading the Gorgias on my own. That being said, the fact that this course is a discussion/seminar shouldn't be overlooked. If you are a younger undergraduate, make sure you are ready to participate for a couple of hours on a pretty high–level discussion, usually involving a couple of grad students who aren't afraid to rope in some tangential Platonic dialogue or a recent article they read. You'll learn a lot from this class, but if you don't have the necessary philosophical preparation, you might also find it a tad bit overbearing.
This class will best serve students with a moderate or strong background in analytic philosophy (a background in classical philosophy is helpful but you can get by without it). Without a grounding in both the content of analytic philosophy, and the form in which it is written and conducted, Doyle's methodology and the ensuing class discussions may strike you as pedantic or opaque. They are neither but a solid background in philosophy and a good amount of work is required to get at the valuable experiences this class offers (a detailed and unique reading of a particular Platonic dialogue and a sound overview of the nature of Platonic dialogue itself).
