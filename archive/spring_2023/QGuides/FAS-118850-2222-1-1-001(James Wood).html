This is one of the best courses I have taken at Harvard. The readings are exceptional (and very manageable), and Professor Wood leads incredibly thought–provoking discussions each week. He cares deeply about his students, provides detailed feedback on papers, meets with students often outside of class, and this year, he even split the class into two groups to keep the seminar feel to the course! If you can take this class, take it. You will get so much out of it regardless of whether or not you are experienced in English.
This was one of my favorite courses I've taken at Harvard. If you love literature or study English, take it!
TAKE THIS CLASS IF YOU CAN!!!! Professor Wood is AMAZING – so so smart, kind, interested in his students' ideas, etc. He is also quite lenient with deadlines, assignment length, readings, etc. and extremely understanding. You will learn so much in this class and read incredible books!!! One of my favorite classes at Harvard.
If you want to read a great set of works from largely the 19th C (Wood only real exception) and hear a lot of insightful thoughts on them from Prof Wood, that’s this class! Don’t be fooled by the participant cap — this tends to be a larger class, so if you’re looking for a smaller seminar (<15) where you or others are sharing thoughts every few minutes and the Professor largely serves as moderator, that’s not this. That said, you don’t necessarily need it to be. Just reading these works in succession is a joy; Prof Wood has thought a lot about how they build on each other (ie, rise and fall of the adultery novel) and has more knowledge to share about them than can possibly fit in 2 hours — that’s where his synthesizing notes, handed out after each book, are so helpful (why don’t more professors do this!?) And he’s an extraordinarily warm Professor who somehow always makes the time to chat
TAKE THIS CLASS!!! I cannot recommend it enough. Prof. Wood is fantastic. He is kind and thoughtful and helps to make this class not be super stressful. This literature list is super cool and extensive. If you have the opportunity, you have to take this class!!
This class has been among the most rewarding intellectual experiences of my time at Harvard, introducing me to so many wonderful novels and allowing me, thanks to Professor Wood's brilliant analytical mind and approach to teaching, to put my finger on just what makes them so wonderful. Class conversation was always fascinating, and I learned so much not only from Professor Wood, but also from my peers' contributions, which often drew from many disciplines (history, gender & sexuality studies, comparative literature) to consider the novels from various perspectives. To put it briefly: I never saw the time pass during the seminars, and believe no one else in the class did.
Prof Wood is one of the most generous, brilliant, thoughtful professors I've had as an undergrad; English 90LV has been one of my all time favorite Harvard courses
This syllabus is such a treat; it is truly the best selection in any English class at Harvard that I have taken in my four years! Prof. Wood is a wonderful critic and writer, and so approachable and kind. Take this class! You will not regret it.
Everything you need to know about Professor Wood as a teacher is that he offered the class an option, which we took, to split into two two–hour seminars rather than one larger one. He willingly set aside an extra two hours of his week for his students. He is a really kind and thoughtful teacher, and he teaches this course as a reader, not a professor –– that is a difference that may be hard to appreciate until one takes a class with a reader. Professor Wood's enthusiasm for the texts –– well–selected, all of them –– was apparent and infectious. His delight in turns of phrase was evident. This is the sort of class one takes to reignite a lapsed passion for reading. Though at times I missed the "deeper" treatment of texts that a professorial approach brought, this was a welcome balance to those courses, which are the majority of Harvard English classes I've taken. The quantity of reading was ample but fair, and the quality of course materials was high. Would generally recommend this class with enthusiasm.
This is one of the best courses I've taken at Harvard! James Wood is an excellent discussion leader, providing many wonderful insights as well! If you haven't taken a course with Wood yet, it's a must before you graduate!
This is a really great class if you're interested in fiction! The lectures are great and the grading and assignments are very reasonable.
If you're interested in the Western canon at all, this course is a must–take. It's definitely one of my two favorite classes I've taken at Harvard – the other being one on Joyce. Professor Wood is engaging, inspiring, and knowledgeable: his formalist approach completely changed the way I look at literature, and some of the things he points out about the novels just makes you say "Wow." He's so accessible outside of class, too. The pacing of the reading is very nice, as you'll only read about 120 pages a week, giving you time to really engage with the readings.
I cannot recommend Professor Wood's seminar enough. Professor Wood leads excellent discussions, fosters curiosity about and cross–examinations of the texts, and gives thoughtful feedback in response to students' work and commentary. The reading list is stylistically diverse while providing a marvelous longitudinal view of the changing representations of the mind and self in literature throughout the nineteenth and twentieth centuries as well as the development of the "novel" as a literary form. The pace of the course is leisurely but wonderfully so, as it affords enough time for one to appreciate and interrogate the text thoroughly before coming to class with questions for discussion. I loved the class meetings, which were intimate, open, and always generative, due to the smaller class size, round–table setting, and free–flowing seminar format.
Many courses fall under the category "you get out of it what you put into it," but this one especially so.

James Wood is wonderful, and I loved listening to his thoughts on the readings every week. His sense of humor is delightful — wry, the tiniest bit snarky. (Ask for his thoughts on the 2013 Crimson op–ed "Let Them Eat Code.")

The class had the feeling of a lecture, but I know some of my classmates would have preferred for the class to have more discussion. Prof. Wood would address the class for a few minutes, and then one or two students would speak, and then Prof. Wood would address the class again. There wasn't much student–to–student dialogue.

I would recommend taking the course for James Wood alone! That being said, I think it can be greatly improved by creating more of a class community, whether that's through discussion posts before seminar or through separate meetings.
