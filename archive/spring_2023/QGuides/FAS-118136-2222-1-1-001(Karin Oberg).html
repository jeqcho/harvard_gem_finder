If you take this class with Professor Oberg, know that you will be in for a challenging semester. While Oberg's lectures are fascinating, they often don't connect directly to pset topics, which requires a lot of outside work and self–teaching, so I would not recommend this class to someone without a strong physics/math background or only a passing interest in Astrophysics. Make sure to attend office hours and section in order to get ahead on psets; my experience with the TFs was mixed (sometimes they were helpful, other times they were cryptic in their answers and downright cold to students) but they usually helped shed insights into the concepts that were not touched on in lecture. Also, the tests in this class are extremely challenging as they apply concepts learned on the psets, not on the lectures, making it difficult to study or know what to expect. If you are taking this class for your concentration, your love of Astrophysics will definitely be tested by the lack of strong teaching or class structure and unnecessarily hard homework assignments and tests. However, I enjoyed the experience of this class, and all in all, it was a fascinating introduction into the fundamental concepts of stellar and planetary astronomy.
Take this class!!!!! Astronomy 16 offers a manageable look into the world of stellar and planetary astronomy. There are so many cool things to learn, and the class is consistently engaging. 110% worth it.

Labs (especially the second) felt more like I was an observing third party rather than working hands on myself. Grading felt somewhat arbitrary and unreflective of content understanding.

Overall, this class did indeed provide an overview of the major topics of stellar and planetary phenomena. I took 15a concurrently and didnt fare much worse than anyone else in the classa knowledge of what angular momentum and the universal law of gravitation equations are will help if youd like to prepare, because the former will be mentioned many times in an explanatory fashion, and the latter will be used several times. Its different from 15a because you dont quite build upon previous phenomenon, conceptually. This is inherently physics applied to selected systems and objects, and you are going to be handed equations, not postulates (there is an emphasis on understanding how we get to these equations, which is nice). Consequently, you will be jumping from topic to topic, and as is inherent to astronomy, you will need to make creative approximations so working in a pset group is practically a must, and also is more expedient for learning the methods/solutions that others got from TFs in office hoursbecause there will sometimes be a RIGHT way to approximate or approach the problem, and it will not always be clear based on the pset phrasing, which is annoying. If you want to really deeply understand how astronomy and physical systems are approached, you will have to put the time in, and if you do it can be quite beautiful. Dont put off psets for the last night before theyre due, and do go to office hours and work to get one on one time to discuss concepts because then it will be very fruitful. Be prepared to put the work in this is not a hard class, but its not a class you can slack off in you need to stay on your toes and up to date on lectures, etc. I took this class because I am considering concentrating in astrophysics and had never taken any astronomy class before. I am glad I took it, I enjoyed learning what I did, but now that Ive worked with lots of equations, I want to now do research to apply what Ive learned. This class is what you make of it. And berg is a great professor, shes super sweet.
If you are taking this course with Professor berg, you will definitely learn a lot of interesting content, but be prepared to work hard! Professor berg is a wonderful professor and lecturer, and I always found myself very engaged during classes with her. She is so incredibly knowledgeable and passionate, and she has a great sense of humor and gives good advice! I enjoyed going to her office hours and talking to her outside of class. If you are an astrophysics concentrator, she is definitely someone you want to get to know!
The psets in this course are generally very reasonable and connected to the lectures, plus most of the time I was able to complete a good chunk of the pset during the Wednesday section with my group. The exams are difficult – you have to be prepared to put in a good amount of effort and time to study – but the course staff are fairly generous with partial credit. Start the lab reports early!! You will feel like you have a ton of time to do it, but it will definitely take longer than you think because there are a lot of requirements to fulfill (this comes from personal experience – I turned in both lab reports late and suffered for it). Also, office hours were pretty limited, so I highly recommend working in groups for the psets, lab reports, and Python notebooks so you can pool your knowledge (and your questions for the TFs).
