This class is really not as bad as you would think.
This class is easily approachable without a background in philosophy and is ideal for someone who believes interpersonal relationships are extremely valuable.
Take this class!
Jen is a great instructor! Take if you have any interest in philosophy/friendship. Not only does this class teach you how to write, but it also teaches you how to make and support an argument––an invaluable skill!
The first thing a freshman needs to know about Expos is that it's not an intellectually fulfilling experience (and it isn't meant to be), so don't pick your section based on how interesting the title is. Even if you find yourself fascinated Jane Austen or con–law, you're likely going to find that the course really doesn't focus as much on the topic as you, the literary/legal obsessive, would like. And chances are you're going to be wasting time being taught how to write a topic sentence or a conclusion paragraph.

This isn't meant to be cynical, it's just the way things are. And once you accept that, you can make a better decision. Yes, this class has the most boring title/description, but it is an absolute gem. The only participation grades are the daily reading checks. (4 multiple choice questions that are as straightforward as possible. As in, you can probably get away with not doing the reading some days—especially when you're reading, say, the plagiarism guide—and still get 100%.) This means you aren't pressured to contribute a nothing–opinion every 5 minutes to save the GPA. The essays are practically graded against a rubric, meaning once you figure out the key–phrases ("in this paper, I will..."), you're pretty much guaranteed an A. The trick is to assume your reader has a double–digit IQ.

Tl;dr: When it comes to Expos, "Friendship" is the path of least resistance. (And to be clear, anyone will tell you that there is no point in choosing "resistance" for Expos, it's all just busy work.)
This class focuses mainly on the writing aspects, but it is much more of a philosophy class than a typical English course. I think the hardest part about this class was grappling with the philosophical reasoning and logical ethics when trying to formulate my arguments. Even then, it felt a lot more like I was interpreting other people's work than coming up with my own theses and proving MY beliefs.
Consider this course if you are into philosophy or want to gain knowledge in philosophy as well as writing. It wasn't hard per se, but a lot of it is coming up with your own ideas and philosophical arguments and conclusions, so it would help if you enjoyed thinking in that manner.
This class is definitely one of the better of the Expos sections. Homework is manageable and the Prof. Jen provides good feedback for drafts. She does have a particular writing style which is definitely blunt, but that is her stylistic preference.
I would tell them to do their best. Philosophy is tough, especially if you've never done it before, but just push through and do your best.
