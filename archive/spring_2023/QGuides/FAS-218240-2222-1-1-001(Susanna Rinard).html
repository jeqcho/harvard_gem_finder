At one point professor Rinard mentioned how a colleague said “Research, teaching, and family; pick two.” I’ve heard she’s an accomplished researcher and it’s clear she has strong bonds with her family. I think it’s fair to say her colleague was right. Never in my four years at college have I experienced a lecturer that is so disorganized and difficult to follow. I like to pay attention in all my classes, I tried, fervently, to do that in this class. It was impossible.

Rinard begins every lecture well, for the first two minutes or so: That's when the first aside about her son, her obsession with beans, or whatever her husband is writing begins. Every single lecture has been fragmented with these unnecessary asides with information about her personal life. It's not that I'm cruel and don't care about any of the things that make her human, it's that I can't learn if every 2–3 minutes she disrupts the flow by telling us a story that is only marginally related at best. And, even then, maybe I'm giving her too much credit: even when she had a long period without interruption (in this class that would be roughly 8 minutes), it was still difficult to follow what she was saying. It's clear she puts no time into planning her lectures and making them easy to digest. I imagine that her "preparation" for lecture is sitting down for 15 minutes, bulleting down anything that comes to mind when she thinks about the reading, then deciding that she might as well just lecture it in whatever haphazard order she came up with it in.

And then, of course, comes the issue of the materials. I read through every single assigned reading, sometimes more than what was assigned. To be frank, the reading list was very interesting and I was constantly intrigued by the ideas put forth. But, I have this inkling that she has not read more than a third of the readings she assigned herself. Throughout lectures, she would make vague counterarguments to the readings that show she clearly has never engaged with some of the readings. I wonder if she looked up book summaries instead. Then, of course, she would often portray the ideas she disagreed with as uncharitably as possible. If it wasn't written by Dan Haybron, it was probably hogwash, and she'll try to convince you of that by shaping the argument to be something it never was. This wouldn't bother me as much if I didn't get so excited to learn more about the readings after reading them. Imagine taking a GenEd thinking you just have to get by, finding yourself intellectually stimulated then wanting to learn more, then having the professor continually prove she does not know what she is talking about and likely knows less than you if you did your reading closely.

Overall, do not take this class. If you're interested, I'd recommend skimming the reading list and making use of it yourself. Professor Rinard does not make it an enjoyable learning experience whatsoever.
