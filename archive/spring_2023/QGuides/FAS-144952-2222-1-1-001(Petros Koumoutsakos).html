The exams are hard, so study hard for them and be prepared. Not unlike many other engineering courses, start the work early and it will all work out.
You will probably have to take this class if you’re a MechE or BioE concentrator. A lot of the concepts are fundamental to an engineering career but might not seem very applicable throughout the class. If you get a chance familiarize yourself with STAR–CCM+ through the class as this is the industry standard for fluids simulations and will get you a job.
The Lecture Notes that the TFs post (if they continue doing so) are the clearest record of the class content. I found the exams to be surprisingly difficult. I strongly suggest starting on your final project early (the support for lab–based projects this year was not well–organized so it was a messy rush at the end).
This class is a true engineering class in the sense that you really have to buckle down to understand things. But once you start understanding the material more, you will be more appreciative of what you're learning. Make sure you put in the hours and really practice because those assignments/exams are not really easy.

Unless the curriculum is changed, the course feels very disconnected, and sometimes you'll question if you're even learning fluids. The section component of the course is great, and the psets are a good way to check that you are learning what you need to learn.

This course is not very difficult but it is time–consuming (particularly with the lab component). If you have a time–intensive schedule, you may want to stay away but the material is interesting and very doable.
If you're on here, you most likely have to take this course, so I won't spend time telling you whether or not to take it, but I'll focus on how to best prepare yourself for the course. (1) Go to section. Section really helped me clear up all confusions I had during lecture, and it was a really good resource to help me consolidate material from the past week. If you pick a section, try to choose a section earlier in the week so that you have the content fresh in your mind when you're doing your pset throughout the week (our psets were due on Fridays and I had section on Tuesday). (2) Collaborate with others. Working with others, whether it be during OH or outside of class, will help you clear up any misunderstandings of the material and will help strengthen your grasp of it. So really recommend. (3) Ask help of the TFs. The TFs really made the course not sucky, and they are there to have open conversations about the material, final projects, and performance in the course. If you need any help, reach out to them and it will be worth it. (4) Continue giving feedback. Although the course has a long ways to go to being a good course, Petros is really open to feedback, and if you keep on stressing important aspects in the feedback, the teaching staff and Petros will soon catch on and will adjust the course as necessary.
Do not get overwhelmed by the lecture component of the course. To complete the p–sets, attend section and go to office hours. It is unlikely looking at lecture slides alone will help you.
Form a pset group and start the psets once you can to get a head start! Go to office hours early!
