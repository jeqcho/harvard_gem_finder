This class is really interesting, graded pretty easily, and is a moderate amount of work but nothing too extreme. Eli is so sweet and really cares about his students.
This class will certainly teach you the fundamentals of linear algebra that are applicable to data analysis, but in general is not a great experience can likely be replaced by another class or combination of classes that cover essentially the same material/teach the same skills and that would be a more satisfying experience. In my opinion, the strengths of this course are that the material is useful, Eli does truly care about the class and the students, and you'll get hands–on experience actually implementing some of the techniques you learn in class. However, some of my complaints about the course are, in no particular order:

– The PSets are long and tedious. Occasionally there will be a programming assignment where you will be able to implement something relatively interesting, such as an image compression algorithm, but this often is just copying and tweaking code that is provided by the course. However, the problems otherwise often take a long time to work through and the results aren't satisfying. They simply require a large amount of calculations, and it is frustratingly easy to make a small calculation mistake (e.g. when entering a decimal point number into Python/MATLAB, which occurs relatively often) that requires doing a large part of the problem again.
– The PSet expectations are not made very clear. The instructions for each PSet say something along the lines of, only use Python/MATLAB when explicitly required, otherwise only show calculations as done on a 4–function calculator. However, following these instructions exactly make the PSets take even longer, and the course staff has acknowledge in many circumstances that Python/MATLAB–aided solutions are allowed, but it is difficult to ascertain this information without asking them about every question individually.
– The course staff is not particularly responsive on the online forum. I understand that office hours are always an option, but some questions on the forum will remain there unanswered for multiple days, which seems to defeat the purpose of its existence.
– The lecture notes are helpful, but they are a work in progress (i.e. they contain some errors). Furthermore, it is impossible to download them locally to a device, which means that without internet connection (which occurs very often in the lecture hall or just randomly due to Harvard WiFi), it is impossible to see the notes. The lecture notes exactly lay out the structures of the lecture and are generally easy to follow, but there are almost no other supplementary materials to use in case those fail.
– There are two midterms which are each 2.5 hours, are outside of lecture time, and for which very few PSet accommodations are made. This essentially means there are three final–length exams throughout the semester. Also, on a typical week, a PSet would be due on Tuesday with lectures on Tuesday and Thursday; on an exam week, lectures are still Tuesday/Thursday, the exam is Wednesday, and the PSet is due on Friday, with another PSet due the next week. Generally, it seemed that the course staff was not particularly understanding of how students' workloads increase during midterm exam periods.
– Many of the calculation–based problems on the practice exams did not have solutions, only answers that were simply outputted from Python/MATLAB, despite the fact that it is expected to show all the steps of work during the exam itself.

In summary, I did not enjoy the class. It's the most popular way to fulfill the algebra requirement in the applied math concentration, and that's because the material taught is genuinely interesting and generally useful, but there are a lot of logistical improvements they could have made, and it often seemed like there was an excessive amount of busywork.
