This course is an incredibly unique window into the theoretical underpinnings of the design questions and decisions behind the programming languages we interact with. The teaching staff very clearly have a deep understanding and appreciation of the course content, and I always found lectures to be very engaging and thought–provoking
Take it, if you didn't take 51, very reasonable replacement. Lots of fun.
Lectures are a bit dull but assignments and material is very engaging and fun. TFs are pretty slow with grading assignments so don’t expect much feedback
This class is weird in that few people felt excited about the material, and the course staff felt a bit disengaged. Nonetheless, the material is somewhat interesting, and it will satisfy various CS requirements. Overall, I found it quite dry, and the psets were difficult in unsatisfying ways. There's only 6 of them though, so the class doesn't have a super high workload, but it isn't light either. Be comfortable with proofs, try to get some OCaml basics in (like matching) if you haven't done CS 51, and make heavy use of office hours. You'll encounter in detail some things you've likely heard before, like the Y–combinator or Curry–Howard correspondence.
This course is what you make of it. If you put more effort into it, you'll get more out of it. Going to sections and office hours is tremendously helpful; talking to Nada will help elucidate concepts you may be struggling with. Although CS51 is "recommended but not required," you definitely need strong OCaml skills in the early assignments. Either self–study some concepts or meet with the TFs throughout those problem sets. Otherwise, the strength of the lecture notes carries the course.
This a great, easy–medium difficulty CS class about a really cool and underappreciated area of theoretical CS. If Amin is teaching it, beware that lectures will probably be hard to follow. I stopped going to lectures eventually because I didn't think I was getting anything out of them. However, the lecture notes are great and the teaching staff is wonderful. Would recommend!
Probably should have a wide knowledge about programming languages to be top performers in the class!
The class is great, however it is very theoretical so be warned. If you are not a theory person you will often be overwhelmed by all the notation. The class is interesting since you are exposed to the theory behind many different language paradigms. I would recommend this class if you are interested in theory.
This course is a relatively low workload CS course, but still not low workload by any means. Lecture notes are great, though lectures are not particularly engaging or entertaining, and it is difficult to grasp concepts in lectures often as a result. Exams are all multiple choice, and pretty difficult. Although many topics discussed in the course are not particularly difficult conceptually, be prepared for a lot of confusing notation that is hard to interpret and makes learning the concepts much more difficult. Overall, the course does need a change in teaching style, but otherwise, it is a relatively decent CS course for those who want to learn more about the semantics of programming languages, evaluation, evaluation methods, as well as a lot about lambda calculus.
This class can be difficult, but reading the notes and attending sections and office hours can definitely help with completing the assignments and learning the materials. Plus, the generous amount of late minutes are a lifesaver.
