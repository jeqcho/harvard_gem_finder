This is the best class at Harvard.
There was a brief period before I took this class when I did not possess the sum totality of human knowledge. In those formative days, I made mistakes, just like you. I wandered the streets, coding for cheap thrills, dereferencing NULL pointers in front of the police and daring them to arrest me. Of course, now, because of this class, I do possess the sum totality of human knowledge.
This course is challenging, but Professor Mickens is great lecturer and is extremely approachable (and relatable), that plus a supportive course staff makes the huge psets bearable. You definitely have to manage your time well, as the bulk of the course is working on the 5 psets, all of which you usually have 2–3 weeks to work on. This course also builds strong community between the students.
This class was my first time being below–average in a course, ever in my life (even after 61, 124, stat 110, 111, 123), even after trying so hard (30+ hours / pset, passing all tests, a few days of preparation for each examination). So, there's that. Be ready for the ego blow, possibly. But I would do it again.

In this class, you put your blood, sweat, and tears into your work, and then it comes back just "meh." It's hard.

Much of it is probably unnecessary. Do we really need to implement buddy allocators to understand modern computer systems? Or multithreading ourselves? Or file descriptor tables? Or the /dev/null file? Probably not. I have probably already forgotten most of it.

But other parts I hope will stick with me for a while: basic principles of virtualization (the VMware story was pretty epic), GPUs and accelerators (although I wonder how TPU's relate), the Unix philosophy (files, files, and more files?!), the way that if something CAN go wrong in a system that it will, Mickens's humor.

It was incredibly refreshing, and I cannot overstate this, to––after spending about a year talking about technology companies with people mostly interested in finance–– to be surrounded, again, by people who actually know how things work and can speak in a langauge other than marketing and investor relationships bullshit. That may have been my favorite aspect of the course. The knowledge that I learned in this class cannot be taught outside of it.

Like the Dalai Lama probably said, the lower way to learn is through books (the lowest is marketing). The higher way is through experience. The highest way is through meditation. In this course, you'll need meditation to design systems that you'll learn through experience. That's pretty beautiful.
Please understand that if you take this course it will be your life. Dont be fooled by the lowballed hours on the q–guide. I honestly barely did anything in my other classes compared to the hours I put into this one. With that being said, if you have the time to put in, you will learn more in this class than any other computer science class.
Take advantage of the professors insights and the office hour times.
it's hard, but worth it

Take it only if you feel like you have the time and mental energy to get through it (ideally the rest of your classes should be relatively chill). After you take this class, you'll have a much better understanding of how computers work from the ground up and will be much better at working with a large codebase/debugging. The course covers so much in–depth technical material through the lectures and problem sets that I not only learned a ton about operating systems and computers work, but my programming and debugging skills improved a ton as a result. I also spent up to 20+ hours per week on some weeks working through some of the assignments, particularly when synchronization became a thing, which might be nice to take into account. But if you're mentally prepared to sacrifice a part of your soul for a semester to learn a lot about applied computer science, this is the perfect course for you. Plus Professor Mickens is the most entertaining lecturer I've had yet in any class, and the TFs are absolutely cracked.

This course is a very intense operating systems course and the pace is quite fast. The problem sets are a bit time consuming and I would recommend that taking them very seriously from the beginning and aiming for elegance in your code. Some spaghetti code I wrote in the beginning of the semester haunted me for the rest of the semester, so take your time to make your code clean and bug free earlier on in the course.
