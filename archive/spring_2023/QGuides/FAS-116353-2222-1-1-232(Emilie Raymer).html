Absolutely take this course! One of the most interesting classes I have taken at Harvard and class discussions are great. This class is definitely self selecting and the kids who take it are really interested in the material. I learned so much about biotechnology and was completely exposed to new studies. This class is definitely self selecting. Also, for an expos class it is on the easier side and Dr. Emilie Raymer is super kind and helpful.
Take this class if you have an interest in biology and a desire to improve your writing. Even if you don't have an interest in biology, this class is amazing and it helps develop your technical writing skills. There is a strong emphasis on moral and scientific writing––distilling complex ideas into easy–to–understand sentences is a skill that you will work to develop in this course. The class can get quite engaging and intensive, but if you put in the work, do the readings, and coordinate with Emilie, I guarantee you will come out a more confident writer and a more informed citizen.
This course is excellent for stem majors because it is relevant to what they study while remaining non–science–oriented. It delves into the ethical issues surrounding the use of science technologies, which I found fascinating as an MCB major.
I actually really enjoyed this expos class. I was a bit afraid in the beginning since the rumors are that everyone dreads expos, but I think the particular set of students in this class made the discussions super interesting and enjoyable. The material Emilie provided was very well selected, and at times, especially in the first unit, the readings were dense, but ultimately what you put into the readings is what you get out of it, so it's up to you. Expos is not too bad, only the three weeks of drafts/final essays are a bit more work. The material of this class was on synthetic biology and bioethics, and as long as you have interest in the topic it'll be a great class. Emilie is super nice and very lenient and always there for questions.
This is a great class for people interested in the relationship between ethics and science. You don't have to be really good at science to take this class because the science aspect of the class is very basic. There are also really interesting documentaries and group discussions in this class.
Informative and fun class that is centered around synthetic biology (eg: gene editing, de–extinction of woolly mammoth, etc), it helps to be interested in the topics before taking the course, but doesn't require and prerequisite knowledge. Dr. Raymer is very accessible and gives great feedback on drafts/final work to help you improve.
This is a great expos section and important opportunity to learn about some amazing things happening in modern science, if you let it be. Others here have noted and will probably continue to mention the philosophical focus of the readings and material. I am of the opinion that the course is all the better for it, because these perspectives help one better understand why, exactly, developments are being made the way they are and thus actively participate in the complex debates guiding scientific progress. Dr. Raymer is also perceptive and incredibly kind and understanding. If critically thinking about the state or future of science sounds interesting, choose this section. Be prepared to stay on top of things to engage with the material, but doing so will change the way you think, and there are few things I would trade for an experience which does that.
Take this class if you're interested in synthetic biology, its applications, and/or its ethics. The class is so interesting, and Emilie knows what she's talking about. In the beginning of units, readings can get dense and heavy but as long as you understand the main gists of the major readings, it's not too difficult and you can sometimes get away with not doing a few readings. Every assignment that isn't a paper or a presentation is ungraded, so that makes everything super low–stress (at least in my opinion) and assignments never take more than 30 minutes to an hour to do after class. Emilie is also super lenient with deadlines, for both assignments and papers, and she gives amazing feedback. Writing the papers themselves was honestly not bad, but that's only because I really enjoyed the class topic and what I was writing about. I honestly believe that as long as you pick an Expos class with a topic you like/a topic that GREATLY interests you, it'll be easy for you to do well.
Please take Engineering Life and rank it first! It is a fantastic Expos course: a clear opportunity to write and explore concepts at the cutting–edge of synthetic biology and ethics. Emilie cares deeply about the course material, which she's curated carefully, and she is well–equipped to teach it. She is always available to support outside of class, gives great line–by–line feedback, and works with you to help improve your writing. I strongly recommend this course to anyone with a passion for life sciences.
I have to say, I am glad I took this Expos course. No, writing papers is not the most enjoyable activity for a STEM major, but we have considered and discussed some very important and interesting topics in this course. For those who love science, I would recommend this class even if you are hesitant about bioethics. You will certainly learn a lot!
Loved the class. Discussions were very interesting and timely, feedback on essays was good and workload was not huge
Take this class! The teacher is amazing and the course is well designed to help you develop your writing.
