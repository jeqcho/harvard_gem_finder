YES!!!
Unless for whatever reason you have a burning hatred/disgust for anything related to constitutional law, I would recomend this course to anyone. I noticed very few q reviews going into this when I signed up for the class, which I think is criminal because of how (in my opinion) this is a fantastic expos. I'll give a rundown of why you should take this course.

For ppl interested in gov/law/sociology/anything related to this field: as someone who was interested in pre–law yet also severely lacking in knowledge about conlaw, this course was so engaging and interesting. Ross strikes the right balance between making sure the basics are covered about differing theories of interpretation, the general facts of landmark cases, etc. but also providing nuanced views/takes about cases. There are some in our class who clearly know a lot more about US law/conlaw than I do/most people, and Ross both speaks to their level of understanding but also by no mean forces the class to operate at their level of understanding. The course, I think, sparks interest in law at all levels. I also appreciate that the law we learn is not insulated or theoretical, he also makes sure to tie it very closely to real–world events and current events.

For ppl who don't care about law: despite the fact that we will reading exclusively written court opinions on cases, every discussion we have on a case is related quite explicitly and meaningfully to non–law topics (policy, race, sociology, history, gender, technology, science, etc.) . The final essay project gives you freedom to not be restricted to a legal or constitutional–history analysis of cases. It could be a gov/policy–type paper, it could be a paper about, it could be a sociology or philosophy type paper, etc. The cases are wide–reaching in their impacts, and if you want you can totally use them as gateways into less law–restricted papers. The other papers we do before the final paper are not as generous in their scope, but they also definitely do not need to be law–analysis papers. (think more like an english/history paper where you analyze the author's biases or theories, etc.)

For ppl who care more about writing skills: Ross is a preceptor who genuinely cares about your writing skills. He makes himself as open as possible to read theses/arguments you send him over email, to schedule extra conference/OHs, etc. He puts a lot of time into giving very specific and follow–able advice for your first draft, and he also ensures you get a conference with him after. Despite the intense focus on law, you also aren't restricted at all (or even encouraged) to learn to write like a legal analyst or gov concentrator. I think that because we aren't reading novels or essays that we are often more accustomed to from high school english classes and because we are given a new kind of writing (SC opinions/case writing) that is new to us, the course really pushes you and helps you find writing skills (analysis, communication, developing arguments, etc.) that help you in any kind of writing that involves convincing a reader. Ross cares more about understanding your audience, developing arguments that work and don't work, and understanding authorial/your own biases–––which are all applicable no matter where you write. I think it may be pedagogically better that we were forced to use different texts that we were used to so that we weren't limited to following formulaic, discipline–specific habits (like a high school English analysis paper) so that we could think more consciously about what we're writing and why we're writing. For your final paper, depending on what field you want to place your essay in (legal analysis, polisci, history, more literature–based, scientific, philosophical, etc.), Ross will help you craft your writing style/arguments to something that is more suitable for those fields too. I've learned a lot of specific skills and internalized specific goals that I've seen as problems in my writing for quite some time now and that I have seen myself improve on/can see myself further improving on in the future.

For ppl who just want a gem/don't want to get grade–curved: this course is not grade–locked, so he does not limit the amount of As or Bs in the class. In terms of reading load, some cases can be definitely tough and dense, but compared to other courses I've heard about, it's not as much. it's 1–2 cases per week, no quizzes or anything like that, discussions to ensure that you know what's going on, etc. Honestly, the good thing about reading court cases is that there are tons of summaries about the cases you read which will help if you find the texts hard to read. The amount of non–essay assignments are limited, honestly (which I like). Ross isn't an easy grader per se (like he won't instantly give everyone As on your first assignments), but as long as you keep in communication with him you'll do fine. Despite your first impression of him, also, Ross is just an incredibly funny guy who makes sections a blast even if you find a certain class or discussion to be a drag in terms of content.
