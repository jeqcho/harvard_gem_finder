Take this course! Some of the concepts involved in it are analytically challenging, but the teaching staff is excellent, and the things you'll learn about the non–objectivity of science and numbers is I think critical knowledge for any citizen of the modern world.
class was easy. The material can be a bit abstract but section is very helpful in gaining a deep understanding of the core concepts. Lectures are a bit unnecessary in terms of what you need to know for the course as are the readings. However, both the readings and lectures are still pretty interesting. So, while they might not be necessary I'd still suggest you go, but tbh I stopped going to class towards the end of the semester and I was still able to do all of the assignments. The only real work you have to do is 6 300 word responses relating to the concepts you're learning at the time. There's also a midterm paper, final paper and final presentation but they all build on one another leading to your final paper. The class overall is interesting and will definitely make you look at numbers differently.
This is a great class, particularly if you are interested in thinking deeper about numbers, models, data, and quantification! I felt supported throughout the entire learning process.
Discourse is definitely worth taking it for your STSGEN Ed requirement. Not only is the teaching staff over qualified and incredibly engaging, but the course has a clear goal of making the students critical thinkers without getting them and necessary busywork. The assignments are genuinely thought–provoking and oftentimes enjoyable. The final project receives so much support from the staff that so long as you choose a topic you’re interested in (they’re flexible as long as it’s applicable to the course) — you will probably honestly enjoy it! Def recommend.
Take it! It's a really cool course with a manageable workload.
This is a fun and easy class with interesting topics and engaging discussions! I highly recommend
Given that a large number of Science and Technology in Society geneds at Harvard are centered around biology and/or medicine, this course was, overall, a refreshing find. The workload is incredibly manageable, and while lectures and readings might not always be the most engaging, you will learn a lot of practical analytical skills. Assignments are very open–ended, so you can really investigate whatever subtopic interests you most, a key component of a good gened in my opinion.
I've been struggling to find a gened in this category that I might enjoy, and this class ended up being a great pick. I especially enjoyed the assignment structure, which allowed us to work towards a final project. It did feel a bit like being in high school, but overall a great introduction to the discipline of STS (Science, Technology and Society).
This class has a really interesting premise — it's hard to go a day without encountering various numbers, all of which are created/produced/interpreted by humans in some way, so understanding how these numbers are constructed is important, especially when society values them as "objective." However, I think I was expecting a class that tried to give a broader overview of numbers, rather than one that focuses in on the "crib sheet concepts" and essentially spends most of its time teaching you how to apply the ten or so crib sheet concepts to various numbers. I remember there was one guest lecture where a professor from a different university touched on a version of the course he taught as his school — a course that included concepts like correlation vs. causation, how numbers are intentionally distorted, and many of the topics we covered like black–boxing and social constructedness of numbers — and it seemed more meaningful than the one at Harvard.
