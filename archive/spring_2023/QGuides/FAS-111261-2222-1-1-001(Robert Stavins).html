I would recommend the course, especially since it counts for the AM/Econ track with less intense math than some other courses. It is unlike any other course in the department and Prof. Stavins is a genius in the field, but is still a good teacher, unlike some other Harvard professors. I will admit he is sometimes a little crude and can be abrasive, especially when someone is having trouble with a concept in the whole class, but the TFs are more than willing to help explain after class or in section. It is not a hard class compared with other Econ classes however, so if you enjoy a more policy side of econ, this is the class for you.
Professor Stavins has decades of expertise and has worked with policymakers in many different governments. Go to his office hours and pick his brain!
Definitely take this course! I learned so much and the teaching staff was helpful and great communicators
I would definitely recommend enrolling in this course, especially if you are looking for an interesting and relatively painless way to fulfill an economics elective requirement. The instructor is great, he has great anecdotes from his firsthand experience working in climate negotiations and policy, he is also dry and entertaining to listen to. The PSETs and exams are all very fair, and the course staff provide lots of practice materials and detailed answer keys to assist with studying. Be warned that the exams are a significant portion of your final grade (75%) if you do not like testing under pressure. Overall, very solid and tangible course in the department that genuinely only requires EC10a to succeed.
This class was a great introduction to environmental economics at a time when climate change issues are extremely pressing.
The two textbooks are really interesting and pair nicely with the class!
The course content and delivery is extremely old–fashioned in its dependence in economic theory to the point that it has clear holes in the realistic application in the policy sphere including the information on the treatment of poorer countries in international policy, the realistic implications of current carbon pricing policies and their actual impacts on mitigating climate change, and the treatment of scientific concepts and progression as concrete and unchanging. Further, student disagreement and questions are largely disregarded and dismissed because of the siloing of policies into the lens of classical economic theory.
