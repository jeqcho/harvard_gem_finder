It would be helpful to know dynamic optimization ahead of time (I managed without it, but it meant I was a little behind for Laibson's portion of the class).
If you are an undergrad taking this class be prepared to do a lot of learning on your own. Overall, the psets and final exam are not any more difficult that 1011 but there is less support than you would find in an undergrad level course—the TFs are great, though. Start the psets earlier rather than later so you can go to office hours.
This class is pretty good! Read Kahneman's "Thinking, Fast and Slow" –– if that intrigues you, then you'll probably enjoy the class. The class begins with Kahneman and Tversky (heuristics and biases, prospect theory) and quickly moves to more recent developments and applications. Some of these are fascinating: modelling stereotyping using the representativeness heuristic, salience theory, the role of memory and attention, and quasi–hyperbolic discounting. The class is also very chill –– there are psets due every other week, which are a bit long but oh well. I cannot comment on the final (worth 2/3 of the grade) since I haven't taken it yet. Shleifer (leads 2/3 of the lectures) is funny but speaks a bit slowly, which sometimes caused my attention to drift. Laibson (1/3 of the lectures) is a great lecturer. Both professors are very nice and approachable.
Take it! Andrei and David are great!
If you are an undergraduate thinking about taking this course, make sure you have a good group of friends to complete problem sets with. You do not need the recommended prerequisites; as long as you have some general intuition and comfort with math, you are fine. David's problem sets are very difficult, but he releases solutions before the deadline, so you can use those to guide you if you really have no idea. The final is hard to study for; work through the math on the problem sets slowly and make sure you really understand the psychology bits presented in lecture.
Undergrad here: this an amazing class—2030 feels like what economics *should* be. The portions on memory / attention are incredibly deep. Do take if you're interested in behavioral economics—it can be challenging at times but course staff is very helpful.

While not hard pre–reqs, having taken Math 21a, Stat 110, Ec 1011b can be quite useful for the PSETs.
The course material was excellent, but it is definitely a difficult course. Expect to spend significant time on problem sets and preparation for the exam.
Really liked this class! Excellent for anyone interested in the fundamentals in behavioural economics –– this class covers the history, theory, applications, and psychological underpinnings in the field, giving students a strong survey of the state of the discipline.
