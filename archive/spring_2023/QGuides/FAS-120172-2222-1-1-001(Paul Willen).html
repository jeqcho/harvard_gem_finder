I took this class my first–year spring, without having taken Ec 1011a in the fall. My relevant background includes Math 25a in the fall and AP Econ in high school.

First, I think that Ec 1011b is more of a math class than an econ class; most of the class focuses on manipulating mathematical models of the economy using algebra and taking derivatives. For the level of math required, Math 21a is recommended, but it should be doable without knowing any multivariable calculus because almost all of the calculus in this class is taking partial derivatives (I personally took Multivariable Calculus simultaneously with Ec 1011b, and was fine even though I used partial derivatives in econ before I learned about them in math). But math is a central component of this course, and expect to do a hefty level of math.

However, the thing with the class being mathy is that you can get away with a good grade without having much of an economic intuition for the models; aka you can do fine without understanding why certain things are true about the models (ex. why is this investment function decreasing with interest rate?) I personally had this issue in the beginning, so if you find yourself being fine at the math but not really understanding why you're doing the math, I recommend reading the textbook and asking your TF/Prof for intuition. They are very helpful, and make sure to ask very basic questions even if you think you'll look stupid (because you won't look stupid, actually), especially if you didn't take Ec 10b (I asked a lot of these). Having a strong econ and math understanding of models will make you appreciate the things you learn in this class WAY MORE, and I think you'll find econ to be super cool :)
