What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Ben will make you a MUCH better writer. His comments are extremely thoughtful and directly useful for both the assignment and writing in general. He takes extreme care in everything he does and I love the readings he chose. This class will absolutely be a turning point in terms of my writing ability. This is, however, a very theory heavy class and not always the most stimulating in the moment if you aren't intrigued by the subject matter. Take this class if you want to be a better writer!!!!! From what I have heard about other expos classes, I definitely got a lot out of this one in comparison to others and am emerging as a much better writer.
I would tell future students in this class to do all of your reading beforehand, and more than that, create a summary sheet where you note the main argument and premises of the text that you read for that class in an extremely structured and concise way. It will help you massively not only in the context of the discussion, but also when writing your response papers, as there is an expectation for you to be extremely rigorous in the way that you deconstruct the arguments you are critiquing/ extending. I would also recommend you discuss your paper ideas with the preceptor prior to writing them, perhaps in office hours, as he is extremely good with offering ideas, and making you question aspects of your argument. Don't be afraid to have slightly non–conventional contributions to the arguments made in class. This will be appreciated, so long as you are argumentatively solid!
Make sure you're interested in civil disobedience as a topic. Class can be pretty slow sometimes, but I thought the topics of our discussions were interesting. The grading is a bit rough, but I think that's true for many expos classes.
Very philosophy heavy
If you're interested in philosophy but don't have a ton of experience, I wouldn't necessarily recommend taking this class since it could end up being very difficult and stressful, but if you love this topic then I think it is a fantastic class, so it really depends on how much you like philosophy and civil disobedience.
The course materials are very interesting, but be prepared for some theoretically–dense readings. Otherwise, the course is very interesting and engaging.
This class is particularly rewarding in that the content is very interesting, and it can provide great exposure to philosophical writing on civil and uncivil disobedience. There was not a reading in this class that I did not enjoy. Every reading in this course was insightful, engaging, and I had a lot of liberty, in many ways, in what I was able to write. However, I would warn students that the grading in the course can be rather harsh, especially considering that the course does not provide rubrics for assignments, aside from (extensive) feedback. If you are looking for a challenging, yet rewarding course, this course is a great option. I love this course, but this is definitely a class that requires twice as much effort to get the grade you desire.
I had really mixed feelings going into this course, and my feelings about it were periodically rocky during the semester, but now that it's finished, I feel strongly hat it was a good decision. Not many people rank this section highly in their Expos ranking survey, I think because it's more rigorous than other sections, but I'm really glad I ended up in this course. First, the readings are awesome. My friend once asked me what we'd read so far in my expos, and I started saying "Plato, Gandhi, Kant, Rawls, Arendt, Thoreau, Malcolm X..." and another kid turned around and asked what class I was talking about, and I said "Expos," and he was like, "WHAT? How is that an Expos class?" Seriously, the readings here are I think way more canonical and cool than those you'd do in most other Expos sections. Second, Ben is totally awesome. He's not exactly the person who's going to rally the troops for battle, or get you super excited about philosophy if you weren't already, but he's extremely smart and clear–thinking. His commentary is spot–on, he's a great discussion leader, and his comments on paper drafts are really thorough and useful. I never felt like anything he asked us to do was a waste of time. He's clearly thought a lot about good pedagogy, and that really shows in his course.

Expos is something you simply have to take, and you can't get out of it. One way to deal with that fact is to put in as little effort as possible so that you can spend more time on things you want to do. But another way to deal with it is to accept that, if you have to do Expos, you might as well work hard on your papers and try to get a lot out of it, and if that's the approach you want to take, this section is for you. I feel like a much better and clearer writer thanks to this course, and especially Ben's guidance. I'd strongly recommend Civil and Uncivil Disobedience.
