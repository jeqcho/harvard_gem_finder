This class will really expand your views and make you a lot more openminded. It's very interesting and Professor Harris is a great lecturer with an awesome sense of humor. However, the readings are really long and sometimes very complicated and honestly I didn't do all of them.
Take this class, you would most likely use the insights from it at some point.
Would highly recommend if you're looking for a gened that actually has some substance and doesn't just leave you with "this problem is important!" yet also allows you to deprioritize it when more pressing academic commitments get in the way.

This class could change your life if you give it the time to do so. But more likely this class will give you interesting readings to gloss over portions of when you're in the mood, good opportunities to dive into one or two thinkers you find particularly interesting with the papers. You can generally leave feeling like you were able to extract as much meaning/purpose from the class as you had the time for. The structure of the course and its grading is forgiving enough to make that possible, lectures are a really good opportunity to get a broad overview of the work and its most popular academic interpretation as well as answer some of your questions, and sections let you think about how to square the text at hand with your own/others' moral frameworks.
Do. the. f******. readings. And ANNOTATE! Take notes! It'll save you so much stress.
Amazing class! It deals with questions that are central to the human condition, and the readings included famous works that have survived the test of time. We read Dostoevsky, Plato, Augustine, Aquinas, Maimonides, Hume, Kant, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Buber, and several others. Some of these books (including Kant!) everyone should read at least once in their lives. While the readings were great, the downside was that most of them were also pretty long. Some weeks we had to read like a hundred pages of some very dense material (again Kant!). So it may be hard to find the time to do all the readings properly. I have to admit that I probably didn't do one third of the readings sorry, Prof. Harris! I am not proud of it. I would have gotten more out of the course if I had read them all, and in fact I actually plan on reading them over the summer.

The readings are difficult and can feel especially hard if you haven't had much experience reading philosophy before, but I think you can get better as the course progresses and you keep getting used to it. I came to this course with no such experience and was struggling at first, but now I can proudly admit that I have gotten really good at reading philosophy (or so I feel). Another thing I owe this course for!

Finally, as to the course logistics, you have to write two papers (6–8 pages each), and there was a take–home final exam involving two short essays. (The final was originally meant to be a three–hour sit–in exam where you would have to handwrite two essays, but Prof. Harris forgot to file the form with the registrar in time!) I was really nervous at first about having to write philosophy–type papers, but it was a great experience. They give a list of prompts for the papers, but you are free to write on basically anything related to the readings. One thing I did not like about the final exam was that there was no recommended length for the essays (so I wasn't sure how long to write), but the prompts were interesting!

This is one of the few courses at Harvard where the material has felt deeply personal to me, and I always felt like I was engaging with the readings not for the sake of course requirements but for myself. I have been profoundly affected by some of the readings, and I believe if there is one course at Harvard the material from which I will remember a long time later and might be referencing in some late night conversation, it will be this!
This class is superb!! I recommend with great enthusiasm. Professor Harris is funny, engaging, and deeply thoughtful, as he leads exploration of complex moral thinkers and questions. Be warned that the reading load is rather heavy for a Gened, so do not take this class if you want a throwaway gem; DO take this class if you want to make genuine use of your time at Harvard to learn and grow as a human being engaging with the real questions. You will not find a better class in which to do that. As someone who has spent most of my time taking highly technical classes in my concentration, this was the first time I really realized what liberal education was supposed to be.
This class has a bit of reading with complicated themes. However, if you are at all interested in theology or philosophy you will love it. The readings, classes, and sections are super enlightening and entertaining.
God this course is SO good. I had concerns that this would be too much of an "overview" course in that the depth of each thinker would be sacrificed (fyi: i am planning on concentrating in philosophy), but I didn't find that to be true...

With the combo of pre and post reading lectures, in–class lectures, sections, readings, and discussion posts, if you are willing to put the work in, you will mine a TON of meaning / info from this course, and be forced to reconcile with many challenging metaphysical and existential questions that will surely permeate into your life even outside of the classroom!

I'll admit that I skimmed over a lot of the texts themselves, but I spent the time that I would've spent engaging with the text in rapt attention while watching the lectures (pre/post and in–class), and I honestly think for a philosophy overview course with as ambitious of a scope as this one, there is probably more utility in watching those lectures than in puzzling over the more rhetorical, structural, nit–picky points about how each writer actually presented their thoughts on the page....
