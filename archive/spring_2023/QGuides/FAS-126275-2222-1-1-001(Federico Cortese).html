What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Take this class only if you are prepared to put in the time commitment. Also the learning experience is frustrating at times but the experience is rewarding.
This class is rewarding not only for future conductors, but also for any musician curious about understanding the process of CRITICAL listening and analysis with symphonies and operas. This class is not unnecessarily laborious; as long as you put the time in before class to understand your intentions with the pieces, you will thoroughly enrich your musical skillset.
I would recommend this class if you are definitely interested in conducting. You will need to take initiative to learn a lot of the technical aspects on your own if you're not already familiar, but Fed is great at inspiring students on the artistic and musical side.
You do not have to have conducting experience. Seriously, genuinely, you can take this class without having ever held a baton before, so long as you are willing to put time and effort into learning the basic beat patterns and such. Do not be intimidated by a lack of experience!

What you will need is a very strong background in classical music, in whichever instrument you play or in opera. I think this class probably had the highest concentration of Harvard/NEC or Harvard/Berklee people out of any class at Harvard... and you will be expected to play the repertoire when you are not conducting (for pianists, this means the piano reduction). You will likely be stressed a LOT for this class. On average, we were assigned one symphony a week + 2/3 arias, and the expectation was we should know the piece well. Fed will call you out for faking it/not preparing enough before a class! I really wish I took this class with more time in my schedule to prepare each week; I tried to compensate for my dearth of score study time with listening to the rep as much as possible, but it definitely showed.

With all this said, I don't regret taking this at all. Maybe some of it is the masochistic classical musician energy, but for all its intensity and stress, I feel like I came away with a lot of knowledge on how to get better in the future (though I am unsure of how much I have actually improved in one semester). I would suggest, if you are on the fence about it, to try taking this as a 5th class, and stay or drop in the first 5 weeks after you have a better sense of what its like. In fact, I really, really strongly suggest this instead of going in based on expectations of previous years, as the class changed quite a lot to accommodate this year's group and I'm sure will do so again, and also because there are a number of factors people of different backgrounds may experience differently. It really is not for everyone, but honestly if you're still considering it after reading this you're probably the type that should try it.
You will learn a ton, and get what you put in. It doesn't take too long just genuinely engage with the scores before coming to class and you will get so much more out of the class.
I would definitely recommend this class to students who are serious about music. It was definitely tough to learn to conduct movements and arias from different symphonies and operas every week, but I ultimately gained so much knowledge over the course of the semester. You get so much valuable knowledge from having the perspectives of Fed and Yonah, and you can definitely apply a lot of it to honing your own instrumental/vocal skills.
