Take it if you wold like to relearn how to approach science. Everyone is really encouraging, and the workload is progressive so that science is not that intimidating
Definitely take this class for your science divisional distribution requirement! I had very little science experience before this class, but found it accessible and interesting. Irwin is awesome and clearly teaches this class because he loves it and loves engaging with students. The assignments are light, but you definitely should study for the exams. But I think I learned a lot and came away with a positive experience from the class. We were on Zoom the entire time which was a little unfortunate, but I didn't mind too much and still enjoyed it. I imagine it's even better in person!
Great class for non–STEM students.
This course is directed at social science students looking for a non–intensive science distribution class, and I think it succeeded on that account. If you are an Astronomy or other STEM concentrator, I would not recommend this course. It is a historical treatment of major advances in science rather than a true science class. It is also not a gem: While the course is fairly straightforward, it required a lot of work (almost too much work, I think).

In terms of the course itself: Previous Q reviews felt this course was disorganized, but I didn't particularly think it was disorganized; in fact, I thought it was very clearly organized. Irwin was a good lecturer although I think the course would have been better with in–person demonstrations (it was remote this year); I didn't feel like I got to know my classmates because it was remote, even though we did have in–person sections. In terms of section, we mostly just recapitulated points from lecture, so I thought it was a missed opportunity for more in–depth historical and ethical discussions (even though we did have a couple).

Overall, I think this course would have succeeded much more in my book if it were in person, and if it continues to be virtual, I would recommend other options for us Social Science concentrators searching for a distributional requirement class, particularly Biology of Plants or Astrosociology.
