What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Be prepared to do lots of work and learning on your own.
Definitely take this class if you want an easy A where you can choose to put in a lot of effort to learn loads of neuro or just sit back and still do well
Material: 10/10 (Super interesting and very interdisciplinary)
Lectures: 7/10 (The slides sometimes are no concrete enough)
Grading: 10/10 (They value the effort incredibly and have good feedback)
Slight warning; one of your group members (you are split into groups of 2–3 and make a project biweekly; however, having someone who *really* knows how to code makes life very easy—otherwise doing the projects will be seriously difficult and time–consuming).
Conclusion: This course shows you how modeling the brain and human behavior using code is a very achievable thing for undergraduates, which is an incredibly unique experience for Harvard's theoretical realm! Take it if you are interested in anything related to behavior and if you ever wondered how we could code that.
Having a good partner/group is crucial. I felt my grade and performance coincided with the capacity of my partner just as much as it did with the capacity of myself. Your group can make or break your experience in this class, so make sure the group you are assigned to at the beginning has members that each cover a different skillset – mine was more in the expression of ideas whereas my partner was more technically–skilled. We balanced each other and it worked, but had one of us been working without the other the class would not have gone as smoothly.
Sam is an absolute beast in terms of raw neuro knowledge; lectures pack a ton of info and offer a great survey. Overall chill class and you learn a ton about neuroscience (that is not just bashing matrix calculus in the same way Neuro 231 might be). The hands–on aspect of the class is also very nice; similar to real research, you read papers and reimplement them (i.e. code up the results).
Would definitely recommend it if you have some interest in learning to think more quantitatively about the brain! Very chill in terms of grading, definitely a very weird class structure that I didn't think was super conducive to learning—for example, we were given a paper or two to read before most classes, but we never discussed our own thoughts on the papers or even discussed the papers at all. The lectures all felt quite disjointed, and the projects were only slightly related to the readings. As for the projects, the directions and expectations are incredibly unclear—was super stressful at first, but then got the hang of it—but they were great experiences to become a better programmer communicator of science.
