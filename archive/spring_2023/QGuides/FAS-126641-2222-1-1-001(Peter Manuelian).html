Would not necessarily call this course a gem, but I still enjoyed learning about ancient Egypt; would have appreciated more support/guidance on the final project
Enjoy it!
Walk into class prepared to ask questions and not necessarily get direct answers, for sure. Keep track of the themes that you see within different sections of the class, and compare them to your own life and experience.
Make sure to take detailed notes during lecture since lecture notes and recordings are not provided. These will be useful for the midterm and final examinations.
Prof. Manuelian's lectures are very informative, but unfortunately too cramped and rushed (spend a good amount of time on Old & Middle Kingdom, but rushed New Kingdom and on) because he tries to pack too much content in this course. Because of this, each important figure or object are only briefly mentioned then moved onto the next; sections also don't go in–depth about objects but focus on broad ideas. I prefer quality over quantity of knowledge so this class was not my favorite. Also, this class requires quite a bit of work for a Gened course: there are both final project (8–10pg research paper, 5–min research–focus video, or 3D model of gallery + 10–pg catalog) and a final exam (2 essays that has quite a bit of requirements). This is a lot of work due at the end of the semester!
Prof. Manuelian is a great lecturer and is fantastic at organizing his materials such that you get to learn a lot in every lecture. The class is not stressful or hard, with only little weekly readings and comments, but the exams do require a bit of work even though they are take–home. Overall, this is a gem but also the gened I learned the most in. Would definitely recommend it!

Also, the final exam in additional to the final project was a little stressful at the end of the semester compared to the majority of the workload in the class. Regardless, it was still a very interesting class and I definitely learned a lot about ancient Egypt through it.

Enjoy the ride! It’s very enjoyable if you show up with the right attitude, but can easily become a burden if you adopt the wrong attitude (this is true for most Geneds). Whether you’re a first year taking classes for the first time or a senior that’s going to be graduating and needs a Gened class, or any other student in between, please show up ready to learn. The biggest mistake you could make in this class is just writing it off as “just another Gened” and skipping it or doing other work in class. Invest in your knowledge not for the sake of a GPA or graduation requirement, but for your own edification and development! This course is truly enriching and the field trips remind me about the history and resources we are privileged to have at Harvard. Please show up to class ready to engage with the material. If you just come in to do other work, you’re doing a disservice to yourself and your other classmates who really want to hear from you – plus you’re being super disrespectful to the professor.

This is probably one of the best courses to take to fulfill the histories gened requirement. Coming from a STEM background without much interest in history, I did find some parts of the course a bit boring. But the highlight is that you get to visit museums and see actual ancient artifacts. Even if you aren't a museum–goer, would you rather be sitting in section in a different gened discussing readings that probably no one did? Speaking of which, there are ~100 pages of reading a week, which may seem like a lot, but otherwise there is basically no other work (and if you choose to just briefly skim the readings, then there is basically no work. But I guess I shouldn't condone that). There is a midterm and final, which are just take–home essays, and they're graded generously so you'll be fine as long as you show that you know some things from the course, put in effort, and don't wait until the last few hours to start. The final project can be stressful if you have trouble coming up with an idea, but the TFs will help you a lot with forming an argument and finding sources, so definitely reach out to them.

Lectures can be pretty dry, but the section component is a lot of fun. Lectures are sometimes all over the place so it can be hard to know where you should be in the course. You will get as much out of this class as you put into it.
GenEd 1099 is a great way to satisfy the Histories, Societies, Individuals requirement. If you're at all interested in ancient Egypt, even just a little bit, the class will be fun and you will learn interesting things. The class is also very chill in terms of workload. There are two essay–based exams which require maybe 2–3 hours of prep each, and a final project. If you want to minimize the amount of time you spend on the project, choose the research paper, as boring as it might seem to you. The paper is doable in a day if you try hard, and easily doable in two days. When it comes to regular coursework, you have to submit a response after every lecture which is roughly two minutes of work, and do the weekly readings, which can be long sometimes. It's possible to get away with not doing all of the readings, but if you have time, I recommend doing most of them, as they are pretty interesting.
