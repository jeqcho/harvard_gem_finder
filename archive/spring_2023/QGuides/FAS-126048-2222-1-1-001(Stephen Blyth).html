This is, hands–down, one of my favorite courses of all time! Prof. Blyth's lectures are incredible – he's a charismatic speaker and a captivating presenter. Moreover, as he says, the textbook (which he wrote) is very terse and distilled, so it definitely helps to go to lecture and see the exposition done out. Some of the material (e.g. LIBOR) is a bit dated, but overall it's interesting stuff. Just don't expect to learn how to become a daytrader; this class is more about the math underlying finance (which is quite beautiful math), and it very much encourages not engaging in risky investments.

The assignments are generally of a moderate difficulty – some problems are just hard to start, and that's okay, but most are pretty straightforward. They do look quite scary because of the heavy notation, but go to office hours, the CAs are amazing! The exams on the other hand are quite difficult – doing past problems is basically a requirement, but don't get lulled into a false sense of security. I will say though: on this year's final, there were lots of problems which looked undoable at first glance, but the more you thought about them, the better you saw how to do it. That is, you can just "try things" and get a good answer out of it in the time limit, which in my opinion is the sign of a well–written exam.

Granted, given that Prof. Blyth is going to Oxford, I'm not sure I can recommend taking this class if it gets offered under someone else... it really is his brainchild, and you get the sense of that through the organization and question style. Hopefully they either rework the syllabus entirely, or this will be the last time it gets offered.
