What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Readings are long, hard, and chosen with a purpose. You are not taught how to read philosophical texts: I have never read a long philosophical text before, so being introduced to reading strategies (outlining, annotation, paraphrasing, etc) would have been wonderful. There were many times where I questioned why I was taking the class because the readings bogged me down with little outward support from peers and my preceptor (I was not connected/comfortable enough to reach out, see below). However, they were ultimately interesting in retrospect, and provided the opportunity to be exposed to ideas re: the origin of inequality and our nation's history (and current relationship) with slavery and subjugation of targeted groups.

Sadly, I wish that there was a greater class culture — at the end of the semester, I still felt disconnected to the students in the class. I think a tighter class community lends itself to richer discussion, greater comfort of sharing writing, and better class morale in general. I was enthusiastically hoping to become close friends with people in my Expos, as usually happens in my English classes (by nature of how personal/intimate the act of sharing writing and opinions is).

Take this class if you’re willing to do the readings, not if you want to improve your writing. The class focus is on the content (not discussion of it, but rather your consumption of it). There are reading lectures (and remember that lectures are not discussions).
Take this course. Lots of people told me that Expos was dreadful, but I definitely had an amazing time learning about the Culture and Politics of Inequality with professor Gilbert. The readings are insightful and although dense, very interesting and intellectually exciting. Definitely one of my favorite classes so far.
This class is interesting, and while some of the assignments might be challenging (not least because of the weighty subject material), we are given more than enough time to work on them.
If you go into any Expos course expecting to hate it then you will, but you truly get out what you put in. The material is really interesting, especially once everything is tied together through the lens of the Anthropocene in the end. Do the reading, stay engaged/participate during class, and DONT procrastinate writing.
Definitely consider taking this course if you're interested in the social sciences (government, sociology, social studies, and econ especially) and their application to real–world problems! Readings may be more complex than other expos courses (would recommend looking at the syllabus before you decide for any course), but there's so much to gain from being able to analyze social theory to research–based arguments that will set you up well for the rest of your courses here. I actually enjoyed writing the papers because the subjects were interesting to me (colonialism, neoliberalism, environmental justice, race, inequality, etc.) and I learned so much about them, helping me decide on my concentration for next year. If you struggle to understand something, feel free to reach out to the preceptor and read your peers' discussion posts for more support. Definitely recommend this course for anyone interested in these subjects!
TAKE THIS CLASS! If you are remotely interested in inequality and learning about racism in America, as well as the environmental crisis, this class is AWESOME! The first paper on Rousseau is rough, but if you can get through it it’s not a good indicator of the difficulty of the rest of the course– and it’s honestly really interesting!! I highly recommend and honestly think everyone should learn the material taught in this class as it makes you such a better informed citizen
