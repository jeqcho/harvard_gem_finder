I cannot recommend this course highly enough. Because my remarks elsewhere in this evaluation comprise what I would tell future students about the class, I am providing sections of those responses below, as my response to this question:

This course has too many strengths to enumerate all of them. Professor Reich has done an excellent job in structuring the course in terms of the broad division between the lecture and discussion formats. Professor Reich's enthusiasm for the material was infectious, and at the same time. Professor Reich was also a very clear lecturer, explained ideas as well, and also made clear early on that the course would be technically challenging (more on this later). Professor Reich also took great care to acknowledge and discuss the fraught ethical and political landscapes into which ancient DNA studies are injected. In addition to these, Professor Reich was very responsive to student communications throughout the semester. He was very receptive to student questions in class. Importantly, he introduced students to the difficult and often tortuous landscape of science research and the enormous amount of effort that underpins the movement of research from an initial idea to a published result. Professor Reich will help students learn not only about ancient DNA but also about how science is done. The class covered diverse temporal and geographic scales, and did an excellent job of demonstrating the role of genes in understanding "deep" history and how genetics can contribute to problems of social science (i.e., this is not only a genetics class but both a genetics class and a history class, capturing the core themes and goals of HEB). The opportunity to work with ancient DNA datasets under the mentorship of highly skilled and committed mentors is extremely valuable and will ensure that students leave the course with a strong appreciation of the rigors and wonders of the role of human genes in elucidating the human story.
Don't miss out on this one!
