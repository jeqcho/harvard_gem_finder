I would recommend to take this class to learn more about the hardware and architecture side of computers. We spend so much of our learning focusing on the code that we forget that all of the code is simply a series of logic gates and binary digits at the end of the day. Taking this class opens you up and lets you see this! A difficult but rewarding class experience! I would be prepared to find a partner/group of people to do well in this class –– from studying for exams together to checking over the psets to working on the labs!
This course follows nicely from CS61 and is great if you’re looking to get more intuition for low–level concepts. Workload isn’t terrible but there might not be much guidance on psets/labs sometimes. Most labs are done in pretty basic Verilog, but two labs require some pretty strong familiarity with other languages (Python and C).
Best course I've taken at Harvard. The content is insanely interesting. VJ is an amazing lecturer and professor! The textbook is amazing. The lectures are great and the labs were very rewarding!!!!
Highly recommend this course. It gives you an understanding of how computers actually work, and you end up implementing a microprocessor design! I didn't have any hardware background and none was expected. Manageable workload, less than the average CS class.
Labs are great. This class is difficult but not too much work particularly if you have a good lab partner.
When I started 141, I did not know how 1s and 0s were a computer's language. Because we go from transistor–level logic all the way to building a MIPS microprocessor, we are forced to come out with that understanding. One does not realize how much they learned until the end when it all comes together, so hang in there :).

This is a well–taught and interesting class, which you should take if you care about computers as real devices (as opposed to the abstract "computers are black boxes that run my code" approach). Find a lab partner; it is possible to do the labs alone but time–consuming. Because there is no grade benefit to doing the labs yourself, there is no reason to do them solo rather than to pair up.
the labs are hard, system verilog is not fun

Pretty good class. Definitely not easy, but also very doable. You learn a lot and the material is very interesting. If you take it – READ THE RELEVANT BOOK CHAPTERS CORRESPONDING TO LECTURE (the book is super helpful).
This class covers interesting material, but that is one of the only things going for it. Lectures were completely unprepared to the point of being unintelligible. Professor Reddi has readily admitting to prepping for lecture "on the elevator to class." He spends most of the time asking questions about material to the class, and sometimes fails to answer his own questions. For his last lecture, he decided to scrap the material that he had "planned" and show us a movie instead. Harris & Harris (the textbook) is where the party's at.
This is an interesting class that covers a lot of fundamental material on how a computer works. It builds your knowledge up from transistors all the way to assembly. Labs were cool when done but frustrating to debug (Verilog is not super helpful). A fair bit of work but most of it feels helpful and the material covered is cool.
Just skip lecture and you'll probably have a better experience. The book will teach you everything you need to know (read it carefully, obviously) and then you can just do the labs and enjoy your extra 2.5 hours a week.
Definitely take this course if you are at all interested in or curious about computer hardware. The course offers an excellent introduction to hardware from essentially the ground up, from transistors to logic gates to ALUs, registers, and memory to full–on processors. It's very cool to better understand how basic electrical components can be abstracted into the incredibly complex machines we often take for granted. That being said, the course was somewhat disorganized this year, with assignments often being released late and grades being returned very long after submission, if ever. Nevertheless, the assignments were still pretty good, and the content of the course is quite interesting overall.
If you’re coming in with a pure CS background (high level programming and/or theory), this class may be a struggle for you. I found the lectures incredibly unhelpful – just read the textbook, go to section and work hard to understand the labs and psets and you’ll be fine. This class got a lot better for me when I stopped wasting time trying to understand the lectures. Don’t be afraid to Google stuff too – there are great videos and articles online. Dhilan was the saving grace of this class, and Eryk and Raymond were awesome too, but all of them are graduating, so I have no clue how this class will fare next year. That said, the content is still cool, so if you’re interested, just be prepared to work hard!
It was very neat and well taught.
