The course policies actively work against students. This should not be happening. Some students who missed an exam due to being in covid isolation got to take it in their dorm room with access to lecture notes, practice problems, and the internet which isn't fair for everyone taking it in the lecture hall and compromises the integrity of the exam. Other students with covid emailed the professor and the head lab TF about missing a lab and received no response for weeks until the makeup lab section was announced (with less than a week's notice) and they were expected to drop everything and attend even if they had other classes or exams during that time. The attitude towards the make up lab was preposterous; the Head Lab TF said they only offer a make up since it is university policy and if you are unable to do the 4 hour (or more if you missed multiple labs) make up then you get a 0 for that lab. There is also so much hypocrisy and a lack of understanding built into the course policy about dropping problem sets; you can't drop a problem set, you can "make up" the point of a problem set since every problem set gives you the opportunity to earn 11 points but they are graded out of 10. This policy is impractical since students can't be expected to get an 11/10 on every problem set or even most problem sets and the graduate student TFs are such hard graders that getting high marks is nearly impossible. The professor emphasizes prioritizing mental health and yet tells students who struggled on Exam 1 to spend their spring break studying for Exam 2 and is unwavering when students ask for some lee–way when submitting late problem sets. Late problem sets receive 50% if they're late within 24 hours and 0% if they're late after, even if you are struggling due to external factors out of your control and notify your TF about these struggles. Additionally, I don't know if the PSLs receive an answer key for the problem sets or not, but they should, since I've received "help" from PSLs several times in the past which was later marked as blatantly incorrect on my problem sets. Either better PSLs need to be hired or they should receive an answer key for the problem sets in order to best assist students. Thoughts on Chem 20 vs Chem 17:
– Chem 20 was very well organized and the teaching was very coherent; I've heard the same is not true about Chem 17
– Chem 20/30 is considerably more difficult than Chem 17/27: Chem 17 claims to cover the material in Chem 20/30 in one semester, but topics are taught much deeper in Chem 20, especially topics related to synthesis (which is practically all of organic chemistry). This class also tends to attract the chemistry and chem/phys concentrators as well as people with previous chemistry olympiad backgrounds, while Chem 17 is mostly pre–meds
– The grading for Chem 20 is less focused on exams than Chem 17 (where the only grades are from exams), but the grading in both is still very exam heavy (~70% vs ~100%)
– The lab component of Chem 20 was not very enjoyable – lab write–ups were excessively long, pre–lab quizzes had almost no relationship to the readings, and the lab staff was much more disorganized than the teaching staff
– All around, if you're looking for a difficult but very interesting and well–organized class with a great group of people and a great teaching staff, Chem 20 is the way to go. If you're just trying to get organic chemistry out of the way, Chem 17 might be a better option
