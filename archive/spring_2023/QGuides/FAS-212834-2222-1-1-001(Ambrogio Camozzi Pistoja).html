This course is interesting, but it is a significant time commitment for a GENED. Although the syllabus and requirements may seem demanding (and sometimes they are), the teaching team is very flexible and accommodating. You will be able to take the assignments in the direction you want!
You take what you put into this class. It is enjoyable, but can be a lot of work from time to time – just be prepared to be relaxing completely one week and be buried in work on another.
The readings in the class are at first very dense, but they get better over time. Professor Pistoja's lectures are interesting but can get boring. He's genuinely passionate about this topic. Michael Mango is a great TF and makes section engaging. The assignments are short and doable; they are either creative assignments or short reflections on the readings. There is a quiz, which was a bit weird but graded leniently. The midterm research paper is not very difficult, and the final project is a creative assignment. The class is overall a good GenEd and would recommend to anyone who's interested in satire.
The energy is going to shock you at first, but you're going to love it. Don't be afraid to get personal and write what you want to write for the creative projects. Express the ideas you want to express, and they'll help you make sure they're good enough to get the A.
This class is not for the uptight; the syllabus will change unexpectedly, and the criteria by which you are being graded will be unclear. With that said, the grading is lenient enough that this is fairly irrelevant so long as you have the capacity to channel some time into this course every once in a blue moon without notice. Professor Pistoja is a chaotic force of a teacher; his lectures are exciting, even if you won't always be sure what they have to do with the readings that you've completed in advance. This class is more oriented towards theory and classical satire than contemporary satire and comedy, so be prepared to tackle more Dante than Hannah Gadsby.

Absolutely recommend.

I came into this class with very high expectations, as I am someone who is very interested in not just writing and reading satire, but understanding the function of comedy in society. Unfortunately, I will be finishing the course very disappointed, as I felt like the question that was posed as central to the course ("Is satire a dying art, and do we need it?") was hardly addressed. The texts for this class were extremely limited in scope, which was very disappointing, because satire was not solely created by and for old white men. Professor Pistoja explains this away by saying countless works by women and authors of color were likely destroyed in their time, which may be true, but that does not account for why the vast majority of the contemporary texts with which we engage are also created by white men. If you are thinking of taking this class in the hopes of learning how to write satire, know that creative assignments are few and far between, and our largest creative assignment put us with a random group of section–mates to write 5 pages of coherent satire in 3 days. Prompts for major papers are extremely restrictive and reinforce my belief that this course is extremely eurocentric and is more focused on examining classical satirical works on their own rather than trying to connect them to contemporary satire. I'm really sorry to say that I would not recommend this class to peers, as I really wanted to love it.
