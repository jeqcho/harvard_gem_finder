This course is a huge waste of time. Do not take it. The upside is that there is no lab or section and that the material is objectively easy. But lectures could not be less engaging, interesting, or attention–grabbing. What's the real bummer is that the content itself is actually interesting (you'll find this out when you have to teach yourself all of the subject matter the night or two before the PIEs because it's impossible to learn in class). The case development is possibly the most inane insensitive project I have done at Harvard. You have to pretend that you are an individual with a chronic/genetic disease and make a story about it. It's as wild as it sounds. If you're looking for a 1.5 hour study hall 2x a week – this course is great for you. If not, look elsewhere.
great course & the best way to fulfill the intermediate bio req for MCB in my opinion! the only assignments are basically the PIEs and your final project, so it's pretty low workload.
PIE's can be pretty detail oriented, don't miss breakout questions, read the syllabus and course info carefully and make sure you're up to date on everything.
It is in a transition period, and the PIES can be graded a bit harshly, but overall an engaging class.
Not a very difficult class to do well in, but not always the most fulfilling experience
Prepare to immerse yourself! The lack of problem sets and section are deceptive, but the course material requires thorough engagement in order to truly get the most out of the class! If you do engage deeply with the content, though, it's a great opportunity to learn more not only about the specifics of disease and biological mechanisms, but also the ways in which they interact with other disciplines (as a social science concentrator, this was great for me!)
The course gets a bit easier over time, the beginning of the course is heavy on research paper discussions which you'd have to prepare for ahead of time, which can be very time consuming, though the number of discussions lightens with time. The earlier you work on the project the better, try to be creative and do lots of research.
You may not have PSets but you have exams literally every other week (we had an exam the Thursday before break AND the Thursday right after) and end with essays disguised as "personal reflections" (the first one is entirely guess what the professor wants to hear and fairly obvious as that). The case development stuff is worth the majority of your grade but there are no examples, the feedback is so slow its essentially non–existent, and the rubric is vague and uploaded very late.
Unfortunately, this course has way too much neuroscience which is taught quite abstrusely. However, the rest of the class is relatively nice and manageable. The PIEs are way too much work, but the final project is quite fun!
This course is in need of some major updates. If you've taken any of LS1a, LS1b, or MCB 60, this course is not for you. For the 80% of the class that had taken one or more of these, we spent most of each class learning biological concepts that we had seen before or that a well–read person would have learned about on their own. For example, we spent a lecture learning Mendelian and non–Mendelian inheritance again, and deeper dives into how cell biology influences people's lives was mostly in the form of the same canned case studies that all biology classes use (genetic engineering, Covid, etc.).

A lot of people ended up taking this class after not getting into MCB 68, but if that turns out to be you, it's probably better to just wait another semester and take a different MCB 60–level class. MCB 64 was probably much too large this semester: the discussions are intended to be seminar style with small groups where everyone can provide their thoughts, but in a class of 80 people, the instructors posed open–ended questions to the whole group ("What are some of the ethical issues surrounding genetic engineering?"), eliciting the same old responses ("this technology could enable a new form of eugenics") from the same handful of people kind enough to keep the class moving. There was practically no back–and–forth discussion, and any small group discussions were aimed at answering some biological question from purposefully incomplete data.

The exams (the only assignments besides the case study) were pretty easy, but this isn't necessarily a good thing. The exams asked detailed questions that required little critical thinking and encouraged copying down the lectures notes onto a notecard and regurgitating the same material only to forget it a day later. When the exams are this easy, basically the only differentiation in class grades comes from who happens to write down some key word or phrase, not who knows the biology.

Additionally, the grading scheme for this class is a little bizarre: all the tests together account for 20% of the grade while 70% of the grade (in what is in principle a STEM class) is a case development assignment that requires more creative writing than it does research or biological understanding. Because this case development is so backloaded, the class finished in May with 30% of the grade in the gradebook and the rest still to be determined, so people have practically no idea how well they're doing in the class.

These do not really determine your grade, which depends more on the character development portion of the assignments. Be careful to attend class and answer the breakout questions on Poll Everywhere while logged in, because those are 10 points of your grade!
