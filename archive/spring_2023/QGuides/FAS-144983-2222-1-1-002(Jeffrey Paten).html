What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Ask lots of questions and make sure you understand what the expectations are. Be ready to put in a lot of time.
Hope for a good topic to work on, because you likely won't do much "real engineering" in this class. It's more about learning how to view a problem from a few steps back and gain a system understanding.
The difficulty and quantity of work are extremely dependent upon the professor that teaches the course, so do research into who is teaching your section.
Experience in this class is entirely dependent on your section leader. Some projects/instructors will have you spending weekends and consistent days in the SEC while others will only expect 5–10 hours a week. Definitely ask seniors about who to go for and try to talk to instructors to get into their specific project.
Try to attend the same section with your friends, preferably those outside of your concentration, because a big part the class's grade is on how your teammates view you.
Please, please do not go into this believing it will be one of those hand–wavy no–test classes. Depending on the project and professor, it may well be the most time– and energy–intensive course you take at Harvard as an engineering student. However, the most intense projects tend to be the most rewarding and the most camaraderie–building. Don't take criticism personally, and be sure to organize yourselves into teams with clear team captains early on!!
As an engineer, you must take this class, and honestly, it's a big hit or miss based on the project you get and the professor you have. My response here only reflects my section as I was not involved in the others who, from what I heard, had a very different experience.
Overall, I heavily disliked how this course was structured. Although there was a rough syllabus, it was very vague and the course never referred to it along the way. In a client project with major stakes, there were no concrete milestones and our progress always felt like we were walking further into a dark tunnel with no clear end deliverable in sight. I have personally worked as an intern at several companies with different corporate environments and cultures and a major theme from them all was that milestones of accomplishment were a key factor in making my experience as a team member enjoyable. In ES96, however, I often felt unenthusiastic to do the work because of this and only worked out of necessity rather than motivation. This class should be a challenge but it also should be an enjoyable learning experience but ended up being an unmotivating grind. I also felt that I was working for my professor rather than for the client at all times. This class should teach us teamwork and collaboration rather than how to appeal to the thoughts of a professor. As a future student, it is imperative that you focus your group outside of the professor since that is what holds the team together. The professor is there to critique milestones, not to critique each class, and it is important to ensure that happens – my team needed more independence and I should have argued more for that. We also got into the core part of the project way too late into the semester and wasted too many days on the problem/missions statement and the stakeholder map in the beginning so definitely get those each done in one day (all it should take). This added a ton of time that could be used in the end when there is real work to be done. Overall, you will learn a ton from this class, but your experience and whether you enjoy the day–to–day is up to the project and the professor you get. Be prepared for a busy end of the semester and a grind.
Choose your path in the beginning. Either just don't care about the course, or make sure your teammates also care when you start work on the project.
You have to take it but make the most of it. Your group relies on your effort and it can be rewarding.
