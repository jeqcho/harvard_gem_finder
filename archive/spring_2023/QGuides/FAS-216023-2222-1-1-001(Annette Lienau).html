What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
I would highly recommend this course to anyone looking to understand all the complexities of colonialism and post–colonial states. Great selection of readings, though the workload can be a lot sometimes. Essays and grading are very reasonable.
The readings are great and will teach you so much – taking this class is a great way to get a taste of a range of really important postcolonial texts, and putting them into conversation makes for an insightful and fascinating learning experience. The actual class seminar discussions though weren't the most fulfilling. It often felt like more of a lecture, disguised as a seminar class. The Professor is really passionate and knowledgable about the course materials/subject matter and it is a pleasure to hear her thoughts and listen to her, but often it felt like the course became a lecture, and the discussions were not very student–driven. I was disappointed in the quality of the seminar discussion due to this (it was also a lot of students talking to/at the professor instead of talking to/with each other...) and honestly, didn't really look forward to this class every week, as I have with other similar seminars.
This course is a really good survey of a lot of important writers and concepts, but it also allows you to drill down into specific areas of interest. I highly recommend!
I would definitely recommend this class to other students who are interested in this course and its subject matter. Professor Lienau is an incredibly knowledgeable and encouraging individual who is open to providing of a space of intellectual intrigue in her classrooms. It was a pleasure taking a course with her, especially given her own scholarship on Indonesian and Egyptian texts and their relationship to colonial and post–colonial realities.
