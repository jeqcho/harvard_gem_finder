What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This course is a decent option for 97. But like all the 97 sections, the pacing will be challenging and stressful. The readings are very interesting and there is generally a good flexibility in range of options for the prompts you write on. Intellectual history will give you a good range of freedom when choosing your area of study for the research paper, as in, you should be able to write about anything of major interest.
This is a HARD class !!! But as hard as it is, it will truly make you a MUCH better writer, reader, and historian. And for that, I think it is definitely worth it. It is a very well structured course, that has obviously been developed over generations of students and their feedback. There is a LOT of readings (do them and PARTICIPATE in class!) especially at the first half of the semester but it cools down in the second half where the focus is on writing the final paper. You will do a LOT of writing but it will make you a much better writer. History97 has much more rigorous methdological training than many other equivalent concentration sophomore tutorials in social sciences and humanities.

Get started on a final paper topic ASAP – feels scary to decide so early on but it will be enormously helpful. I think the best thing to do is build off an idea from a previous history class you took or a book you've read or some topic of interest/expertise that you are deeply interested in. It will inevitably shift and develop over time but it is good to have a somewhat clear general idea from the very beginning.

Prof Blair is incredible! Despite being the Chair of the History Dept and being super busy, she dedicated so much time and effort into teaching us. Her weekly emails, her lunch out at the end of the semester, her generous office hours, her prompt email replies – I don't know how she has the time but you feel that she really cares for you. Her attention and care to the students is almost unmatched from my experience.
This is a great intro to the history discipline and historical writing. However the grading process is pretty painstaking, there are very stringent requirements for how to write, and often hard to understand how to earn a better grade on revised versions.
Professor Blair's Hist97 on intellectual history works like a clock–each piece fits perfectly with the others and constructs a magnificent whole. From the wide–ranging readings to the extensive feedback students received, the course provides the perfect introduction to historical writing and intellectual history. While I wish that I could take every Hist97 offering, I would not exchange this experience for any other. If you have the opportunity of taking Hist97 with Ann Blair, I would urge you to do so.
This course allows you to see whether the History concentration is the right fit for you. You will graduate from this rigorous but ultimately quite fulfilling course with an understanding of what it means to study history, and precisely what history is.
Be passionate about what you study.
Intellectual history is great because it's super versatile. As someone who came into the class with strong preexisting historical interests, I found 97b very helpful for taking a step back and situating my own work within the field. Aside from whatever you learn from your substantive research topic, probably the most valuable takeaways are having read a smattering of canonical/exemplary literature (and thereby becoming acquainted at a high level with scholarship on important areas like American revolutionary pamphlets, causes of the French Revolution, etc. etc.) and being able to speak and write somewhat knowledgeably about the broader landscape of the historical discipline. Some of the history–of–disciplines stuff feels a bit too meta and navel–gazey, but it is what it is. The required Maza reading is excellent and genuinely quite enjoyable.

Assignment structure is weird at first—for the first paper you're asked to make an argument about intellectual history as a discipline using just secondary "framing" readings, without actually having done any substantive work, so it can feel unhelpfully abstract—but things eventually settle into a standard seminar paper. Ann Blair and the TFs (Sergio and Sophie for S22) are incredibly caring, down–to–earth, and supportive. The Hist 97s are famous for being writing–intensive and rigorously graded, but 97b feels pretty reasonable on both counts. 97b also seems to attract joint concentrators and people with very diverse research interests.
I strongly recommend Intellectual history. You will gain a deeper appreciation and understanding of methods and styles of studying history which you can use for your other history courses.
Intellectual history is a dry subject at best. It's poorly suited as a 97b because the assignments are standardized but intellectual history is not like other kinds of history. The diversity in the class is likely to be bad. Know that you'll be writing a lot – and come in with your topic beforehand, especially how you're going to find primary sources. Switching topics is tough since it moves at such a fast pace. Ann Blair is a great prof though – she really cares about you.
This is a transformative class, for concentrators and non–concentrators alike. You get invaluable instruction and feedback on assignments, you come out with a sense of how to really do proper historical research. It puts the discipline in context, and helps you understand its limitations and its strengths. And it invites you in the history department – which is the place to be!!
