This class is lovely and a lot of fun. You can get a lot of exposure to new topics and ideas in astronomy and in sociology for those who are more STEM based. The instructor and TFs are very fun and put together great simulations and activities to apply lecture topics.
I loved this course as a social sciences student, I was pretty intimidated by the other offerings in the SEAs department. But, Astrosociology hits the perfect balance between science/social science/humanities. I thought that it was incredibly accessible and interesting for someone with a non–science background. Moreover, I appreciated how the course took a discussion–based and hands–on approach when it came to both lectures and projects. If you're remotely interested, would absolutely recommend taking this course!

I felt at many times there was no structure to the class. For the majority of the semester there were no lecture and instead we spend class time "debating" one another over various topics such as "should we communicate with aliens?" and UFOs. This debating really just turned into people yelling at one another and that lacked structure as well. Much of the class deviated from the readings and actual discussion other than a rushed group presentation that is to be done in class once a week. There was barely any instructions to the assignments. If you are truly passionate about the material, this is something you should definitely (it is not a huge time commitment). If not, it will feel like a huge waste of time –– which of course can be said of all classes.
Even as a student who was afraid to take a STEM course after three years off from anything involving science or math, this course was too easy. I do not think I learned anything that will stick with me. That said, Prof. Sonnert is a wonderful person and made my day brighter, so I'm grateful for that.
– Fun class
If you are someone worried about fulfilling the SEAS distribution requirement because you are not STEM–inclined, take this course. The workload is incredibly manageable and the concepts explored are fascinating. You can look at space through any perspective you want, whether that be social sciences, humanities, or otherwise. Additionally, students were given incredible latitude for the final paper. You can even write a sci–fi story if you want. In sum, take the course. Just do it.
I would def take this course! It is such a gem for the science divisional distribution requirement, there are no psets and only written assignments and presentations. I also was very interested in the material itself –– understanding the possible outcome of the discovery of aliens is very intriguing and I loved the course material and lectures (especially the guest lecturers from NASA).

My only qualm is the start time of the course: 9 am. I and many other people had trouble waking up that early so if you are not an early bird as well, please consider if you can wake up on time. I missed so many classes and wish the start time was later.
What you get out of this course depends on how much effort you put into it. The readings aren't essential to do each week, but make sure to show up to class and participate, because that is truly where you will get the best experience. Prof. Sonnert is passionate about this field and that will make you interested in it as well. Mia was a great TF!
This is a great class if you are a humanities/social science student to get your science distribution out of the way. I didn't think it would be an issue for me, but I often found myself missing class or zooming in. I think the Prof. said he was going to make it start a bit later next year, but just something to keep in mind. You get the most out of this class from the discussions and in–class activities.
Professor Sonnert is amazing!
