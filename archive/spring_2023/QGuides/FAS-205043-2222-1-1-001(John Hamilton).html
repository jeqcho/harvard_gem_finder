If you're at all interested in German literature, the Faust legend, salvation, predestination, or––frankly––even if you aren't particularly interested in any of these things, take the class, full stop. You will not regret it. Hamilton is one of the most incredible professors I have ever studied with–– the breadth and depth of his knowledge is truly something to behold, and you will be the better for having gleaned some of his insight. His enthusiasm for literature and the humanities broadly conceived were wonderfully refreshing and have inspired me to maintain my passion for these works throughout my life. I should also mention that he is very willing to engage with students and is––in addition to being a brilliant scholar––an especially kind and generous mentor, so if you do take the class, take advantage of the time and try to get to know him a bit, because he certainly seems to be invested and interested in getting to know students
If you're interested in taking a literature focused class, this is a great one!! The texts are very interesting and you'll get a lot out of reading them and thinking about what they have to say. Prof Hamilton is an excellent lecture –– he is always very prepared, clear, and accessible. It's a lot of reading to stay on top of.
This is one of the rare occasions where I enjoyed the course much more than I can express in words. The readings themselves were rich with wisdom and creativity. They were often the lifework of the authors and it's evident that the magnitude of each work goes beyond much of the literature we as students normally read. Each literary work and the connections to one another gave me a much more profound understanding of the world and especially of German culture. I did not expect to learn so much about music theory, religion, etymology, and history but it was a welcomed addition that always elevated our understanding of the texts. Before taking the course, I was only somewhat familiar with the Faust legend. I did not realize how deeply this legend is tied to German identity. It explains Germany's historical journey, and we specifically focus on its connection to WWI and WWII. Moreover, the Faust legend captures something so innately close to our humanity that the questions raised and the works based on it illuminate much about ourselves, our purpose, and how to make sense of humanity's trajectory. I have always loved literature, but this course took it to another level. I felt so transformed by this course and it left me thinking, this is one of those courses you dream of taking. This is a class is the epitome of what makes Harvard an untouchable academic experience.

Professor Hamilton is an amazing lecturer –– very humble too –– who can keep you captivated for the two hours we meet each week. Every lecture is like an intricate story that ties together knowledge from all disciplines to explain and analyze the text at a foundational level. It's beautiful to see how literature and the decisions made by the authors evolve from one culture to another, across time periods. I still remember our first class, when Professor Hamilton began by explaining the difference between literature and philosophy. Literature is for you to interpret. It doesn't retain a concrete meaning. It is meant to evolve, and it will connect to each individual uniquely. I have taken philosophy classes before and he is exactly correct. This class embodies an openness to ideas, no matter how unusual or unpopular. Unlike philosophy, literature doesn't have rules nor does it tell you how you should think. The joy and curiosity I thought I would find in philosophy, I actually found in literature. With every essay, you can feel free to write wherever your connection to the literature takes you and Professor Hamilton is always supportive and encouraging of your interpretations. Sometimes, it felt intimidating to have to write essays after hearing Professor Hamilton's lectures which expertly made connections you can only make with his breadth of knowledge and after spending years ruminating on the literature. I truly was in awe after every class because his revelations about the text and about themes would be things I've never heard or even considered before. Oftentimes, perspectives and interpretations on common motifs are repeated so you hear them in one class and then another. This was completely different, like studying literature for the first time again.

Having said this, please view the lectures as an incredible educational experience that should only inspire you to reach to produce your best work in the course. I would encourage you to challenge yourself in finding new connections, still, or ones that weren't touched on much in class. It's not easy, but it will be rewarding when you prove to yourself that there is no ceiling to literature. No matter how much you think you have learned and understand, there's always more to discover. Interestingly, the course structure doesn't appear to be heavy on workload or reading, but the beauty of only assigning work that is necessary is that we have the time to discuss and reflect, reread and do our own research. I spent considerable time outside of class thinking, talking about what I read, and over time that relationship with the text allowed me to make the most of each essay I eventually wrote. The course also isn't a writing course –– it's not meant to nitpick on writing skill/style –– but I felt a natural progression in improving my writing style because the material we wrote about has such gravity to it. I'm left with a greater sense of awareness of who we are and why we do what we do in addition to a deeper connection to humanity of the past and present. This course ultimately made me decide to continue studying literature at Harvard and the lectures and readings are things I will be thinking about, fondly, for years to come. No matter what your background is, if you are curious about humanity, its relationship to nature, and the ever–present balances in life –– if you are willing to dedicate yourself to the readings with care and attention –– I recommend this course with the greatest enthusiasm.

This is a deeply engaging, inspiring, and fun class. In it you engage in a close reading of three very influential texts based off of the classical Faust legend. The narrative and thematic content of the works also have a great degree of philosophical import; Professor Hamilton does a great job at drawing out philosophical themes from the dramatic or novelistic framework of each text. There is also a substantial amount of music involved, particularly in the context of Mann's Doctor Faustus. In addition to discussion of the musical components of Mann's work, an in–class student performance of a Beethoven Piano Sonata was quite stimulating way to engage with the musical themes and motifs that pervade the novel. Overall, a phenomenal class!
