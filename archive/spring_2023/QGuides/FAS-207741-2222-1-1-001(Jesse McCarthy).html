If you're interested in James or Baldwin or just gender, politics, American history, race or sexuality, this course could be really good for you. We got to explore big questions and themes particularly relevant to race, gender and sexuality in America while reading great fiction. The readings were very well picked: besides reading novels we also read non–fiction, which I thought was helpful in giving us insights with which to analyze the fiction. It was great to discuss the books with students who are interested in the same questions. Not only did the readings and class discussions help me understand American literature better but they also triggered personal reflections that helped me question some of my opinion and form new ones. The instructor, Jesse McCarthy is really great, super knowledgeable, and very available to meet students outside of class.
Take this course if the reading is attractive to you, then do the reading. This is the kind of course to do because you like the actual process of reading; it gives you the opportunity to slow down and do the damn task. You will not be held accountable to doing said reading, so you must derive the motivation for doing so from your own self. That said, take it. It involves a level of sophistication, rigor, and beauty that few classes at this school do. It's especially a chance to engage with old–school canon stuff –– James –– without being constricted by cringe, old–school, apolitical thinking.
Not quite a gem. The readings will take you a while, but if you can kinda read 300 pages in a week, it'll be a really easy, really fun class. Always interesting and worth getting up at 9 am for.

If you think you might like it, you probably will. On top of that, I'm going to paste my answer to another question, "In your opinion, what preparation or background is necessary to take this course?", because I'm not sure the answers to that question are published in the Q Guide:

You need to really like reading, and you need to be––how to put this?––capable of reading difficult prose. Baldwin's style is pretty accessible, but James's, notoriously and especially in his later period, is not. I would recommend that anyone interested in the course take it, but if you're on the fence about whether you have the right preparation, try out James's essay "The Art of Fiction," with which the course (or at least the iteration of the course that I took) begins. If you find that manageable, you should be good.

It makes me feel prepared to tackle reading any author like Henry James and James Baldwin.
