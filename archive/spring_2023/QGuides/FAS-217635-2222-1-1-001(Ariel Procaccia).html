This class is great if you are interested in the mathematical and algorithmic side of voting and allocation rules. The content is very mathematical and theoretical, so don't expect much or any historical/social discussion of democracy. Also, the problem sets are purely mathematical/theoretical, and there is no coding involved in the course unless you chose to code in your final project. Problem sets are few but challenging and very time–consuming, but there are no readings. Professor Procaccia is an excellent lecturer and very open to student discussion, so if you want to interact directly with an expert in this field, this is your chance.

Ariel is also an amazing professor and lecturer! His effort in making the course, from inviting guest lecturers to creating the lecture slides, is so so appreciated. I had so much fun taking this course! Would highly recommend checking it out.

This is the only CS class I've taken that made me feel more educated about areas beyond CS. I learned a lot about the trade offs involved in designing democratic systems. That being said, the models used in class can sometimes feel a bit divorced from reality, and some of the more proof heavy lectures could be a bit dry. I found the problem sets frustrating at times, but each time I finished one I felt like I better understood the material.

Overall, I'd say take this class if you're interested in the use of algorithmic analysis to understand democracy, but be prepared to see lots of proofs in lecture and write proofs on the psets.

Not only is Procaccia and amazing and incredibly engaging lecturer, he has also structured the course so well such that it is interesting, accessible, and non–stressful. This class was one of the best I've taken at Harvard – you can't do better than Procaccia's lectures, with his well–curated curriculum and slides that are always filled with examples and fun. Procaccia clearly has put a lot of thought into the best way to present his material, and it is concise, effective, and engaging all at once. If you're considering the class, I will say that the mathematical requirements are nontrivial (there are a lot of proofs required and psets generally have a lot of notation and can be difficult), so it is likely necessary to take a proof–based class prior. Having CS124/121 knowledge will be extremely helpful, in addition to proofs.
