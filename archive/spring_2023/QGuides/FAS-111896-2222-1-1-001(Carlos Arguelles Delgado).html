15b is the hardest of the Physics 15s, but that does mean that it is terribly difficult. There won't be many concepts that are very difficult to grasp; rather, the difficulty is that the class moves very fast with concepts that build on one another. Don't be scared to take this class, but just be prepared to be on top of your work.
This is a great course! All of the content is very interesting, problem sets and exams are challenging, but interesting and generally reasonable, and the demos are excellent! PSI lab is much more related to the content of the course than it was for 15a. The textbook is very helpful and clearly worded, particularly when it comes to the example problems. Make sure to work in groups on the problem sets, and go to office hours and physics night!
Learn multivariable before, go to Office Hours early in the week and start the problem set early

Take some multivariable calculus before this class. It would definitely help a lot in not only keeping up with how it's used in the course but also help you understand the concepts more intuitively. The core goal of the course (deriving and understanding Maxwell's equations) heavily relies on this.
This is a pretty good class, but not up the same standard as 15a in my experience. Lectures are reasonably good, although I miss Morin's lecture notes with gaps to fill in, which certainly feel like they would be a major boon to a class such as this where you can easily get lost in what's going on, as some lectures move fast/ jump topics a bit. The lab component is far better than 15a, and doesn't feel like the same complete waste of 2 hours as in 15a. Section and office hours are both pretty good, there aren't that many of either though so best to find a group to check your work with. The midterms were pretty forgiving with more than 3 days to do them plus bonus point problems to cover any mistakes or main questions that you couldn't tackle. The final was brutal in my experience, I've since seen last year's one and that seemed much more reasonable so mileage may vary on that front.

I now understand how to work with vectors a lot more than I did before, and I now know how to use Mathematica.

I have no issue with hard homeworks, but I wish we had gone over problems of the same difficulty in class as preparation.
The more work and time you put into this class the more you will get out of it.

This class is very difficult (especially if like me you take multivariable concurrently), but it is very fascinating and rewarding. The demonstrations in each class period are also very cool, and the demo guy was a great storyteller (if you have him). Main advice: 1. find a pset group, 2. go to section. Even if you can do the psets by yourself, checking with other people and making sure you can explain why you did what you did is crucial. Section is also where I actually learned most of the material, because in class you "learn" it but just from that it's often hard to apply. I guess instead of section you could do the textbook readings, but I much preferred attending section. The exams are difficult, but having them as take home was great (though you should manage your time well if you want to have a weekend). The final was very difficult but it was actually interesting and fun because of the types of problems given, and it also shows you how much you've actually learned even when it feels like you know nothing. (But we really all do know nothing. Physics is crazy!) If you're interested in physics (or if you like to see a shower of sparks at the end of class) I do believe you will enjoy this course! And make some great friends! Good luck!!
