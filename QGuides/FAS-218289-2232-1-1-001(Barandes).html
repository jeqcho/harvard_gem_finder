THIS CLASS IS WONDERFUL. If you are at all interested in taking it, you probably should. Definitely take it if you are interested in learning about our current best understanding about the nature of reality and how the world works. You will get to learn quantum mechanics in an understandable and digestible way. This is a gem at Harvard; not in the sense that it is easy, but in the sense that it is such an amazing and wonderful and fascinating course that you won't find anywhere else. Additionally, you'll read some of the classic papers of the field, written by the founding fathers of quantum mechanics.
You will also get to learn from professor Barandes, who is the best professor I have had at Harvard, and probably objectively one of the best. In the end, you will not only have taken a great course and learned a lot, you will also have a fantastic set of lecture notes that you can turn to in the future if you ever forget anything or want to review the content.
The course is not very hard if you are comfortable with math and logic (having taken calculus is probably enough). If you are more leaning towards the philosophy side, it won't be bad. Professor Barandes tries to make the class very accessible, even for people who are not in a STEM discipline. You'll just have a to work a little harder on the PSets, which are primarily composed of (relatively straightforward) math problems.
Cool class, you get to read original papers on quantum mechanics, its basis, and implications. Really incredible instructor that's passionate and good about teaching, always willing to answer questions and really understanding. Interesting homeworks that are reasonable amount of work and always interesting/doesn't feel like busy work
This course was amazing! I saw another Q–Report that said that this course is what courses should be like at Harvard, and I completely agree. There are not many places where you can learn about the history and foundations of Quantum Mechanics in this way. I think this course also gives a very good opportunity for students not studying physics to have a chance to engage with the subject in a deep, but approachable and supported way. Professor Barandes is an amazing professor who brings so much passion to the subject, it is so worthwhile to take a class with him, even if you're not studying physics.
This class is great but it is more work than you’d expect. Jacob is an amazing teacher and the lectures are very interesting but the readings can be pretty dense. The homeworks also take longer than expected and might be difficult if you do t have familiarity with math and physics.
This is the course you take after 143A to make the best of your experience. It seemed like we were doing some basic and very abstract probability and linear algebra in the first half but then we quickly transitioned into talking about quantum and I was lost. I also think that the class was unnecessarily math heavy.
This is rather unique class that changes in every iteration. I would recommend speaking with Professor Barandes prior to registering just to get an idea of what the class will cover. However, if you find that the topics align in any way with your interests, please take this class. It shines light on a lot of background material that isn't normally taught to undergraduate or even graduate students studying quantum mechanics and really puts the idea of quantum mechanics itself into perspective. It's a wonderful course that has left me with a much better fundamental understand of how quantum theory works and the state of quantum theory today. Professor Barandes is both an incredible instructor and a wonderful human being, and he is always more than willing to answer any questions. Highly recommend.
This class is SO cool, but it definitely requires a high level of comfort with abstract mathematical ideas and notation. Lecture can be a little bit hard to follow sometimes if you've never encountered the topics we discuss, so definitely try to brush up on the basics before starting the class in case you are also coming from a place with little background knowledge. Jacob is an amazing professor who will really walk you through classical and quantum theory over the course of the semester, and having a good understanding of these abstract topics will definitely make lectures more valuable. Problem sets tend to get progressively more difficult with the weeks, so office hours is a great resource to have your questions/confusion clarified (and just to discuss quantum theory more generally). I definitely found this class challenging, but it was also incredibly rewarding to watch my understanding of quantum theory improve with time. Beyond just improving understanding of the foundational components of quantum theory, this class also intermixes philosophy in a super thought–provoking way (also – make sure to go to section for more philosophical discussion!). I think this class is super unique, and if you are at all curious about quantum theory, this class is a fantastic opportunity to learn more about it and the historical/philosophical context of quantum theory's development!
chill class, Jacob is amazing at teaching
I highly recommend this class. The way quantum mechanics is presented in this class is so different from anything you'd see in an intro quantum mechanics course. It presents a new framework for thinking about the subject, and one which makes the very weird and unintuitive processes like measurement much more transparent. For anyone who has taken Physics 143a and felt like things were inexplicable and weird, take this class, because the class will question those ideas directly. But also if you haven't taken Physics 143a, I think there's a lot to be gained by being introduced to quantum mechanics in this way. It was really cool seeing familiar ideas pop out of the math in such a different way. The lecture's are very clear, and the material is really fascinating.
Cool class, can be slow for those with math/probability/physics background, but the readings and discussions make up for it.
