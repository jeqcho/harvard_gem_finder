What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Take it if you want a deep anthropological dive into Africa, esp the DRC. Don't, if you want a more standard economics course that reviews the literature properly.
I expect the course will be heavily teacher–dependent; I had a visiting professor.
A very unique opportunity to learn first–hand from one of the few experts in the world on political economy in early–state societies, especially his focus area of the rural DRC. Not only is Raul an academic genius on early–state political economy, but he is an incredible professor who seeks to demystify academia and form empathetic human connection with everyone he meets. This course covered far more than economic development — it discussed its corrupted international history, intersected development with socially embedded institutions in local cultures, connected broader trends to the complex individual stories that constituted them, and challenged dominant narratives by offering sometimes eye–opening, sometimes questionable, and always discourse–widening alternative evidence. This has been an absolutely incredible course and I cannot recommend it enough for anyone interested in economics beyond dominant theoretical narratives, anthropology of communities forgotten by Western academia, or the individual people and communities who interact with “international development.”
This class is phenomenal and you should absolutely take it. Professor Sanchez de la Sierra is brilliant and kind, and this class pushed me to think about topics and aspects of development and decisionmaking that I would never have thought of otherwise. It's a low–stakes class with an emphasis on exploring other conceptions of societal development and organization that the mainstream, and there was a lot of focus on how to be good researchers and academics along the way, which was much appreciated. Could not recommend more!
This course is both a reasonable way to fulfill the econ department's distributional requirement and a good course for those whose research will touch the topic of political economy and development.
