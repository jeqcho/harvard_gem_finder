i would take this class just for the field trip. you can take it even as a non–bio concentrator, like one person in our class did. the field trip is absolutely incredible– 5 days of snorkeling in gorgeous panama, seeing another whole world in the water and all the life that lives there. one of the best things i've done at harvard and honestly one of the best weeks of my life. however, this class requires a lot of time, and so you will be spending a lot of your semester with the people in your class. an entire week basically 24/7 and then 7 hours a week of class time during the semester (this class doesn't require that much time outside of class although studying 30 min most days would def make things easier. i don't think anyone did that though.) by the end of the field trip i was ready for it to be over, ie i couldn't spend another day in that class environment. gonzalo is kind to everyone and he is a good teacher, but he has favorites and that favoritism is very obvious. if that would be unbearable to you, i wouldn't take this class. the course content is phylogenetics heavy and the lectures can be a bit obscure and general. lab is awesome, tons of live animals
Gonzalo is awesome, and a great teacher. The class is hard because there is a lot of material, but that is to be expected. The exams are tough but fairly graded: he understands that it's impossible to memorize all the anatomy of the lesser phyla and therefore they focus more on higher–level concepts. I definitely learned a ton from this class, and the Panama trip is one that I am so thankful to have gone on and that is one of my high points at Harvard. That being said, the people in this class really make or break it. You will spend a lot of time with them (~7 hours/week in lecture + lab, plus an entire week on the fieldtrip) and by the end, I had had enough. Gonzalo is really kind to everyone and he wants everyone to succeed, but he also clearly has favoritism towards underclassmen (2 people literally joined his lab by the end of the semester), which made all the seniors in our class feel a bit left out, as though we were being discounted as not being worth investing in from the get–go. But again, the opportunity to snorkel in Panama and live out my marine biologist fantasy was one I will never forget.
TAKE THIS COURSE! Yes, it is difficult. Yes, you will want to pull your hair out at times because you can't remember which phyla have protonephridia. BUT! This is one of the best courses I have ever taken, if not the best. The Panama trip is amazing. You'll see and catch animals you've never seen before. You'll be able to observe them in the lab and maybe even try to make sea urchins reproduce. I don't think a course has ever given me so much knowledge. The course requires attention and dedication, but if you put in the work it will be one of the most rewarding experiences of at least your college career.
This class is awesome and wonderful but is also a huge amount of memorization for exams
If you need a mid–level OEB class and you are willing to put in the work to care about these underappreciated animals, this class is amazing. If you want to coast through a semester, do not take this class. There is so much material, but it is so manageable to learn. It is so cool. I never expected to go to Panama, snorkel twice a day, collect animals from PHYLA (phyla) that I had never heard of before this class, work directly with 7 experts in the field of invertebrate zoology, and see animals many invertebrate zoologists have never seen in a mid–level OEB class, but this class gives you that chance. Gonzalo is the most wonderful professor I have had at Harvard — he is genuinely so invested in all of his students, in all of these animal groups, and knows so much. Yet, he never comes off as talking down to you or anyone else and gives so much to his teaching. Arianna killed it this year, given she had enormous shoes to fill. If they are both offering this class next year, you should take it.
If you take this class you are sure to walk away from it with a greater appreciation for the diversity of life. Assignments are infrequent (just weekly labs, two exams, a 5 min presentation on an invertebrate of your choice, and a week of field work over spring break) so keep up with the material on your own time. I found myself coming away from class excited to tell my friends all about the new invertebrate phylum I learned about that day. The spring break trip was long hours but it was such a cool experience to get to study at the Smithsonian tropical research institute, and even inspired me to look into more tropical marine opportunities post grad. The small size of this class was also a fun change from my past OEB course experiences, and after the trip you will definitely be bonded.
Be prepared to study a ton for the exams, but the class itself is fun and engaging and the labs are really cool. Gonzalo is a wonderful teacher and explains the material well
