CS 262 is an interesting introduction to distributed systems. It provides a good overview of the general problems in designing and building distributed systems, and discusses some solutions to those problems.

There is no real background requirement, except that you should be fluent in at least one common programming language. You should also be ready to think about the design and architecture of systems.

The volume of assigned readings is usual for a graduate–level course. However, some of the readings are fairly dated, and do not really reflect the current state–of–the–art in distributed systems. In general, Waldo focuses on very broad and general problems, rather than discussing current research. Depending on what you're after, this can be good or bad for you. I found this to be pretty good, as I was looking for a broad introduction. There is no real enforcement to read the assigned papers, so you can slack off, which can come in handy during rough weeks. However, please do the readings, they are definitely worth it, and you will get much less out of the class if you don't.

The assignments were design–based, and they were fairly broad, as is typical for a graduate–level course. They required designing a system and thinking about trade–offs, and then implementing the system and demonstrating that it works. It worked well to reinforce some of the things that we did in class. The final project is very open–ended, and you can pretty much work on anything that was covered in class; it doesn't even have to be novel research. A general theme in the assignments in that requirements are slightly vague –– depending on your preferences, this can be a good or bad thing. In general, each assignment resulted in a lot of clarification questions, and people overall seemed to be dissatisfied with the lack of clarity.

One problem with CS 262 this year was the lack of organization. The number of people enrolled in the class was about 3x what Waldo had expected, and the teaching staff was absolutely not equipped to deal with such a huge number of students. The result of this were substantial, on–the–fly changes to the syllabus. All assignments except the final project were peer–graded (the staff never looked at them). As of the time of this writing, before the final grades are released, it is unclear what the grade weight of the assignments vs. the final project will be. This lack of clarity about expectations and grading can be unnerving sometimes. Don't expect to know your grade until the final grades are released. Of course, this might change with the future editions of the course, as Waldo will probably hire more TFs.

The workload highly depends on you. You should be able to do little work and get a decent grade, but you can also put in much more effort. What you get out of the class is directly proportional to the work you put in, but how the work invested impacts the grade is not very clear (see above).

Overall, I recommend taking this class late during your studies, like senior year. You will not gain any mind–blowing knowledge, but it can be fun engaging with the material, and the work is very manageable.
