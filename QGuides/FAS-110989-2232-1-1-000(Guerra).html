amazing class. very hard. 3 psets a week. but I learned how to learn and how to think more abstractly. Definitely good for the brain!
This class is very well structured and a fantastic introduction to linear algebra that does not require any prior experience. If you've taken Math 21a, then the overall structure of this course is almost identical. The instructors are enthusiastic and great at explaining concepts; they are also all very knowledgeable about each lesson. The worksheet–based course sections are engaging and can be quite fun if you get an interesting table group. The three psets each week are frequent but manageable. Individual psets typically take longer than those in 21a, so the overall workload of the class is appreciable. But there are plenty of resources from the MQC to office hours to other students to help you through everything; definitely take advantage of these if needed!

As a subject, linear algebra and the underlying techniques of dealing with matrices/vectors are very different from multivariable concepts. Nonetheless, this is a very useful and applicable subject; the class does a good job of not only teaching the fundamentals of linear algebra but also introducing the diverse applications ranging from CS to statistics to physics. Overall, this class is very useful, organized, and engaging; and with some time commitment, you can be very successful in this class!
Be ready for quite a bit of work, but it is very very rewarding
Don't take this unless you have to. Honestly try 22a before this class. It is horrific especially with the 3 psets a week and exams are awful. It is by far one of the worst out of all four math classes (1a–21b) and you will hate yourself and math by the end of it.
I think 21b is a class that is designed for a specific type of student, and I was unfortunately not that type of student – though I had to take it anyway as a required class. I expected 21b to be similar to 21a in terms of straightforwardness and a style of problem that was "learn an equation, apply what you learned". 21b is instead more about learning a set of rules, and then finding the specific rules that apply to whatever math problem is put in front of you. Whereas for 21a, you can be reasonably certain in the process leading to an answer, 21b is staring at a problem for an hour or more on a PSET, trying to figure out how to approach it, and then spending the next hour after that performing a very long series of basic calculations until you wind up with a result that still has a 50% chance of being wrong due to you forgetting to carry a one ten steps ago.
Don't get me wrong, section time when you learn the content is great, it's just everything afterwards that's painful and tedious. You'll learn a concept that seems simple enough in section, with maybe a couple niche variations for specific situations. Then you'll open up the PSET later that evening, and see a problem with seven sub–parts that applies the concept you just learned, but to a starting equation/matrix that is needlessly complex or large for no good reason besides to make you spend twice as long on the calculation without learning any more of the conceptual material you already know. That will be followed by a problem which requires you to derive a new equation from the ones you learned in class first, meaning you spend a significant amount of time just trying to figure out where to start (a problem most easily solved by just going to MQC and asking a CA), before you spend the same amount of time as you did on the first problem to solve it as well. The tests are more similar stuff.
If you enjoy that sort of thing, then hey, this course might be for you. Be warned though, it's not for everyone – it wasn't for me – and if that's the case, be prepared to spend a LOT of time on it.
Review your notes constantly throughout the semester and take great notes during section.

The final was VERY difficult and not at all like PSETs, but the practice exams prepare you well.
Stay on top of the psets and start studying early.
