This course attempts to do too much in too little time. I never felt like I gained a decent understanding of anything. The psets are difficult and I had to rely heavily on the course assistants in office hours every step of the way. Even after getting enough guidance from them to write a line or two of code, I struggled to understand what the code actually meant.

Office hours are extremely crowded and you will rarely get the help you need, just due to the limited availability of course assistants. Additionally, you cannot rely on other students to help you because the collaboration policies are strict. Overall, there is not enough support considering the intense workload.

My advice: If you really must take this course, TAKE IT PASS/FAIL. Additionally, don't be afraid to turn in incomplete psets...sometimes you will just not be able to get the help you need during office hours. Just turn them in (even if you can only do the first step), show up to section, and do your best on the final test. Lower your expectations, aim to pass, and consider anything you learn along the way a win.

It means you can finish your pset earlier, and since the different problems in the pset build upon each other, you basically know how to do everything in it after section(to a limit of course).

Oh, CS50. I remember being pretty afraid when I registered—all of my friends who took this class had a rough time in Fall semester and discouraged me.

It is SUPER doable for those who are comfortable in technical and computational thinking. I have no experience in CS, but I know rigorous math and physics; I'd like to think that is why I had an easier time. Don't be discouraged if you have no background, just go in understanding this will require work! It's an intro class only in spirit and content, not commitment. If you have a technical background but no CS experience, you'll probably be just fine.

Don't be overly confident and get help. Struggle through the psets and get help after you're really stuck, otherwise you'll hate the class and feel like you learned nothing. If you're taking this for a letter grade, you'll really have to work hard for your grade.
The spring version is nice in that the lectures are asynchronous but be prepared to have much less support.

It's a tough course, especially as a beginner. The 'late hours' extension policy saved me this semester (you're allowed 72 late 'hours' worth to use across all psets throughout the semester). Also, if you're choosing between CS50 and CS32: in summary (paraphrased from a CS advisor), CS50 is more of a 'tasting menu' going through many different programming languages and concepts, and gives you a more comprehensive overview and intro to all of CS, whilst CS32 is more of a linear course, sticking to one programming language (Python) and going deeper in it and every lecture has a clear problem–solving motivation behind all the content. (Over Christmas I was extremely undecided about which one to take, and in the end, I chose CS50 because I wanted that broader overview to all of CS, as well as the fact that CS50 is just such a classic 'Harvard course' that I felt I just had to take)
Despite the potential time commitment, taking this course is definitely worthwhile.
