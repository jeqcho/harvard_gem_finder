Professor Chong is amazing as a lecturer and he and the teaching staff really care about the students. Chong isn't the regular teacher for this course, though. Other than that, though, I just found the course material to be very dry. Though it's a relatively low workload for a CS class, I wouldn't recommend the class unless you're REALLY interested in the topic (think CS51 final project but way more in–depth)
Neat content! I found it a bit slow at the start and a little bit repetitive –– it feels like a good chunk of the course is providing different formalisms to describe the same phenomena, so I guess it depends if you're interested in learning about those different perspectives. That being said some of the content near the end was pretty interesting –– defining polymorphism rigorously and seeing how purely functional languages can implement state using monads.
Decent class if you want to learn more about programming languages. Take note that this is a theoretical CS course, so you won't actually do a lot of programming. Would say course is moderate difficulty and moderate workload. The assignments were interesting, thought–provoking, and challenging. The material can get pretty subtle, so make sure you do practice (by reviewing the section notes).
Don't let anyone tell you this class is easy. While it's not as hard as the most difficult CS courses at Harvard, it is still very challenging. Most psets are time consuming but the generous amount of late minutes (10 days worth) helps make things a little less stressful. Exams are challenging. About the CS51 prerequisite, you can get away with not having taken 51 but this class makes heavy use of OCaml so go to office hours as much as possible for the programming parts. You will not learn anything useful or practical, which is fine as long as you're okay with that. Like you'll have to use Haskell for one assignment and then you will never code in Haskell ever again. Also it's kind of annoying that students aren't allowed to see their autograder scores while working on psets which makes debugging a lot more difficult.

All of that being said, Steve Chong is one of the best professors that Harvard's CS department has to offer. He makes lectures interesting and he genuinely cares about his students. I probably would've dropped the course if he wasn't the professor. I don't think he's teaching this again for the next few years and I'm not really familiar with the professor who usually teaches it so do some research on that before you decide on CS152.
This is an excellent class, one of the best CS classes I have ever taken.
Can't say what it looks like under Nada since I took it under Professor Chong.
So, all I can say is if Steven Chong is teaching it then don't hesitate to take it.
Great course! Material is really important, and taught very well. Some of the assignments are tough but the TFs are great and office hours are very helpful.
If Stephen is teaching it, take it! He makes the material interesting, really cares about the students, and is a great lecturer. If Nada is teaching it, shop it beforehand.
This class is pretty dang hard.... I went to every single office hours that I could, which was around 3–4 a week, each for around 2 hours, and basically required that environment with all the other students and the TFs in order to complete the psets. There aren't many, but the questions are pretty tricky. Midterm and Final were pretty tough.. Good class though, glad I took it and even more glad I survived.
Professor Chong is great. He really cares about his students and wants to see them succeed. He tries to be engaging and clear in his presentations. I think the material is difficult and a bit esoteric/obscure. You pretty much need to love programming language theory to stay engaged and motivated, and that wasn't the case for many students. I would have preferred if the class covered fewer topics but with more depth and reasonable practice for the topics, as to have mastered the concepts albeit not having gotten to some of the topics at hand. The first couple psets were fair and fun, but they quickly ramped up as to be very difficult. Overall, I wouldn't take this course if you don't have to.
Content is cool, if you took CS51 and thought the final project was pretty interesting this class would be fun. Lecture/section notes help a lot with psets.
Very esoteric and basically just pattern matching. PSETs are pretty hard and not fun, and material is pretty confusing. Wouldn't recommend.
Not an interesting class, conceptually. It feels so... mechanical. But Steven Chong is an amazing person, and the homework assignments are phenomenally written. Highlights are: isomorphism, algebraic type systems, and a bajillion different kinds of lambda calculus with different features. If you don't consider these to be highlights, then perhaps this is not the right course for you!
I would say that this is a fantastic course for CS upperclassman. I found the material incredibly interesting and also "easy" enough to get through. The best CS theory course I've taken in my time here, hands down. HOWEVER, much of this is because I found the professor, Stephen Chong, to be an exceptional lecturer (easily the best I've seen at this school) – he knows exactly how to make material organized and intuitive, and has a great feeling about where students are at the best way to approach material. I know some students weren't a fan of the psets and thought they were unfair – I would push back on that. The coding portions at times were a tad difficult, but not more than I expected in this course. People overall did very well on these, and you shouldn't be worried. HOWEVER, what you should be worried about is if someone else is teaching the course. The usual professor, Prof Nada Amin, came in and guest lectured. I'm not going to fully form an opinion after one experience, but I will say that I certainly do not think I would have such good feelings about the course if she was the lecturer. If it is not taught by Chong, proceed with caution.
This is a fun course! In many ways, it feels like solving new puzzles with pieces that are new (because they are all defined in the course). As such, I felt like anyone could take this course because so much is self contained. ALSO STEVE CHONG IS THE NICEST MAN IVE EVER MET.
Overall, this is a fairly reasonable class and definitely a much more feasible alternative to COMPSCI 124, if you are using this to satisfy that requirement. Problem sets are not that overwhelming and are only assigned every two weeks (this is actually a consistent cadence, unlike other CS classes that advertise this but then give extra assignments). There are usually coding questions on the problem sets (mostly in OCaml, so make sure you are familiar with the language or have taken CS51). Make sure to know what you're getting into content–wise this class is very notation–heavy, and it is sometimes difficult to connect content across different units. It is also incredibly theoretical and won't necessarily be helpful for interviews, recruiting, etc. in the way that 124 can be. In my opinion, Professor Chong really made this class amazing, with his clear, engaging lectures and intuitive materials. I wasn't a huge fan of the content, but Chong made it worth it. I'm not sure what the class is like with the usual professor, but I overall had a great time! It was a good class to take during a busy semester.
Chong is an excellent professor who gives great lectures and cares about his students. CS 152's lecture notes are also excellent. Bi–weekly problem sets that aren't too bad. Exams are multiple choice on Canvas.
This course is well taught, but the content is challenging and was not very rewarding to me. If considering this course, do some reading beforehand looking at what concepts will be covered, and what questions will be answered. If they interest you, then take the course! If not, look elsewhere
