What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Expos is a little intimidating at first, but provides you with a unique opportunity to step out of your comfort zone and learn how to share your ideas in writing at a collegiate level. It really isn't that large of a workload, and you truly get out of it what you put into it. Try your best to actually do the readings and contribute to class discussions, and along the way you'll find yourself learning so much more not only about the class content material, but how to verbally express yourself in an academic setting as well as how to ask questions/reach out when you need assistance.
The professor of this course grades very harshly and sometimes the grading does not make much sense. I would recommend this course if you want some variety in the sources you analyze and the formats you can use (ie. you get to watch married at first sight and analyze the dynamics that occur there and do a podcast for the final project). This specific EXPOS class is not a lot of reading and is minimal work, but again, the grading is harsh.
While the course topic is interesting, the course structure is extremely quickly paced (comparatively speaking, we had tighter timelines for final projects) and graded harshly, to the point that unless you can prioritize this course above all other courses, it is difficult to receive good grades in the course. The preceptor also comes across as inflexible in her approach to teaching. If you have a heavy workload already, choose another expos.
The essays can take a lot of time and preparation so start early
It's really difficult to fall behind in this class, but when there is an opportunity to skip a reading or assignment, don't! You will get the most out of the class if you stay up to date on assignments and take revision feedback seriously.
I would tell future students that this specific EXPOS section has the strong potential to provide you with materials, critical thinking questions, and skills to engage with scholars that push you to think about your perspectives on identity and its relationship to language (i.e. sociolinguistic behaviors, language cultures, and more!). This is an incredible opportunity to examine real–world examples of how daily social interactions and behaviors can reinforce big–picture power dynamics.
Be wary of starting assignments too late
I liked this course because aside from the assignments like response papers and final papers and podcasts, there was not a ton of out of class time required on a regular basis because the readings were pretty quick
This is by no means an easy class, but I personally found the topic to be really interesting which makes it easier to get through assignments and readings. Dr. Schwab is a pretty hard grader but still reasonable. She provides really useful feedback and pushes you to write your best.
