If you have any background in music, this course shouldn't be too hard. The music you listen to and analyze will encourage you to diversify your taste and notice musical concepts in the music you hear around you every day. Stay on top of the readings because you may be called on in lecture!
If it's not the usual professor teaching this class, DO NOT TAKE IT. The assessments and structure of the class were so focused on rote and lucky memorization of seemingly insignificant and irrelevant facts. We were frequently quizzed on which writer the professor had mentioned wrote about a specific piece of music from the class or small random details about works that did not at all contribute to understanding music history but was rather a test of if you happened to catch something the professor happened to say and remember it during the exam. The textbook and lectures also seem heavily imbued with personal bias and subjectivity, with frequent blanket statements such as "this composer was undoubtedly hailed as the best/most influential of his era" with little to no evidence in support of such statements. There was very little turnaround time for responses, exams were all rote memorization of song identification and lucky memory of certain facts, and I came away with the class immensely frustrated that a class that was meant to be an introductory course into a new interest for most students became a pile of busy work that we were expected to take way too seriously.

This was among my favorites this semester! Engaging lectures, eye–opening exposure to new genres and specific pieces of music. Clear enthusiasm and passion from Prof. Callahan and the teaching staff. A really quintessential Harvard course that extends beyond music appreciation, really gets you to think about music in new and deeper ways. Fair assignments, helpful and detailed feedback, and a community of students who all come from different musical and song appreciation backgrounds yet all were excited to take this class, which is (IMO) a testament to its organization, structure, and planning.
2 quizzes and a midterm and final make this class a bit stressful, but in the end, tests are not bad at all. You'll likely look back and realize you worked harder than you expected, but came out with a confidently good grade (as long as you do the bare minimum).

Having some musical experience is not necessary, but definitely makes the class easier (but don't think you'll breeze through it unless you happen to know by heart arbitrary Early Baroque operas and such).

I really enjoyed this class! I gained so much from it and felt like I left the class better off than when I started. I learned a ton about music history, was introduced to a lot of "classical" music that I had never heard before, and I think this has partially changed the way I think about modern music now that I know so much context. However, this year, the teaching staff had very unrealistic expectations. Despite this literally being Music *1*, the class was so intense and rigorous. It had a lot of necessary required readings from both the textbook and supplemental Canvas readings, and we were expected to not only know the content of supplemental readings but to know, by name, which author said what about specific songs. We also had "low stakes" short essays, but they still required lots of time and effort. And then there were the listening quizzes that were difficult and required lots of studying long before the quiz because we had to know niche knowledge about songs after identifying them based on a 20 second or so snippet of the song. This class simply required too much. I've heard that it was gem–adjacent in the past, but it definitely was not this year. In fact, it was probably my hardest class. Also, Professor Callahan is super enthusiastic and super knowledgeable. He has cool stories and is very excited to hear people's thoughts. He clearly acknowledges the biases in music history and reminds us why we learn about so many white old men. By extension, he teaches general sociopolitical history well and isn't afraid to skirt around uncomfortable truths of historical problems relating to race, feminism, or treatment of children. With all that being said, however, Professor Callahan oftentimes acted snobby or pretentious and tries too hard to let the world know that he is a legitimate musicologist. Overall, I think this was a really fun class, it's way more Bach and way less Beyonce (she was mentioned literally in the class), and it's very rigorous. I still would recommend it despite all of the negatives. But definitely take this all into consideration before deciding to take it.
