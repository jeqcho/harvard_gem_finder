This is a great alternative to CS 181 if you're interested in more of the statistical theory of supervised learning methods. The workload is very reasonable, with only five problem sets, drop lowest, and a midterm and final presentation instead of any exams. The concepts covered span from linear regression methods to ensemble learning methods and current research, and are very thoroughly covered at every step at a mathematical level.
Interesting subject material, but be warned that grading can be a bit high variance! Go to office hours if you can.
This course definitely assumes a reasonable degree of mathematical and statistical maturity. The former is critical to getting the most out of the homework, and the latter is critical to getting the most out of classes. Even though this is a machine learning class, it tends more towards the statistics within machine learning.
This course is a very thorough introduction to supervised learning, and Prof. Sur is an amazing instructor. However, this course does effectively require STAT 210 as a prerequisite.
This course had a lot of overlap with CS181 and did not go very far beyond that material. The homework assignments are challenging because they are often unrelated to the material covered in class, so some background work/textbook reading is required to make progress. The course project also felt somewhat out of left–field. So, as currently structured, it was a fine class but you might be better off taking another ML class instead.
Professor Pragya lectures well, and you should get started on the problem sets early and go to office hours
This course focuses on the statistical side of ML, which means you focus on considering the characteristics of datasets and how different ML methods would perform on them, and learning lots of theorems and doing lots of math. If you took MIT 6.036 (which I did), this course is almost nothing like it. 195 also basically just follows the textbook. If you're really interested in theoretical ML topics but can't motivate yourself to learn it on your own, this course may be for you. But otherwise, it'll probably fall short of your expectations. Pretty much the only part of the course I found quite interesting were the no free lunch problems on the PSETs where you run simulations to basically find different scenarios in which certain ML methods outperform another and try to explain why. Otherwise, PSETs were tedious math and not that related to stuff from lecture. Sections mainly covered topics that weren't covered in lecture, so you don't get a chance to review and thoroughly understand the topics. Lastly, the literature review component of the course does expose you to ML research, but the key word is expose––tbh I understood <5% of the different papers that everyone presented and am still not sure what types of ML research are most interesting to me or how the stuff we learned can be applied to my own ML research. Overall, not a bad course (grading is lenient if that's something that matters to you), but the material can feel pretty disorganized and disconnected.
This is a great follow up to MIT 6.036/CS 181. Unlike CS 181, this course is well taught, and Professor Sur is a good lecturer. The only issue is that you need to take Stat 210 before taking this course, and it seems a bit odd for a graduate level course to be a prereq for an undergraduate level course. Yanke Song is an excellent TF and very helpful in OH/section. However, the problem sets are not about what is covered in lecture or section, which makes them challenging.
This course was truly awful, one of the worst I've taken at Harvard. I enjoyed CS 181 and so I was excited to continue learning about machine learning by taking this course. I fully regret the decision to take it. Here are the reasons why:
– Pragya just lectures from the textbook (Elements of Statistical Learning), but she is less clear than the textbook. You're better off just reading the textbook on your own
– The problem sets are a complete waste of time. Instead of allow us to practice using concepts or methods from the course, Pragya's problem sets basically just asked us to try to come up with pathological corner cases that contradict common sense that applies to 99.9% of real world cases. These problems appeared on every single problem set and it was such a headache to try to come up with some stupid counterexample that provided zero insight about machine learning. Then when I saw the problem solutions, there would be absolutely nothing insightful about the solution. It was just simply about whether you were clever enough to come up with some stupid pathological distribution and function that happened to break common sense. It made me so angry to have to slave away for 10 hours to try to come up with these pathological counterexamples and these problems appeared on EVERY PROBLEM SET. And these problems were instead of actually practicing the course material. It just felt like Pragya was so tickled by these mathematical curios and wanted to force every student to be tickled by the same things, when theoretical corner cases are usually NOT what people want to learn about machine learning
– The two projects are also a waste of time. Every group gets assigned a theory paper and presents it. The presentations are all completely comprehensible because everyone is trying to explain an academic paper's worth of math that is too high level for the class in only 10 minutes. I learned absolutely nothing from anyone's presentation. I'm sure there are some geniuses in the class who probably did learn something, and so if you're one of those geniuses, by all means, take this class.
