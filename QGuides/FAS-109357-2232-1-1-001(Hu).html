TAKE THIS CLASS IF YOU CAN!

This is quite possibly the coolest class offered at Harvard. It is really a one–of–a–kind experience. The structure consists of one 2–hour lecture and one 4–hour lab each week. This lab time may sound long, but it is well worth it. No background in microfabrication is assumed. I came in with some background in electrical engineering, which I think allowed me to better appreciate some of the topics we discussed, but this is still a VERY worthwhile class without such background.

The amount you learn in this class is incredible, while still being a very reasonable choice workload–wise. You gain access to the cleanroom facilities at the Center for Nanoscale Systems (CNS), and will be trained extensively. Beginning with safety training and basic cleanroom orientation/wet bench training, you then do specialized training in a variety of techniques. As part of a 5–person lab group, everyone will specialize in different areas for training, but you will work together in very cool labs, gaining exposure and experience on all sorts of techniques. We focus on photolithography, chemical & physical vapor deposition, sputtering, reactive ion etching, test & measurement. This gives you a very solid foundation for future microfabrication work.

The labs are super interesting. First, you fabricate a PN junction from scratch (a diode). This is one of the most basic semiconductor devices. The second lab is when you make LEDs – it's very satisfying to see them light up! Next is a photodetector (basically a solar cell), and finally, you will fabricate transistors. The focus of this course is on fabrication techniques, not the theory behind semiconductor devices. Some of these may seem like simple devices, but working through this course you really gain an appreciation for all of the care and attention to detail that goes into microfabrication – it's almost an art. You won't be making computer chips with thousands of transistors, but you will be fabricating as advanced devices as one possibly can with only one semester of training. This training is much more accelerated than typical for getting started with microfabrication.

As the course progresses, you as students take more and more agency in the fabrication process. I really like this form of structure. It allows you to dive into fabricating cool devices from the beginning, but makes it more of a thoughtful exercise than simply following a pre–determined recipe to the t. It helps prepare you to be more independent and capable in the cleanroom.

The teaching staff for this course have been terrific. Professor Evelyn Hu is an incredible lecturer – she is so knowledgeable, but very skilled at explaining things in an engaging and effective way. It is clear that she has mastered her art. She puts a lot of care into her lectures, and makes sure to engage with the students, forcing us to think critically about important questions and discuss as a group. She is also very accessible to talk with, and a very kind person. Professor Kiyoul Yang is also terrific. I believe he is teaching this course next year, and if so, you are in for a treat! He is very smart, kind, and thoughtful. The teaching fellows were wonderful as well. You will work with one TF during your labs, and I have found them to be very knowledgeable, supportive, helpful, and kind. This course has an extensive teaching staff: three instructors, eight support staff, and two TFs, for an ~20 student class. You will be well supported.

This course comes in two versions: the undergraduate version (ES177), and the graduate version (ES277). The only difference is that ES277 requires a final project. I really enjoyed this aspect of the course, and so if you are interested in doing microfabrication beyond this one–semester course, I would highly recommend enrolling in the graduate version. The project is to propose a process sequence for a device you would like to make – basically, you pick something interesting that you could use microfabrication for, and describe how you'd make it. People picked a lot of interesting things, from microelectromechanical devices (MEMs) to hardware for neuromorphic computers. After the course, they pick a few projects to fund with $1000 for fabrication, so this is a great chance to get some funding for a project in the cleanroom.

All in all, this course is WONDERFUL, and I HIGHLY RECOMMEND!
