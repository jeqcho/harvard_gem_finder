Only enroll if you are willing to put in the time learning. You can breeze past with a decent grade without learning anything too, but that defeats the purpose of such a class.
Grad CS classes at Harvard fall into two categories: (1) have a similar structure to undergrad classes but with fewer and perhaps more difficult assignments or (2) students give lectures in the form of paper reviews and the vast majority of the final grade comes from the final project. This course falls into the latter category –– after the first few lectures, Valiant doesn't play a large role in leading the course. I'd say it's a great course to practice reading research papers (and very interesting ones at that), and a good opportunity to do a cool big project at the end, but not a heavy workload at all. Background in neurobiology and CS theory are helpful but certainly not necessary.
cool way to learn about bio/cs applications and be surrounded by smart people! pretty chill class
Pretty easy class if you put in the effort to read and comprehend the papers.
This is probably one of the better classes available to do the theory requirement for people doing AB/SM, as the workload is really reasonable. However, I did find myself wishing that the class had more content about machine learning, given that the class is about biology and complexity.
You'll get the most out of this class if you really engage with the literature that is given.
You can learn about a lot of cool topics in biology that are related to computer science in a non–stressful way through this class. I wish there were more assignments to reinforce my understanding, though. How much you get out from the class really depends on how much you put in. The research project is a nice way to explore a topic, but there isn't a huge amount of support for it. Prof. Valiant is cool.
Great class. It's highly student–driven. Most of the lectures are given by students, so be prepared to learn from your peers and prepare lectures.
A good class for the A.B./S.M. theory requirement.
