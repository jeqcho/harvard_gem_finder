This course is somewhat representative of foundational engineering courses at Harvard. It gives a lot of assignments; none are especially difficult on their own, but the abundance of things to do does take a significant time commitment. Although, for better or for worse, the class does not expect a high quality of work. The overall class feels somewhat unstructured at times, but there are definitely resources available; definitely reach out to TF's and former students who took this class if needed! The instructor, Seymur, is enthusiastic and friendly—he makes an impressive effort to know each of his students. He is also a SolidWorks wizard! That being said, he is not the most compelling lecturer and his explanations can be confusing. Lectures and slides are not the most helpful. While aspects of the lab component, particularly keeping up with a lab notebook can get annoying, the overall experience is fun if you like designing/machining things. The first few weeks of lab introduce the various tools available, then in the last few weeks there is time to work on the final robot design project. This project is quite exciting; however, teams are chosen randomly and the strength of team collaboration and contribution can either make this a fantastic process or an awful time.

From what I've heard, this class—and most engineering classes in general—can present helpful concepts but definitely is not a stand–in for real–world engineering experience. Nevertheless, it is a fairly good gateway in that it gives an idea of what to expect from Harvard engineering. Generally, for this department, you will get out what you put in; there is a certain amount of initiative needed on the part of the student.

This class is a big time commitment. We had 3 lectures per week, a weekly ~3hour lab, problem sets, 2 exams, and a final project. The actual course content was not very difficult, but the class itself just throws a lot at you. Be prepared to spend a lot of extra time in the lab leading up to the final project deadline. Also, there was a heavy Solidwork's CAD component of this class which was not really formally taught in lecture. We did go over some CAD methods/tools in lecture sometimes, but most of the learning comes from outside of class. Professor Hasanov did provide many tutorials to help students get through the problem sets.

With all of this said, the class is definitely doable and can be a lot of fun and very rewarding. The teaching staff was very good. The class seemed to focus heavily on manufacturing methods, and this topic was central to the final project. Overall, I would recommend it, if you are interested in Mechanical Engineering and like building/doing things with your hands.
