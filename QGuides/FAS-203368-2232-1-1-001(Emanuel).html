Take this class, either only if you have friends to take it with, or if you are confident in your ability to stay on top of work consistently throughout the year (around 30 min–1 hour per week, which is NOT A LOT) and be ok with people not doing their own work. That was really what made the class for me. Aside from that, the class is not too much work at all—around 2–3 hours a week. And Shai is pretty great.
I would be cautious with this class. I think Shai is a wonderful professor, and his passion for the topic is amazing. However, the TFs were quite harsh and sometimes left mean comments on assignments. The reading expectations were quite high and they required a lot of work wihtout any consideration for studetns circumstances. I did learn a lot from the class that I found to be very helpful, but would definitely have prefered a gentler TF.
This is the best course I've taken at Harvard. Make sure you care about the topic and you will greatly enjoy it.
This class is really fascinating. The teaching staff is great, and the content will seriously push you to explore new concepts in and around the public/private/non–profit spheres. There is so much to find interesting, and you have the freedom to explore your own interest in the research paper. Also, make sure to do the readings to really get the most out of this class. It will really make a difference.
If you are at all interested in the social impact space take this class!! You get to donate real money to non–profits of your choice and build your own non–profit venture! Really unique class.
The material in this class is interesting and important, but all the other aspects of how this course is run really weigh it down. Before taking it, think carefully (especially if you're a senior) about if you want to take a class that meets at 9am, has a mandatory Friday section, and will require you to spend 80% of your time doing group work in a large group of 6–8 people that you chose in only the 2nd week of class. The group projects are impactful because you receive real money to give to nonprofits. However, you're encouraged to split up the work so that not everyone has to work on every assignment, which means that 10% of your final grade will be determined by work that not even you, but rather your peers do. Shai is very nice and knowledgable. The course staff, though, at times seems disorganized and disconnected as a team. Your section TF does not grade your group assignments, but the TF who does grade them does not even come to class or section, so you don't know them, which makes it hard to ask questions and see what they expect from you. Overall, while the material this class teaches is important and eye–opening in some ways, being in this class is not an enjoyable experience.
It's a chill and interesting class on philanthropy—the only part that's a bit more challenging is the grant writing and venture component, which is done in groups selected at the beginning of the semester (whether or not you pick a good group is really going to make or break that experience)
DROP THIS CLASS IF YOU DON'T LIKE YOU'RE GROUP. You will have to meet with them weekly, text them almost everyday, and have to reach out and work with nonprofits on your own schedule. Without my group I would not have survived the class.
Could have been so good—the professor is great and the topics are interesting, but the way that the assignments were designed and graded was so disorganized and inconsistent that it made it hard to appreciate the course material at the end of the day.
It's a very very fun class that really encourages you to learn about the sector. The teaching staff are all very friendly and are there to support you!
This is a transformative class if you let it be. Group work is the best part of the class. You will get to work with existing nonprofits and distribute a lot of grant money to them! Take this course with an open mind and you will understand and feel more confident in starting a venture of your own by the end of it. PS. IRS–990s will be your best friend.
