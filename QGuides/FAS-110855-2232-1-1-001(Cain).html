General (not teaching staff related):

Personal academic experience: I came in with 25a and a pretty reasonable analysis background from high school. While this made the class significantly easier, and I definitely spent less time on it than 25a, it was still a difficult course, especially at the very beginning and very end of the semester. I still thus recommend taking 25b, even if you have previous analysis experience. For me specifically, when I took analysis in high school I didn't have a great linear algebra background, and I lost a lot of the intuition and appreciation for the material that I was able to gain in 25b. The course is an incredible deep–dive into real analysis, and you come out of it with a very good understanding of the fundamentals. Notably, if you're thinking of taking 55b, that course doesn't cover real analysis in nearly as much depth––from what I heard, they spent a week on it. Additionally, at least with Wes, he made sure that we got computational practice and worked with applications quite a bit, so for a non–pure math concentrator I feel like this class might be more useful than 55b.

The intensive nature of this class and the small size made the community very strong. While I feel like it was accentuated with our specific teaching staff, I think that this is probably a constant for 25. For 90% of the class, it was basically impossible to complete the problem sets without at least some (and often a lot of) collaboration. You often found at least 12–15 people of the 20 person class at Tuesday night office hours. These office hours are some of my favorite memories of my freshman year, not just because of Saint Knut, the CA for those office hours, but also due to the fervent communal energy that would often last late into Wednesday morning. It was a truly collaborative atmosphere––everyone contributed, and everyone benefited. All of these qualities made the class feel more like a seminar–style class than a classic lecture–based one in terms of the tight–knit community that formed. Adding to the fact that most of us had taken 25a and had known each other since the beginning of freshman year, I can't think of a better class in terms of community. I'm serious: especially for STEM majors, you're not gonna get better than this.


Wes:

A gem among gems. Potentially the best teacher I've ever had, and definitely the best math lecturer. I assume this applies to any class he teaches, so this almost certainly applies if you're considering taking 19a, 118r, or some other class that he's teaching. His lectures are sometimes transcendental experiences, Picard–Lindelof proof in particular. (I'm not joking––I was literally dreaming about how beautiful the proof was the night after the lecture.) His enthusiasm for the material, particularly 25b material, is obvious and so infectious. He made the class feel like a journey––with the Picard–Lindelof proof, for example, he hyped up that proof specifically for weeks before we got to it, and when he finally proved it in class, the way so many of the concepts we had been learning about fit together so perfectly in the proof was just beautiful. He forces us to participate in lectures, and in a very constructive way that, combined with the clarity and deliberateness of his lecturing, made if feel like I could have proved the given proposition or theorem myself if I was given enough time. He also clearly and genuinely cares about his students, hosting numerous office hours and making himself available to schedule individual appointments whenever. His (sometimes intentional, sometimes not) humor also added to the lecture experience. Yes, it's a 9am three days a week, but it was so worth it. Wes does have a few annoying principles that he sticks to very stubbornly: the worst of these is absolutely no flexibility in deadlines (like if you submit something five minutes late you might get a zero). The exams are also in person and quite difficult, although if you study from the problem sets and lecture notes they're usually doable.

All in all, the 25 sequence, including 25b, was one of my favorite and most memorable experiences from freshman year. Both 25a and 25b were my favorite classes each semester, due in part to the interesting material, but much more due to the fantastic people in the class, the community and the wonderful teaching we were privileged to experience. I got a little emotional at the end of the final lecture. Take it, I implore you.
Math 25B is an excellent course. For those who took 25A, it continues where the course left off in the fall, using real analysis to build and make claims about the spaces on which we can do calculus. The courseload is heavier than that of Math 25A—proofs get longer and more technical, and I expect that people spend an additional ~5 hours per week on each problem set. While the material is beautiful, unifying a lot of concepts and (I think) pushing students into developing serious mathematical maturity, this means slightly more of a tradeoff. Real analysis and multivariable calculus are highly important in statistics, economics, and physics (slightly less so in computer science but plenty of students in our year were taking CS). The community is also wonderful and likely even more tightly–knit than that of the fall class. One thing to note is that the extremely high scores in the class are both a function of the course itself and Wes Cain, who will not be teaching the course this year. I would highly encourage taking this course but with a grain of salt depending on how you feel about the (possibly increased) workload in the first few weeks.
Overall: Math 25b is a good notch more difficult that 25a. It focuses on the development of real analysis and multivariable calculus. 25b aims to improve upon the proof–writing skills developed in 25a by building your stamina for longer arguments. Proofs are also less straightforward; whereas in 25a, you could almost always start with “let u_1 through u_n be a basis,” that does not work here. One should also pay significantly more attention to theorem requirements (differentiable vs class C^1, pointwise vs uniform convergence, etc.) and quantifiers (for all/there exists) than in 25a.

Professor: If you read my 25a review, you would know Wes is the best. Unfortunately, as of the time of this writing, he won’t be teaching 25 in 2023–24.

Textbooks: Wes, at least, does not follow Marsden–Hoffman as closely as he did to Axler in 25a. Abbott and Lang are downloadable through Springer for free.
1) Marsden–Hoffman: The primary book for this course. Useful to read ahead of the lectures.
2) Abbott: Some excerpts here and there were useful.
3) Spivak: Not really necessary.
4) Lang: “Written more at the level of Math 21a” though a good intro to multi if you haven’t seen it before.
J.E. Marsden and M.J. Hoffman, Elementary Classical Analysis, 2nd Ed., W.H. Freeman, 1993.
S. Abbott, Understanding Analysis, 2nd Ed., Springer, New York, 2015.
M. Spivak, Calculus on Manifolds, Addison–Wesley, 1965.
S. Lang, Calculus of Several Variables, 3rd Ed., Springer, New York 1987.

Topics covered: Constructing the Reals; sequences, convergence, completeness, Cauchy; cluster points, lim inf/sup; topology of metric spaces (open/closed/interior/accumulation points/boundary); compactness (Bolzano–Weierstrass, Heine–Borel) and connectedness; (uniform) continuity; EVT/IVT/MVT on R–>R and higher dimensions; differentiation; Riemann integration; pointwise and uniform convergence (fun), Weierstrass M–test; interchanging infinite limits/sums and integrals; constructing elementary functions with series; contraction mapping; diff eq; differentiable functions R^n–>R^m, “Df”, Jacobians; differentiable curves, partial/directional derivatives; chain rule for R^n–>R^m–>R^p; gradient; higher derivatives and Clairaut; Taylor for higher dimensions; inverse and implicit function theorems (even more fun); Integration/Fubini/change of variables/polar/spherical; line/surface/flux integrals; divergence/Stokes theorems; Hilbert space L^2; orthogonal families of functions/Fourier/Bessel/Parseval; completeness/convergence of Fourier series.

Course structure notes: 3 lectures a week; weekly psets due Wednesday afternoons, LaTeXed. TA OHs throughout the week. Psets tend to have fewer questions but contain longer more–involved proofs, especially after the third pset. The first few psets focus on metric space topology, which can be frustratingly difficult compared to the innocence of the results proved. Trust that the whole course comes together in the end. Oh, and don’t procrastinate.

Some differences are that the first few weeks of 25b were more intense than 25a, after which it chilled out and by then I was spending less time on 25b than I had on 25a. I also found the readings to be more difficult in math 25b, but they were still useful to do after lectures on the corresponding material. But as long as you ask for help when you need it, and put time into the class, you will be completely fine. An AMAZING class!!! A very rigorous and broad introduction to real analysis that was always presented with enthusiasm and in an understandable and motivated way. If you are interested in higher–level math and want a solid understanding of real analysis and you are choosing between Maht 25b and Math 55b, I would definitely recommend this class. You can then later take other courses such as Math 122 that can cover the material that they go over in 55, but we don't in 25.

That being said, be prepared for a looooot of work as the problem sets can take many many hours to complete, and there are three exams (two midterms and a final). I really enjoyed doing the problem sets, but still, this course was by far my main commitment this semester.

Exams account for a very big part of the grade, and Wes does not curve, but he always looks for places to give partial credit where you've shown an understanding of the material and helpful ideas.

I cannot recommend this course more highly. I took this course to decide whether I want to concentrate in math and to see whether I enjoy this kind of ‘pure’, ‘abstract’ math and the answer to both turned out to be a definite yes. You will spend a moderate to high amount of time on this course, but if you start psets several days in advance, I believe the workload is not as bad as it is sometimes made out to be (some weeks, you could probably spend 8–10 hours on work outside of class). The difficulty is harder than 25a, and unfortunately the grading policy is very difficult (no curve, so standard 92.5 = A, 89.5 = A– etc.) which means you must do well in exams. However, I believe it is more important to consider what you get out of the course overall than your grade. You will learn SO MUCH material (I sense, considerably more than 21a/22b) and feel confident in the material through prepping for exams, that I feel all these positives outweigh a potential sacrifice to your grade (I may end up getting something in the B range, but I don’t care). There is no flexibility to pset deadlines at all, but in hindsight I think this is a good thing (I have 55’s extension policy is pretty/very lax(?), which can’t possibly be good for keeping up with material). Finally, one of the special parts of this course is the inclusion of analysis of multivariable calculus (standard first real analysis courses often only cover single–variable calculus, as is the case for Math 112), which shows how much content we cover (we even do Fourier analysis at the end of the course). Wes is also one of the best instructors you could ask for.
