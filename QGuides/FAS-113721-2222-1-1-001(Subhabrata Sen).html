I liked that I learned a lot of abstract concepts for the many stochastic processes that were covered, but I didn't like how abstract it was since I felt like I would not easily be able to apply or use the things I learned. I think the professor is really nice and genuinely cares for students, but I despised the fact that this class is graded based on 75% tests (2 midterms and a final). Given that tests are a huge portion of your grade, I feel like this course did not help me improve my problem solving skills at all. I felt like the support for actually solving problems related to the processes was lacking. Lectures were nice and organized and helped me understand 'big ideas', but there was a huge jump between understanding the concepts and solving the problems on problem sets and homeworks. The problem sets are really long at the beginning of the course, and the types of problems on the homeworks and the exams are so different. I joined this class right after taking STAT110 and felt like most people in this class were very experienced in math and stats, so I felt like a lot of skills were assumed. There weren't any practice exams for the tests and I just generally felt very lost in how to do well on the exams.

I took this as a fifth class (not the biggest mistake but whatever), and didn't really go to lecture. Subha is a wonderful professor and a kind person, but most of the lectures feel like they come straight from the textbook (a notable exception being the ones on Markov Chain Monte Carlo). This isn't really the worst thing – the textbook is wonderful in my opinion – but it makes the value proposition of a 9am lecture not great. Similarly for sections. That being said, I went to CA office hours regularly (shoutout to David and Christine) and found a ton of support and camaraderie in the class.

The psets are a good mix too. I personally like the probability side of the class (i.e. Poisson processes, Brownian motion, Ito calculus) far more than the computational side (Markov chains, Metropolis–Hastings, etc.), so I enjoyed the latter half of the class exponentially more than the front half. The first couple psets are quite bashy, and the fourth pset on MCMC is literally all in R (you should be ready to learn R, because you'll be using it liberally), but the rest are generally fine, and often quite elegant.

Finally, the exams are hard, and they're a big chunk of your grade. If I had known how hard, I would have seriously considered not taking the class – unlike the psets, which are almost entirely textbook questions, the exams are hard because you have to apply course concepts in ways that were really never explained or described in lecture/section. If you're not used to that (for ref., lots of people in the course took stat 210 before this or are upperclassmen stats concentrators), the exams will be hard – I'm a potential math concentrator who took three other math/stat classes, and they definitely were my hardest exams. Even having them be open–book exams was not nearly as much help as you'd think. If you're down to learn the material, don't let the exams discourage you. Just be warned.
