Here's the deal with GENED 1039: Manja is so kind and lovely, but her commitment to "open debate" is taken to the extreme. You are required to state "both sides" to every argument, and debates are always posed as theoretical with your position assigned to you. While this definitely makes the debate impersonal (with the intention of avoiding fights) I am not sure that is an altogether positive thing. I felt like it made the debates just sterilized talking points rather than peoples' passionate, actual opinions, which, while it would have made the class more controversial perhaps, would have been far more interesting. Another thing to note is that if you are paying attention at least a little bit to your experience at Harvard as an institution of higher education, then you are probably already aware of at least 70% of the course material (like affirmative action, student debt, declining interest in the humanities, hookup culture, etc) which means this course isn't going to really teach you anything new. Don't get me wrong, I enjoyed the novelty of dissecting higher ed from the belly of the beast, but I personally felt that I didn't necessarily need to take this course in hindsight given the fact that I am at least a little tuned–in to the goings–on of higher ed just by nature of being a current college student. Nonetheless, the teaching staff are absolutely incredible, and for a GENED, most people are highly engaged, participating in lecture and section, and asking insightful questions of the many intriguing guests. You will not be bored out of your mind in this class, but just know that it will not change/challenge your conceptions of higher ed, at least if you've been paying attention to current events a little bit. It's a solid class, but most of the hype you'll read about it is due to its teaching team, not necessarily the content.
