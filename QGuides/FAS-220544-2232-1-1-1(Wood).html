This class is amazing! The material is super cool, and it's a very different way to look at math that I found very fulfilling. The psets/projects are not bad at all (def less time than the average 100 level math class), and Prof Wood is a super nice guy. I would highly recommend to any math or CS concentrator looking for an elective.
For non–standard classes in the math department, this is definitely above–average. The material generally is interesting and the labs and problem sets are appropriately difficult. The class is definitely not easy – do not take if you're looking for a gem and be prepared to work, but it's not going to give you as many nightmares as something in the 130s, for instance. The projects are very open–ended – especially if you're a procrastinator or you don't have a lot of experience with programming, get started early, go to office hours, and make sure to pick something that works well in Lean. It can be easy to get stuck in a project which is not fun and also really hard to implement formally. Really, the main perk of this class is that it's super light on theoretical ideas and feels much more like CS exercises, and pairs well with a theory–intensive math class in the course schedule.
Phil is a great and kind teacher, who wants to help you through the material. However, the material itself (formalized mathematics) is procedural and some coding experience will help with doing assignments fast, even though you technically don't need any programming background to learn the content. In any case, the class will help you think about math in an even more structured and goal–oriented way.
Phil is a great teacher! But Lean is quite difficult and very very time–consuming. Would only recommend if you already know Lean.
Take this class if you like proof classes, or if you have heard of Lean and want to learn it but never got round to doing so. Even if you've never heard of Lean, I would still recommend it! (I didnt know what Lean was until the first day of class)
This is a great class. The topic of formal verification systems is super interesting, and the projects that you will complete for this class are very rewarding.
This is a wonderful class. I would recommend choosing project ideas that seem easier than you'd assume to be "twice as hard as a pset." Much of the work for a Lean proof of a theorem is building and structuring the machinery required for the proof. The psets have done this work for you. In the projects, you are expected to do this; this is kinda the point of the projects.

If you know some functional programming (specifically some language from the ML/Coq lineage), you'll have a nice time. Even if you don't, you don't need to understand the type theory underlying a proof assistant to use them. Now, using a proof assistant is a great way to learn type theory, so you can very well knock out two birds with one stone.
Math 161 was without a doubt the best class I've ever taken in the math department and the best–taught class I've taken in college. It is clear that Phil really cares about the class and its students. He invests a ton of time and effort into making the class feel seamless and is just generally a really good person. This class would not have existed or been the same without him and his incredible knowledge and interest in Lean. He's a very patient and kind teacher, and he is always going out of his way to help students and advocate for them. He also stayed late during office hours consistently and made sure every student was at a good stopping point by the end of his office hours session. His lectures are really fun and engaging. You can tell that he really loves what he's teaching and loves teaching in general by the way he explains things. Also, as a math and CS concentrator, I thought that Lean was soooo cool. You get to use code to write math proofs! It was like two worlds colliding for the first time. I might have written my thesis on theorem provers if I had taken this class junior year instead of senior year. I could not recommend this class enough. It completely changed my perspective on what math could be and left me with incredible memories and friends. Phil really did a great job of cultivating a community, and connecting with the people in the class makes it so much easier to connect with the material too. Also the assignments were super instructive but also accessible. The problems on the homework were actually based off of the topics and examples we covered during lecture. Both of the projects were so fun, interesting, and personally meaningful. I felt like I learned so much from them. I also appreciated that there were no exams. It took way the sense of impeding doom I sometimes feel during a semester. It made it so much easier to focus on and invest in what we were learning as well as who we were learning the material with. Overall, I am so grateful that I was able to take Math 161 during my final semester. I would highly recommend this class and any class that Phil teaches.
If you are interested at all in how to automatically verify proofs on a computer, then this course is for you! The Lean syntax seems very foreign at first, which might put some people off. However, after taking this course, I really feel like I've learned a new practical skill that will be useful down the line. Some basic computer skills are required, and it can sometimes be difficult to figure out how to use certain parts of the Lean math library. However, I found that the amount of work I put into the course was quite low compared to what I got out of it.
You will not learn too much about type theory from this course. However, it remains pretty interesting, as proving stuff begins to feel a lot like putting together pieces of a puzzle. Lean can be really frustrating to use at times, but you get familiar with it.
