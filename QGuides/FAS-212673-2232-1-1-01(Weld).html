What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Prof. Weld is a phenomenal and fun teacher, and it's an engaging course. She really encourages class discussions, and is a good discussion moderator. The assigned readings are thoughtful and interesting. Only take it if you have a deep interest in the topic, though—the course spends lots of time examining particular ways of memorializing the past (museums, archives, monuments, etc), and you'll enjoy the course the most if that sort of thing is your speed.
There is no room for discussion or coming to your own conclusions about anything you read in the class. In our first class meeting professor Weld starts the seminar by apologizing for the fact that the three readings for the week were all written by straight white men, she then assures us she has made sure that is not the case in any more week's readings. If being told how the United States, capitalism, and white people are all evil by a white woman wearing an N95 mask sounds like fun then by all means take this course.
Definitely take this class if you are a museum person or think about monuments and their role in society. Like any good history class, it leaves you with more questions than answers but it is really challenging intellectually and that is very important. Definitely skewed towards department people but don't have to be a history person to be successful. Also Professor Weld is one of the smartest and (lowkey) funniest instructors I've ever had. Sometimes it was hard for me to keep pace with her, but it was definitely rewarding when I pushed myself to meet her level. One of the best in the department in my opinion.
I would tell future students that this class is a strong introduction to the ideas of historical and collective memory, and allows for critical engagement with ideas of the nation–state and nationalism. The case studies and ideas explored in the course are especially useful in thinking about the making and sustenance of the myths through which the nation comes to be.
