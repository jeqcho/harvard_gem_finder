Labs are generally pretty fun, though your lab experience will range drastically depending on who your lab partner is; they change every week, so some weeks you might be doing partner programming with someone who doesn't do anything at all, and other weeks you might be doing partner programming with people who basically do the whole lab by themselves (which is really annoying! don't be this person!).

Although probably not a very popular opinion, I enjoyed taking CS51. This was my first CS class at Harvard; I did not take CS50, but I had a decent background in CS from high school.
A few things to know:
–> expect to put in a lot of time doing the reading for class (very helpful), solving psets (some of them are quite long), studying for exams (hard)
–> the format of the class is "flipped classroom", so you do the reading from the textbook before class and solve problems with a partner during class (sometimes annoying since it probably took me longer to understand certain things this way)
–> exams are hard and the allotted time is not enough!! (this might change when prof Shieber returns) Nevertheless, this year, the exams were particularly difficult and since they are mostly the only source of variation in grades (pretty much everybody does well on the psets), they will most certainly determine your grade. This is definitely not ideal since exams do not test style/design but rather speed (?).
–> OH are veeery helpful, but they also tend to be packed (especially the one right before the pset was due)
–> the first part of the class is quite chill, but the second half gets quite hard very quickly
–> the final project is not too bad (roughly 2 psets worth of work)
–> psets are graded quite randomly. Let me explain. each pset is worth 15 points – 10 points on correctness (your code is tested against some unit tests) and 5 on style/design. Those 5 points are given quite randomly since getting a 5 is nearly impossible (~5% of the class gets a 5), while nearly everybody else gets a 4 (~50–60%). Nevertheless, the amount of effort required for a high 4 (basically a 5 is they did not have the very annoying policy that only a few people get a 5) is way more than the effort required for a low 4.
