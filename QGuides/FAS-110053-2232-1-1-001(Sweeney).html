TAKE THIS COURSE! I was not going to stay in the class originally due to a lack of belief in my abilities but I went to Prof. Berlye's office hours (which were readily bookable) and she was able to dispel all of my worries about the course. There is a need for either a social science (qualitative or quantitative) or Statistics/CS background. Personal coding knowledge is not required for you to be successful in this class but a strong research background is very helpful. Also, if you do not have a coding background, partnering up with someone who does for the final is a great idea. The teaching staff (professors and TFs) is knowledgeable and engaging. The guest lecturers have a wealth of knowledge that really helps conceptualize the big–picture ideas in the course.
The class is great if you want to work on a project throughout the whole semester and maybe publish it.

HOWEVER, Professor Latanya Sweeney hardly ever turned up and was not responsive to emails at all. Given that this was a big part of why I took the course I found that disappointing. Leonie is fantastic but it was a shame Professor Sweeney was hardly in attendance.

Definitely definitely take it, it's perfect for anyone wanting to learn more about this intersection!
As someone studying Government and Statistics, I enjoyed that this course enabled me to apply my quantitative interests to a policy context. I think there's really something in this course for everyone, and you get out as much as you put in. If you've ever wanted to take a step towards tackling a real–world problem but have been lacking the support, this is definitely the class to do it the feedback you receive is excellent and course staff are always ready to help you. Coming out of a class with a final project you're genuinely interested in is also a major plus, and this course is designed to provide you with that. If you aren't too interested in the rest of the Gov department and have a STEM background, this is definitely a great way to explore what the department has to offer (though of course quantitative experience is not needed at all). It's also not much work outside the few weeks throughout the semester you'll be working on your unit assignments, so it's overall very chill.

Really good class, definitely should take it if you are interested in the intersection of tech and society. More student–driven than other classes in the series, but final project is a really cool opportunity to dive into something of interest.
Gov 1430 did an excellent job of highlighting ways in which technological innovation can sometimes have negative effects on society (e.g., privacy loss, misinformation spread, etc.). Even more, the course was effective at drawing on very current topic areas, which was very engaging, exciting, and fascinating. For example, a major focus area this spring was ChatGPT, which was just released in November!

The greatest strength of the course consisted of its teaching staff: Ms. Leonie Beyrle and Professor Latanya Sweeney did a fantastic job helping students navigate each unit, and they worked with their outstanding teaching fellows to provide very thorough, insightful, and constructive feedback on coursework. Lastly, the class assignments were very fun and engaging to work on. Because they also focused on very current/important topic areas, I felt like I was actually contributing to commentary on these subjects in a meaningful and insightful way.

I would highly recommend this class to students with an interest in technology and its impact on society. Absolutely zero technical expertise or experience is needed, as there is no coding required in the course. In short, take it!

Also, a HUGE SHOUTOUT to Grace Park for being one of the GREATEST teaching fellows on this campus. They are truly a legend of Harvard, and I cannot rate them highly enough!
