What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Anshu teaches this course from a CS/algorithms perspective and does very little physics. The course overviewed a lot of topics, many of them close to current research in quantum computing. As an undergrad, I felt like Anshu often breezed through certain things very quickly, which made it so that at points I only felt like I was getting about 50% of the ideas and getting lost on many of the details. He also has a tendency to state things in an imprecise and confusing way. So if you want to want to understand what is going on, be prepared to stop him and ask lots of questions.
This course doesn't seem to spend enough time on fundamentals and the foundations of quantum information/computing. Though the topics and scope are wide–ranging and interesting for this reason, many questions that regularly arise in class could be more easily resolved if we were careful with definitions and statements of results/theorems. The resources we had lacked clarity and consistent notation, including the homework assignments which sometimes contained consequential typos and had questions that we changed or recalled. Be prepared to reference other texts to better learn the material and formal justifications of results. To this end, even though some course staff/instructor(s) were wonderful and generous with their time, there was simply not enough time put into creating the assignments, the final, and course resources overall.

This includes changes to the homework, office hour changes with last–minute notice, final project prompts which were not viable, etc. Some of these are matters of logistics, but overall, this course needed much more time and care to better represent a field that is ultimately interesting and important. The feedback we received on homework assignments was not very helpful for better understanding the material either. The feedback we (at least those I have spoken to) received largely overlooked significant subtleties which made the problems challenging and instead focused on minor details which were largely independent of the core concepts, to meet the assignment.

All in all, the material of the course is very rewarding to study and it might be more interesting to those with a background in CS. But, ultimately this is not a course that necessarily rewards rigor and building a good foundation for understanding quantum information/computing for those not already studying/working in this field. You will get just as much out of this class as you put in, and it would be worth considering just self–studying this material using the text by Nielsen and Chuang.
Do not take it if you expect to learn quantum computing from a physics perspective. The assignments were very doable, but they barely taught me anything. It was a very disappointing experience that taught me nothing about the physics behind it.
I think course material depends heavily on who teaches it, since QIS combines both physics and computer science. I really wanted to learn the physics of QIS but learned the CS side instead because of Anurag's background. However, this perspective was really interesting and I'm still interested in learning more.

Background required: I felt that the prereqs for this class were misleading (which were know quantum mechanics at undergrad or grad level, linear algebra at level of 21b, and how to write proofs). I wouldn't say you even need to take quantum mechanics or know physics for this version of QIS. Instead, you need to know some TCS, information theory, and the math of tensors –– none of which was covered. We did have a section 0 that might have covered some material, but most of us didn't know about it because it was announced on Ed as a post (so none of us got notifications about it) 10 minutes before it took place. There was no announcement about it during lecture, which relates to my following comments about the course structure/staff etc.

There were a lot of things that made taking this class frustrating. Anurag is a kind professor and knows his stuff. However, lectures were hard to follow (handwriting hard to follow; order of writing on blackboard was not left–to–right/top–to–bottom but more circular) and this made it difficult for doing scribe notes. Homework was only 3 problems and 15 points, and grading was inconsistent. You could make a computational error and lose one point. Or you could do half a problem but lose only one point. Or you could handwave an entire problem, not have your solution be correct, and still get full points. The inconsistent grading wouldn't have been a big deal if each homework wasn't worth only 15 points. Additionally, we used Ed for course announcements. But some important announcements (like section 0, office hour time changes, etc) were announced as posts, so you never got email notifications about them. A lot of times, you would retroactively find out about something. Finally, we were given a very short timeframe to do the final project. Discussing the final project was pushed back by several lectures, until there was basically no time left in the semester: introduce project and then the project proposal due 2 lectures later, draft presentation the next week, final presentation the second week.

Overall, I felt this course has a lot of interesting material and promise, but would benefit from restructuring things like course timeline and grading. I do not regret taking it and did learn quite a bit.
This class has a lot of potential and would advise students to shop this class!

My experience is that there is a pretty large spread of the skill/ knowledge base of the students in the class. I think the material could've been covered more slowly and more intuitive understandings should've been built up, but also I think some of the students in the class have been exposed to a lot of this material before.

I think this class requires a higher level of understanding of linear algebra and tensor calculus than I expected so I would advise reading up and making sure you at least know the basics really well to follow the concepts and intuitions in this class. Neilsen and Chuang is also a good resource to build foundations before taking this class.
