Comments
The course is quite mathematical, so expect to be working on problem sets every week and reading through the notes to supplement what is covered in the lectures. That said, I know feel as though I have come out with a solid understanding of the tools that go into the big results of the class (e.g. Godel's Incompleteness Theorems), which is exactly what I was hoping for going into the class!
This was a hard class, but it was so much fun. The course staff know that the course is hard and they will help you and be flexible with deadlines if you ask for help. Although the course was challenging, the staff are so supportive and the material is so interesting that each problem set feels more like a series of brain puzzles than rigorous proofs.
On a high level, this course teaches fascinating, mind–bending material. The notes provided are thorough and very well–written, although I recommend re–reading them a couple of times to fully grasp what they say. I found the problem sets to be (unnecessarily) hard to do on your own, although they did succeed in improving my grasp of the material. Doug, the TF, was very helpful, and Goldfarb himself is extremely enthusiastic about the material, and an expert in the area. The exams were hard, though, and I wonder if I would have appreciated the course more had I taken it P/F so as to be able to focus on (and enjoy even more) some of the bigger ideas without getting caught up in the weeds of understanding every single thing in the notes so as to not lose points on a problem set or exam. Overall, though, I have few regrets about taking this course –– it expanded my mind and is very cool material. If you're at all interested in the philosophy of Math, take it.
This class was so interesting. The material (mainly Godel's Incompleteness Theorems, Tarski's Theorem, and computability theory) are things that are often mentioned in other courses (like mathematical analysis or theoretical computer science) but taken for granted. Seeing where these things come from, and deriving them yourself, was so fascinating. I cannot recommend this course more strongly, it was one of the most intellectually stimulating classes I've seen at Harvard. Having background in writing proofs is essential, though.
This was a great class that reached many interesting results and also allowed me to think critically about the foundations of mathematics. Take this course if you are at all interested in the subject matter, even as a non–mathematics concentrator I found this to be a rewarding experience.
Certainly worth taking. You will learn a lot. As long as you put in enough time to do the p–sets properly each week, by the end of the course you will feel that you have learned a lot even if much of it didn't make any sense the first time you heard it in the lecture. The textbook is very good as well. Problem sets and exams are interesting and well thought out. I would recommend it, although I am not sure if and when this course will ever be offered again considering professor Goldfarb is retiring soon.
