CS 181 starts with simple models like regression, but quickly covers a lot of cool and useful machine learning models like neural networks, latent variable models, and RL––the latter content being typically introduced in graduate level courses. This course is more theory heavy than code heavy; I did a lot more math than coding, and we don’t implement every single model that we covered in class. ML is one of the most satisfying applications of statistics that I’ve seen, featuring a lot of creative and nice mathematical algorithms––after taking this class, you’ll be able to code machine learning models that perform very nontrivial tasks, like recognizing handwritten numbers or playing flappy–bird.

The most important prerequisite for this class is Stat 110––almost everything in machine learning uses statistics uses machine learning, so make sure to have a solid foundation in probability. Math 21a/b are also important to understand the mathematical derivations; 21a is important for optimization (trying to find the “best” model), and 21b is important for efficiently manipulating data (which usually comes in the form of vectors and matrices). CS 51/61 is not a hard prereq, and the programming in this course is not particularly difficult compared to these two courses. The class is taught in python.

I have mixed feelings about 181. I'm not sure how much of all of this is because of the way CS 181 works or because of how Weiwei was teaching it, and I believe Finale is returning to teach the class next year, so take all of this with a grain of salt. As someone with a fair amount of background in ML that I had picked up on my own here and there, I took the class because I wanted to learn the basics of (statistical) machine learning well and rigorously rather than diving straight into a more specialized class like 229br. I'm not sure if that latter hope was fulfilled. On the one hand, I think it did a good job at introducing us to a wide range of different approaches and techniques in machine learning. Weiwei's lecture videos were great at giving some intuition for different techniques and provided nice, graphical examples that were complemented by more technical notes. On the other hand, the class felt disorganized at times, grading was slow (but ended up being very generous), the p–sets were surprisingly short, a large emphasis was put on interpretation of models and results (which is certainly useful for applied work but can also be derived more generally if you were to do the math really well), you had to rush through the midterms, and the course schedule (including assignments and how much they affected your final grade) were changed throughout the semester, sometimes at relatively short notice. The level of technicality and rigor varied greatly from topic to topic: for some (like Bayesian inference), it was sufficient to know "that it's difficult and a few reasons for why it's difficult," for others, like the derivation of the ELBO, we were only asked to explain the derivation steps rather than be able to derive them, and for the universal approximation theorem of neural networks, the provided link to the proof required a lot of background in measure theory which I believe made it inaccessible to most people in the class. So overall a decent class, especially if you haven't had a lot of prior exposure to machine learning and/or are interested in applied machine learning. If you're really into the math behind statistical ML, you might be better off with a class that uses ESL as its textbook or just by reading ESL for yourself.
