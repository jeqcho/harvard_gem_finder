TAKE THIS COURSE. I've only taken eight courses at Harvard so far, but this has been my favorite one, with Math 101 being a close second.

Every component of this course was wonderful. Professor Simmons' lectures are engaging, exciting, clear, and I genuinely only checked my phone a couple of times during lecture across the entire semester. They were that captivating.

Almost all of the readings were extremely doable, roughly 1–2 hours per reading/class (so, 2 or 3 times per week). More than doable, the readings were like the lecturesengaging and exciting. Not always clear, but that's what the lectures are for!

Office hours were one of my favorite aspects of the course; I liked them so much that I ended up going to almost every one I could physically attend. I sometimes came with questions prepared, either about concepts I wanted to clarify further or curiosities that went beyond the lecture. Sometimes, though, I just showed up ready to hear what other people had to ask about/say. Because it's an intro class, office hours had people from all (prospective) concentrationsphysics, neuro, math, philosophy, and more. This made them a million times better, since everyone would bring their own unique perspective (either based on their concentration or other interests) to the discussion.

Finally, assignments were extremely manageable and rewarding. The courses is not too writing–intensive. You will write no more than a total of 3,000 words across the entire semester, split up into four short (250–500 word) essays and one longer (1,250 word) essay.

I had always been slightly interested in philosophy, but I had never done much reading before, and I had not read a page of metaphysics. I was totally fine, and came out of the class loving metaphysics, wanting to take as many more philosophy courses as possible, and potentially concentrate.

DO take this class if you:
– Have no background in philosophy
– Have some background in philosophy
– Have a lot of background in philosophy
– Are curious about philosophy
– Are not curious about philosophy
– Have read any/most/all of the readings in the syllabus (yes, even you! You'll get so much out of Professor Simmons lectures and office hours)
– Are a first–year, sophomore, junior, senior, grad student, or any other kind of learner
– Are concentrating in philosophy
– Are not concentrating in philosophy
– Are curious about the world around you
– Are curious about what it means to be you
– Haven't taken it before

(Maybe) DON'T take this class if you:
– Have taken it before (honestly, you could still get a bunch more out of it a second time)

Disclaimer #1: this class is geared to people who are totally new to philosophy, so if you've read a bunch of metaphysical Descartes, Hume, or Kant before, some of the information might be redundant. However, this introductory–mindedness allows you to get as much out of the class as you put into it. If you just go to lecture and section without doing any of the readings or going to office hours, you'll still get a bunch out of the class. However, you will get so, so much more if you do all (or most, like me!) of the readings and go to office hours with questions or just to listen to other peoples' thoughts.

Disclaimer #2: be prepared to rethink everything you thought you knew about the world around you and what it means to be you.
