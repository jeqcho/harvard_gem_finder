What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
EAS soph tutorial is a fairly manageable class. The readings are not excessive and they are often quite interesting. I think both the greatest pro and the greatest con is how each week/lecture is taught by a different professor. The pro is that you get a feel for what all the EAS profs each specialise in. This could be helpful in helping you decide on whose classes to take / a potential thesis advisor. The con is that each lecture/week seems disjointed and unrelated. You finish the class feeling like you know a little bit about many niche fields instead of a strong background in East Asia. I suppose that is the point of the sophomore tutorial – to be a launching pad for other classes. Tbh just write about things you are interested in, do all the readings and try to stay engaged with the material, and you will get what you can from the class.
I thought this was a great sophomore tutorial. Not only did it challenge me with the subject matter, but also I felt equally well exposed to the variety of topics that intersected with EAS. I also thought the class gave students a great opportunity to learn about who is in the department, and I feel more at ease reaching out to a random EAS professor. Also, as an East Asian student in the class, I thought it did a pretty good job educating about other EA cultures. Although, I wish there was more information about non–Chinese, Korean, and Japanese cultures. Definitely recommend taking it if you are interested in EAS, or just want a class to provide you with alternative perspectives.
This course was amazing since I was able to learn so much and be a part of a great community. The course really tries to disprove misconceptions about East Asia and to introduce you to the various methods/approaches that exist within the department. Each week a new lecturer will come and talk about their work. This means that if you do not like a topic, it will quickly change, which is good. I really enjoyed the first five weeks since it focused on critiquing and re–evaluating definitions of East Asia and East Asian Studies. I enjoyed how each weekly assignment required you to try different skills (visual analysis, literary analysis, poem writing, anthropological thick description, making a StoryMap, etc.). I will say that the second half of the course had a lot more literature based lectures, which for me were not as interesting, but still felt like I learned stuff from them. The main professor, Melissa McCormick, is so nice and very helpful. She was always a good source of information and support, especially for the final paper. Highly recommend this course to anyone interested in East Asia.
Lots of interesting guest lecturers.
This course was an amazing way to learn more about East Asian Studies. As an applied math concentrator, it was super cool to learn that even just having an interest in East Asia meant I had a place in the class. I highly recommend this class if you are even remotely interested in East Asia Studies.
Because this is a survey course, each week covers a different facet of East Asia ranging from writing systems to colonial history. However, I believe this is the downfall of this course. If you are someone that is interested in East Asia because of a specific facet (e.g. pop culture of East Asia, economics of East Asia, political science of East Asia, etc) then you will be disengaged for 95%–99% of the course. The only saving grace is that the midterm and final papers can be on topics of your own choosing. Otherwise, you will have to sit through lectures on topics that you have no interest in.
