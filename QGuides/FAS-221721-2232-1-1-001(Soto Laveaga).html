What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
This course was interesting, but it caused a lot of confusion and frustration because of improper communication and disorganization. The professor was unorganized and difficult to reach with questions or concerns, which was a big barrier for me and other students. I relied a lot on communication with my classmates to gain information because the professor was inaccessible and sometimes reacted with irritation when students reached out with questions. The readings, content, and discussion were interesting, but the disorganization made it difficult to enjoy them.
Regrettably, this course struggled to provide meaningful engagement or coherence on the subject of “water.” The enterprising nature of the literature we were provided (“Does water have a soul, or a body? Can water transcend physical properties and hold spiritual significance?” Is water truly lifeless?”) hindered the development of meaningful critical discussion and left us students feeling adrift in a sea (pun intended) of fragmented scholarship. If you’re seeking a substantive exploration of water as an undergraduate, perhaps consider alternative educational opportunities outside of the classroom, far away from Harvard, that offer a more coherent and meaningful understanding of environmental issues writ large.

In fairness to the wonderful History of Science Department and to the professor, it is worth acknowledging that devoting an entire semester to the study of water poses inherent challenges. There’s only so much you can say about this topic before having to grasp at ideological straws; by the fourth or fifth week, it became increasingly evident to many of us the course was stretching thin and struggling to sustain rigorous analysis. It soon became a running joke for enrollees to figure out what we were going to say next about “the multifaceted nature of water” as we walked into the classroom.
