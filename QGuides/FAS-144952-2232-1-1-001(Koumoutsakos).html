You've probably heard horror stories about this course from past years, and there's a good chance you'll have to take this course for a degree requirement anyway, so I first want to say that the course has improved substantially from last year. The lectures have improved somewhat, even with the many tangents Petros goes down, and the TFs are really quite helpful in making sure the concepts are clear. Most importantly, the grading policies have become much more lenient, with the course staff giving everyone a free 1–week extension on a problem set, one exam drop and one problem set drop, and all of these made the course a relatively low–stress experience. The content is also some of the most interesting in any engineering or physics course I've ever taken here and is super useful for a variety of disciplines (yes, even for bioengineers), and if you put in the time to pore over the lecture slides, stare at the equations until they make sense, and read over textbook examples to understand how to use the equations, you'll get a good feeling for just how elegant and conceptually simple yet powerful fluid mechanics is. The labs and final project in particular genuinely made me want to incorporate fluid mechanics into my future research because of how cool they were.

That being said, most of this understanding came from me investing my own time to understand the material outside of class. The lectures move too quickly and have too many digressions to get a good grasp of the concepts in 75 minutes, and it certainly doesn't help that they're at 9am. Sections are often left to pick up the pieces and try to cover both concepts and problem–solving instead of just problem–solving as they should. While the problem sets were mostly painless, I really wished they covered more real–world situations so I felt better prepared for future work. Exams were of reasonable difficulty but far too long for anyone to complete in one class period. And while I appreciate them reducing the weekly labs down to 2 labs, I was left wanting much more computational fluid dynamics (CFD) experience to feel better prepared for industry and research, for which CFD experience is often critical.

This is all to say that there's no need to worry much about doing well in the course anymore, and if you're willing to put in the extra time to really learn the material on your own it's intensely rewarding, but this course could be much better if the course staff took some time to organize the lectures more strictly and include more CFD practice.
