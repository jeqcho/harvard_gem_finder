I think overall math 101 is a cool course, but there are a number of things that I was frustrated with regarding how it was run in Spring 2023. I'd like to preface everything by saying I think Janet Chen is an awesome and really kind person though. At the very beginning of the course, some of the things you do will feel very strange. Writing a proof is a very particular skill and if you've never done it before, it can feel sort of unusual. My advice at the beginning would be to practice, practice, and practice more! Really work hard at the beginning to fully understand what is going onit will serve you well for the remainder of the course. One thing you might notice early on though is that some people have stronger backgrounds in proof writing than others. I was sometimes working with people during problem sessions who knew what things like "Without loss of generality" were and it felt discouraging to be writing proofs with people who already knew all of the steps and the general formulation of the proof. I think if this course wants to fully serve as an intro to proof based math, it needs to be designed particularly for people with absolutely no proof experience. Knowing how to write a proof is essential to this course and having knowledge of that prior to the course is a huge disadvantage for other students. The anti–requisites should be more enforced in order to make sure all students have an equal background coming into the course. In terms of the grading of the course, I think that an intro course should really focus on growth and allow people the opportunity to progress as the semester goes on (some course have this built in to how the course is scored). It at times did not feel like there was this emphasis on the grading for the course. In fact, I'm of the belief that an intro to pure math course should be scored in a way that is encouraging for students to continue to take challenges and take more pure math courses, but it often did not feel like this was the case. The grading at times felt like it was very strict and nitpicky and there weren't a lot of opportunities to earn points back (on the problem sets there was, but I think on the exams there should have been). Having the opportunity for extra credit would be an awesome idea for a course like math 101 where people are so new to what is being taught. The final paper in particular felt like an illustration of the course instructors being unnecesarily strict with their expectations. Firstly, it's a lot to ask students to write a math paper and take a math final. In addition to this, the paper was graded with a really high standard which felt unfair because this put students who had knowledge of math topics outside of the course at a clear advantage. Also, the paper was the first time a lot of us ever wrote a pure math paper, so grading it so strictly without at least having multiple rounds of edits or multiple opportunities to conference or even a practice paper earlier in the semester feels like an unfair expectation. I know that grades shouldn't be the foremost concern when deciding what courses to take, but I think people should take challenges and do things like take a pure math course as a non–math concentrator. In order to incentivize people to do this, you need to create a course centered around growing and one that eliminates inequities that can exacerbate the challenges and frustrations that come along with the course. One other thing I'd note are that it's pretty frustrating to have timed exams in a course like math 101. I think proof writing takes time and I often felt like there was time pressure on the exam, and this made things more challenging.

I know a lot of this has been about some of the ways this course can be improved, so I just wanted to take the opportunity to highlight some of the positives of this course. Firstly, it will likely challenge you in ways you've never been challenged. This is a good thing! It is a rewarding intellectual experience to think through a lot of the problems in the course. Secondly, the course staff is great! Janet is a great person and explains concepts very clearly. The CAs are awesometry out all the CA's office hours and find which CAs you most vibe with / understand their teaching style. Doing this as early in the semester will definitely benefit you.

Overall, I would say I definitely enjoyed learning a lot of the concepts in math 101 and am glad to have faced a challenge with the course material. My TLDR of where I think this course can improve is: make sure this is really at the intro level, eliminate inequities by being stricter about the anti–requisites, create a culture that makes people want to continue taking higher level math classes and challenge themselves. If you're really excited about this course material and willing to put in the effort this course requires, definitely don't let these concerns stop you since math 101 can be a rewarding experience.
