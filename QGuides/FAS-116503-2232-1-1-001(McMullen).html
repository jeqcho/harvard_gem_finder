I think people's experiences with 123 depends largely on their interests and their background. I have some friends who have described the content in 123 as the most beautiful math they've seen so far, while others have expressed more dismal views toward the topics of the class. Personally, I took 123 solely to satisfy the algebra requirement for math joint concentrators, and while I thought some applications of topics (e.g., Galois theory for the unsolvability of the quintic or the norm in quadratic number fields to solve Diophantine equations), I found much of the course content broadly not very interesting.

Nonetheless, the content in 123 feels important to know as a math concentrator, and I'm glad that I chose to take 123 to satisfy the algebra requirement. One of the main factors was probably Prof. McMullen, who is a captivating lecturer and makes the content feel tangible (e.g., through geometric explanations of algebraic topics) and clear. His exams were fair (potentially on the easier side if anything), problem sets were really helpful, and the course notes he posts provide some of the best explanations I could find. He's also just a great person, and it's really clear that he cares a lot about teaching and his students.

I'd recommend spending time to really understand the course content and the problem sets. I often found myself thinking that I understood content before the next lecture really demonstrated that I indeed did not understand the content.

Ring theory and Galois theory sound really interesting if you don't know anything about them, but in practice they're more dry than what I was expecting. In particular, I mainly took this class to learn Galois theory, yet we only spent 3 actual lectures on it and we moved through the core parts of the theory at lightning pace. I wished more time was spent going through Galois theory since it is definitely the hardest and most interesting part of the main material. Ring theory is not super exciting. Beyond the material, this class had less than 7 people who came directly from 122 and was instead filled with a majority of people who took/ were taking 55. So, unless you are a 55er, know that your classmates will probably be smarter and better at math than you. I'm someone who finds camaraderie and friendship in the ''community struggle" part of doing problem sets, but this class didn't offer any of that. I did almost all of the problem sets alone or with a CA and a maximum of 2–3 other people at office hours. It seemed to me that the overwhelming majority of this class, i.e. the 55ers, found the material to be very easy but everyone who didn't take 55 thought it was fairly difficult. For the non–genius' like me who enjoy math because it is interesting, it is demoralizing (to say the least) when the freshman in the course get 100s on all of the exams and problem sets while you are fighting for every point. Overall, I liked the material but the class is not catered towards people coming from 122. Rather, this seemed more like a class for the people coming from 55 who know they want to pursue graduate level studies in mathematics.

Definitely utilize office hours and/or math night when completing every single PSET. This course, like all others, is based in mastery of one topic before you move on to the next. If you don't know your stuff, you're going to be in trouble, because the new material is only ever built on the previous material; if you're like me who did that, you will definitely struggle in the last quarter of the course. So just practice consistency, read the textbook (I did not do this) sections before each class (despite how lengthy they might be), and start the PSETs early. The midterm is extremely fair and is a straight application of all the important definitions you should know, problems that you've done from problem sets, and major theorems that you should be able to prove having completed just the first half of the course (which is largely a review/enrichment of Algebra I and some Field Theory). As an added caveat, I would also only take this course if Prof. McMullen is teaching it and you have a good idea that the TF staff will be good that semester as well, as a lacking in either would probably make taking this class a nightmare.
