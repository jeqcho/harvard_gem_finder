ECON 1343 is better described as an ideological passion–project than a class.

On the one hand, the course does an excellent job at synthesizing different disciplines from history to politics to economics. The use of evolutionary psychology to understand development incentives is particularly deep and is one of my main takeaways from the class. Matt clearly cares deeply about his students' well–being and the material which he teaches. The teaching staff is excellent; the exams are fair; and the class provides many avenues for intellectual growth.

However, the ideological nature of ECON 1343 detracts from the educational experience. The class harshly critiques the economics literature that would oppose its agenda (e.g., growth theory, 1st Welfare Theorem / individual optimization) but takes premises from work that supports its agenda as basically true (e.g., income–well–being–saturation, ROI on PSH, cost estimates of interventions). While I very much agree with the critiques that the class provides, these critiques should be presented as opinions, not facts.

My other criticisms are that I fear that the course seems to exhibit the phenomenon of "when you're a hammer, everything looks like a nail" with regards to a number of core concepts. For example, I fear that the course gives the impression that different viewpoints are "immodest claims," when they are, in fact, just valid differing viewpoints. And, the class takes Foucault's social construction of reality as true, when it is just one social science theory, and seems to apply it to examples that feel like a stretch (e.g., causal link between US–USSR competition and the emphasis of individual choice First Welfare Theorem).

In short, the class seems to want to guide you to a set of answers and actions, when the purpose a class should be teach in a balanced manner. I think this class has great potential and awesome teaching staff, but in my opinion, it should be more balanced and less ideological in the future.
