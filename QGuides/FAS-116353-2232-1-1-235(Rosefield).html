This is one of the best expos classes! Hannah has developed a very interesting curriculum with lots of great texts to help you think about money and the economy in a new way. Our first text in the class was the film The Big Short, and we also got to read some interesting pieces on the banking pipeline at Harvard, paid surrogacy, wages for housework and more that all lead to interesting class discussions. The assignment structure of this class is also great. We started with a 4–6 page close analysis paper on the big short, which is typical for expos, but instead of doing the 8–10 page research paper as our last assignment like most expos classes, we did it as our second paper. Then for our final project, we did a podcast instead of the 8–10 page paper, which made for a much less stressful finals week. Hannah also gave good feedback on all assignments, pushing us to create strong, unfolding arguments. overall, would highly recommend this course for its interesting content and course structure.
Take it! Hannah is a really incredible teacher who is super available outside of class and is always willing to help you reach that next level. Her grading is certainly not a breeze, but it's fair and if you put in hard work, you will be duly rewarded for doing so. In Expos especially, you get out what you put in, so do the readings and really engage with the course material. Especially in a small class, the opportunity to learn from your peers is next to none! Expos is a community so take advantage.
It is important to have decent writing skills. I thought I had okay writing skills but struggled with being the writer Hannah wanted me to be. Some finance/econ background may be helpful. I don't think it is essential, but as a person who didn't have this experience, I felt left out of the convo a lot by my more economically–inclined peers. I learned how to write with better research and more cohesively to present a novel idea. I think if you want an expos class that actually improves your writing, then this one will do it. However, it is definitely a hard graded expos, so if you want to cruise, this one is not it.
The topic of the course really does not matter very much. In the end, the value of expos comes in your preceptor. If your preceptor gives good feedback, the course is an excellent expository class.
Hannah is literally the most amazing and insightful preceptor. The grading is kinda harsh though

I don't understand how my writing improved while taking this course. We had discussions about many topics and papers, but what I learned about how to write well (the purpose of this class) was mainly accrued through osmosis. By this I mean that we were exposed to texts that were written by influential people (not renowned for their writing itself but rather their ideas) such as Michael Sandel, but I can't remember a single class during which we focused on craft or style or anything of that sort. We did spend a day on citation formatting. It seems as though this class is a 'choose–your–own–adventure' in regard to how much you actually improve your writing, because that improvement would be nearly entirely self–directed. Class was dominated by people sharing their opinions about a variety of topics, but such discussions, although interesting, did not actually contribute to the missions of the expository writing class.

What I will say is that I learned to use evidence for EVERYTHING, but it seems as though this course was more focused on providing warrants for every single one of your claims instead of broader learning about writing. If you are already a 'good' writer, take this class. But don't take it for the cool name and the movie. Having the research paper be the second essay was too much work in an incredibly short period of time, and it led to half–baked papers. Further, the research assignment itself was poorly explained which caused confusion within the class. I don't understand how Hannah grades and it seems vaguely opinion–based.

I will say generally to first years selecting their expos: GET CLOSE WITH YOUR CLASS! My favorite memories from this class were going to berg after class and sitting around and chatting. If your expos ends around 6 pm, do an 'expos dinner' as we called it. It's a really special time and you have to do this, regardless of the class that you choose/rank. I have the fondest memories from my expos class (not academic!) because we got close as a group outside of class, just by walking out of section together. It's truly special and one of my favorite first year moments.
If you're looking for the Expos section with the easiest readings/material/grading this is not it – there are much gemmier sections out there (I believe that the grading was fair and reflective of effort though). But if you are looking for an Expos section that will truly help you become a better writer, take this class! Hannah is a brilliant and incredibly understanding preceptor who is always open to talk, and she also does a great job at creating a welcoming class environment. Some people suggest that you shouldn't choose Expos sections based on how interested you are in the material because the class won't actually be about the topic anyway – I disagree. Being interested in the topic makes the writing assignments much more enjoyable, and generally makes Expos less of a drag. American Money has some incredibly interesting material which really forced me to think about the way I viewed money, and the relationship between money and ethics in society.
The teacher for this class is a tough grader, but she is a wonderful person. She is very lenient and understanding about deadlines and other commitments, and the workload is not heavy. The only caution is her grading.
