Professor Bernhard Nickel is one of the best Philosophy professors at Harvard. You would be hard pressed to find someone more dedicated, more welcoming, or more perspicuous. So, absolutely take this class. The philosophy of language has implications for the rest of analytic philosophy. Once you enter, you'll find that this subfield has contemporary stakes –– in domains such as pornography, Twitter, and hate speech. The material is knotty –– you'll be bewildered plenty of times –– but you have the best guide leading you through. Professor Nickel has a way of breaking down the most complex material into succinct explanations. Additionally, expect to find amazing intellectual diversity among the students. My year, we had, among the undergraduate and grad students, people from mathematics, computer science, economics, physics, biology, linguistics, engineering, history, and philosophy. That's another tip: for anyone who pooh–poohs the humanities, just enter the philosophy department and find a cognitive link between ALL disciplines. These classes are challenging but incredibly rewarding. They will ground your thinking in the fundamentals of logical rigor –– a quality indispensable to any serious academic work.

Very difficult unless you have some adequate PHIL background or semantics background. I went into this class with LING106, and I think maybe that it wasn't really enough (for context, I have no analytic philosophy background). But the material is interesting! Even if some readings seemed tough or unmotivated, there's lots of variation in what people found interesting in readings/topics (outside of a few lectures/sections where everyone seemed confused), which I think is pretty good for keeping discussion engaging and productive. Prof Nickel also has a tendency to cold–call, so be prepared for that (and, I guess, be clear about your pronouns, if you think that this may be an issue). Prof Nickel is accommodating and open to student feedback. Also, go to Prof Nickel's OH because there may be a dog (Cooper, I think?) and he's very helpful with clarifying questions and objections to the material.
This course will be hard for some, but definitely informative and exciting in every class. You need to pay attention to every class and make sure to take all the detail because the class is wonderfully structured.
Quite a niche field, so would advise briefly looking up the authors to get a sense of what the material's like before diving into it. Prof Nickel puts a lot of thought into designing and delivering the class, and both him and the TF were super passionate, knowledgeable and make it a point to make themselves available to you. Grading is on the harsh side, but really helps to discipline you as a thinker and writer. How much you get out of the class has so much to do with how much you put into it.
Welcome to the thunderdome.

This is a very cool class! PHIL 147 gives a very wide overview on the philosophy of language, which is a very rich, very interesting, very deep subject.

Readings: Range from hard to very hard. You'll have to read everything at least twice to understand it. Prof. Nickel's materials are fantastic, and really help you understand in more detail. Especially helpful for the esoteric, tough to read stuff like Quine.

Assignments: Essys in this class are probably the biggest downside. While the topics are interesting, you will walk away feeling that the grades you got did not at all scale with the effort you put in. After doing not great on the first, I put in probably more effort than I've put into any essay at Harvard into the second, only to come away with an evern worse grade. Justin the TF gives fantastic feedback, but the grading timing is really not great (I got my first essay back at 10 PM on the night of the add–drop deadline), and you'll often feel that you deserved better given the quality of your arguments and the amount of work put in. Sadly, it's just one of the things you have to deal with.

Section: Pretty chill! Justin is very smart, you'll learn a ton talking to him.

Overall: A fun and very interesting class. Unless you need it for a requirement as I did, I'd recommend taking it pass fail. This way, you get all of the learning benefits without having to deal with the grading and general administrative stuff.
This course is rather difficult, which makes it such an engaging and fulfilling experience! Do NOT take it as a fifth class or just to listen to cool lectures, because the lectures really don't make sense without the readings, and the readings are often fairly dense and complex. If you want to engage deeply with how we use language, though, you should definitely take this course. Professor Nickel is the best, the theories we discussed are so cool, and section was always a great time. This is one of the best classes at Harvard if you're willing and able to work very hard to understand some confusing and awesome philosophy.
