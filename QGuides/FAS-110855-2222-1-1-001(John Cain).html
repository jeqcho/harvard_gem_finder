Make sure to do practice problems and really drill down the concepts you study in class. Take time to start the PSETS ahead of time, and make sure to go to office hours.
Math 25b with Wes is a wonderful class. The material is so insanely well taught. Wes's strength is in analysis so he motivates examples and provides intuition really well. The lectures are very good, and he answers all questions during the lectures. However, this results in the fact that most classes end around five minutes over time, and additional videos may be posted if the material isn't completely covered. Also, this class was a 9AM three times a week (with no recordings), so just make sure you're prepared for this. The community is really great, and many students in this class actually did not come from a high school math background but were more so interested in fields that require knowledge of real analysis. Because of this, a collaborative environment was fostered. I think it's really important to find people to pset with, if not the most important. Definitely definitely attend Wes's office hours. He is an amazing professor but also such a kind person, and it's very nice to get to know him! He can also clarify gaps in your knowledge about lecture material. For psets, I think it's more helpful to go to the CAs office hours since you can collaborate with students, but Wes gives great intuition behind how to solve the problems. This class is extremely rewarding, in my opinion, if you put in the time. Because the psets are not graded harshly, it felt that a lot of my energy was spent truly learning the material instead of perfecting pset solutions like in some other classes. Overall, this class is tough but rewarding. Make sure you're interested in this class for some reason, make sure you allocate enough time for the class and think about what you want to gain from this class before taking it!
This course is quite difficult, but definitely worth it. Wes is a fantastic lecturer, and the psets are fairly interesting, though quite difficult. It will definitely be a time commitment, but if you are interested in learning real analysis definitely take it. You will learn material from multivariable calc and gain a much stronger intuition for single variable calculus. It gives the theory behind a lot of things that were taken for granted in my high school calc classes. A word of warning: the exams are extremely difficult and not curved as far as I know, so if you are someone who is just looking for a good grade, probably don't take this class. All in all though, I feel that this class was one of my most worthwhile this semester, since I got to know a lot of cool people and learned to think in new ways.
Math 25b IS A BLAST! The second half of math 25 covers real analysisput very simply, calculus with a lot of formalism. In this class, youll ask things like what does it mean for a function to be continuous? or why is the mean value theorem true? or what kind of functions are not integrable? I think its one of the most foundational and important college math classes; not only is it fundamental to the study of subjects like complex analysis and economics, it introduces you to a lot of important mathematical concepts and definitions that you will use for the rest of your career as a mathematician. Definitely take this course if you took Math 25a; I actually enjoyed the content in 25b much more than 25a!

For those who took linear algebra in the fall/previously:

If you took Math 25a, then taking Math 25b next will be the natural flow of things (this is what I did). In 25a you learned the basics of proof writing, but in 25b youll learn to write proofs more eloquently, and also encounter proofs that are much longer compared to those experienced in 25a (so you can build your proof writing endurance, which is an important skill to have!) The linear algebra concepts that you learned in 25a will also appear a lot in the second half of the class when we talk about functions from R^n to R^m; taking 25b will let you re–appreciate the power of linear algebra as it is used to describe these complicated objects in higher dimensions. The workload is pretty similar to 25a, but I found the content to be a little more challenging (and the averages on the tests are lower). But if you found 25a to be manageable, then 25b should be manageable for you, too :)

Also, if you are a CS concentrator that took 25a, but has decided not to take 25b: RECONSIDER. JUST TRY OUT THE CLASS (YOU CAN DROP IT)! I personally found analysis to be much more interesting compared to linear algebra; the proofs are also less tedious in my opinion (no more having to write so many basis vectors) Math 25b offers a broader introduction of math than 25a, so I recommend you, at least, try it out. Also, if you are planning to take CS124 and are concerned about the combined workload; it is definitely ALOT of work, but it is also doable. Many 25bers (including me) took CS124 + Math 25b this past spring and have succeeded in the class. So try 25b! And a CS concentration with a secondary in Math is kinda cool.

If you took Math 55a, then you are definitely prepared for this class. For the topology unit, we will be covering point–set topology, which is useful for many analysis proofs, but we wont be covering topology at the level of Math 131 (for instance, we wont be doing algebraic topology, since 25a doesnt cover abstract algebra/group theory that much).

If you took Math 22a: I have a friend who took 22a in the fall and 25b in the spring, and I think it is doable. I think the transition may be a little difficult because you need to 1) get used to longer and more difficult problem sets, and 2) make friends/PSet buddies, when people have already settled into their PSet groups from 25a. Make sure you go to office hours, because the CAs are SUPER helpful and thats where you make friends :)

For Math 21b and other linear algebra courses, I cant offer great advice, but if you havent taken a proof based math course yet take Math 101 (per the Math Concentrator info packet). Or even Math 25a, although there is a lot of overlap.

Wonderful class, but substantially harder than 25a perhaps not in terms of workload, but certainly in terms of amount of material covered, and the difficulty of some of the concepts.
If you liked Math 25a and have the time second semester to take the course, I would really, really strongly recommend Math 25b. Math 25b is kind of a different breed compared to Math 25a the proofs are longer (and feel more rigorous), the class seems to move a little faster, problem sets take longer (25a psets probably took me 5–10 hours, whereas 25b psets probably took me 10–15 hours), and the exams are significantly more brutal. Still, the content is really engaging, and Wes is a great lecturer if you liked him in 25a, you'll love him in 25b; it's clear that he really enjoys analysis (moreso than algebra), and he will continue to show you how much he loved differential equations by tying content back to it. Still, if you're not going into a discipline/concentration where analysis is important, it's not a "must take" course: plenty of people who took 25a did not take 25b (lots of them took cs 124 instead), and they don't seem to regret that decision too much.

Advice–wise, be prepared for more work, but know that the proofs in 25b (like the proofs in 25a) get easier over time not because they are actually getting easier, but because you get more accustomed to them and to the tricks for approaching them. If you took Math 25a, you'll likely have pset groups already, and you probably already know how important it is to do the psets with others (although trying the problems by yourself first is super helpful); on a related note, lots of pset groups kind of merge (because some people leave the course). The community is really great, so make friends and be kind to one another.

Reading the textbook is not super necessary, though it definitely doesn't hurt. Make sure, though, that you understand the content each lecture because the content rapidly builds upon itself, and the course is not kind to people who fall behind. Try your best to keep up with the course and ask questions if you're confused Wes is super great at taking questions during lecture and during office hours, and the CAs really try to help you too (big shoutout to Wittman and Edward).
Start your problem sets early, do them with a friend, and go to office hours. Ask questions in class. Math is hard to understand. There's no point in pretending to understand when you don't.
I would highly encourage other students to take this course. Math 25B teaches real analysis in a very thorough and detailed manner, and you will come out of the course with a very, very solid understanding of the course content. Professor Cain is also an amazing teacher and presents the content in a very clear and understandable way.
