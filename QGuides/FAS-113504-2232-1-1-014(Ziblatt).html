What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
It is a challenging class with a lot of writing but it is fruitful in that you get to flesh out your thoughts a lot more before you write and also get to engage in different ways of thinking/writing (more conceptually and theoretically)
I took the section on Southeast Asian political theory, which I think I initially ranked outside my top four given that my interests in the Government department are international relations first and comparative government second. I was initially skeptical about taking a political theory course because I had little to no prior exposure or interest in the subject. Although I found the first couple of weeks in the course to be quite challenging in my understanding of the very dense theoretical texts, the discussions were low–stakes and were ultimately helpful in foregrounding my theoretical analysis of the Southeast Asian historical texts we read later on in the course. I think Yi Ning did a great job at making the discussion sections intimate and encouraging student participation, which I really appreciated, and it made the vast amounts of reading I did worthwhile, which I can't say for many courses. While I generally find more motivation to work on problem sets than essays, it was nice to come out of this course with a final paper I could be proud of as a culmination of my work throughout the course, especially as I got more interested in Southeast Asia as a region. Even though this course teaches a very specific type of writing that I don't see myself needing to be familiar with outside of other Harvard government courses, it's still a required course, and your experience in it will probably be very dependent on who your section leader is. If Yi Ning's section is offered in the spring, I would highly recommend it, even if political theory isn't your forte.
