This course was one of the more challenging courses I've taken at Harvard in a good way. The lectures and sections challenged us to think critically about all of American imperial history, which was both difficult and rewarding. If you're at all interested in American nation building efforts abroad, I would highly recommend this course for you.
This is a really interesting course, and the professors are fantastic lecturers. However, there is a lot of reading, and you need to do it in order to contribute in section and to write the essays well. Not an easy Gen Ed, but good if you are interested and motivated in the material
This is a challenging course, but if you put the time and effort in, it is so, so worth it. The course covers the history of US nation–building projects from the end of the nineteenth century onwards ––– each unit builds on the last and covers material that should be but rarely is acknowledged in standard US history classes. (It was a treat to learn so much about the Philippines in particular!) Lectures were always fascinating, and sections were incredibly engaging and thought–provoking. Get started on readings and papers early ––– they're rigorous, but I learned a great deal from them.
Great class for students who are interested in US military engagements abroad and the US' unique role in the world.
There are probably easier courses to take to fulfill the GENED requirement. However, this is an interesting course if you like learning about American history. There are quite a few readings and given the amount of essays that are required, you actually should do the readings.
This class was amazing if you love history, and even if you don't it provides a great background on American Imperialism, which is super applicable to today.
It can be a lot of work. While the week to week is manageable in terms of readings, the few essays you are assigned throughout the semester require lots of time and writing. Probably only take this course if you are really interested in the history of American imperialism and occupations. If not, you are better off spending your time elsewhere. Additionally, there were a few questionable moments in the class that felt a bit colonizer–y.
Learn how to skim secondary sources cleverly and be skeptical of primary sources. It will help you get the most out of the (many) readings without spending 10+ hours a week.
This class is a survey course in various American occupations throughout the 19th, 20th, and 21st centuries. It is a pretty good history GENED, although it does not really shift the paradigm in talking about American history.
Great class, just be careful of all the essays.
This class is probably a bit more work than your average gen ed (3 papers 1500, 1500, 3000 words respectively) and final (2 essays 1000–1500 words 24 hour take home). Topics on essays and final were sufficiently general, but you need to do a lot of readings to have good ideas to work on them and get an A. Charlotte was a great section leader, so try to be in her section.
The readings are essential to the course and will enhance your understanding of the material a lot.
