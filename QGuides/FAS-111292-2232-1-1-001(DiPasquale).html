This course is an effective survey of both theoretical concepts in urban economics and current events and challenges concerning American cities. I would highly recommend this course to any government, economics, or history concentrator interested in global and/or American urban issues/challenges or any college student in general interested in cities. Both Professor Glaeser and Professor DiPasquale are busy people but each will take the time to meet with you if you're proactive about it. The lectures were always went by quickly and were always thought provoking!
This is a great class. Both professors were very generous with their time and extremely interesting to talk to. The problem sets were very intuitive. Do not be nervous about the coding – I didn't know stata or R coming in and they were all very doable.
Definitely take this course especially if you are interested in Real Estate or Urban Planning. This was an incredible course and literally the only one I went to in person this year because I loved it. You will not regret taking this course!!!
I took this while taking 5 classes and thought it was too intensive for a semester like that, though people with stronger econ backgrounds might find it easier. The assignments were long and kind of unnecessary in my opinion, but I thought the class content was cool and was happy I took it. I needed another ec class to finish my secondary and probably would've had an easier semester if I took a different class, but it was fine.
Anyone at Harvard with an interest in cities should take this course, regardless of whether you are studying economics. It is tough, but incredibly rewarding. I am a masters student in urban planning and this course gave me new perspectives about the forces that shape the built environment that I care so much about. Even if I did not always agree with every opinion or reading, I gained a fundamental understanding of the economics behind why and how urbanism happens that will make me a better planner.
Don't take this class if you 1) are not ready to commit to a very high level or engagement and attentiveness a very wide range of material is covered very quickly, 2) do not feel comfortable with difficult economic concepts being covered very quickly or 3) anticipate yourself ever needing an extension on an assignment.
This class is interesting and definitely gives you a newfound appreciation for cities, how they came to be, why they thrive or don't thrive, and the decision–making processes of people and businesses who locate inside of cities. Glaeser is a legend in urban economics, and his lectures are a joy to listen to. They're what you think of when you imagine what a Harvard education would be like. Professor DiPasquale is a great teacher and explains things pretty well. However, there are some aspects of the course that made it exceedingly frustrating at times, since the material isn't in and of itself very complex, but the way that the class was run made everything feel more difficult than it is. The burden to teach is placed on section leaders, since lectures just fly through material at a speed that makes actually grasping it impossible. My section TF (Peleg) was the goat and had an amazing ability to break down complex ideas into digestible chunks; he was the one who taught us the empirical models and the math in the coursestuff thats actually useful for psets and exams, which is really aggravating because it leads to sections being sped through, covering too much in one sitting. Material goes in one ear and out the other sometimes as a result. Take this class if you're ready to put in a decent amount of work to understand how to do everything and to listen to long historical rants about esoteric things related to cities. The reward at the end is a pretty satisfying sense of accomplishment though.
This is a great class for teaching the many aspects of city development, life, and economic implications while remaining consistently engaging. The two lecturers are some of the best I've ever had for Econ classes and are able to even make the technical aspects interesting. While some of the work is difficult, the exams are tough but fair.
Solid Econ elective!
I would recommend this class if you have an interest in real estate or urban development. The professors are great and it isn't too difficult.
This class is a great class for economics students who are interested in public policy. It does a great job at combining some of the intermediate theory of Ec1010A with practical public policy concerns.
TAKE THIS CLASS!! Having lived in cities all my life, I learned so much from this course about why cities look & function the way they do, as well as the problems that cities face today. Prof. Glaeser and Prof. DiPasquale are very passionate about these topics, and I found all the lectures very engaging. The class covers a wide range of topics (urban form, public transport, crime, disease, real estate markets, big data & cities, urban economic development and more), which kept the course interesting. The paper is pretty flexible – you can pretty much write about any topic relating to cities. Problem sets are pretty straightforward too. Both professors were pretty accessible after class and Ziqi was a great TF!
