If you are checking out the Q guide for LS50B, then you either haven't decided whether to take LS50A and you want to see what people had to say about the second semester or you already took LS50A yet you are unsure whether to take LS50B. I personally found myself in the second category.
While just reading these comments won't be able to make a decision for you, here are my thoughts on LS50B.

LS50B is definitely more focused on biology than LS50A; other than linear algebra, dynamical systems (2 lectures or so), and one lecture on Markov Chains, pretty much everything else is bio. In my case, this wasn't great since I enjoyed the more math focused lectures better. Nevertheless, for some reason, I felt less stressed this semester. Not sure if it was because I knew what to expect or I simply knew how to manage my time more effectively, but I certainly put in less effort this semester that I did the previous one (although we covered more biology) and did equally as well. Moreover, since we did not have a final this time (rather an essay where we explained the 10 things we took away from LS50), we had one exam less to worry about.
In terms of exams, expect them to be equally as hard as the LS50A ones. The psets were a little easier than the ones last semester.

Now, should you take LS50B? Hard to say. If you enjoyed LS50A, then sure, go for it. If you hated LS50A, then probably it is not worth going through another semester of LS50. If you had mixed feelings, then here are some things to consider.
You will get much closer with your friends from last semester and this will make your LS50 experience much more pleasant. While I do not think it is worth taking a class just because your friends take it, I think there is a high chance that you will continue being close friends with your LS50 buddies throughout college.
LS50A does not seem to fulfill as many requirements alone. There are certain courses that require ls1a or LS50a/b which honestly does not make a lot of sense to me. Also, other concentrations like the Chemistry one counts LS50a,b as 4 courses but I am not sure whether LS50A alone will count as 2.
Since LS50A does not cover everything that ls1a,b do, then you might need to take ls1b in case you choose not to take LS50b.
If there were certain things that you found annoying in LS50A (like confusing lectures), then do not expect them to change in LS50B since they probably won't.

All in all, I am happy I took LS50.
TAKE THIS COURSE!!! If you consider yourself a scientist ever in your life, take LS50 over any other introductory science course (not limited to LS1a, PS10, etc...). This course offers such a unique perspective of approaching biology, with exposure to cutting–edge research taught by established professionals in their respective fields. One thing that strikes me most is how every branch of science can shed light on biology. As intense chemistry and CS learner in HS, I saw how computational tools come together in understanding biology, and how microscopic atoms lay the foundation for biology. The lab component is also very cool, last year we tried on homology–based protein folding prediction right at the time when AlphaFold2 stood out. I took LS50A 2020 fall and continue this year, it has been such a journey, and I will really recommend any future scientists to take this course.
Take this class only if you are absolutely sure. LS50 is a very challenging course that will take up most of your academic time. You will learn a ton of material, but it will not be easy. Be prepared to struggle, especially leading up to exams, which are very hard. Don't take the course for the classes it satisfies, it will be easier to jut take the classes. Only take LS50 if you really like all aspects of science and want to see them integrated together.
While challenging, LS50B has been an incredible course and an even better community! Where else would you learn Markov Chains, cricket embryology, and computational biology all in one course? Certain units are harder than others based on your strengths and weaknesses. Working and bonding with other people in the class over p–sets and studying for exams has been one of the highlights of my freshman year.
Don't take it to knock off a requirement.
CELLS ARE NIFTY! LS50!
This course has really changed the way I think about science and biology in such a good way. I'd also really recommend this course for people interested in doing research –– you learn a LOT of things that will be useful to you / are not taught in other intro courses (you'll be introduced + learn how to work with concepts that you'd otherwise have to have a lot of prereqs to take a course in, and the environment is basically risk–free and very very supportive). Additionally, the course staff and professors are all great + super approachable!
This class is difficult. But it is also worth it. Find a study group early on, and make a habit of working on the problem sets together early in the week. Go to office hours and ask questions. And remember that even if you discover that you do not like the life sciences, you will have learned so much from this class. If you KNOW you're smart and can handle this class, don't flaunt it. The tightknit environment that you want from this class is only possible if everyone puts in the effort to make it that way. If you take this course, which you should, remember that everyone comes from different backgrounds and levels of experience. If you were privileged enough to have a strong science background in high school, remember that that is a PRIVILEGE and not something everyone has.
If you really like math as well as biology, take this course! The material is super interesting and the community is great.
LS50 WILL BE THE DEFINING PART OF YOUR FRESHMAN YEAR if you decide to take this course. This is simply fact. You will find a great community of students and really welcoming teaching faculty who will assist you in every way they can. Some people might describe this class as scarring yourself with knowledge such that you build scar tissue to protect you from the trauma of future classes at Harvard and beyond, but it is important to remember that this metaphorical scar is composed of learning the most wide array of topics possible for an introductory course and that in and of itself is valuable. It can help narrow down what you are interested in studying or fill in the gaps of a subject you may have felt initially weak in. Another benefit is that the speed of learning and organization of this class will likely prepare you for rigorous courses that have plentiful resources and study aids down the line since LS50 is much more amorphous in the material it covers. Regardless, you will have to be dedicated to the pursuit of learning and thinking like a researcher over worrying over the granularity of your grades. If you focus on the minutiae of points, this is not the class for you and you will likely feel that the outcome of this class is not worth the work entailed. The perception of difficulty and challenge within this course is only natural and there are many points throughout the course where no student has a detailed background in what is being taught, creating a more even playing field. Personally, the second semester has been exponentially easier than the first in terms of psets and exams although it's not a complete pushover. Given that LS50 restricts 5 out of the 8 courses you take within your freshman year, I believe the second semester's lowered difficulty will allow you to take on an additional 1 or even 2 courses. With all that said, this class is definitely not for everyone. If you are not interested in exploring the integration of mathematical and physics based components into biology or chemistry, immediately walk away and run in the other direction. Ls1B and LS1A are much better fits for people interested in pure bio and chem. If you are somewhere wavering in between and wondering why this inclusion of math in biology is important or even remotely interesting, it allows you to dive deep into the mechanics of cells and life that are often taken for granted in typical life science courses and presents a useful paradigm for research questions and modeling of biological systems. Personally, I entered this course much more interested in medicine than research but in exiting, I have grown to appreciate research more in tandem with medicine. Deciding to take this course is a pretty huge decision and one that should not be left up to your ego. It's important to level your expectations by consulting with a previous student or some members of the teaching faculty but even in cases where expectations were not necessarily aligned with the reality of the course, there is a general consensus of feeling extremely rewarded by all this class has to offer.
LS50B is an incredible scientific experience which I would recommend without reservation to students deeply passionate about applied life sciences. The most important strength of the course is the small cohort of motivated, kind, and similarly interested students that LS50 attracts – many of whom I now number among my closest friends at Harvard. In addition, the excellent teaching staff, broad range of course support options, and curated teaching material (i.e., the lectures in LS50 are clearly assembled thoughtfully) all contribute to a phenomenal course. It is well worth the hefty workload and sacrifice of more than a few sleepless nights.

Also, you get to meet some very interesting guest lecturers!
