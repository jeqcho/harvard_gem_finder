Professor Nelson cares a lot about this class but it’s really not great. I think this is the class I have liked the least at Harvard. But in terms of fulfilling a physical chemistry requirement I think this was the best option for me, so I can’t complain that much.
Fully believe it has the best work:benefit ratio of any thermo requirement satisfier. Relatively good and engaging lectures.
I liked the class. Well–taught and section was really helpful. The biggest problem with this class was only having one TF and one opportunity to ask questions on the problem set.
Prepare for a blizzard of algebra.
Among the alternatives, this is a pretty tame thermodynamics course, that doesn't rely heavily on quantum mechanics. However, the midterm and final can be very difficult, so make sure to study hard and well in advance. The lectures can be a little derivation–heavy, so reading the textbook is very important.
The overall class was ok as long as you do the readings (the lectures are at times indecipherable without it) and go to section. However, the final was the most harrowing experience of my entire life as a student – I honestly do not think I scored above a 50. This course, for all intents and purposes, actually achieves its goals of teaching the fundamentals of stat–mech while offering some biological applications such as protein folding and dimerization. Professor Nelson is passionate about the material and willing to answer any questions about it, and I was actually really enjoying the class just up until the final which completely devastated me. I would recommend the class, but just make sure you know how to derive everything for the final rather than knowing the general results of the derivations and applying them, which is what I assumed we would be tested on.
David's enthusiasm for the subject matter is evident, but sometimes the class feels more like a "special topics" class where we listen to him talk about ideas and there's some math. I think lectures could have been more consolidated or having a final "key takeaway" slide would have been helpful. I think also assignments could have been designed differently— perhaps we could have done a final writeup on a paper of an experiment talked about in class rather than psets.
If you want a relatively low–stakes but nonetheless engaging course at the intersection of physics, chemistry, biology, math, etc. that can either fulfill an upper–level MCB or a Phys credit (i.e. there are so many reasons!) then take this course! Prof. Nelson accomplishes so many things at once: he integrates knowledge from a wide range of STEM fields in a way that remains accessible to undergraduates, 2) he presents coursework that is mathematically rigorous (challenging enough to be fun) but still steps back to consider the bigger picture (large–scale, conceptual, macroscopic ideas). The material covered in this course strips back the nitty gritty of the life sciences to the basics and allows you to peer into the ways in which each field builds upon and informs the next (statistics in understanding entropy, thermodynamics in understanding mechanics, mechanics in understanding biochemical/molecular behavior, etc.). Richard is also an amazing TF; he explains everything so clearly!

If you take this course, lectures and section are both optional, but highly recommended; they're super helpful, but they won't affect your grade, so no stress about that. The psets can be a slight bit challenging but rarely were they too frustrating and there's also a lot of extra credit; either way, just go to office hours and Richard will make everything make so much more sense. Midterm was a bit weird because there were only four questions — the first three were super easy but the fourth one confused everyone. Overall, a very chill course. You get what you put into it. Highly recommend.
This course is great if you are comfortable with calculus–based physics and biochemistry. It integrates a lot of concepts that were explained with hand waves in previous classes, and provides in–depth understanding of concepts like enthalpy, entropy, why specific processes occur, etc. It is a lot of work however, so be prepared. It is a class where you get out what you put in.
This is a more applied version of stat mech. It's much less math–heavy than Phys181 but for someone who is primarily bio and not math or physics it's probably easy to feel lost at some points in the course.
