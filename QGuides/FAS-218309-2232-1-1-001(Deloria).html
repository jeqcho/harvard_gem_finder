I highly recommend this class for anyone interested in the subject material! Professor Deloria is one of the best professors and lecturers I have had. He is incredibly enthusiastic and intelligent. However, only take it if you are willing to engage with the readings and the class materials because otherwise it probably will not be all that valuable to you.
Please take this class! This course is the definition of interdisciplinary humanities!

This class is amazing! Really interesting perspective on American history and studies. Everyone should take! NOT a gem with no work though, essays can be intense –– but no discussion posts or anything.
This class should probably be called Introduction to Arts and Humanities. The professor is a very engaging and great lecturer and is enthusiastic about the course material however it unfortunately felt like the majority of the class was made up of guest lectures from the heads of other departments within Arts and Humanities at Harvard. Also, be aware that this class requires you to write in a different style, creative non–fiction, and the expectations for this style are not always clear. Furthermore, course materials for this class are relatively contemporary and while interesting they can also be very dense and complicated.
Take this course if you're interested in HISTLIT, especially if you're trying to figure out if it's right for you. Additionally, the course itself involves a bunch of guest speakers from numerous disciplines, which is REALLY HELPFUL for freshman exploring what they might want to concentrate in.
Deloria is an amazing professor, super fun, open and warm. The class can sometimes feel very discontinuous and it's hard to feel like you're actually making progress on any of the content presented to you because it's only connected by overarching themes. In that way, it's a true intro course. Because of this lack of depth, it can feel a little silly/frivolous at times ––– still, the unconventional field trip to the Art Museum, a walking tour, and screen–printing were huge highlights that made the course genuinely fun. Reading workload is not a burden at all, and entirely disconnected from writing assignments, with a Creative Non–Fiction that was a major perk of the course and a welcome reprieve from hardcore academic writing.
Professor Deloria is very kind and charismatic, but the course is a mess. It is way too broad and many of the lectures seem irrelevant or disconnected from what we're learning about. We get very little guidance for the assignments and the feedback is very limited and basic.
Avoid this class, it seems like an easy course due to the syllabus, and it can seem like it for the first couple of weeks. But this course's assignments are so subjective to TF grading that you could easily get a B in this course. Be very careful on TF assignment on this course if you do take it.
Unless Deloria takes out the fluff and focuses on real questions that are fun to think about, you shouldn't take this class.
The early part of the course on American Studies writing was by far my favorite/most engaging part of the course, and I feel like I really learned a lot. I really didn't like the guest lecture structure of the course –– the guests were great, but there were too many of them and the course ended up feeling a little incoherent. I understand that it was meant to be an American Studies taste–tester, but it felt like we went through too many subjects too rapidly without ever really delving deeply into any of them, and we didn't engage much with course material in sections, which exacerbated the feeling of going too fast without real coherence. I actually found the early parts of the class where we were studying American Studies historiography –– the history of the field itself –– the most interesting part of the course. I think any class should be teaching something that it is better at teaching than any other class can teach it, and a taste test of each subfield of American Studies is never gonna be as good as each subfield's full dish. This class would play better to its strengths if it really centered more around the question of defining American Studies and practicing it, rather than sitting through long lectures that offer minimal participation in a big room with a new professor each time. I think it should be smaller (and maybe have a cap of ~25) to enforce attendance and participation –– I and lots of others stopped really wanting to attend, and I never felt like I learned anything particularly new in section that I couldn't have learned in lecture; the TF didn't really guide conversation or asked questions that prompted discussion. Everyone was very kind and well intentioned but I think the class needs significant restructuring/refocusing –– mostly sad because the first two weeks were great and I was SO excited for the class (and some of the writing prompts were incredible) but we rarely received feedback on writing (in a class that was structured around writing) and so I felt pretty disengaged by the end.
This class is awesome, it covers a variety of interconnected topics that are relevant and interesting.
I thought this class would be a Gem. It was not challenging really, but my grade was not the best and I would have taken a harder class that I could have done better in.
They want a very specific writing style in this course (New Yorker Style) and if you don't write like that, you should learn to quickly. They grade based on your ability to mimick the syntax and prose of the magazine.
This class is a very interesting introduction, but be warned that the writing is HARD. I personally felt really challenged by the creative nonfiction writing, as it definitely felt like something outside of my academic writing–wheelhouse. Professor Deloria is an amazing professor who is very personable and cares about his students, and brings in a great collection of guest speakers. Be warned that this is not a history class, it's much more about the methodologies involved in American studies and about the creative nonfiction writing style.
