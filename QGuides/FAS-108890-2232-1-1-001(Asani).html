This class is an excellent and challenging introduction to Islam that will leave you feeling well–equipped to understand the current state of the tradition and how it has developed over time and place. There are a lot of moving parts––namely weekly requirements––which might feel different from typical courses, but staying on top of things will yield a deeper understanding of the course material. The course staff, especially Professor Asani and Adnan, are incredibly knowledgeable and kind, so communicating with them ought not be overlooked. The structure of watching a pre–recorded lecture and focusing on group discussion in the two–hour block might be a hit–or–miss for some, I still found the course readings and section experience to be very worthwhile. By nature, the material covered in this course will require open–mindedness and a good work ethic to really get anything out of it, but especially if you have curiosities or pre–existing notions about Islam that you'd like to develop further, this Gen Ed is a great choice.
LECTURE: Pre–lecture and lecture combined amount to 3 hours 45 minutes each week. The first half of lectures is a bit dry, as there are typically few activities and Professor Asani will lecture the entire time. The second half of lecture is more interesting, as there are group discussions and plenty of opportunities to ask the TFs or Professor Asani questions.

SECTIONS: Mix of group discussions and activities that were surprisingly good at synthesizing the material.

ASSIGNMENTS: Weekly assignments consisted of a ~400–600 word recap of that week's 1 hour 45 minute pre–lecture video and 3 discussion questions based on the readings for section. These were really helpful as resources during exams.

BE WARNED ABOUT EXAMS: The expectations they list on the exam themselves don't wholly match what they're looking for. CITE YOUR SOURCES – ALL OF THEM. Also, I'm an IB concentrator, so I'm used to giving succinct answers on tests. In this class, you need to really spell out anything you can on exams, as there are details they're looking for that you're bound to miss otherwise.
The course has a lot of work involved, and the grading is not transparent or consistent which makes it a more stressful course than it needs to be.

Although it is taught through the lens of Islam, and focuses heavily on misconceptions about the faith, its history, and the way it is practiced across the globe, it teaches a framework of thinking that can be easily applied to any world religion. It fosters religious literacy, which will help students understand religion and culture on a deeper level. This course prioritizes the development of emotive knowledge, and music and art are therefore part of the course material, which was refreshing for me to engage with as a student. The workload was moderate, but if you're interested in the subject matter, very doable.
