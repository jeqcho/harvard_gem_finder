Good class
THIS CLASS IS A MUST TAKE. I don't care if you've fulfilled your language requirement or if you are sick of the humanities. SPANSH 40 is an incredible class, and you should not miss out.

If it's been a while since you've taken Spanish, please don't worry. I am an upperclassman who hadn't taken Spanish since high school. I was fine. You'll be fine, too. The class even covers the subjunctive, conditionals, etc., which levels the playing field and gives the people who need to brush up on their Spanish the opportunity to do so.

Also, it's actually a good thing that the class meets three times a week because the class meetings are really fun and valuable to attend. Profe explained everything so clearly, and the in–class activities were highly interactive (a lot of small–group and full–group discussions and little icebreakers –– but not the awkward kind!). The discussions of the movies were quite rich. And I loved the grammar lessons; Profe used a lot of examples and gave us lots of opportunities to practice what we'd learned (through written exercises and through speaking), which I found SO helpful. Also, speaking of grammar lessons, Profe's slides organized the grammar we had to learn in a way that was very digestible.

Moreover, the quizzes are not stressful to study for. If you do your homework and brush up on the grammar covered, you'll be more than fine. And the oral exams are low stress as well. Just look at the prompts before going in. (I'd choose two, and Profe would have me discuss one of those two.)

All of the composiciones were very interesting to write. I loved the prompts and was grateful that we had several to choose from.

The short film was one of my three favorite final projects from my time at Harvard (and I say this as an upperclassman). It was so interactive, fun, and challenging. We wrote a script for a short film and brought it to life through acting, filming, and editing. (And yes, you can make yours funny. :))

I loved the format of watching movies. Movies are so much more fun than readings. And the movies are REALLY GOOD. I was seriously absorbed in all of them. My favorites were Los colores de la montana, which explored war, corruption, and loss of innocence; Contracorriente, which explored identity, isolation, and loss; NO, which explored rhetoric and loss of innocence; and El secreto en sus ojos, which was filled with all sorts of crazy plot twists, explored the corruption in Argentina's government and police forces, and featured a fascinating scene in which a lawyer leveraged a suspect's fragile masculinity to get him to confess to a crime.

The grammar discussed in the beginning of the class may seem familiar, but you'll get a lot better at using it. And for me, the grammar that was new entered the picture toward the middle and end of the course and was SO USEFUL.

And you'll find a lovely community. :) You'll talk a lot with your classmates in class, and you'll learn a lot about them through participating in the aforementioned icebreakers.

Some closing thoughts:
–If a movie doesn't have closed captions, don't freak out. You'll understand more of it than you'd expect. :)
–I had not taken a real humanities class in college before taking this one. Now, I want to take more.
–The feedback you'll get on your work is SO VALUABLE.
Super great class! The professor makes a big difference.
Ricardo is FANTASTIC! Definitely take with him.*
