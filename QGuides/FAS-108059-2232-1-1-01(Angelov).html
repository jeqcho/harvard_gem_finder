I recommend this course both to people considering a concentration in history as well as non–history concentrators who are interested in learning some history in a chill setting –– in short, anyone who enjoys learning about history. The course serves as a good introduction to how history scholarship is done at an academic level, so instead of just memorising facts and dates the course emphasises a broad understanding of the Byzantine Empire, and close analysis of primary sources from the Byzantine period. Thus, each lecture had an "in–focus text" which was a 1–2 page long excerpt from a primary source that we would discuss. Similarly, sections were based largely on analysing texts written by Byzantine authors. This approach was very effective for me, and at the end of the course I have a detailed understanding of Byzantine history, and I feel that I can imagine what it was like to live in Byzantium. In terms of academic requirements, this course was fairly manageable. There were many readings each week, and although not all of them were technically necessary I found them useful to give more background information, and many of the readings were quite interesting. Attending lectures and section, doing the readings, and rereading my lecture notes were enough to prepare me for the midterm and final –– and since they both took the form of an essay there was a lot of freedom to play to my strengths. There were two response papers, but they were each just 2 pages so they also did not take too much time. Overall, I very much enjoyed this course –– lectures were interesting, sections were engaging, and I've learned a lot thanks to it.

I would tell future students that this class is an excellent one and a great opportunity to learn about the Roman Empire transition into the Byzantine Empire.
