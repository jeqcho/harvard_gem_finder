I would not recommend this course. The professors love to mention how they've been teaching this course for 20+ years, and they are excited about the material, but I would not be as pleased as they are if I had been working on a course for so long for this result. There is so much terminology in the course and it is all treated like it is essential, but then most of it disappears and never returns after the week's assignment. The material covered mostly focuses on plant development at the cellular and organ level, rarely discussing plants in a wider context except in passing. When groups of plants are discussed, the lectures follow phylogeny, which results in 90% of the class covering 10% of the existing plant diversity, and whole lectures dedicated to things like bryophytes and lycophytes while all angiosperms (90% of all living plant diversity) is condensed into one lecture.
The workload in this class can be pretty variable but overall is manageable. The field trips are mandatory but totally worth it (and this is me saying it having only been able to attend one of them). Don't come into this expecting you can just take it easy, because there is a lot of material covered, but if you keep up with it you would be surprised by how much you actually learned by the end of the semester!
This is a very solid class to get you introduced to the diversity of plant life. Aside from some pacing issues, I think the material is presented in a way that is accessible to the majority of students and labs give you hands on experience with the topics (fruits and flowers being the highlights). Field trips can be a little dry, but you get to experience some of Harvard's underrated resources (the Forest and Arboretum). A little bit too much focus on development for my personal taste, but I understand that's the way science sort of skews these days. A few lectures seemed to go into more depth than necessary or topics were covered over multiple lectures (patterns, forms, and auxin being three lectures about basically the same thing) while I felt learning traits and the phylogeny did not get enough attention (only two days on Angiosperms aka 90% of plants). In general, a good course to get you acclimated to plant sciences and offer a taste of different areas in botany.
