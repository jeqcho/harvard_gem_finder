Comments
This is quite possibly the worst experience I've had with teaching staff at Harvard. Though the TFs and Professor Smail were very friendly and open to meeting with you, the expectations they conveyed for all aspects of the course were so unrepresentatives. For one, the TFs often gave very different impressions from Professor Smail. Also, when you worked with them on a draft and they gave specific areas for improvement and told you about your standing in the class, their grading and comments would then contradict all they had said. This is a very rigorous and intensive course, and this professor and teaching staff created an incredibly frustrating environment where they gave basically no indication about how to improve. I had no issues with the nature of History 97 in general, because I expected its intensity, but I have never felt more disrespected and toyed with as I did with this specific teaching staff.
I loved the teaching team so incredibly much — they put immense care and effort into your learning. I did not know this beforehand, but this course focuses much more heavily on historiography than history itself. While I am less interested in historiography than history, it is a unique and interesting exercise for historians. However, this course is much more work than I had expected. Even though there are only four essay assignments, three of them require first and second drafts, both of which are graded the same way, and you are expected to make significant changes between the two drafts. Effectively, that's seven essays (averaging one essay every two weeks, and the last one is longer!). Be prepared to put in the work.
I loved this course! If you are interested in legal history or legal proceedings, take this class! You will learn about the changing landscape of legal history and what exactly is history. In the History 97 Tutorial course, most importantly, you will learn how to write as a historian, which will set the fundamentals for writing for your other concentration courses. Professor Smail is also an amazing professor! He has a plethora of knowledge on material, Medieval Europe, and legal histories ––– so if you are interested in any of these fields, definitely go to his office hours (he is very accessible). Office hours are very helpful before you start your papers! Also, don't cram your papers (don't write them on the day they are due).
This course combines history, the law, and writing in ways that are practically exciting yet administratively frustrating. From harsh grading that penalizes small oversights and authorial choices to stringent deadlines that make the revision process sometimes arduous and rushed, this course occasionally made my question whether or not I wanted to continue with the History concentration. It's unfortunate because at the core is a teaching staff that truly cares about students and the development of their skills, especially Elizabeth Ross, who might be the most dedicated and hard–working TF I have ever had at Harvard. The readings are interesting, as are the skills that this course aims to develop with regards to close reading and comparative analyses. However, looming above students is an understanding that their hard work might not truly be reflected in the grade they achieve, because of puzzling grade cut–offs and occasionally unclear expectations. Should you choose to stick with History 97, you will undoubtedly become a more nuanced writer and researcher, and understand what it means to really "do" history. You will work with an excellent staff and equally–dedicated students who will provide you some of the best feedback you've ever received on a college paper. Sadly, all of this comes with a persistent fear of "am I really doing enough?"
This class is great but a good amount of work.
Unless you want to be a future lawyer, have experience in a law firm, or read legal journals for fun, you should take any other topic for your history tutorial. It is not welcoming to people not on a pre–law track.
I mean I wouldn't advise people to take it if they weren't concentrators unless they wanted to think very critically about historical writing, but for any concentrators I'd say it's tough but fair and I understand why it's a required class.
