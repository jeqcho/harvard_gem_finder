Prof. Stern is great, and the field trips in this class are super cool. The course is manageable, fairly graded, and genuinely interesting.

I think it's best taken as a freshman (you'll learn about a lot of cool places at Harvard!) or as a senior (you'll have the space, after thesis deadlines, to have some arts–and–crafts fun with the capstone project).
Through this course I feel like I've gotten the most out of being at Harvard by getting to see so many rare books. The weekly readings and reading responses are not insignificant, so keep that in mind.
This is such an amazing class and I would DEFINITELY recommend taking it if you can. Professor Stern teaches the material with an infectious passion and he is very open to feedback and available to answer questions. The course load is not difficult at all and the final project is very fun!
Great teaching staff!* The reading responses each week can be a little time consuming but they keep you on track of the readings. The course is very well organized by the TFs.
This class is genuinely really interesting if you like books. Prof. Sterns is very knowledgable and clearly puts a lot of effort into preparing the lectures for this class (he even reads some lecture responses *gasp* instead of letting his TFs do everything). This was the first year the class was run so it was a little bit of a work in progress, but this meant that Sterns and the TFs were very receptive to feedback and made adjustments when requested. The course's final structure was one lecture response (sometimes about the readings and sometimes not) and one section response a week (usually about our site visit or working towards final project). Sections in this class are really fun—we went to the HAM, the Fine Arts Library, Bow & Arrow Press, Weissman Book Preservation Center, and Houghton. It was really cool to see and touch the books that seem really inaccessible on the screen in person. I also think the final project will turn out really cool, people are doing all sorts of cool things. One thing to note, attendance was taken at all lectures and if you were absent a page–long response was required. (also hopefully its not in the science center next year, vibes were off for a book history class lol)

This course is generally easy and interesting sometimes, but lectures are pretty boring and there is an emphasis on religious texts, which I did not always find interesting or engaging. The course isn't a lot of work but its hard to feel interested or excited about most of the material.
TAKE THIS CLASS!! You will learn so much about books, and gain a valuable new perspective on every book you read.
If you enjoy reading and appreciating books, this course is the one for you. Gives you a very clear understanding of the history and development of the book and allows you to explore campus.
Lectures were pretty dry and most of the class did not pay attention to them. Dr. Stern is very knowledgeable but the class didn't spend enough time covering his area of expertise –– lectures were better when he did. This could be remedied by getting more guest lecturers to speak about their fields. I also think the class could have benefited from a more narrow focus.

Sections are usually field trips to various Harvard libraries/book–related places. These were really fun! We got to look at (and touch) an absurd number of old books.

Readings were often way too long although this got better toward the end. Students will only do the readings for a Gened if they're manageable, so being more mindful about what you're assigning could help.

Workload: readings, weekly post–section responses and reading responses (about 2–3 pages per week), capstone project and essay.
This is exactly what a GENED should be! You learn so much in this course about the history of the book, you get to have hands–on experience with older book–making techniques, go to a printing press, numerous museum visits, and more. This is such a well rounded course, and I couldn't recommend it more!
