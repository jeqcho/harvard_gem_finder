What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This class is one of those classes where you realize what you came to Harvard for. Everything, from the readings to the discussions in class, are so unbelievably academically stimulating that it makes your brain hurt. That being said, this class is one where you truly need to give it your all, so I would only take it if you are actually very interested/passionate about political philosophy. Even if you have never explored it before, as long as you're interested and willing to learn, that's all that you need. It is a lot of work, so you will definitely learn how to read and write quickly, but the learning curve can be a bit steep as you figure it out and it will be a LOT of time and work (but is completely worth it, in my opinion). Another small thing is that the class is 2 hours and 45 minutes, which is a long time to sit with no laptop and without a break, so be prepared, but the interesting discussions make it a lot better than it could be.
This class offers you a good introduction to political philosophy and you will learn a lot. However, readings are lengthy, discussion posts are graded quite harshly, and writing the 20 page paper is no easy feat.
This class is really great for preparing you for Social Studies 10a, however, it is really difficult. The two assignments a week take a good amount of time, and as someone without a theory background, I felt very overwhelmed all semester. However, Arthur and Matt are really enthusiastic about the subject matter and you learn a lot. This class will change how you think about government and civil disobedience.
This class is a good start for the social studies concentration, but it is a lot of work on top of three or four other classes. Be prepared for some mind–twisting readings and some difficult time management puzzles. If you are looking for class on a relevant topic, civil disobedience and legitimacy are topics that will never lose relevancy, and they were fascinating to learn about.
It's easy to feel daunted by the syllabus of this course — biweekly discussion posts, a final term paper, lectures, and sections — but nobody does it quite like Arthur and Matt. Arthur talks about the material with infectious enthusiasm, and devotes considerable time to hearing what students have to say, inside and outside of the classroom. The 2–hour, 45–minute lecture flies by, and you'll have the most fun in the course (and succeed) if you show up having at least done the readings. Your lingering questions and clarifications will be covered during lecture, section, and office hours. Matt is also incredible and helped us break down complicated concepts and readings on the board. Similar to Arthur, he encourages people to be active participants and respects what all of us have to say. Although the questions we were discussing are fundamental to our understanding of government and our relationship to it, classes felt like an extremely inviting place to take academic risks.

The course is also a great introduction to what Social Studies will be like in terms of the questions asked and historical ground covered. Overall, it made me more confident in wanting to take Social Studies 10 next year.
