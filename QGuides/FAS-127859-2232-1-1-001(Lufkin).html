What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
Take this class!! You will learn a ton about the history of quilt making and how it connects to industrialization and slavery and activism and American history! You will also learn how to sew or become better at sewing! Everyone who took this class was so lovely and I made a lot of friends, and Felicity is a fantastic instructor. She is super knowledgeable and also very kind and a great teacher.
This class is a great option to both learn about textile history and pick up some sewing skills of your own. It dispels some of the myths surrounding early quilts in America and highlights a variety of quilting communities and movements I wasn't aware of before taking this class. There is a moderate level of reading to do each week that you should do to participate in the discussions, and the quilting labs can take up some time outside of class if you're being meticulous about it, but the workload is very manageable and worthwhile for such a unique class at Harvard (also a fun way to explore Folk & Myth if you're interested!)
I adored this class!! The section time has fundamentally has altered my perspective on American history. I know we always talk about how important it is to get alternative perspectives when learning history, but I've never had the privilege to take a class that actually does so. Lufkin is extremely knowledgeable and more than happy to share her opinions about it all. The only potential issues I had with the section component was (1) the length of the readings and (2) the section feels more like a lecture because it ends up being a lot of Lufkin talking (but that could have just been our class).

The labs were amazing. I really appreciated the opportunity to get to learn how to sew, both by hand and on the machine, and working on the final project gave me a chance to solidify all the skills we learned throughout the semester.

If you take this class, be prepared to have the urge to bring up quilting in far more conversations than you expected!
Only take this class if you really really want to learn to make a quilt. I really enjoyed the class. Lab is super fun and it is always a good time talking to Lufkin. The final project was super rewarding, and if you put the time and effort in you will get a pretty cool quilt. However, making a cool quilt comes at the great cost of suffering through a semester of boring readings. Lufkin brings enthusiasm to the seminar, but this does not trump the painfully boring readings that this class puts students through. I have never taken a class with a reading list that was this boring. I cannot emphasize this enough. Additionally, I think that the week topics were relatively lackluster and a little too straightforward to garner my interest (e.g. 18th century quilts, Amish quilts, civil war quilts). I would be surprised if anyone found these week topics captivating, but if you love boring readings and are dying to know about civil war quilts then this is the class for you. I usually did the readings (or at least skimmed them), but you can certainly get by in this class without doing them. Also the weekly discussion posts were kind of waste of time (but isn't that how it always is).
Take this class if you can get into it! The professor is great and clearly cares about the topic, and you will learn things which no other course at the College will teach you. That said, be prepared for a lot of work if you do a quilt as your final project.
Definitely take if you have the chance! This course has totally changed how I think about quilts/quilt making and has planted the seeds for a lifetime interest in sewing. But, don’t forget that this course also requires a fair amount of reading and writing alongside the sewing projects. You’ll learn a lot and have fun if you put in the work!
The best part of the course is the quilt lab. The seminars involve summarizing the assigned readings, which can get old very quickly. There IS some conceptual work done to situate the readings in their historical context, plus trace the development of quilt–making throughout American history, but I would have appreciated fewer, more evocative readings to allow for more student participation and thus a true seminar format –– rather than what became the professor lecturing most of the time.
