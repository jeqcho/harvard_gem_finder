This class moves pretty quickly and covers a lot, so it's honestly pretty easy to get lost if you aren't paying attention. Definitely take this course, even if you don't have a vested interest in Southeast Asia! Harvard (and the United States at large) has such complicated ties to Southeast Asia, and it is important to learn about this region's history for that reason, at the very least. Besides that, Southeast Asia has such a rich history outside of the time it was colonized, and this course does a great job of diving into that. Finally, Harvard offers very few intellectual opportunities to engage with Southeast Asia, and this class seeks to remedy that. The course staff is incredibly kind and knowledgeable, and the assignments never felt stressful. There is a fair amount of reading, so make sure to stay on top of this throughout the semester, especially since the two essay assignments require an understanding of the readings. I took this class as an elective, and it provided a really nice balance to the heavy problem–set classes.

This is a great class if you are interested in Southeast Asia! It is such an understudied region, especially at Harvard, but is so rich with history. It is a sweeping class that covers a diverse and complex region but I think it was fantastic at teaching not just SEA history but global history. SEA is really a crucible and a proxy for international trends. It's the seat of colonial conflict (e.g., British vs Burmese), imperial competition (e.g., French vs. British vs. Dutch scrambling for SEA), and crucial historical concepts (e.g., imperialism, anti–colonialism, militarism etc.). I wish it spent more time on contemporary era – it is mostly 1800–1950, with a big focus on the transition from pre–colonial to colonial to post–colonial states. There are so many links with South Asian and East Asian History so if you study either of those regions, I HIGHLY recommend taking this class. It'll contextualise so much of what is happening inside those regions too. Also SEA history is very helpful if you study Buddhism.

It's not too difficult – I would say a regular history class. Of course, lots of readings, most of which is fairly dry textbook stuff, but once you transcend the names and dates (of which there are a ton), the concepts are pretty cool. If you do all the readings and pay attention in class, you should be good.
