Overall: Physics 15b is an introduction to electromagnetism. It is helpful to have seen E&M and some vector calc beforehand; compared to, say, AP Physics C, it has significantly more Calculus and requires an understanding of vector calculus. Lectures do contain quite a few proofs (in the physicist sense of the term); following them helps with conceptual understanding, though you are not required to write proofs yourself. Also covers some relativistic E&M effects and diff/eqs for circuits (epsilon/R/L/C/AC/Z) (props if you know all those letters).

Professor: Carlos is amazing and great to talk to. They do extremely cool neutrino research, if youre interested. Personally, I am not a fan of the fill–in–the–blank lecture format, though.

Textbook:
1) Purcell–Morin: Required for homework (make sure its the right edition, check with the syllabus when it comes out). Especially useful are the sections on image charges and Thevenin.
2/3) Not necessary for this course. TFs might draw from Griffiths for section questions, but thats it.
Edward M. Purcell & David J. Morin, Electricity and Magnetism, 3rd edition. ISBN: 978–1107014022.
David J. Griffiths, Introduction to Electrodynamics, 4th edition. ISBN: 1108420419.
Richard P. Feynman & Robert B. Leighton, The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Vol. II, New Millennium edition, ISBN: 0465023827.

Topics covered: Charge, Coulomb, E fields, energy; charge distributions, electric flux, Gauss; energy in electric fields, electric potential (15b uses phi rather than V), gradient; dipoles; divergence, differential form of Gauss, Poisson/Laplace equations; the conversion triangle between charge distribution rho, E field, and potential; Curl, Stokes, conductors; uniqueness theorem, image charges (fun); capacitance; current; circuits, Kirchhoff, Thevenin; relativistic transformations of E fields; moving charges and the B field; vector potential (you probably wont use it much) and Biot–Savart; surface current; Lorentz transformations of E and B; Faraday/Lenz, mutual/self inductance; RLC; AC, complex current, impedence; displacement current; E&M waves, Poynting vector (what a beautiful name). The sizable bit on diff eq and impedence/complex current part are not taught from their mathematical principles upwards and can be a bit confusing at first (diff eqs, complex Ansatzen, linear combinations of normal modes, and general/particular solutions) (shout out to 16). I wouldnt be scared of it though.

Course structure notes: 2 lectures a week, cool demos!; section once a week was very helpful; 2–hr lab once a week, though the timing doesnt really line up with the lectures; OH throughout the week; physics night Wednesday. 4–6 question psets usually due Friday nights. Dont procrastinate.
