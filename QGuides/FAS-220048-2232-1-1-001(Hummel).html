this class is easy and requires little work outside of class. the teaching staff is really nice and wants you to do well. it can get boring at times but if you are really interested in social change i would take it.
Overall, I would say I enjoyed this class. I learned a lot about the different complexities of protest movements and it opened my eyes to a lot of different aspects of success/failure when it comes to social mobilization. We covered a lot of different political concepts and did a good job of applying them to a interesting, comprehensive mix of protest movements from around the globe. The topics we covered were engaging and interesting. My only reservations would be about the the out–of–class assignments. They weren't particularly hard, but they felt a little tedious at times and they were very particular with what they were looking for. I felt like for all of the assignments, there wasn't any room to take creative liberties, which can be good or bad depending on your preferences. Just keep that in mind, but I would still say that this is a good class to take!
Sometimes a lot of what affects whether or not protest movement succeeds is just chance, so I would say that while the theories and case studies we learned about were fascinating, be prepared to learn that there is really no right answer or algorithm, and there is only so much you can learn about to predict the impact of protest movements.
Take this class if you are looking for an introductory gov course, something to fulfill a gov requirement, a class that will help you learn a lot about the inner workings of protest, or just want a good and chill class for the semester. Professor Hummel is great and knows a lot about the subject, and genuinely cares about teaching it in a way that makes sense to everyone. There is only one reading per class and they are manageable/interesting, and there are mini–assignments along the way to prepare you for the final paper which makes it a lot quicker to finish at the end of the course. Great class, cool subject, and great professor.
A great class for the comparative subfield requirement. You learn a lot about protests and what "success" really means within the context of movements. Professor Hummel is awesome, and Molly is a great TF! Just make sure to keep up with the reading responses, and start the final paper early!
I would absolutely take this class if you're interested in upper level gov classes, if you're interested in activism and protest movements, or if you need to fulfill a comparative gov requirement. It's genuinely super fascinating and gives you a great sense of the government department at Harvard. The professor is engaging and incredibly well–researched, with all of the lectures drawing from real world movements and highly specific sources. I learned so much that I'll take with me throughout my life and my time here as a gov concentrator.
It's an excellent class for anyone who wants a primer to revolutions, protest movements, and resistance. Professor Hummel teaches with passion and enthusiasm making the course more enjoyable
This is an excellent Government course that will undoubtedly change the way you think about protest movements, exposing you to a number of interesting and complex cases both in the US and abroad. Assignments are manageable, the final paper encourages exploration and personal interest, and the memoirs you read for class are a true highlight. Professor Hummel is a kind and effective lecturer, and Molly is an equally wonderful TF who always worked to empower students. Whether you are an avid participant in protests or have never thought about taking part in one, this class offers more than just a simple overview of different protest movements, but histories, narratives, and complexities that are worth diving into. I can't recommend Gov 1125 enough, whether you are a concentrator or seeking to learn more about the concentration!
