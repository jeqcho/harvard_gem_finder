Comments
You will do most of your learning from the homework. The actual lectures are quite dry, especially in the first 2/3, and often not relevant to the economic models that might be important for doing research. If you are a graduate student, David's section is fairly similar to what is taught in 2010B, and Andrei's section can feel meandering and too focused on the psychology at times. Only take if you're very interested in some aspect of the course content (like memory) prior to the class. Otherwise, I don't think the course is a good use of time.
This is a great class! Both professors are really cool, and you get a good overview of the field from its beginnings in the 70s to today. Taking this class as an undergrad, I was concerned about my level of preparation, but I ended up being fine with 1011a + Stat 110 + general math familiarity. Problem sets could be tricky but contained genuinely interesting problems. Highly recommend!
This course is amazing! You get a great overview of the history and current state of behavioral economics. Andrei's portion is substantially better than David's, mainly because his models are more innovative and he assumes the students are more capable, so the lectures are more interesting. You will really get a sense of how behavioral econ is remedying the flaws of traditional economics.

In terms of prereqs, I would say Econ 1011a and Stat 110 are both necessary and sufficient. The workload is also pretty light: only six problem sets (which were easier than 1011a/1126) and a final. The final is difficult to study for, due to the lack of study materials, but it was not difficult itself (although tbd how strictly it is graded).

In summary I would highly recommend. Treat it as a more chill version of a quantitative class and also a great way to get to know David and Andrei and get acquainted with grad–level econ.
It's a relatively moderate class (psets are due every second week and they are not hard, especially if you are comfortable with math). Knowledge gained is quite valuable for general erudition and will be also very engaging if you haven't done too much microeconomics/modeling for people's decisions so far through other courses.
David and Andrei are legends. They are both very kind and it's worth talking to both of them!
Behavioral Econ gets an emerging interest in Economics in general, so it's worth trying to understand early of whether you would like to jump in this train. That being said, I think the course is very effective for future researchers. Someone with a focus on industry will also find interesting topics (e.g., Behavioral Finance), but I might assume there are some other a bit more applied courses in the Econ department.

I think the final will be quite challenging, so that might be the only non–smooth part of the class.
This class is excellent! The professors are always willing and really excited to talk to students about questions or extensions of the material, and make themselves super accessible to students. The TFs are amazing and understanding, and very supportive. The material is difficult, but the course staff is a great support system.
I have only taken 2 econ classes at Harvard: Ec 1011a and this one, and I liked this one 10x better. The math was less tedious, and I learned much more economic intuition and ideas from our discussions on the psychology background and working through the mathematical models.
