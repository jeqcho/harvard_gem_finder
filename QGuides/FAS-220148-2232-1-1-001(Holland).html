This was by far my favorite class of my first year!! Lectures were absolutely fascinating, and I learned about the law in a way that I never had before. We discussed the inequalities and shortcomings within the law, but it also never felt hopeless or overwhelming. It was great to learn about the problems of legal systems in a way that is still hopeful for future solutions. I did have some trouble understanding the readings for the class, but sections were incredibly useful in helping me with that. Overall, I loved this class, and I would highly recommend it!
Subject is super interesting and Holland/teaching staff are very nice, BUT for an intro course, there are a LOT of readings (~150–200 pages per week). They are dense and incredibly important. This isn't a class where you can skip readings: section leaders ask questions directly related to them, and the midterm is basically a variety of short answer questions + an essay where you have to use the readings to answer them (you're expected to refer to specific authors).

The readings themselves are the biggest part of the class; lectures are sort of like "Cliff's Notes" versions of all the readings. Every two weeks or so we'd have Memos which had us synthesize a few readings. These were pretty straightforward to complete. Sections were great and really helped with understanding the details. If you want to read court cases, we only read 2–3 though there's an option to read some for the final paper.
Take this course! It is fun and engaging + the people who take this course with you are also really smart individuals.
Great class! Highly recommend! Covers a lot of different areas involving inequality (race, gender, labor, housing, migration, etc) and provides multiple perspectives on each. As such, the course content and readings are likely to overlap with at least one of your other classes, which is super helpful for bolstering a lit review for a paper in that class. Professor Holland and the TFs are incredibly engaging, knowledgeable, and kind (they all act, seem, and are Real people)!

This course could be improved by reducing the overall number of readings and providing an overview of reading dense political theory articles. The course was marketed as a beginner course for those who have never taken a political science course at Harvard but the weekly readings regularly totaled more than 200 pages and there wasn't in–class instruction on how to go about reading academic articles. If you're going to take this class and get the most out of it, since the content is very interesting, I'd recommend taking it during a lighter semester so that you can devote time to the readings. Also, print them out as reading 50+ pages on the screen for one article can get bothersome.
