i had really mixed feelings about girma's stat mech class for most of the semester but now im proud to be a big fan of this class and girma himself. girma has a very unique and i would say chaotic style of lecturing, where he goes into his own world and rambles on while scribbling on the whiteboard. he doesnt really try to explain himself to people, so you have to try very very hard to keep up during lecture. by keep up i mean understand what he is even trying to say, because the math is impossible to follow since he likes to skip steps and go straight to the result ("we dont care about the math, only the physics" in his own words). he is constantly thinking during lesson so a lot of his weird mannerisms and quirks tend to take over the class very quickly. but this is precisely what makes the class so entertaining and keeps me coming back for more. although it is definitely a true struggle to keep up and understand what he says during lecture, the weird movements and sounds he makes during lecture are rather endearing and pretty hilarious, to the point that several of us sitting at the front spend most of the lesson keeping track of how many times he did this or said that. pretty fun times, and definitely a big factor in making me come to class even though i understand very little from class.

another major thing is that girma's approach to teaching is definitely an acquired taste. at the beginning, many people dropped the class because girma is very messy and not very careful with his reasoning or the stuff that he writes on the board. i really empathise with these folks and understand why they would want to drop the class. however, i would argue that these people missed out big time, since girma really emphasizes conceptual understanding and physical intuition instead of mathematical derivation and interpretation, which is uncommon and very profound at times in a subject as mathematical as statistical mechanics. even though most of his class is a blur sometimes, he likes to drop little gems of physical intuition/justification for why certain things are true here and there, which truly makes his classes unique in my opinion. at some point, people get used to girma's way of teaching, and the bunch of us that soldier on and continue turning up to class eventually get a feel for how to interpret girma's words and ask him the right questions. my personal realization is that the weird stuff that girma says is usually correct (except when he is blatantly wrong, duh. youre gonna have to figure this part out by yourself unfortunately). if you dont understand something, its probably because girma skipped a million intermediate and crucial steps, so what i personally did was raise my hand and pester him like a mosquito whenever i failed to follow for too long during class. often times, it turns into an argument between me and girma in the middle of class, but i always come out learning so much. besides, I think girma really appreciated the questions as well. even though he might not appear that way, he really really cares about student learning which is awesome.

sections were mostly just review and not much else outside of lecture, but i loved it and so did a lot of other people, since girma is lowkey incomprehensible during lecture and we really need recaps every week. assignments seemed kinda boring and were just convoluted calculations at the beginning, but towards the middle and the end when things got really confusing, we really appreciated the homeworks that were, even though simple and routine, showed us concretely how to apply the principles we applied in class. girma's course notes are really amazing and literally saved my life during every assignment and the midterm + final.

to conclude, definitely a recommendation, especially for students who are looking to not think so much like a mathematician studying physics, but a physicist trying to understand physics with math as a walking stick. i dont think i understand stat mech at all after taking an entire semester of it, but i think its more to do with the inherently confusing nature of stat mech, rather than girma being horrible (though i do admit that his teaching has a lot to improve on. but his intentions are good, which is what really matters to me in the end)

Overall: I really enjoyed this class! Material is interesting and very important for physics (thermodynamics, free energy, phase transitions, BECs) and would definitely recommend even though it's not required for physics major.

Teaching staff: Girma is incredibly enthusiastic. It can be difficult to follow everything he's saying, but he's always willing to stop and answer questions. TFs and CAs were top–notch. They also asked for anonymous feedback and slowed down the pace per request.

Workload: Some problem sets had annoying tedious math, but I felt that the math we had to do was necessary for learning the material e.g. showing canonical ensemble/grand canonical ensemble/microcanonical ensemble produce the same result. Overall, exams and problem sets were not extremely difficult but could be pretty long. However, all material needed for them were covered, so you never felt like you had to teach yourself something to do psets.

Background required: definitely know some quantum (fermions and bosons), but other than that, I wouldn't say you need particular physics classes, but rather have comfortability with taking physics classes. You should also be okay with certain math tricks like taylor expansions, some combinatorics/counting methods, probability distributions & expected values. However, Stat 110 isn't necessary.
Statistical mechanics and thermodynamics are extremely important to almost every area of physics, but they are extremely confusing to learn. The material in this class felt very disjoint and it was often difficult to see the physical meaning of a lot of the topics, but once you take enough physics classes it will be clear how important the material is and where it is actually relevant.
If you're a physics concentrator, you should strongly consider taking Physics 181. Statistical mechanics and thermodynamics are fundamental to so many fields within physics and related fields (astrophysics, chemistry, etc.), so the principles you learn from this course are widely applicable. In addition, the approach of this subject is very different from previous physics courses, and I found this approach very refreshing and stimulating. Professor Hailu's enthusiasm for the subject was infectious, and he was a very engaging and approachable professor in lecture as well as in office hours.

With that said, be prepared to work hard in this class. On average, I spent 8–10 hours on each problem set, and some psets took up to 15 hours to complete. I think the time was worth it when the problems were intellectually challenging and gave me further insights about the physics, but most of the time I was left feeling frustrated because I was hung up on some very specific approximation we were supposed to know to make or another technicality like that. Try to go to office hours to avoid these kinds of frustrations, and have a good pset group. The take–home midterm and final exams were similarly time–consuming (out of the 48 hours given for the final, I probably spent 25–30 hours working on the exam), but were reasonable in difficulty and grading. The lectures could be confusing and hard to follow at times, but I found that doing the readings beforehand made it much easier to follow. Professor Hailu also posted very good lecture notes after the lectures, which was helpful for review. However, although a lot of effort is needed in this class, you don't need to worry too much about your grades since grading is very pset heavy (60% in our year) and the midterm and final were take–home and only weighted 30% combined.

Tldr I recommend this class for anyone who wants to get a good understanding of statistical mechanic over a broad range of applications, but make sure you can dedicate at least 10 hours a week doing the readings + psets to get the most out of the class.
