This is a great course to learn about how computing hardware works and the building blocks for the hardware systems modern computers rely on.
Overall: CS141 is two courses combined into one semester: digital computing hardware (transistors–>logic gates–>combinational/sequential logic) and digital design for computer systems (instruction sets, MIPS, processor architecture, memory, caching). A borrowed FPGA board and a Linux VM (Intel–based computers only) with Xilinx Vivado are provided; at least in my year, if you have ARM/Apple Silicon, you either have to figure out your own setup, use Dhilans no–guarantees unofficial setup, or use the SEC Computer Lab.

Professor: Vijay is very knowledgeable in the subject area and gives great insight into the history of computer engineering. Personally, I am not a fan of powerpoint–based lectures, though it does emphasize that you go to class to understand the diagrams and all of his annotations.

Textbook: The required textbook is Harris & Harris. While the course does not explicitly draw from Harris & Harris, its a great read and really helps to bring the course together and solidify understanding. There are also several recommended texts that are not necessary for the course itself.
David M. Harris et al. Digital Design and Computer Architecture (2nd Edition). Morgan Kaufmann.

Topics covered: binary, sign/magnitude, ones/twos complement; transistors (nmos/pmos), logic gates, Boolean algebra; K–maps, logic minimization; half/full adders, ripple–carry adder, carry–lookahead adder, ALUs; muxes (<3), decoders, shifters; programmable logic devices (PLA, ROM, PAL); circuit delay (propagation vs contamination, short vs critical paths); sequential logic, bistable circuit, SR Latch, D Latch, D Flip–flop, registers; FSMs, state tables, Moore vs. Mealy machines; setup/hold time constraints; memory hierarchy, volatility, static vs dynamic, SRAM vs DRAM; CPU datapath overview, ISA, MIPS assembly, instruction types (R/I/J), MIPS registers, procedure stack/register management; single–cycle datapath; Harvard vs Von Neumann architecture; multi–cycle datapath, instruction fetch, decode, execute, memory access, and writeback; multicycle control signals; pipelining, hazards (structural, data, control), stalling, forwarding, static/dynamic branch prediction; memory hierarchy, latency, bandwidth, caches, temporal/spatial locality, eviction policies (LRU); performance, Amdahls law, Roofline model.

Course structure notes: lectures M/W; short weekly psets for theory/calculation; weekly coding lab assignments in System Verilog/Python for application on the FPGA; pset/lab OH throughout the week. There are several guides written by the one and only Dhilan Rapramasad that are extremely helpful; hopefully you also get his section videos. At least in my year, you were responsible for section material, whether you attend synchronously or asynchronously. Also, beware there is a sharp uptick in the course difficulty, which landed for me a week or so after the midterm.
