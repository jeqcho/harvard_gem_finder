Have fun!
Material is pretty dry, but generally well run in terms of course notes/problem sets/exams.
213 is a lot more technical and analysis heavy than 210/211/212, but I think it's very much worth taking to understand the full generality of the results presented in 211 and to understand the types of things people care about in large sample theory (i.e. local analyses). Learning about QMD was kind of mindblowing to me, as it's kind of hard to understand why anyone would think that something like this was the correct thing to try to do. A large number of the results in class will be given without a full proof, so you will need to read them on your own, which often is harder than it might sound due to different notation and reliance on intermediate results not covered. There are not many psets, though the difficulty of them varies quite a bit, and the exams can be hard.
It is a good class to take to gain a more foundational understanding of statistics but be prepared for a large amount of difficult and intricate material that you cannot really learn well without a lot of time and attention to detail.
The 24 hour exams are brutal
I would like to comment on the Course TF: Ritwik

Ritwik is a legend, a maestro, of undying proportions. When you enter section and see the Ritwik wielding the chalk, you know that he will set straight the indifferent, cruel currents of the unsolicited game of life. Going to the RITwik's section was a RITual of seemingly unbounded possibilities, a nirvana... a sheer utopia perhaps, of learning. When the Ritwik was in the room, Classroom 705 turned into a Citadel of higher thought, a true intellectual haven.Â 

Each section was a symphony in its own right. Each lemma, example, and proposition was a separate element that combined and complemented each other in ways initially unclear, perhaps even contradictory to the senses. But only as time progressed, with the mystery of local asymptotics and "Bayesian Statistics" slowly unravelling, did one finally understand the true genius of Ritwik. Like a composer, or perhaps the coy mystery novelist, the Ritwik dangled the plot's conclusion forth of the student in a display of agonizing suspense. Only approaching the ringing of the bell did Ritwik reveal the connections and relations of each separate lemma and proposition, allowing the coalescence of each component to fuse into a glorious creation whose value is truly worth more than the sum of its parts. In this sense, the Ritwik's sections can only be compared to the greatest of human artforms: Rachmaninoff's 2nd, the Mona Lisa perhaps, and surely the myriad remakes of John WIcK movies.

If the sections were the orchestra hall, then the office hours were the place of salvation. The "homework" assigned were tasks of Herculean proportions, but Ritwik was Hercules. The problems fell under his glance and cowered amid a swift slash of chalk. Their only remainder being the gentle sprinkling of chalkdust that fell gently to the ground, like the lingering mist after a summer storm. He was the beacon guiding us through the fog of ignorance towards truth; without his light, perhaps we would never have made it out of the mist, doomed to drift forever amidst the waves of unintellectualism.

Here concludes the telling of the tales of Ritwik. But alas, why do I try? Try as I may, my words are but a loathsome depiction of the glorious truth. After all, how can the words of a man describe the musings of a God?
