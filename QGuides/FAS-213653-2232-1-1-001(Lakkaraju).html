Take this class if you want to learn about the latest work in ML interpretability in a systematic way.
This is an amazing class with an amazing instructor and course staff. You will certainly learn a lot, not just about interpretability topics overall, but you will also become more confident in your independent research abilities. You really get what you put in from this class. If you're a student who's taken CS 181 or equivalent and really wants to do a deep–dive into some topic in ML interpretability, this is the class for you.
This is a great survey of the top papers in ML interpretability (which is becoming a more and more pertinent field as AI models grow larger and better). The lectures essentially cover two dozen key papers and their findings. Another great benefit of the course is being able to meet other students interested in the topic and work in a small group on a research project of your choosing. The research project is a bit unstructured but provides a good avenue to learn about a specific topic. My only qualm is that I wish there was a bit more guided teaching from Hima / the teaching staff, rather than the majority of the course being student presentations.
This class has a great selection of readings and course staff and overall a light workload. However, I would say that you get out what you put in, since the class is mostly student presentations on the readings. Take this if you want to work on interpretability research or if you are an ML researcher and want to learn more about the subfield of interpretability.
great selection of papers that cover a good balance between depth and breadth, although there could be more focus on e.g. mechanistic interpretability and language modelling, given that ML is accelerating scarily fast and the most cutting–edge work tends to be the most impactful (and cool)

interpretability is important! much of ML is really dumb by just scaling up and seeing how capabilities advance, without any effort at understanding what's going on under the hood! reading a lot of these papers gives the impression that there is a LOT to do here!

some cool speakers, but I will say that this class follows the seminar–type format where the bulk of class is just student presentations of papers (which doesn't really advance understanding very much at all given that you're expected to read the papers too—perhaps there is a better way this class could effectively use its time).
Great course! ML interpretability may be the most impactful thing to study right now –– the future of human existence depends on it. Thorough overview of the interpretability literature, and discussions were engaging and interesting. Posters at the end of the course were great and showed that the class is provides a strong foundation to do interpretability research, especially with all the checkpoints, reproduction of baselines etc., and meeting with the TFs to discuss, were helpful with the research process. In fact, this is probably the class that provides the most professor/TF guidance for its final project among any research class I've taken, and I really appreciated the professor/TFs' commitment to making sure everything is on track, which is a breath of fresh air compared to many other CS classes at Harvard.

More emphasis could have been placed on mechanistic interpretability and future research directions in this area. The course is currently too focused on applications in healthcare, etc. It's a little sad that very few projects turned out to be about mechanisms and language modelling since these were covered very late in the course and were probably inaccessible to most students (although there were some exciting ones!)

If you're interested in doing impactful interpretability work or generally worried about risks from advanced AI, I highly recommend getting involved with the Harvard AI Safety Team (haist.ai)
This class has a lineup of interesting papers. The subject area is interesting and quickly growing, which is exciting. The class is pretty relaxed and goes at a good pace throughout the semester. The final project was fun and gives a good opportunity to explore an interesting topic. It was great for Prof. Lakkaraju to provide a lot of project ideas that would all be promising and substantial enough to find significant results. The course staff was also great in giving helpful advice/feedback along the way to guide us with our project. The presentations were fun and not a lot of pressure. Some feedback is that there were no rubrics given for participation or presentations ahead of time, so grading felt a bit arbitrary.
This class teaches you all about ML interpretability, which is one of the most important and impactful areas of research. You’ll cover everything from what interpretability is, the theory behind it, and all the different methods such as inherently interpretable models, feature attribution, algorithmic recourse, unlearning, mechanistic interpretability, and what interpretability looks like in the face of transformers, diffusion models, and RL. The course gave me more confidence in reading ML papers and understanding what the interpretability field is all about, and is overall an excellent course to take. The final paper was a joy to do and I felt very empowered to be able to create novel interpretability research because of what I learned in this class. It definitely pushed me to consider doing more ML interpretability research and there are just so many research ideas floating around after our class discussions. The class is a seminar–style class, where each week students take turns presenting on a paper. In my experience, this has been very good method at teaching the material without being a slog. The people in the class are just so smart, the guest speakers are all leaders in their field, and it’s just an amazing learning opportunity. You get started with your research project pretty much right away, giving you plenty of time to work on it with many (nonscary) checkpoints in the middle that keep you on track. The number of office hours and support staff is small, but it didn’t really get in the way of seeking help. There’s not much I have to say on how this course could be improved.
Pretty interesting course with a manageable workload! The class is pretty heavily structured around student presentations though, which aren't always the most engaging. But if you're interested in interpretability, would definitely recommend!
the topic is so unique and it's hard to find people who really rigorously combine conceptions of ethical tech with research into the mechanics of that tech like this class does — so i think it's overall worthwhile to take this course if you're interested in that space of rigorous ethical tech, even as there are some minor qualms with how the course is organized. there are some barriers of background but imo the beauty of research–based cs classes is that you can read and read and read more in order to gain the background you need :)

Great course to understand current advancements in Interpretable ML! Also good way to work on a substantial research project.
