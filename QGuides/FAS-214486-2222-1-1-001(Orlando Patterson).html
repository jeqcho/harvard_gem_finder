This class has no work aside from studying for the two exams and writing the final paper. I recommend it if you're looking to get an easy A, but if you're genuinely interested in learning about human trafficking, I did not feel that this year's course format was very conducive to that.

The lectures from Prof. Patterson were great, but I found the sections a bit of a letdown. While Patterson's lectures covered human trafficking related topics and incorporated philosophical questions about the nature of trafficking/how we should address it, Ben (my section leader) pretty much only discussed big picture ethical questions. As someone who took the class to learn more about human trafficking, this was disappointing, especially because Ben frequently went on tangents and seemed pretty disorganized overall (he sometimes blatantly blamed delays on exam logistics, etc. on Professor Patterson which was also odd and slightly disrespectful?). We only received section assignments 3–4 weeks in the semester (very late relative to other classes), which forced me to rearrange my work shifts.

I was also in an Engaged Section. We did no actual “engaged” work with a nonprofit or human trafficking–related org until very late in the semester (mid–April) when we began working on final projects. Aside from the late start date, I really enjoyed my Engaged Section project (with HEAL Trafficking). It was led by an undergrad who was very organized (especially relative to Ben) and clear about her expectations. I wish we had had more time to work with her and HEAL.

TL;DR — This class is an easy A but needs a new teaching staff, as everything outside of lectures felt very disorganized.

The course content is interesting and gives a good overview of trafficking and modern day servitude. BUT be warned, it is logistically a mess (Spring 2022) – in fact, it was probably the most disorganized class I've ever taken at Harvard. First off, the professor refuses to share his slides or lecture recordings. He oftentimes mumbles during the presentation, so it's extremely difficult to copy down the information on the slides while also focusing on what he's saying. The slides have SO many words on them, and quite a few graphics as well. We are encouraged to cite those numbers during the midterm and final exams, but because it's often unclear which stats are the most important, we have to rush to copy down as many as possible. I suppose it is to encourage people to attend the lecture in person, but it makes no sense to punish the entire class of 170 people for this. The lectures are literally just a dictation exercise. Everyone furiously types to copy everything down, and when the professor goes through a slide too quickly, there is a collective sigh in the auditorium. Second, there is very little feedback in this class. It took the TFs a month to grade the midterms, and by the time you're in April, that is the only indication of how you're doing in the course. Finally, I feel there is probably a huge disparity in experience with this class based on if you are in the "engaged section" or not. You can apply to join a special section where you work with an anti–trafficking NGO, while the rest of us sit through lukewarm (and oftentimes off–topic) discussions instead. We don't even get to hear about what those sections worked on throughout the semester. I feel this set up is quite unfair, as students can get two completely different experiences. Overall, this class is a 2/5. I'm sure it was better when it was a small sociology elective; the GENED program has truly killed many formerly–good department courses. I will say that this topic is quite relevant and interesting, so I would encourage people to audit this class.
