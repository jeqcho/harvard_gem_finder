What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Attend the section, and explore the material in nontrivial ways. You will gain a far greater understanding of what is going on if you try to think through the problems and apply various approaches than if you try and apply a memorized set of steps. Appreciate the beauty and applications of what is being taught, and you will enjoy this class.
I thought Math 21A was a very manageable course. While there are 3 PSETs a week, they are all doable and really helped me to get a better understanding of the course material. In addition, there is SO much support from the teaching staff from MQC to hours upon hours of office hours nearly every day. The tests closely resemble the practice tests, so if you do all of the practice, the exams should not feel too stressful.
I enjoyed this class a lot more than 1B. The content itself is very engaging and it's just a cool subject to learn about and see the applications it has in the broader world..
It's pretty fun if you like math, but keep in mind that it is 3 psets a week, each of which take 3–4 hours each. It used to be 1 pset a week but they recently changed it. The exams are very difficult. It is very interesting though, more applicable than linear algebra I'd say. If you are interested in finishing calculus, I'd take it, but be prepared for a decent amount of work. Find friends in the class who you can do psets with because honestly, 3 psets a week is not sustainable if an emergency or something comes up.
This course cares way too much about its own pedagogy. It feels to need to justify the way it teaches everything; one problem on a PSET told us to explain a certain concept as if we were talking to a friend, and then included a footnote saying that "studies show teaching a concept to someone else helps you learn it better." No one cares.

On the surface, this is just mildly annoying, but it speaks to a deeper issue in the course: they refuse to teach you anything upfront. During every section, the TF will talk for like 5–10 minutes max, and then you spend the rest of the class working though a worksheet that will ultimately help you figure out what the formula–of–the–day is. I get the pedagogical rationale here — by having us try to figure things out on our own, we will hopefully gain a better fundamental understanding of it. But at a certain point, I want you just to teach me! Instead of spending an hour trying to figure this out on my own, you could just tell me what the formula is — explain why it works — and we can call it a day!

What's especially frustrating is that this course eschews any attempt to get at a deeper theoretical understanding of what we are doing. You learn (read: teach yourself) one, fairly straightforward concept per section, and that's it. The material is very surface–level yet is also taught very inefficiently.

In general, though, aside from these frustrations, the course is pretty good. The material is just generally quite fun — doing things in 3D is cool! The 3 PSETs a week are a total drag but it does drill the material into you. And they're really not very hard most of the time.

Still, I'd recommend people take the 22A/B series instead. I took 22A and really wanted to take 22B but wasn't able to because of a silly scheduling issue. Dusty isn't back, so who knows how it will be, but I think that's a much more in–depth, theory–motivated way to go.
This class is a good class to take if you love math. I was a second–semester freshman looking to find my concentration and this class just killed me. I was doing work outside of the class constantly and it was very negative to my mental health. That being said, I have a bunch of friends who are planning on concentrating on math and they loved it.
