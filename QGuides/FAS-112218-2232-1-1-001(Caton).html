You will learn lots of words that are meant to describe pretty common sense things, and the class made me question the relevance of the Gen Ed program. That said, Professor Caton was funny and I think the course would do well in the anthropology department.
I really wanted to like this course because the TF and Professor are so kind and enthusiastic about their subject, but unless the structure of it improves I would say that you will likely feel lost and aimless at many moments during the semester.

have fun with the class professor caton is great
Do not take this class. The class itself is interesting, but there is too much disorganization with grading and evaluation. Additionally, while each lecture may be interesting, it is often times hard to see the bigger picture or understand what the class is working towards.
I thought the fieldnote component of this class was interesting, and it forced me to think more deeply about the use of language in my everyday life. However, I don't think the dense readings on linguistics and semiotics that made up a large part of the course were really for me, and it was a struggle to stay interested in them during the 9 am class time. I came into this course wanting to learn more about the case studies – Western Apache placemaking, Yemeni poetry, hip hop, Korean evangelical song – which all seemed really interesting, but ultimately I realized the course (and section) was mostly theory, which didn't appeal to me as much. Still, I'm not sure what other aesthetics/humanities GenEd would have been more worthwhile, so this course is still a decent one overall if you're interested in really researching a place in the Cambridge or Boston area.

Don’t take unless taking pass fail. Caton and TFs cannot explain highly technical concepts well. Readings usually seem either overly complex or irrelevant. Attendance is mandatory and taken in the first 5 minutes of class (the old fashioned way). However, workload is very light (1 response paper/wk and a couple of 3–5 pg essays)
If you have any interest in linguistic anthropology, this is a great course to take. The content we learned in the course felt equal parts anthropology and equal parts linguistics. The course did feel lopsided at times with significant time being dedicated to the professor’s own anthropological work. While his work was fascinating on its own, other parts of the course suffered in terms of the depth of study and analysis. Professor Caton, though he is retiring, was a captivating lecturer who was always trying to engage the class.

This course is writing focused with weekly short responses, multiple fieldnotes, one midterm essay and one final essay. As someone that enjoys writing, I did not find it a bother but I understand some of my peers did not feel as equally. I think I also enjoyed writing in the class more because the requirements were very loose. Sections in this course felt absolutely useless and significant improvements could have been made. If it was not for the mandatory attendance requirement (which was not always enforced), I would not have attended the sections.
Overall, it was an interesting course but I felt like there may have been other options in my GENED category that may have been better taught. I did find an interest in linguistic anthropology and the direct exposure to the subject matter in the course provided me with plenty of insight on my own academic and personal interests.
this class feels more like an anthropology course than a gened, not the most accessible, lots of writing
This course epitomized the flaws of Harvard's transition to the GenEd system: a passionate instructor who taught a Linguistic Anthropology course smushed in the GenEd to disillusioned students who simply needed to fulfill a requirement. The class material, professor's lecturing, and written fieldnotes and essays (aside from the reading responses) were interesting, but the disengagement made the course lackluster.

Professor Caton is a fun professor; his experiences are unique, and he definitely has a strong opinion on the topics he is teaching. Be prepared for the field work component of this course.
