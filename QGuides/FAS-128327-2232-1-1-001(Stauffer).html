This course sheds light on how questions of racial injustice have always been at the root of American democracy, and how the story of the civil war has been re–written throughout various points in American history in order to achieve various political ends. The course offers great flexibility in how you approach your written assignments (i.e., creative projects versus formal analytical essays) along with very helpful feedback, so students can engage with the themes of the course through whichever angle they prefer.
This course covers material about the "long Civil War" – from Nat Turner's Rebellion to Birth of a Nation. You'll learn about not just politics and history, but also culture, art, literature, and the like from this time period (and specifically, how they played a role in the bigger arc of history). The course really does tell a compelling story, from the origins of the American slave society to revisionist post–Reconstruction history, and will help you understand America in a different light. Professor Stauffer has a very unique lecturing style which takes some getting used to, but it makes you feel like you've learned something profound and have learned about things from a different "angle" from how the Civil War is usually taught. The readings span from contemporary (1800s) archives and major speeches to modern analyses of the period, and you will also read a few complete books, but the reading load is ultimately very manageable and not unreasonable at all. The assignments are also of a reasonable length and give you a lot of agency to explore your own passions and what you find interesting in certain readings.
If you need to take a gened in this category, this course would be a very good pick. There is a fair bit of choice in exactly how much work you put in, but it is generally rewarding to put in more work. The lectures are unusually engaging.
