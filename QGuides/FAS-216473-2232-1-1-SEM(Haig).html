What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
I would tell future students to listen to other students and see if they can use other people's perspectives to form their own views on evolution and its drives.
If you are coming at this as a biologist like I was, don’t be intimidated by the philosophy majors in the course. David Haig puts in an honest effort to make sure everyone’s interests are addressed, but it’s up to you to engage with what your classmates are contributing. Don’t be afraid to steer the conversation, because in a 12 person class your participation means a lot.
If you're looking for a science distribution rec this is pretty good and interesting (assuming you're a humanities kid). It's not a mindblowing inspiring course, but it's solid.
This is an excellent course if you are interested in talking with fascinating people once a week about a variety of topics, from the mechanism of consciousness to Wittgenstein's theory of colors.
This class is a mixed bag. It has lots of pros: diverse subjects from diverse opinions, scrutiny of an interesting professor, and long open time to explore in a beautiful discussion space. The same pros can easily be cons, though: not really spending the time to critically assess ideas, a more indignant perspective without much airtime for other ideas, and long conversations where you could notice eyes closing or distracted actions. Sometimes, I worry that there can be more talking than listening, and it borders on posturing.
The class is enjoyable and engaging. The workload is rather light, and you get a lot of value out of putting the necessary time into the reading and assignment each week. I would recommend this course if you are interested in thinking about the philosophy of biology, even if you are only moderately interested in the subject.
Take OEB 128! Prof. Haig is just absolutely lovely and so knowledgable and the book is interesting. Class discussion varied wildly, which was fun. Of note: take a look at the first few chapters first (I believe you have to read Ch. 1 to petition for the course anyway) and if the book is not for you, then you may not enjoy the class. I found it interesting, and thoroughly enjoyed my experience in this course.
This is a great course for humanities folks to get the SEAS requirement out of the way! It's accessible, challenging, and rewarding.
