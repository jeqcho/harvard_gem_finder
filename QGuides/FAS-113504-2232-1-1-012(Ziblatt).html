What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
Your GOV 97 experience varies based on what section you get. My section which was on redistricting was a great course. I believe it had less readings compared to other courses but lottery for the sections you have an interest in and you will be fine! Gov 97 overall is just a course for discussing reading materials and getting a final paper as a result of the readings/discussions and research you do.
The difficulty with this type of course is that everyone is coming to it from a different baseline familiarity with research. GOV 97 basically assumes you have never written a research paper before or searched Hollis; we spend an hour talking about the difference between a causal and descriptive question. So if you're new to all this, this course might be helpful. But otherwise, I'm not sure you'll learn much about research and writing in political science, beyond that the exercise of writing a research paper is always good for research skills.

The surface–level depth here was especially a problem for my section on redistricting, because the material is such that it doesn't make much sense to do anything other than a quantitive paper. It would have been helpful, then, to spend time reviewing quant methods and honestly they probably should have made GOV 50 a prerequisite for my section.

Anyway, you shouldn't be discouraged from concentrating in GOV by this course. All humanities/social sciences sophomore tutorials suffer from the same issues. At worst, you'll get some interesting conversations on what is hopefully a GOV topic that interests you, but don't expect this course to improve your research/writing or give you a deeper intro to poli sci methods.
I highly recommend this section. It was really interesting, readings are not too heavy, and Chris explains things so well!
Beware of arbitrary grading in Gov 97. You'll think that you're a good writer and then your instructor will basically spit on what you wrote, stomp all over it, and call it trash. Okay, maybe I'm exaggerating––but only a little bit. Be prepared to really rethink the way you write (and reshape it to fit the department's expectations). Apart from that, be open–minded about whatever section you get placed into––you'll learn something regardless! The lectures are also very informative and portray a fairly diverse array of perspectives.
