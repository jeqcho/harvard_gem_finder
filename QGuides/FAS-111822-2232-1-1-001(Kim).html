Philip Kim is a great teacher!! He’s kind, organized, and approachable in a way that many physics professors unfortunately are not. The most common criticism of Philip is probably how difficult his tests are—they are designed to have a 65–75% average—but I think if you study well then none of the material being tested is unreasonable. Philip certainly takes the class seriously (i.e. it moves quickly!), but he’s really nice and will always listen to students. Unfortunately I think this is his last semester teaching 153, but if you have a chance to take any class with him, I would seize the opportunity.

153 contains some pretty challenging material, but it’s definitely manageable. If you’re deciding between 15b and 153, definitely shop 153! I did not have much in the way of E&M background before this class, but did have the trial–by–fire experience that was Physics 16. 16 prepared me very well for learning physics I had never seen before, which ultimately was a skill that helped me much more than I think any content knowledge of E&M would have. I think the way this class is designed, it is very accessible (in that you start with the same content as in 15b) but it moves much faster—so it’s doable as long as you commit to putting in the time to study and make sure you stay on track. Everything will be explained and derived in sequence, but you just have to make sure you don’t get left behind!

Looking back, we covered some pretty fun topics in 153 that I wouldn’t have seen otherwise in 15b. So I would definitely encourage you to take this class some time in your 4 years as a physics concentrator!

My first course in E&M and the most difficult course I took this term, Physics 153 was a great experience and was certainly one that helped me mature a lot as a physicist. Professor Kim's expertise in the material was instrumental in all aspects of the course, and I very much appreciated his choice to lecture on the subject by helping us build intuition through examples. Our course community also very much appreciated his flexibility regarding the exam components of the course which I believe to have had a reputation amongst the physics concentrators for being notoriously challenging.

With regards to what prerequisites are needed for the course, I would say that a good understanding of basic mechanics and special relativity as well as a comfortability working with vectors and with calculus are definitely sufficient preparation – the course develops the theory of Electrodynamics from coulomb's law so there is certainly no strict requirement on having done 15B or 15C. What I would say however, is that all students of the course would benefit from ensuring that they are comfortable with vector calculus by reading Chapter 1 of Griffifth's textbook – from what I have seen, Math 21A provides insufficient intuition for what the mathematics actually does and says. In contrast, I came in to the first lecture having learnt multivariable calculus from Griffifth's text the weekend before and have found no gaps in mathematical understanding since. I would also remark that Professor Kim tends to skip over long computations in lecture unless they are conceptually important, which I was very grateful for as a lot of the mathematical objects that we work with in 153 (Fourier series, Laplace's equation) have been discussed at (too much) length in 143A which I took in the fall, and detailed discussion of these objects is usually included in the textbook anyway for those interested or confused.

In terms of the course content, I found the most challenging aspect of the course was overcoming the learning curve presented by the Electrostatics in Matter subunit where the concepts (multipole expansion, polarisation, electric displacement field) were very unintuitive upon first encounter. This unfortunately fell right before the first midterm which I consequentially found to be very difficult. Fortunately, I was a lot less confused after having encountered a renaming of the same ideas in the Magnetostatics unit and the course flowed much more smoothly from then on. Do bear in mind that 15B does not save you from having to overcome this either as this is not covered there.

Our community in 153 this year was both larger than usual and younger than usual, and as a result I was very fortunate to make many good friends in the course, all of which are very bright and passionate, who have successfully convinced me to double major in maths and physics (instead of just maths as I originally planned). If this is the case in your year, please take full advantage of this as the p–sets are a grind and trying to finish them by yourself from start to finish each week would definitely be quite arduous and a lot less fun than working on them with a good group.

Despite the course's rather intimidating reputation, I do encourage you to try out Physics 153 as your E&M option. Witnessing the whole theoretical treatment of the subject take place from start to finish was a beautiful and fascinating experience, and you will be rewarded handsomely for the work you put into understanding each piece of the puzzle along the way.
This course moves very quickly and it takes effort to keep up, so be conscious of that when structuring your schedule for the semester to allow enough time to synthesize the material and spend time on psets. Exams were quite difficult, but reading the textbook thoroughly was very very helpful in studying for the exams. Ultimately, you leave the class with a broad overview of electrodynamics, which I feel will let me build off what I learned or be able to pick up new related topics easily when I see some concepts again in other courses or research.

This is a great course, and I'd recommend it! The material you cover is challenging, but it is very interesting.

We aimed to cover all of Griffiths' Introduction to Electrodynamics in one semester – this was an ambitious goal. This material is often taught as a two–course sequence at other universities, and so this class operated at quite a fast pace. I enjoyed it, but would have liked to spend more time on the applications at the end (chapters 9–12). Unfortunately, there is only so much you can squeeze into a semester, and so if you can't cover all of that material in depth, I'd still recommend reviewing it, because it's just super cool.

In this class you cover a lot of really interesting and cool material. It starts with electrostatics, moves to magnetostatics, then we begin the electrodynamics, looking at EM waves and Maxwell's equations. It wraps up with applications such as waveguides, reflection, dipolar radiation, relativistic electrodynamics, and more. Lectures are fast, and so reading the textbook is a helpful review activity to solidify your understanding. Problem sets are also great for developing better intuition and understanding of the material.

Make sure you are solid with multivariable calculus going in. I'd recommend being familiar with E&M at the level of AP Physics C: E&M before the course. I took this without having taken 15b, and found it manageable, but I know people who were glad to have taken 15b before, so if you're debating between the two, shop them for a bit. At the beginning they both cover electrostatics, so it should be relatively easy to switch from 153 to 15b if necessary for the first few weeks.
