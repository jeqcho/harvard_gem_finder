However, DO NOT TAKE THE CLASS IF YOU'RE NOT PASSIONATE ABOUT THE SUBJECT. The workload is intense. You read one novel per week, every week, and complete 6 essays throughout the course of the semester, PLUS revisions on all those assignments if you are trying to do well. DO NOT TAKE THIS CLASS FOR THE GRADING STRUCTURE. Although it's self–graded, the requirements to get above a B+ are extremely strict. It is an automatic B if you miss more than a single weekly discussion post.

Overall, this is a great class if you're interested in the material. However, if you're not, please consider taking a class you're more passionate about, as this class is a heavy workload for those who only take it to fulfill a requirement.

By and large I thought this was a fine course, but I don't know if I'd ever recommend it (there are so many great courses to take!).

Things I liked: a course like this encourages you to read a lot, which is really wonderful. The writing assignments are also smaller and often somewhat creative, which gives you a lot of room for inventiveness and experimentation. The TFs put in loads of effort to give feedback to all of these assignments. The un–grading model is actually great in my opinion and really provides a constructive experience without adding needless stressors and punishments.

Things I didn't always like: the books :( ... Sarraute was incredible, Gary very entertaining, Louis and Guibert quite readable – but most of the rest inspired indifference at best. As far as I can tell, Professor Kim had two main goals in her text selection: first, to advance an anti–identitarian argument; second, to shock you with gratuitous content. The former is perfectly fine but should be noted to attune your expectations – themes of identity and associated issues of social justice are the focus of almost all discussion. The latter goal is where I more particularly object – Professor Kim makes fine arguments that we should attend less to our shock and avoid discounting texts just for covering unpleasant material; however, she does not always compellingly prove that these particular texts are worthy of this effort. For instance, just because French critics were wrong to attack Darrieussecq's All the Way for its swearing and graphic sex (notably of the underage protagonist and her crusty adult babysitter) doesn't mean it is immune to being bad for other reasons – like its oddly TERF–y tone – and certainly doesn't prove that it is good.
