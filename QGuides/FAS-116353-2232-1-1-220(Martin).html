If you actually would like to improve at writing and thinking, then you should definitely consider taking this expos. If you’re looking for an easy A with no reading and few papers, then keep searching.

Ross is a fantastic teacher. I came into this class confident about my writing skills, but Ross pushed me in every paper—and while I spent a lot of time writing and thinking and revising—I can most definitely say that my writing is much better than it was at the start of the semester. Ross’s ability to keep challenging me to think more deeply about how to construct arguments and deliver them to readers is remarkable.

This class is probably on the more difficult end of the spectrum as far as Expos classes go. The readings actually are very manageable – they are all court cases, and there are a few readings that are long (30+ pages of case opinions) but for the majority of the semester, it’s <5 pages per class. And there aren’t really any assignments besides the essays. BUT the essays will kick your butt if you don’t work on them. Ross is very generous about providing feedback, but you will have to be proactive about asking for it and be willing to commit time to this class. That’s certainly not the expos experience that most freshmen want, but if you are looking to push yourself and have some remote interest in politics/government/constitutional law then this will be a great fit for you. I think an A is very doable in this class, but you’ll certainly have to put in the work. I probably spent ~15 hours on each of my essays.

If you really care about becoming a better writer, look no further than Ross’s class.

Expos is a class that you're required to take, so you should definitely make the most of it! I know it can be tempting to take expos classes that have reputations for being "easy" expos classes, but I highly encourage you to select a topic that you'll enjoy and that will push you. Taking Ross' Personhood in U.S. Constitutional Law expos was one of the best decisions I made my first–year and I say this with full confidence. Ross is the best and he makes this course what it is. He's simply just an incredible educator. I feel like I had really outstanding teachers in high school, so it was so amazing getting to connect with a college instructor who I felt cared for me like my high school teachers did. He is genuinely invested in seeing you grow and thrive as a student and this is something that you will come to see and appreciate more clearly as the semester progresses. He will constantly check–in with you and he's always happy to chat with you (one of the highlights of my semester was talking with Ross after our expos section ended). Besides the fact that Ross is just an incredible human and terrific preceptor, the course content is super interesting. A variety of really important Supreme Court cases make up the core of the reading for this class which is really cool. The discussions you'll have in class are nuanced, engaging, and really meaningful. You'll feel closer to all the people in your section because you collectively take on talking about very difficult topics. This is certainly a class that pushes you, but I think that was one of my favorite parts of the course. The rigor never feels arbitrary or unnecessary; it feels deliberate and productive. Ross has spent a lot of time fine–tuning this course and that comes across when you're a student in the course who can see how well–run it is. In short, take Ross' Personhood in U.S. Constitutional Law expos—it might be one of the most rewarding opportunities you have in your entire time at Harvard.

Take. This. Course. If you have any interest in law (I didn't really) or politics or US history, this is a great course for you. If you don't have those interests, this is still probably a great course for you. Ross is an INCREDIBLE professor, both from a professional standpoint and a personal one. I have grown so much as a writer based on his feedback (and I started off as a very confident writer), and he leads the class in a very engaging way, actively trying to promote original ideas and discussion about these court cases. From a personal standpoint, Ross is incredibly kind, funny, and caring. He clearly wants every student to do the best they can and get the most out of his course. He is flexible (within reason) with deadlines, understanding when issues come up, and almost always available to talk about ideas for your paper. In feedback, he does not just give surface level notes, but is able to look at the fundamental strengths and weaknesses of your writing, and he makes sure to note both in a way that you feel satisfied about where you are as a writer but confident and determined to improve. In terms of class dynamics, our class got really close with one another, and our discussions always remained respectful, even as we dove into serious topics like abortion, forced sterilization, and slavery. Ross also does a great job managing discussions, making sure that no one is put in a position where they have to defend a practice or position they disagree with, while also encouraging us to dive deep into the judicial opinions. I definitely am doing a bad job describing how he does this, but just know that if you are worried about the seriousness of the topics at all, do not be. Overall, this course was incredible, and I am so grateful to have taken it.
