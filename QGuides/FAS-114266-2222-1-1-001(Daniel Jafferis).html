Really consider if this course and its material are right for you and your future before taking it. It is a lot of time and effort, and you should not pursue it unless you really want to.
Cannot recommend with more enthusiasm. The pacing is pretty quick (we basically got 90% of Carroll while covering some content more in–depth than the book) and the workload is high (~20 hour psets) but the theory is beautiful and lots of fun. One of my all–time favorite courses.
This is a really worthwhile class if you're willing to put in the time and effort. It's a lot of work, as it requires getting accustomed to a whole new notation. The lectures were often difficult to follow, but they didn't end up being super necessary for the class (though it would have been nice for them to be a bit more comprehensible). This is a very cool class that opens the door to a really interesting and important field, and I recommend it even though it has a few weaknesses.
This is a fairly reasonable class. I recommend taking it after you've seen quite a bit of physics, because I think you'll get more out of it (i.e. you can take it freshman year but I think it'll be much harder for you and also less rewarding). There's a pset each week and a take home final. The lectures have little to do with the psets but the textbook is great and David was an amazing TF.
The physics we did GR was so different than anything else I had every seen before and you learn a great deal. But it is very hard. If you're willing and able to put in the time, it is very worthwhile and the stuff you learn is super cool (black holes!). Additionally, taking math 136 (differential geometry) isn't necessary but probably helpful.
Take it, it is a great experience, the material is extremely interesting.
This course is great. You learn a lot of fascinating physics, including some that you may have read or heard about from, say, Stephen Hawking's books. Knowledge of differential geometry and continuum mechanics/field theory is helpful but not required.
