I'm in a superposition of feelings about this course. And if you ask me in the morning and then ask again later, you might get a different answer. First: Barandes is extremely dedicated, enthusiastic, and accommodating. Second: the TAs were incredible, too. The course content, though, left me wanting. I wasn't always able to draw ties from one piece of material to the next. It felt like each class we would walk out having heard, "now you have the tools to begin doing quantum mechanics," but we never hit the punchline. I know it's a foundations course, so just expect that going in. The philosophy aspects are on you to weave into the course. It's there and ready to grab! But I do think the application (seeing particle in a box and other canonical experiments) should motivate the material, too—not just the mathematical beauty and evolution of the field.

In all, calibrate what you're looking for. This course does a good job of most things, but know for sure that it is what you want!
This is a great course! Don't expect it to give you a more advanced mathematical understanding of QM, unless you do that research instead, but instead it can provide reassurance about the "naturalness" of the QM axioms, as well as an overview of current competing interpretations of QM.
I would recommend this course to students who have already taken PHYSICS 143A and want to dive deeper into understanding the underlying physical formulation of quantum mechanics. This course definitely succeeded in giving me a deeper understanding of the foundational principles of quantum mechanics, and taught me a variety of concepts that I'm glad to have learned in a more concrete fashion prior to more advanced physics courses, such as density operators, spectral decompositions and entanglement. The format of the class was also very relaxed since there were no exams and the only assignments were weekly problem sets that only took a few hours each. That being said, if you're considering this course expecting it to be a philosophy course about quantum mechanics, this may not be the course for you. We spent very little time on the philosophical implications of quantum mechanics, and while there was ample room to explore these topics during the final paper, minimal time was spent on philosophical topics during lecture. Overall, while this course may have only made me a slightly better philosopher, it definitely made me a better physicist, and that's undoubtedly still useful.
Classes like this are why you came to Harvard. It's absolutely fascinating stuff, so well–taught, and a positive, joyful intellectual environment. Take it, you won't regret it!!!!
TAKE this class if you can!! Jacob is a great lecturer, the puzzles of quantum theory are unsolved and it's so gratifying to grapple with the current state of affairs. Be aware the last 3 problem sets were significantly harder than the others.
This course is fascinating, especially for those who like open questions. You won't learn the answer to how to conceptualize quantum mechanics, and you probably won't discover it. But you'll do a lot of productive thinking, and you'll derive textbook quantum theory from classical logic, which will make you understand the theory much more. I definitely recommend you take it. If you do, here's my advice. Go to section and office hours. Some of the most interesting discussions happened there. Work on the homework with others — it can be a lot of fun, and it can make potentially confusing problems/readings less overwhelming. And don't be afraid to suggest something radical. The theory is weird, and a lot of cool discussions came out of radical suggestions.
