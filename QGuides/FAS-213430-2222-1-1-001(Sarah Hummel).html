I was recommended by a friend to take this course. The Q–guides in prior years praised this course as a great comparative government option. I feel the class took a nosedive this semester, however. To start, the grading among the TFs was inherently unequal. There were multiple instances of students with different graders being graded on grammar and where they started a paragraph. But then, someone would not be penalized for writing two pages over the page limit on an assignment but someone would for writing a single page over. I suspect this is because the TFs were either philosophers or Near Eastern Language graduate students, both of which have significantly different grading standards than a Government TF. The way dictator groups are assigned also needs improvement. Some groups, likely when they believed they would be graded fairly, chose very interesting dictators like Genghis Khan. However, be warned that all the course assignments require qualitative and quantitative data to back up your claims. Do not choose a very old case to analyze! Lectures also often felt mundane. I will say, however, the lectures were helpful in pinpointing the key themes of all the readings. In terms of the quiz, I also think more time is needed. 75 minutes for three short answer questions and a long essay is not enough time, especially with the breadth of evidence they want us to use. I also wish we spoke about more authoritarian examples since this class is very Russia and China–dominated. Overall, I think with the right TFs, this course could be (and seems to have been) amazing.
Definitely take this class if you need a gov elective. The workload is very light, but the lectures are great and you learn a lot.
