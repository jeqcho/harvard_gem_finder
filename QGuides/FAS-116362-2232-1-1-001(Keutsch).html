It's a good course! Frank and Bryan are great instructors. The courseload isn't too much for a pset/lab class, and the material is manageable if you pay attention, ask questions, go to some office hours, etc. It's a good complement to PS11 (which is a bit too much work in my opinion). If you don't have any coding background you should know that you'll probably struggle with that component of it (either Python or R), but I encourage students to make friends in the course and work through the coding with someone who has more experience – people are happy to help out and work together. The labs are pretty fun! If you're interested in ESE or EPS or ESPP it's a great intro class to expose you to a bunch of topics so you can get a feel for what you actually care most about / what you want to study more in the future.
I would highly recommend taking this course. Not only is it a fantastic introduction to the major of Environmental Science and Engineering, but in general, it was incredibly interesting. Labs and lecture discussions felt very relevant and the topics that we discussed were fascinating. Further, I found the workload for this course to be very reasonable and felt that the instructors were incredibly encouraging and supportive.
This class is very interesting and make it a rule for yourself to attend as many lectures as possible because Brian is a great professor.
This class is ok. It exposes students to various introductory topics in the environmental science field. Enrolled students are primarily in the first year. Lecturers are knowledgeable about what they teach, and TFs are dedicated to students. However, this class can be very messy and complicated. Class content changes drastically every week, and the information given tends to be very shallow. Labs are particularly confusing because they are often not strongly tied to what they teach in lectures. You don't learn much unless you put a lot of your efforts into going over the details of lecture slides outside of the class. Personally, I don't really find it problematic, as one of the lectures clarified that this class is intentionally designed like that as an intro ESE class. But, you may want to be very cautious about taking this class if you have any previous knowledge in environmental science or look for knowledge deep into certain topics.

ESE 6 is a great course for those who are interested in environmental science but do not have a lot of previous experience/strong sense of specialty in what they want to do within the field. I particularly recommend this course for ESPP or social sciences students who want to supplement their knowledge with STEM.

The course presents a variety of environmental topics such as stoichiometry, water, and soil, which are each taught in enough depth to get a birds' eye view but not so in depth to where you feel like you're sinking if you struggle with a particular unit. The workload is reasonable with no exams, although the coding portions of the PSETS and labs can be challenging if you have no prior experience. Going to office hours is very important for success, and time management is crucial due to the alternating pattern of lab reports and PSETS.

Rather than being an average lecture course, this class was special because it was more like an introduction to a community. There were many out of class opportunities to enhance learning, and I got to know many of my classmates as friends throughout the course. This class was definitely not the meaningless grind that STEM courses are sometimes typecast as at Harvard, and instead, I felt like I had the opportunity to be curious and learn naturally.
I really loved the enthusiastic teaching staff, and small class of ESE 6. The labs are super fun and Nick is awesome. The PSETs and lab reports are pretty challenging, but there aren't many of them so it feels reasonable. The hardest part of this class is definitely the coding if you have never used python/R. I struggled with the coding quite a bit, but there are a lot of office hour opportunities to get help.
Definitely take this class if you may be interested in Environmental Science and Engineering, or even ESPP! It covers many topics and is helpful for overall understanding of environmental systems.
Fun labs, diverse topics, cool people.
This class is fun and you will get an overview of different paths of work/study in environmental engineering.
I thought ESE 6 was a really fun class! There are no exams, so I could learn the material for understanding rather than for memorization which made me like the class a lot more. The PSETs were a couple of steps up from the lecture content, but there's a lot of office hours available to help and I think I learned a lot from getting through all of them (there were only 4 this year). If you're interested in the environmental sciences or climate from any angle, everyone in ESE 6 is passionate about climate and environmental issues and are also studying a lot of different things. For me, hanging out with and learning from my peers made the class so much better. Although the class is from a science vantage point, I found that the lecture content is extremely relevant and important even for those who want to take a different approach, and they try to relate it to many other disciplines.

Unfortunately, the coding instruction for R in ESE 6 was not very approachable for beginners this year. I think it was partially because we were constrained for time (we didn't have enough sections to really get into detail and get enough practice with the concept) and also because we didn't spend enough time really talking about why the coding was important in the first place and how to approach new problems with the techniques we learned. I had prior coding experience which was extremely helpful in navigating the coding instruction. If you find yourself wanting to take ESE 6 but hesitating about the coding portion or a lack of coding experience, one helpful resource to prepare for using R (if you do end up using it in the class) is "R for Data Science", a textbook available online that covers a lot of what we covered regarding coding in more detail.
This course is interesting and provides a good overview of various topics in environmental science. Since the course is introductory, however, it tends to cover topics on a more surface level, rather than in depth. The labs are engaging and the final project is particularly interesting, as it provides freedom to explore an air quality–related issue of your choice. Many opportunities for office hours are provided, and they are useful not only for working through the problem sets but also for understanding concepts better in the process, though they are particularly useful if you have started the problem set in advance of coming and can ask more specific questions. At times, the workload can seem variable, but it is generally manageable.
As one of the professors described the class, it's kind of like a buffet where you try out different "foods" (or environmental disciplines) throughout the semester – through this, you find what you're curious about and what you'd rather never study again. This was helpful because it helped me understand the depths of the environmental space. Labs were really fun and in the SEC which is an amazing building, and there's a field trip as well which helps connects our class concepts locally in real life. I like that there is a lot of flexibility from the teaching staff, no exams, and ample support on psets – the focus is really learning the material. However, your understanding of course content will be HEAVILY augmented if you do have a strong background in the natural sciences (it builds a sense of intuition and foundation that I did not personally have, so there were some lectures where I didn't feel like I learned much only because I was missing a lot of key foundations). This was said in the syllabus though so I knew what I was going into.

The coding portion was difficult – Coding cannot be taught comprehensively in one or two sections, and having this as a part of the class was only useful to students who had previous coding experience and could thus rely on their intuition to get through coding–heavy problems.
This course is a great introduction to Environmental Science and Engineering.
