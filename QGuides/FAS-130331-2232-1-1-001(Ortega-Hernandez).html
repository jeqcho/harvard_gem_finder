While the lectures could be interesting and the sections/labs were great, there were multiple major communication issues around exams and logistics that really made it hard to enjoy the class. Additionally, there were labs but no psets so the exam and the midterm were each worth around 30% of your grade.
As an OEB concentrator, I went into this class excited to learn about fossils and prehistoric fauna, and the class does get to that, but there's a lot of earth science (sedimentology, climate regulation, tectonics, etc.) and a few billion years to get through before then. Communication/the lack thereof in this class was frustrating at times, and some of the more EPS–friendly content was new to me, but the material is genuinely interesting and the spring break trip brings you to fossil sites you'd never see otherwise, which is an amazing opportunity. The weekly workload is very manageable, despite some issues with the exams and these midterm group presentations, and the teaching staff are all very approachable and passionate about their respective fields.
don't underestimate the magnitude of this class being cross–listed as both EPS and OEB! the mix between OEB and EPS is a bit much but manageable. but coming from OEB I was definitely pretty bored/confused with the EPS elements. the course communication was quite poor (info about the midterm, student presentations, general updates about what's going on in the class). the lectures were ALL over the place, but such is the nature of the content that the class tries to cover. each lecture is a bit of a fire hose of information. lab felt very disconnected from the rest of the course
SPER COOL STUFF, as a History concentrator the actual course lectures are very accessible to non–STEM and everything is explained super clearly. The labs are a little worse to work through, but still interesting material!
This course is really fantastic––it's a combination of OEB and EPS, so no matter what, you get challenged in some ways and interact with more familiar content in other areas. As long as you have a bit of background in one of the areas, you'll be fine. I would recommend going to all TF–led review sessions prior to the exams, because they are really useful for synthesis of all the information––lectures are often quite fast and pack in a lot of detail at once. If you can go, the field trip is a really incredible hands–on learning experience.
If you're an EPS concentrator, you'll love the first half of this class, and if you're an OEB concentrator, you'll love the second half (although it's very invertebrate–focused, so if that's not your thing, I'd avoid the class). Labs are difficult without help from the TFs, and lectures pack a lot of content so be prepared to study hard for exams. That said, the class is a surprisingly well–planned tour of the history of life, and the field trip is so incredibly worth it (but be prepared to work!).
The content in this class is really fascinating and the teaching staff is really passionate about what they study. If the lectures were a little more organized and the labs were related a little more closely with the rest of the course content, I would highly recommend this class to anyone in either EPS or OEB who's interested in learning more about the intersection of the two disciplines. In its current format, the class is still enjoyable and not too much work and the fieldtrip was a blast.

Take it!! Class is fun and content is super interesting if you're at all interested in evolutionary bio, zoology, geology, paleontology, climatology, or honestly just science in general.
If you are interested in the history of the Earth, take this course. The first half was very much focused on geological processes and wasn't particularly focused on life, which I found a little boring because I love environmental science, but I appreciate the broad understanding I have of geology and the formation and early stages of Earth. I absolutely LOVED the second half of the course, as we spent each lecture talking about the evolutionary feats of life and extinctions during subsequent periods. I particularly love ecology, and this course does an incredible job at teaching the evolution of ecological relationships and developments throughout time. This course barely has any work outside of lecture. Lecture slides are engaging and very visually–friendly. If I could I would take this class again.
