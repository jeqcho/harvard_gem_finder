What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Prepare yourself for one of the most disappointing experiences to be had within the Faculty of Arts and Sciences thus far. In this course, it became evident that many students had developed the university’s broader penchant for embracing post–modern notions of meaningless discourse, leading to frustrating discussions that left one questioning the purpose and coherence of the subject matter (ostensibly, “the Constitution”) at hand. If this course is emblematic of what most humanities courses at Harvard are like, I am seriously worried.

Throughout the course, it was not uncommon to find ourselves immersed in extended recursive conversations that seemed detached from reality, centering around abstract concepts such as the very definition of a “foundation” or the supposed distinctiveness of the “current constitutional project.” These discussions often meandered aimlessly, lacking the intellectual rigor and substantive depth one would expect from a graduate–level course. It appeared as though the professors themselves were unprepared, as they were the ones posing such abstract questions in the first place in order to start the class. This decision allowed for a disorganized and fragmented learning experience. (They alleged that this was done purposely, in order to allow them to exist as mere facilitators of student–led conversation; not only do I not buy this claim, but I also think it’s a bad idea in a course full of pompous twenty–something–year olds who each all believe that they alone can solve all of America’s problems in one fell constitutional swoop.)
One memorable class session on "progressive constitutionalism today" epitomized the prevailing atmosphere of intellectual obfuscation. The typical question posed to the class, "What about this time – is this constitutional moment different?" elicited unanimous agreement, with students hastily citing examples of increasing election denialism or the shifting post–2016 political landscape. However, as the conversation unfolded, it became increasingly clear that the students' contributions lacked meaning and coherence. Sentences were uttered that seemed divorced from any discernible purpose, with individuals veering off–topic and questioning the relevance of discussing constitutions in the first place. The absurdity reached its peak when one particularly smug student, a repeat offender, proclaimed "I think we are talking about democracy, not constitutions!" followed by – I kid you not – a personal anecdote about his conservative parents from Texas. The professors responded with smiles, seemingly amused by the discordant nature of the discourse.

These instances exemplify a pervasive sense of intellectual aimlessness throughout the seminar, leaving one to end the semester pondering the value and direction of the course. The students' inclination toward Foucauldian–like thought replaced any attempt or pretense at substantive legal analysis, leading to a brutal twelve weeks devoid of meaning and purpose. In retrospect, it is disheartening to consider how a course at HLS, supposedly renowned for its academic rigor, could devolve into a realm where discussions are characterized by incoherence. Your time at the various schools within the broader Harvard University umbrella is valuable; I strongly urge prospective students to invest their studies and valuable time elsewhere, and not on this course.
This is a superb class with an enormous amount of content. Only take it if you are willing to do what is, for an undergraduate FAS class, an enormous amount of reading (the whole course reader took up three 2–inch binders). But the reading is great; none of it is superfluous.
This is a really fun and illuminating course. It covers the whole sweep of U.S. constitutional history, and exposes students to a range of perspectives and scholarship about contentious legal/historical issues. It's awesome to be in a class with HLS students too.
This course is an incredible opportunity to be introduced to constitutional history. Professors Lepore and Mack are exceptional, the former bringing a phenomenal historical perspective and the latter grounding our discussions in the law. Prof. Lepore takes the time to hand grade your assignments, meaning that you have a renowned writer for the New Yorker helping you improve your writing and argumentation. At times the class size did feel a bit large, would probably have been better with 16–18 people as opposed to 24 or so. However, you definitely get a chance to contribute to discussion, and everyone knows each other on a first name basis. Would highly recommend!
This is a discussion based course, where you will hear mostly from other students during the weekly two hour discussions. Beware as the assignments are graded very harshly, so make sure that you meet beforehand with the professors to ensure your work is in line with expectations. The readings were absolutely fascinating, but the discussions each week often strayed from the readings into vague discussions.
This course is rather challenging in terms of both the amount of reading that is required, so I would recommend taking this course if you are able to balance that with the workload of your other courses as the readings are a large part of the course and its weekly conversations. Overall, I recommend this course to anyone who is interested with Constitutionalism and want to become a better historian or aspiring legal scholar.
Take this course if (and only if) you are deeply interested in constitutional history and want to learn a lot! In this course, you really get as much as you put in. The readings are intense but worth it if you have the time. On the flip side, completing the readings was always a struggle.
