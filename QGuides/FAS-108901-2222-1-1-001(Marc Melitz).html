Though the TFs were great for this course and Prof. Melitz is very unpretentious, I am pretty disappointed in this course. I think it sacrifices critical discussion about the theoretical, ethical, philosophical, etc framework of classical economics in favor of pretty blind plug–and–chug math problems, which is not what I'd expect from a higher–level economics concentration requirement. I think Ec 10a had more meaningful discussion and applications and even comprehension, and quite frankly the more technical mathematical approaches can be learned pretty quickly elsewhere (on YouTube, with a good teacher/friend, etc) and will be forgotten quickly after not using. To be fair, though, it is interesting to finally use some calculus tricks in a non–math class and the problems are do–able enough to be somewhat enjoyable. Go! To! Section!
The course is weighted heavily on exams (2 midterms and one cumulative final that together count for about 75% of your grade). Don't underestimate the time you need to prepare for them. Definitely take advantage of sections, as I found them to be the most helpful way to develop a solid understanding of the class material, as the problems we do in section are similar to the problems on the problem sets and exams.
Section is the most important thing to attend. It will help you a lot with homework and prepare you for the exams.
Frankly a waste of time, but if you need to take it, you will survive it just fine. It is not a hard course; however, you will have to give it some amount of time greater than 0h/week, which is a shame, because you could be doing wonderful things with your talents.
If you're deciding between taking 1010A in the fall or spring, 1010A is a lot smaller in the spring, so there's less support, and it becomes a lot more of a "you get out what you put in" type of class. I would strongly recommend attending section to make sure that you're understanding what's going on in lecture, and to do all of the provided past exams in preparation for the midterm and final. Marc is also super sweet and will stay after lecture to answer any questions, if you have them.

What I've heared from friends is that 1011A gives you a much more solid and holistic mathematical approach to micro concepts, and 1010A gives you more intuition and less of the theoretical background. Overall, 1010A is not too huge of a time commitment – weekly web problem sets + small quizzes, along with two midterms and a final, comprise your entire grade. Not a spectacular or lifechanging course in economics, but I think it was interesting overall and I got quite a bit out of it!
Go to Section!! Even though they may be optional, they are super helpful when lectures are confusing. Also utilize office hours and sections to clarify any concepts you may be confused about.
melitz is a great lecturer/professor, and I really enjoyed taking 1010a in the spring. not sure what the fall version is like, but I like how the psets are in the form of Smartworks, where we get infinite tries + hints + explanations to the problems. overall, weekly psets/quizzes are very fair and great to learn from. i also like how the course is structured so lecture is for conceptual understanding and section is for problem–solving. i debated between taking this class and ec1011a in the fall, but as a math/cs–leaning person, taking this class in the spring just made a lot more sense for freeing up space for more math/cs classes in the fall.
