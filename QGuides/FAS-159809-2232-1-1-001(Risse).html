This was an excellent class to take, even as a senior. The readings were often dense, but Prof. Risse does an excellent job of breaking them down. He is a stellar professor who really makes progressive viewpoints feel welcome at Harvard and encourages dissent with esteemed philosophers. Learned a lot of new things and felt like it was a comfortable introduction to poli phil, something I've always avoided!
This class is great. You get as much as you put into it. The course material is truly interesting, and you might find yourself enjoying the readings a little too much. Apart from that, the essays are tough, and do not be scared of the final exam.
This class is fine, but I was disappointed for a few reasons. The main one is that the class was very abstract and philosophical, which made it hard to relate to issues in the real world sometimes. As someone who is interested in economic inequality in this country but who doesn't have much econ/gov background, I was hoping to gain more practical knowledge on the subject but don't feel like I necessarily did. I may have just misunderstood the purpose of the class, but I would encourage those who are interested to carefully look over the readings in the syllabus and get a better idea of what the course is really about because I felt like the course description was a bit misleading. Although Professor Risse is generally pretty engaging, a lot of the lectures ended up feeling dry and the sections did absolutely nothing to contribute to my understanding of course material. The assignments and final exam were fair––not incredibly easy but also not too much of challenge.
The lectures and professor Risse were very good and interesting. However, the papers were graded way too harshly. They expected you to be a philosophy student and this is a gen ed. I would not take this class unless you are studying philosophy and know how to write a philosophy paper and analyze philosophy. They grade you as if you know that.
This course is really hard to stay engaged with at times, the theoretical nature of the economic justice discussed in class makes it feel distanced from real life.
*** Great class! Assignments/readings are not too intensive (only three essays + a final exam, and all of the readings are covered by the professor in lecture anyways so no worries if you do not understand everything on your first pass through). A very accessible introduction to political philosophy/economic justice (I came into this class with little prior exposure to philosophy, but everything is explained so well and builds on each other.

The one minor criticism I would have is that the class (rightfully so) touched a lot on the African American experience and how attention to racial issues can not be separated from class–based analysis of society. However, at times, hearing discussions about the African American lived experience from the perspective of an all–white teaching staff felt a little awkward at times; I am not sure how to deal with it, since I think the course would be far worse without this aspect of the class, but maybe bringing in more guest speakers could help resolve this gap?

Otherwise, a very good class I would heartily recommend!

As an economics concentrator, I enrolled in this course with the idea that we would be fixating heavily on economic theory, referencing various economic laws and principles, etc. I was completely wrong.
Understand that this is a political PHILOSOPHY course, not an economics–focused course. You will be spending the majority of the course discovering various historical thinkers and philosophers within the realm of economic justice – which can be an extremely beneficial and valuable in a multitude of ways. Dr. Mathias Risse is at the forefront of his field, and the opportunity to enroll in a course of his is simply awe–inspiring. However, if you're seeking a purely economics–based course, I would potentially recommend seeking alternative options. Nonetheless, the course is extremely rewarding, and you leave the course viewing the world from a completely different lens and perspective.

Professor Risse's lectures are pretty engaging and informative in presenting arguments and context (adding value beyond the readings), and he has an emphasis on highlighting underrepresented thinkers.
It had a reputation for having tough grading so I took it pass/fail; in retrospect, I think the grading scheme might be tough but it's still definitely a reasonable task to get an A. I honestly wish I had taken it for a grade so I would've been forced to learn the content much more deeply. I think the most intimidating thing about this class was that a lot of students are concentrators in economics or philosophy, which shouldn't matter in a class with a fixed grading scheme. The exam was also very, very chill (the multiple choice questions were mostly simple and straightforward, while the short–responses were all drawn from a question bank that was provided beforehand).
While the topic is interesting and I learned a lot, be warned that most people who take this course are social studies, philosophy, or adjacent concentrators. The grading also reflects that standard. I'm very glad to have chosen to done so––I very much doubt I'd get an A or A– in this class.
