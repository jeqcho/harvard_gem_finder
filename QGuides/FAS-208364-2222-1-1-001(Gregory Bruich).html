Professor Chetty is one of the biggest names in economics today, and that alone makes this course a useful one to take. Lectures tend to mostly just be him talking about latest research, while labs are completely separate (ostensibly they relate to that week's lecture, but you don't need to have remembered what occurred in lecture to do the labs). For Stata, the instructions give you all the code you have to do, so coding background is not at all necessary – especially near the end of the semester in the Jupyter notebook labs where all you do is press run on code they wrote for you. The only homework besides two projects is the labs, and those you complete within section so there's essentially no out of class work. The midterm has some trivially easy questions, and the instructors try their best to be lenient on grading. The course attempts to accommodate everyone regardless of background, and as a result this course is a great way to have a stress–free low workload class that satisfies the quantitative requirement and is just generally interesting.

One caveat is that Professor Chetty is inaccessible out of class, so if you want to ask him questions make sure to do so during lecture itself. Additionally as part of its nature of trying to be accessible by everyone, none of the units ever go much in depth so at times there can be whiplash from covering so many topics so quickly.

Some of these best approaches may surprise you, such as the fact that nutritional disparities exist not because of lacking access to healthy food but because of nutritional choices/lifestyles, or that criminal justice can be partially be addressed by giving judges longer lunch breaks. If you are also involved in addressing social and economic issues, you might find our lecture content useful in informing your work or providing knowledge bases that can support your work and efforts to mobilize outside support/resources.

Beyond content, the course is very easy – the professor truly try to encourage all students to understand the course concepts (e.g., the assignemnts provide you the code/answers; conceptual understanding is emphasized more) and pursue the discipline by being lax with grading.
Contrary to what this review might suggest, I really did like this class overall. Professor Chetty is an amazing speaker, and I learned a lot from his lectures.

This class is marketed to students as a class that can be taken by anyone, regardless of background but THIS IS NOT TRUE! As someone who has never taken an econ/stats/computer science class + didn’t know anything about coding, I found this class really difficult — but not in a good way. I took the class because I wanted to be challenged, wanted to learn something new, and wanted to learn R. I don’t really feel like I’ve gained much from the class, though. It tries to do two things at once — teach about big data and economics while also teaching about R and statistics — but by trying to do both, it kinda does neither. I would have much preferred a class that focused solely on one or the other. I found that every assignment throughout the semester (labs+projects) were focused on R/stats and felt blindsided when the midterm/exam focused almost entirely on more conceptual understanding of economics and graphs. I also don’t feel like I came away with much knowledge of R because most of the complicated code was given to us verbatim, and there was no intro class for anyone without prior R/stata knowledge.

If you have a background (even a very small one) in econ+coding (any language), I think this class is great. I genuinely learned a lot from lecture, and I think Professor Chetty is fantastic. The idea of this class is awesome — I think Professor Chetty's work illuminating the benefits and potential of big data is important; I just think the class should not be taken by complete novices.
