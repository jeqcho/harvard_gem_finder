Here are my thoughts on Dr. Mettetals version of 1661:
Let me preface all of this by saying first that Dr. Mettetal seems like a great person. Really kind, someone who you can easily approach after class, and whos interested in the work she does. But she is not a very good teacher. She is not an engaging lecturer and lacks command of the classroom, and runs through such a high volume of slides that its hard to see what is important. Lecture often feels like youre just having facts listed at you without any real analysis or synthesis of what you should know.

Here are the pros of 1661: the workload is light, the tests are easy (if youre not a braindead ESPP/ec major), and the lecture notes are well organized so you can read through them quickly to pattern–match the answers to the psets when you inevitably stop going to lecture.

In general, I had some other qualms with 1661. The class is designed essentially as an intro–level econ/ESPP class, so the economic theory discussed is very shallow and not that interesting. The first half of the class is focused on theory, of which there is probably 45 minutes total of valuable content, and the second half of the class goes through policy examples and case studies. 1661 is very much a survey class that covers a wide range of topics in environmental and energy economics, but at the end of the day does not leave you with the satisfaction of feeling like you can confidently understand any individual topic at a deep level. Its not a bad class (since the workload/grading is quite nice), but it will not be a world–changing or particularly fulfilling experience. Go ahead and take it, but with very tempered expectations.
