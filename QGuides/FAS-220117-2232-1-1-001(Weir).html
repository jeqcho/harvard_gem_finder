Probably the most interesting gened I've taken so far! Weir is an amazing lecturer, and each of the readings are incredibly interesting. If you are looking for a chance to explore Russian literature and haven't done so before (like me), I would definitely suggest this gened if it is offered again! The section component was also pretty good, and I found that it was accessible to students from non–humanities backgrounds (most of the time). One thing that I definitely noticed was that there were two separate groups within the class: those who had read Tolstoy & Dostoevsky before, and those who hadn't. However, it seemed that most students were reading the stories and novels through a strictly ethical/moral lens for the first time, so everyone was able to engage in interesting moral discussions in section and in the midterm paper, regardless of background.

I do wish this course had a paper for the final instead of a timed final, as personally I loved having the chance to conduct my own exploration of the moral dilemmas in Anna Karenina (in a non–timed setting), and wished I had the chance to do so with The Brothers Karamazov, as I found its dilemmas far more interesting.

Initially, I had no qualms with the timed final, as it seemed to me at first that it would be strictly focused on the moral questions of the class readings. However, one thing that was not made clear until later in the semester was that the final would consist not just of written essays, but would also have questions gauging whether we knew the readings or not. As a significant chunk of enrolled students had already read these works before (with some having read them *multiple* times beforehand), this was a tad frustrating, as it meant that I would have to revisit every reading again before the final to internalize minute details that had little to no bearing on the plot (or the moral questions we had been discussing all semester), and would not be picked up on a first read of these works. While this wasn't impossible, it definitely caused this class to take up quite a bit of time, especially during reading period. This was probably my only issue with the class, and I wish we had more of a heads–up that we were expected to be able to answer these types of questions earlier in the semester, as it would have changed how I approached the readings. With that being said, if you take this class, be ready to spend a lot of time on the readings!
