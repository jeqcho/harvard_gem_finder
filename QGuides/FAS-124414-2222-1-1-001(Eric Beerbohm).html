GOV 10 is a strong introduction to political theory. If you enjoy considering the moral questions behind different forms of government and different governmental policy decisions, you will probably appreciate this class. It provides a strong overview of class political theory while also pairing those texts with more modern and practical interpretations. Of note, however, is that the reading load if fairly heavy for an intro class, though the foundational texts that make up the bulk of the reading are all fairly well–known and summaries can be found if you're running short on time.

Professor Beerbohm is an excellent professor and a wonderful human, and I would absolutely recommending going to his office hours and getting to know him if you can. The TFs were also very solid when I took the class, and I thought the sections were engaging (though they're bolstered if you have classmates also willing to engage with the material).

If you are already familiar with political theory, you will probably find this class straightforward; the reading and writing expectations are not meaningfully different from other classes in the field. That being said, the class is still worthwhile for those experienced with political theory for two reasons: 1) You get to engage with Professor Beerbohm, who is fantastic and has done really interesting work on applied political theory; and 2) The course provides a strong overview of many of the most important thinkers and can help fill in some gaps in your knowledge if you've previously taken more specialized political theory classes.
