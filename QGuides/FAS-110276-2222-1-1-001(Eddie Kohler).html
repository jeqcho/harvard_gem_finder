Eddie is great! Loved all the readings. Class was a little unstructured but we had more than enough notice about upcoming assignments and topics.
Please take this course.
You should definitely take future iterations of this class! Even if you look at the topic area and are on the fence, do it anyway. I did, and wasn't disappointed!
Be prepared to spend a lot of time reading research papers. It is time consuming, but you do get a lot out of it. It's very nice when you're able to participate in the discussion. It happened to me often times that I only got to truly understand a paper after someone else spoke about it in class. You are technically able to get away with skimming the papers, but then you might as well not take the class. Apart from the final project, we only had one other assignment, which was fun and relaxed. You have lots of opportunity to do whatever you want for the final project; please start early. It is nice when you are able to produce actual results by the deadline, and it feels like a very rewarding end to this course.
Great class – you get out what you put in though!
If you like the topic this class is a great way to meet the concurrent CS master's 26x/24x requirement. Eddie is a great professor who really cares about student engagement, listening to feedback, and helping everyone get the most out of the course. This year it was distributed computing – which have always been a bit interested in – and I feel that I really learned a lot. The course covered important papers that everyone should read like MapReduce, thought us practical skills like Dask and Spark, and even touched on some modern topics like cryptocurrencies.
Pretty chill seminar where you read papers, do one assignment, and then an open ended final project. I think there are pros and cons to this approach. The topic this year was distributed systems for ml+big data problems, which was interesting, but not a lot of people had background in this area so the small amount of work wasn't ideal (because it was hard to learn about the ideas without implementing them in my opinion). A small amount of work probably would have been better suited if the topic more directly branched off of 161/263. However, I'd still recommend this class regardless of the topic as an elective (undergrad or grad) because Eddie is awesome and steers the discussion to help you learn, the topic is likely going to be interesting, you'll be in a class with some really passionate and smart students, and you'll learn something (think of it as a reading group + project).
Take any class you can with Professor Kohler! All of his reading courses are so much fun, you'll get a really solid grasp of the research space, and you'll make friends with everyone in the class. This class was a lot larger than usual, which seemed more difficult at first, but turned out to be very nice. All of the papers we read were enjoyable, and Prof. Kohler was so receptive to picking papers based on students' interest. I don't know when 260R will be offered again, but take it if it is. You won't regret it!
This course changes each year, so ymmv. That being said, its a great class with Eddie as a professor. Its very chill, but expect to read 4–5 academic papers a week, which is not trivial reading. Otherwise, dont worry– youll have a great time
Overall a fun class – I would take it just to get to interact with Eddie more. You get to read a lot of foundational papers in the field of distributed computing and systems. You end up absorbing a lot of the high–level information regarding the field, but I'm not sure how well some of the details (i.e., the differences between systems and how they all interact) stuck. While the class is not very difficult, it's a bit more work than I expected (reading four papers, even if only skimming, can add up and the projects are non–trivial).
