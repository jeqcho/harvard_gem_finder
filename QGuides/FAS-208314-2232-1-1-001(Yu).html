Professor Yu is a great lecturer, and you get to learn a lot about networks and datacenters in her class. Assignments can be difficult sometimes, with P4 being an obscure language with little documentation, but course staff is generally very helpful.
The course in general will teach you a lot about networks and how they working in large scale data centers. The lectures are pretty dense but can be hard to follow at times due to the uncohesive nature of the slides. The problem sets are not very difficult but the testing portion can be tedious and involves a decent amount of busy work. The midterm is not that bad and there is enough extra credit offered that one could end up with like 105% despite a median performance on the midterm.
Don't take this class because of the psets, but audit it and actually pay attention in lecture and you will learn a lot!
I want to say what you read from the previous year of Q guide are mostly accurate. Here are some pros and cons of taking this course:

Pro:
1. you learn the nitty gritty of some protocols like TCP and BGP because these are important. But you also get to learn how these are modified and extended in datacenter settings. So this blend of old and new stuff is pretty cool.
2. You learn to implement some protocols and test their performances in artificial settings in the programming assignments.
3. Biweekly assignments, once single assignment, and exam are fair and grading is not hash. Very abundant opportunities for extra credit from coding assignments.
4. There are some cool people (college students+extension school students) who are building websites and apps already and you can always learn from them.

Con:
1. Due to the first point of the pros, the course can feel like covering a lot of ground at times, and although Professor's Yu's slides are very information, it could still be confusing for network newbies to figure out the concepts and making connections between them.
2. Might actually waste a lot of time debugging if the programming assignment handouts are not clear (don't worry, we are almost always suffering and waiting for an answer on Ed forum all the semester). Also, the p4 programming language is kind of a domain–specific C language with syntactical restraints that you just need to cope with.
3. Exam might rely on understanding somewhat but you need to memorize a lot of details from slides. Good thing is that the exam doesn't take up much portion of course grade.
This class is a really good introduction into networking concepts. It will feel disorganized at times, but the staff is very friendly and understanding, always there to help. I think that networking is an important subject to learn and this class definitely introduces you to almost everything you need to know in order to study on your own. The class requires quite some work, but is not a hard class by any means. There are many opportunities for extra credit and the exam was very approachable.
As a computer science concentrator, I'm really glad I was able to take CS145 before I graduated. The exposure to concepts in computer networking like networking protocols, sending and receiving packets, thinking through creating and setting up computer network topologies, ISPs and networking policies were all new to me and seem like very foundational ways to think about systems of computers. Additionally, there seems to be a strong analogy that could be drawn between networks of computers and networks of humans (from social networks to networks of companies) and how information moves through these networks. While taking CS145, I continuously saw how concepts from class came up in my own life every week. And seeing that connection was a really valuable and cool process and experience. Professor Yu is very knowledgeable and very considerate professor and I'm glad I was able to take the course with her as a professor. The only thing that is tough about this course are the projects for the course that involve P4, a computer networking programming language that you'll probably be exposed to for the first time in this class. Simple ideas that were easy to understand and grok at a conceptual level were often difficult to implement in P4 because it's not always clear how the language works. Office hours and Ed are your best friend. Ask lots of questions at both and know that lots of students in the class are encountering similar bugs and hiccups for the projects that you are.
Unless the assignments become more aligned with the lecture content and are more about applying the concepts than dealing with P4 syntax, I would likely not recommend taking it. The lectures move so fast that one is not able to absorb most of what is going on. The course staff is really nice, but work is needed to improve the logistical/curriculum issues.
Only take this course if your interest in networking surpasses the pain you will experience of parsing unclear instructions, buggy boilerplate code, and high workload. Definitely would recommend it over the other networking course, but it was quite frustrating at times.
