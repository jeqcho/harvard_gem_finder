TAKE THIS CLASS! As a person living in the US, it is everyone's responsibility to learn about poverty– how it affects different people, how prevalent it is, and how to reduce it. The intersectional lens of gender and race added a necessary and wonderful element to this class, and it humanized the readings in a great way. We also listened to lots of podcasts! Dr. Mtshali is a brilliant, intelligent, wonderful lecturer and sociologist. Sara is the best TF and so so knowledgeable about social issues. The workload is reasonable and the content is very interesting.
You should take this class no matter what your lived experience or understanding of poverty is! Especially if have never experienced poverty, you will be exposed to a lot of eye–opening knowledge.
The teaching team for WGS1274 are really great and I think poverty is a super important topic not highlighted nearly enough on Harvard's campus, so everyone should take a course like this to understand that. That being said, for a low–income student, the material was not very new, surprising, or enlightening. The course covers a lot of breadth kind of repetitively, but has lots of potential nonetheles*
This class is great, but since it has to cover so much and understandably should especially highlight Black people in poverty (since this subset is significantly more marginalized than other racial subsets), it has to glaze over other important topics such as elderly people in poverty, youth in poverty, LGBTQ+ people in poverty, Asians in poverty, etc. I wish we somehow delved more deeply into these topics without losing emphasis on the intersectionality of being Black and living in poverty.

I also feel like a huge piece to comparing incomes in America is considering them in the context of 'cost of living.' I come from an area with a slightly higher cost of living index than average. So, the incomes are a little higher than average, but it also means that the minimum amount you have to make in order to sustain yourself is much higher. You can take the income of someone who has to couch surf in our area and say that it's greater than the income in another area, but that person still can't afford basic needs even on a higher salary. I feel like we generalized the experiences of those who don't live in poverty to ultimately simplify the scary truth that even those outside of poverty struggle (albeit at a MUCH lesser extent) to afford a livable lifestyle. I remember that we were talking about Harvard's COVID response, and someone said, uncorrected, "well, people with money could just fly off in their private jets." If you look at the wealth distribution in our country, the number of people in America who can actually fly around in private jets is ridiculously small. The statement said in class wasn't wrong, but it distorted the perception of living outside of poverty in the US that diminished how almost all of us still struggle with money in some way, shape, or form. Maybe the class could have covered the 'middle class myth' or introduced some sort of budgeting exercise so we could see how much you actually have to make to stop worrying about money forever. I think it actually would have *strengthened* the teachings on just how hard it is to survive in poverty to learn that it is still hard to afford life outside of poverty in the US because it would have shown us that people in poverty even have it worse than those who suffer outside of poverty.

All of that being said, this class was really, really great and filled with fruitful discussions. Professor Mtshali was awesome, so I hope that whoever is teaching it next year did the course title justice like she did!! I'm really grateful for the professor, our amazing TF, the wonderful class, and the tight–knit class community.
DR. MTSHALI is one of the best professors at Harvard and genuinely cares about her students.
