Consider this hypothetical situation: extreme inflation has caused the price of eggs to decuple (increase 10 times), so you decide to buy a chicken to get some fresh eggs with your own hands. From Stat 110, you know that the chicken will lay Pois(lambda) eggs per month, so you’re very excited knowing you can expect to get lambda free eggs each month! Yay!

But suppose this was the real world, with a real chicken. No one knows the true value of lambda! Instead, we will have to estimate the value of lambda using data––and this is one of the first things that you will do in this course.

In Stat 110, you developed the theory of probability––Stat 111, you’ll apply this theory to the real world, and see how Stat 110 can be very useful. If you took Stat 110, I highly recommend taking this class, since it puts all of the difficult theory you learned last semester into context. It’s super satisfying when you see statistics describe the world––from predicting volcanic eruptions to baseball batting averages.

The material one learns in Stat 111 is a lot more applied than Stat 110, for example sometimes during daily activities I think of things that I can apply Stat 111 concepts on, and I feel confident attacking any more advanced Statistics concepts after finishing this 110–111 series. The class emphasizes the trident – theory, simulation, and data, and they do a good job facilitating the understanding and seeing the connections between concepts and real–world practice in the problem sets. One small caveat is that the lectures and the tests heavily focus on theory while on homework we focused quite a bit on the other two branches, so it is crucial to do all the practice exams before midterm and final to do well in them.

The class had 11 units in total. Before the midterm, we focused on: likelihood function, score function, fisher information, maximum likelihood estimators and method of moment estimators, bias–variance tradeoff, asymptotic behaviors of distributions and estimators, sufficient statistics, and exponential families. After the midterm, we focused on: regression, hypothesis testing, bayesian thinking, bootstrapping, sampling and resampling, and causal inference.

Stat 111 is a classic Joe Blitzstein course. His lecturing/teaching style is fairly uniform across his classes, so you'll get many of the familiar experiences: exams are difficult but fair, problem sets help solidify understanding of abstract theoretical topics, and the textbook he co–wrote to accompany the course is really a backbone for the course (though less so than in 110). As expected, lectures are somewhat slow, there will be complaints about exam difficulty, and Joe will spend a solid amount of lecture time going over somewhat tangential topics (though it's always entertaining!).

111 builds directly off of 110; familiarity with content from 110 (e.g., well–known distributions, counting, CLT, LLN) is critical for doing well in the course and understanding the content. If you're uncomfortable with the content from 110, 111 is fairly unforgiving – the course assumes you're solid on 110 content and uses it without much background. Some of the stories from 110 also return – some problems (mostly on problem sets) assume that you're familiar with chicken–egg, bank post–office, etc. If you've taken 210, some of the topics will come easier – 111 uses Slutsky's theorem, delta method, CMT, and some representation – though it certainly isn't necessary.

Taking 111 with CS 181 will also make your life a lot easier – while the two diverge after the second half of the semester, the first half of each spends a lot of time dealing with likelihood functions, MLEs, etc. Definitely worth considering if you want to take both classes.
