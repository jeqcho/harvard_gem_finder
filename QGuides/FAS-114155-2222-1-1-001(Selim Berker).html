It is definitely challenging and not a gem by any means, but I found it to be incredibly rewarding. It pushed me to think more deeply about important philosophical issues than I ever had before and even develop my own views.
The course is very well taught and would definitely recommend to someone who already knows that they are interested in epistemology, however, I did not find the subject matter itself to be very engaging.
This is probably one of the strongest philosophy classes you'll get in the department––the Berker–Morano combo is must–see for anyone interested in philosophy courses.
I would very warmly recommend taking this course! Professor Berker is unbelievably good, the course is very well structured, and you will definitely gain a thorough understanding of epistemology. The material can certainly at times be rather challenging but the lectures and sections are extremely helpful for understanding it.
Prof. Berker is one of the smartest people here, and also so humble and down–to–earth –– go talk to him! This class is not everyone's cup of tea, but if you like thinking through basic questions, it's a good one.

Don't take this course if you don't have at least a foundational knowledge of philosophy. This was my first philosophy course and although, with hard work, I was able to do well, I presume that it would've been easier if I had some sort of background in philosophy.
The class is tough and intense, especially the essay writing. If you don't do the readings for a lecture you might feel very lost, even if the notes help. But take it. It is so so so worth it
Classic A+ philosophy class. Good readings, engaging lecture, section. Recommend even for non phil ppl
If you're thinking about it, take this class! Professor Berker is an amazing professor and it's clear he cares about his students. The readings are incredibly interesting and thought–provoking – I felt they impacted my life outside of this class too. They can be heavy but it's definitely worth it to do them. The papers are a great opportunity to explore ideas brought up in class and really engage with the readings in a way that's interesting to you. One of the best classes I've taken at Harvard so far.
I would highly recommend taking this course – it provides a structured and clear introduction to an important field in philosophy.
Think very carefully about whether you actually are interested in the course content before taking this course. I liked philosophy going into this course and thought that I wanted to take it because epistemology seems very relevant to the real world and it seemed important to investigate how people come to know/ justify knowledge. However, this class successfully convinced my that most parts of philosophy are obscenely detached from reality. The course itself is probably great if you enjoy engaging with questions and theories (about whether you are not dreaming all the time/ are justified in believing that things are colors/form beliefs about sensory input etc) that are extremely technical and have absurd totally infeasible counterexamples. The professor and tf are enthusiastic and care a lot about the subject and are also very very very smart and clearly think about these questions a lot. However, the course itself kinda just felt like pointlessly plowing through content. On paper, it's a great course, but think very carefully if you want to spend a semester thinking about the content even if you think you really like philosophy. I thought I loved philosophy and technical arguments etc going into this course as well –– I no longer feel this way. If you are interested in taking it I would recommend shopping it for the first few weeks or taking it as a 5th class and just dropping it if you realize by week 4 that it is not for you. (I wish I had done this). I would recommend this EVEN if you like philosophy.
PHIL 159 is an exceptionally well–tought course. Selim is incredibly well–versed in the subject material, and the class is taught with a structure that encourages dialogue between the professor and students. If you want a chance to generate novel philosophical ideas and learn some enlightening subject matter, PHIL 159 is the class for you!
The lectures are AMAZING! Do not be afraid to raise your hand. The more that you challenge the material, the more you will get out of it. You will become a much more cynical person after debating whether or not you actually exist, but it is worth it. I left every lecture stunned. You will be prompted to consider things you've never considered before surrounding the nature of knowledge.
Selim is a great lecturer and his classes are very engaging. Readings and essays are quite difficult – it's a very analytic–style class. Definitely take a philosophy class before this and take advantage of office hours to discuss your papers, especially with the TF. Note that there is a 3hr final exam. Suggest making up your own examples to better understand the different theories. Highly recommend but won't be an easy A.
Enjoy!
Even though you are not particularly interested in the theory of knowledge, I strongly recommend you take this course just because Professor Selim Berker is amazing. I was extremely lucky to have a great and nice TF as well, but I think even in the case where the TF is not as great as this year’s TF, the lectures themselves are very engaging so that you can learn really a lot on the topics and can develop your critical thinking skills much better than before (which would be helpful in any field you want to do).
For perspective, I am a math concentrator taking this course to fill a divisional requirement; this is my first philosophy course.

Professor Berker is an amazing lecturer and I would 100% recommend taking this course with him. It said on the syllabus there were no prerequisites and, while there may have been a couple terms I had to look up after class, I agree that no prior experience was necessary. The course readings were fairly short (maybe ~15–20 pages per lecture?) yet interesting. This is the first course I’ve taken where students were encouraged to interrupt lecture to ask questions which definitely made lectures more interesting and understandable. Section was also very much discussion–based and interesting, made writing the essays a lot easier. Never writing a philosophy paper before definitely made the work a bit harder for me, but Berker specifically provided resources to help first time philosophy students.
