Enjoy the ride.
This was an AMAZINGLY fun course to take this semester. I don't think that it was as life–changing as some past q–guide reviews have indicated. You'll read books and discuss very relevant issues, but it probably won't change the course of your career. We read 7 books this semester, and you could honestly just read them on your own and learn most of the same material (I think the syllabus reading list is available online). I do recommend reading the books fully, but you could just skim them and be fine. However, if you genuinely enjoy thinking/arguing about economic issues from either a left or right perspective, you will really enjoy this class. Every Monday morning, I could look forward to two hours of enthusiastic, often humorous, discussion, and it was a really nice highlight of each week. Greg Mankiw is one of the sweetest, most knowledgeable people here at Harvard, and getting to know him in a group of 12 people is a really special opportunity that you may not get again. Also, if you tend to lean conservative, this is one of the only spaces on campus where you can actually have a respectful discussion about your opinions without fear of getting canceled, which is a nice bonus.
This is a great class –– definitely take it if you get in! It's not as life–changing as some of the previous years' Q reviews made it out to seem, but that's a really high bar to reach and it's still a superb class (definitely one of my favorites this semester). Make sure to put it as your top choice on your freshman seminar application since Prof. Mankiw said the class gets ~100 applications every year, so you won't even be considered if you rank it lower than first. Don't expect it to be like other freshman seminars with have no work, though –– you will have to read a book approximately every week (usually around 200 pages). You might be able to get by without reading all the books completely, but you definitely need to know the books relatively well in order to contribute to the discussion. The discussions were one of my favorite parts of the class –– the class is ideologically and politically diverse, which makes for really interesting conversations. I've learned more about some of my own political opinions by being exposed to new ideas from the books and being challenged through some discussions in the class.
