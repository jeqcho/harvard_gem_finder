What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This is a nice class to take for the vibes and to learn more about Buddhism and Japan. Professor Abe is very kind and supportive, so lectures always had a chill atmosphere and the class was very understanding in terms of assignments and extensions. There are quite a few readings in the course, and I would recommend trying to do them since some of them are genuinely quite interesting, but Professor Abe will recap what was in the readings in each lecture so you are able to follow along even if you haven't done the readings. Even though there were only about 15 students, the class was mostly in the form of lecture instead of discussion. If you don't mind that, this is a very fun class to take as Professor Abe fills the lecture with lots of stories about different parts of Japan and personal anecdotes. In terms of the content, the course begins with a discussion of Buddhist philosophy and the types of Buddhism practiced in Japan, followed by an in–depth analysis of the Lotus Sutra, which in turn is followed by an exploration of Zen Buddhism in relation to Japanese Noh Dramas. Then, we analysed Japanese traditional paintings through the lens of Esoteric Buddhism, and finally we considered the influences of Buddhism in modern Japanese pop culture.
Take it!
This course was absolutely fantastic – one of my absolute favorites that I've taken during my time at Harvard. Professor Abe is a wonderful teacher who cares deeply about and tries to get to know all his students. His lectures are great, and he is clearly very knowledgeable on the subject. The content itself is extremely interesting, and forces one to grapple with/question their understanding of the world if they don't have any prior exposure to Buddhist teachings. The way in which the class explores how Buddhist ideas manifest in Japanese arts (performing arts, visual arts, etc.) is fascinating, and afterwards you will almost surely start to notice how these ideas manifest in the types of media you consume. I also liked the structure of the lectures/content, where the introduction of different Buddhist schools was introduced alongside the types of art that are characteristic of them. The assignments are very fair and flexible: Professor Abe emphasizes that the final project is meant to be an enjoyable experience for you, and is very open to different kinds of academic or creative projects. The amount of reading is reasonable, but more importantly the readings themselves are interesting!

I can't recommend this class enough, even if you haven't taken classes in the EAS department before – it is very accessible, so long as you keep an open mind and follow along with the class materials.
Some may be concerned at first to learn about Japanese culture and Buddhism without any prior knowledge, but Professor Abe provides proficient guidance and will excite one's interest in these fields.
