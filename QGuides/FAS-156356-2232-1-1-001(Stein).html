Prof is amazing. One of the best classes I've taken at Harvard.
Can be very helpful to be comfortable with econometrics, otherwise you won't really understand the papers that you are expected to read and critique. The kids who had experience interning in finance and investment banking were at a significant advantage. Same with the kids who had taken some other sort of financial economics class. I also think that you would significantly benefit from the class if you were considering an Econ PhD down the road. The class definitely expects that you know a decent amount beforehand, like acronyms of random financial concepts, but you could always look these up or just ask the professor what they are. The class also has no textbook, so if you don't really understand lecture but want to grind the material at home, you'll really only have the lecture slides, which are kind of confusing when not paired with the lecture (and office hours, of course).

That being said, if you're somewhat of an outsider to the field, you can still do well –– just expect an uphill battle and the frequent feeling that everyone somehow knows more than you. Professor Stein goes out of his way to make sure that you feel comfortable approaching the topic. He really likes it when you ask questions and in fact encourages it. He's a very kind and brilliant professor –– he memorized all of our first names –– and this course is one of a kind at Harvard. Even if you don't understand everything he says at first, the mere fact that you're hearing from someone who served on the Fed Board of Governors has a great impact on your thinking in the long run. You'll get great insight into the decisions that policymakers and bankers face, and you'll get great exposure to contrasting economic ideas. Rather than just learning how to compute rote financial metrics, you'll learn how to critically think about financial concepts and dynamics. I think that the type of thinking Stein teaches is not only valuable for those aiming to be leaders in the world of finance, but for anyone seeking to use analytical skills in decision–making roles.

Professor Stein is such an incredible person to get to learn from! Highly recommend!
Truly amazing class that places itself in the intersection of economics and policy making. If you want to develop broad mindset and intuition how economy and financial system works in the world, that's the best class to take.

Some tips:
– Absolutely go to all the lectures. The material is generally very interesting, but more importantly, the intuition Jeremy provides will be crucial for the final exam.
– Ask questions and be engaging in class, Jeremy loves it!
– Start homework early, doing it one day before the deadline would not give you the full score nor a thoughtful understanding of major ideas in the papers.
– Go to OH to consult with the TF about what is expected in your homework to get full credit.
Take this class. While it is difficult at times, this was my favorite class I took while at Harvard. Professor Stein is brilliant and does a great job.
I have learned more in this class than I have learned in any other class at Harvard (senior about to graduate). Professor Stein is an incredible lecturer who fostered a really great learning environment, making his lectures more like a seminar discussion than an actual lecture. I actively looked forward to going to every class. The workload itself is very reasonable but the grading is rather obscure and it at times feels like there is a disconnect between the TFs and Stein. I would say this is a phenomenal class if we are looking at the end of it that is controlled by Stein and 100% a class you must take but having TFs sections and office hours only 2 times a week at really tough times did make the class feel like you were doing the best you could on your own and hoping for the best. Take it with friends to have a good support system in the class.

I would not recommend this course lightly. Be ready to work hard but you wont find a better or more qualified professor at Harvard
I learned a shit ton from this course. After this class, I feel like I really actually for the first time understand how the financial system works and why it fails. It was also a crazy experience that I took this class while the regional banking crisis was happening and CS, SVB, FR and others failed. It felt like a textbook example of exactly what we had been talking about in class. Lectures were super interesting, but Stein prioritizes participation a lot, and I felt like often the same kids kept asking questions to the point where they were asking questions just to get participation points, and it made lectures annoying. Still, I never wanted to skip a single lecture. However, other than the lectures, the rest of the class isn't so great. Stein is hard to catch hold of. The TFs were not plugged in at all. Assignments were legit graded 2–3 months after submission and most of them were just binge graded during finals period. This means no feedback on lengthy papers that you had to write regularly. Overall, the resources were just terrible. Office hours were scarce and badly timed. However, I'd still recommend taking this class because you really learn a lot and I think it's worth it.
