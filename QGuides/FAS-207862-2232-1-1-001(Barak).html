Fun and very up–to–date! But rather disorganized after the first few weeks. Lack of assignments was appreciated but also left me questioning whether I understood the material. The final project is a good chance to explore a modern research topic.
Boaz's lectures are great, I learned a lot from this class. This year the logistics were absurd: a lengthy pset 0 was required for admission (and by the way, it was both announced and due during winter recess), then there was a pset 1 relatively early on which took 2 months to be graded, and then no other graded work besides the final project.
Take it if you're interested in ML research. The amount of effort outside class is pretty low and the way it's structured, you don't need to like all the topics covered. Rather, the idea is to be exposed to a wide range of topics, be aware of what work is done out there and then be able to make a more informed choice regarding what to work on.
Check the syllabus, as I assume the content will change year–to–year.
TAKE THIS CLASS! Best class I have done at Harvard.
BE CAREFUL. If you are really interested in the research of ML foundation, you can try. Otherwise, you probably just skim through a few topics without a deep understanding of any of them.
The course was, as advertised, a topics course. In effect, this meant every week was focused on a different aspect of modern ML which is nice for breadth but also causes the course to feel scattered and incohesive. Boaz decided to give one problem set and then seemed to give up after that, so the workload was very light.
CS 229br is a good topics course on frontiers of ML; it is one of few grad classes that is almost all PhD students (and even a few professors that just sit in!) with real informal seminar–type discussions. We covered a really broad range of material, but admittedly most of it was not "theoretical foundations." I appreciated how well–curated the students wereeveryone really cared (likely because there was a very hard HW0 that prevented slackers from joining). The guest lectures, including Sham and Horace, were great!

If someone is taking this class with the intent of learning rigorous theory with psets/that jazz, this isn't the right course. If you want an excuse to read interesting papers and work on a cool project, this is the one!
This course is a good introduction to all things deep learning. Boaz selects really topical readings that are highly interesting and cutting–edge. The lectures are decent quality and you learn a lot. Despite the material being difficult, the people in the class are incredibly smart its an amazing learning opportunity that you cant miss. We cover an ambitious amount of readings, going from transformers to VAEs and diffusion models to differential privacy and fairness and to scaling laws and training dynamics to RL and AlphaFold to practical model parallelism and the history of ML. The course gave me more confidence in understanding the math and theoretical ideas underpinning the most important advances in deep learning, and it is truly the best course you can take on the state–of–the–art. The final paper was a joy to do and I felt very empowered to be able to create novel machine learning research because of what I learned in this class. It definitely pushed me to consider doing more ML research and there are just so many research ideas floating around after our class discussions.
Overall, a pretty interesting class! We covered a broad range of topics (although sometimes it's not immediately clear how they all connect), and the workload was very manageable –– only 1 real pset. Lectures can feel long though (~3 hrs), but not sure if the class format will change in the future.
The greatest benefit to taking this class is the students who you will be taking it with. The class is pretty stacked.
Great course to survey ML and work on a cool project!
definitely take this class if you want to do machine learning research at Harvard. Boaz provides an ~exquisite~ reading list and this is a good way to get to know ml profs at Harvard.
This course is relatively low on theory, more on empirical observations or existing practices, and the justifications for these are often unsatisfying.
