This course is a wonderful way to be exposed to a wide range of media from a diverse range of experiences, artists, and periods from the last 100 years. There is a strong communal element to this class that makes it different than other classes you may take at Harvard. This contributes to a greater appreciation for how media is produced and consumed with others. This class challenged me at times and also held space for exploring that confusion. this is a wonderful gen ed to take while you're at harvard.

While the course bills itself as a video art class, it is really not good at teaching you how to apply those skills and make videos/shorts yourself. Feedback was minimal to non–existent on each assignment. You will get to see a lot of interesting video art projects as a part of this and discuss some aspects of their film techniques, but some of them could get tedious fast. The readings were also mostly unengaging.

Additionally, the course takes a heavy nosedive into critical theory discussion in the second half that mostly seems disconnected from the actual techniques of filmmaking, and falls into the standard traps of talking at the students of the political premises without really evaluating or challenging their assumptions. The lectures especially became tedious with discussing the ideas in the annoying way that made them seem unchallengeable, as well as increasingly jargon–ridden (you'll grow very sick of hearing the word "space/spaces" by the end). So many of the texts grew increasingly obtuse and overly didactic that they were not engaging to read for someone not as into those ideas to begin with.

Also, the end of the class had this weird group reciting of a political treatise that seemed very propagandistic.

This is a really well–designed course to introduce students to a particular type of filmmaking that they likely would otherwise not experience. The course staff is very thoughtful and responsive to feedback and always came in prepared for class. Students should be aware that this class can, at times, ask a lot in terms of work. Making a film every one to two weeks takes a considerable amount of time in brainstorming, shooting, and editing (not to mention the time required to review all of the weekly readings and films). While the evaluation of these projects is pretty lenient, which allows for maximum creative expression, they have to be completed nonetheless, and this takes time. Overall, the course positions filmmaking as an art form with social justice and activism, allowing students to see the ways in which this medium can be used to convey complex subject matter like identity, cultural appropriation, and social protest.
If this class is reorganized a little, it is definitely worth taking!
