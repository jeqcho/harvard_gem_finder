Relatively easy class for the chemistry concentration. Prof. Mason is an excellent lecturer. An in–depth investigation of molecular orbital theory as a grounding for trends in the reactivity, optical properties of transition metal complexes. Expect assignments to be based in replicating problem solving strategies from lecture, rather than the synthetic or mechanistic thinking from organic chemistry.
I adored Chem 40–– the material is super interesting, the teaching staff is so lovely, and all the assignments are so fair! They make sure you're really supported throughout the entire semester (both emotionally and with extra donuts), and make the class super fun and really worthwhile to take. You'll come away with so much more knowledge about MO theory, catalysis, and the more theoretical side of why molecules are the way they are. Because the class doesn't require group theory or linear algebra as a prereq, some concepts are a bit skimmed over, but it sets you up really well to take future inorganic classes. Absolutely would recommend; Professor Mason and the TFs are truly wonderful.

I really liked Prof. Mason's lecture style and the psets and exams all felt like very fair assessments of our understanding. There are definitely some concepts that have a learning curve/didn't necessarily come naturally to me, but overall I felt like I learned a lot and gained a new appreciation for inorganic chemistry through this class!

Why did I take this class? No idea. I think for what it taught, it was pretty well executed, but some parts of the class (especially the group theory explanations) were a bit lacking in my opinion. Some things were thrust upon the students as a simple algorithm to perform without too much understanding behind the math (which I suppose is what CHEM 155 is for), but I'd like for there to be a more satisfying, cohesive explanation than what I haphazardly searched up online after hearing Professor Mason tell me not to worry about the specifics of how things were derived.
