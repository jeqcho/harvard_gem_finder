This class is definitely a grind/one of the harder undergrad math classes, but the material is quite important and fairly interesting. Note that we covered all of Daniel Marcus's Number Fields with one lecture to spare (that's eight chapters lol), so material goes by pretty quickly. Expect psets to be long and kind of computational/bashy at times; exams were a similar flavor. That being said, while he does go quite fast at times, Salim is one of the best lecturers in the department. He's so nice and really great at making the classroom feeling like a place where you can ask questions. Also, his office hours were very helpful for both psets and content questions. In summary: would definitely recommend the class, but it is a decent amount of work.
This is an exceptionally difficult math class. I am a senior and this was probably more difficult for me than any other math class I've taken at Harvard, including Math 55a/b. At times it felt like the class was moving really quickly. But still, algebraic number theory is a very beautiful topic, and once I wade through all of the complexity, I'm finally able to see some of the beauty in the topic. There are fascinating results from number fields that act as tools for solving all manner of interesting problems. I would recommend this class, but it is difficult. I spent more time reading and rereading the textbook before I could understand the homework than actually doing the homework!
Especially if Salim is still teaching this class, it will be *hard*. There is a lot of material to cover, and it comes very quickly, so staying on top of things is necessary. That being said, we learn some incredible concepts and techniques in this class, and prove some very strong theorems (even famous ones, such as Dirichlet's theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions). If you're really into number theory and want to learn more about the "basics" of algebraic number theory, this is a great course, but it's not for the faint of heart.
This is one of the most difficult classes I've taken at Harvard. Problem sets were very long and overly computational. Only take this course if you are very solid with Galois theory and are okay with very tedious problem sets.
Definitely take this class if you are interested in number theory!
