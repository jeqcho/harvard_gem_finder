What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
If you have an interest in Native history, incarceration/carcerality/carceral logic, or both, I really recommend you take this course. This course can be a bit tough to wrap your head around at times, as you're dealing with abstract concepts frequently, like what is "carceral" or what is an "empire," but if you like to have these types of complex discussions fueled by interesting and diverse course materials (such as art pieces, books, journal articles, etc), you will love this course.
Take this class!! Dr. Gill has prepared a super thorough syllabus to walk you through an ambitious exploration of whether or not the entirety of settler colonial projects like the U.S. and Canada can be deemed "carceral," through they way that they have treated Indigenous people. I learned a lot about Native history and culture, but the main focus of this class is the effect of settler colonialism and systems of carcerality on Native peoples. You'll learn a lot about the semantics of talking about settler colonialism ("removal"? "genocide"? "dispossession"?) that is pretty important to having effective conversations with people to begin to grasp what it really means to be living on stolen land.
Please take this class! It was quite small and could benefit for more students – the topic certainly merits study.
The professor was very knowledgeable about their field. You will get a great introduction to and through overview of the history of Native Americans. This course has a heavy reading load and you will be expected to keep up with all of the material. This class had a small but great community, facilitated by the professor.
