I found discussion of adaptive data analysis, differential privacy, and algorithmic bias and fairness to be really really interesting. It's also incredible to learn from Cynthia Dwork herself. Professor Dwork cares a lot about the students and it really shows –– definitely go to her office hours and try to talk to her outside of class. The final project was a great opportunity to do a little research in this area, and was left fairly open ended so you can explore whatever you want, which I really liked.
In terms of workload, I would say some of the material is really difficult to understand, but that doesn't necessarily make the course "hard" because the assignments are pretty manageable, especially with help at office hours (Pranay was especially great!). I tracked my hours each week and this course took around 6.67 hours outside of class each week.
My only criticism is that slides and assignments frequently (basically weekly) had typos or errors in them, which would then be corrected in the next lecture or in canvas assignments. None of them were major errors but I found it confusing having to keep checking whether the slides I was referencing were the correct ones or the outdated ones. Aside from that, really glad I took this class!
The problem sets are very difficult and strongly recommend going to office hours for help on them!!
The course is challenging and time consuming, but extremely valuable if you're looking to work or study in the domain of DP or algorithmic fairness. Application wise, the course focuses more on DP. Research and concepts in algorithmic fairness is more exploratory through papers (flipped classroom) and final projects.
CS 226R will teach you about various topics, like privacy and fairness. These topics may not seem like they are technically extremely complex, but a lot of interesting methods are needed to obtain these results. Oftentimes, there are very clever ways of proving that a certain algorithm is private, and this has applications to areas like adaptive data analysis.
Cynthia Dwork is amazing. The course material is quite interesting too. The psets can be pretty vaguely worded though, so you end up spending hours trying to figure out what the questions are asking, but the TFs are stars.
In the beginning, you will be frustrated when it comes to problem sets that are not well formulated and the first unit will be chaotic overall. Trust me, it gets better. The problem sets get easier later in the course, and the lectures get better. Overall, the material becomes more interesting and the work on the project is great. The class itself is not a huge amount of work.
Take this class! Prof. Dwork is incredible and literally famous for having created these fields, so it is such an honor to learn them from her directly!
This class is really useful, relevant, and interesting. It's quite similar to CS 126, but it focuses more in–depth on technical aspects of differential privacy and algorithmic fairness in particular. Expect this class to be really hard. I also highly recommend you have (or make!) a support system – knowing others in this class and having groupchats to set up time to collaborate on psets and to discuss concepts will be so, so helpful. This class will suck up a good deal of time and brainpower, but I think it was very worth taking, especially if you are interested in these topics.
Really great course that will introduce you the privacy. If you have taken CS 126, you will really enjoy the continuation of the material. Even if you have not taken CS 126, you are more than able to learn the material and gain a lot from it. It can be easy to get away without doing much work/going to lectures, but for your own growth, I highly recommend that you go to lectures and do the readings (especially during the first half of the semester). The readings are really effective in explaining the main idea, although they are inconsistent with notation.
I would not recommend taking this class. The material varies tremendously. The first third of the course is adaptive data analysis, which is quite boring. It's basically just proving random inequalities in six variables. The next third is differential privacy, which is pretty interesting, and the last section is fairness, which some love and some really don't like. The psets were pretty unpolished –– I often found myself spending more time trying to decipher what they were asking than I did trying to solve the problems. Also, the way they handled the final project was really not cool. It was due before reading week, and we only got our proposals back with comments 2 weeks beforehand, which wasn't really enough time for a real project. On the bright side, prof. Dwork is a pretty cool person to have taken a class with (she literally invented all 3 fields), and the psets had extra credit and were not graded super harshly, so it was easy to do well. Also, the TFs Pranay and Elbert were quite helpful.
If you are looking to satisfy the theory req for CS AB/SM, i'd honestly recommend another course unless this one improves. The course doesn't really do a good job of showing real world applicability of the material, although maybe that is an issue with the research field itself rather than the way the course is delivered. If you are interested in fairness, you could honestly skim the literature yourself and work in a lab that focuses on applying/coming up with new algorithms to ensure fairness in decision making. Also, I was a bit frustrated that much of the fairness research we read seemed to ignore fact that datasets could be the source of unfairness in algorithmic decision making – if you are taking the course or looking into fairness research, this seems like an interesting area to explore.

However, I will say that both Cynthia and the course staff are both extremely nice and helpful! They are all very understanding in terms of helping you understand the material and setting very reasonable deadlines and expectations.
A bit disorganized, but very interesting course. Ask questions in class! It will help both you and other students, and Cynthia often doesn't realize when she's skipped over something that may seem obvious to her but not to us mortals.
This class is definitely not easy, and not necessarily because the psets are insanely difficult or the proofs are super long or whatever, but because the concepts themselves are sometimes hard for you to wrap your head around. Additionally, there isn't a lot of existing literature on some of the topics because differential privacy is just a very new field. Definitely focus within the first few weeks of the class and try to really understand the picture, otherwise you will find yourself very confused. The last portion of the class is honestly very, very interesting , and fairness is a very important topic. I do expect that there will likely be some level of vitriol over disorganization in the class, etc. and while I think that this class could use work in terms of being a bit better organized, a lot of people in the course honestly just seemed kind of bitter over things that are to be expected in a CS grad class (e.g. literally being asked to come to class). Also the TFs were great! I believe they're seniors so not sure who's going to do it next year, but office hours were so, so helpful. Also, Prof. Dwork is really, really passionate about a lot of these topics, and in the classes where we did discussions, I found it interesting to hear her questions, thoughts, and interpretations. This class is definitely you get out what you put in in terms of engagement with the material, and as someone who could have engaged more, know that now before you take it!
This course is difficult and fast, but the TFs really pull through with helping you on psets as much as you need, so go to OH if you need it. They were supportive and generous in grading psets and the final project. Do the readings, even if you only get through the abstracts of technical papers fully and can't understand the rest that much. It'll make lecture accessible and the discussions more interesting. I learned a lot not just technical material but also fairness/privacy from a socio–technical perspective. We did this really cool reverse classroom day where we talked about non–technical talks/readings that had been assigned, and it was one of the most interesting set of talks and class periods during my time at Harvard! Love those type of discussions.
Hard course, but Pranays office hours were amazing I dont think I would have lasted in this class without the OHs
This class can be overwhelming and be a lot of work, but I think it's so worth it! You get to do an original research project (I know, I didn't think I could do it either with no background, but I did and it was really fulfilling), and you also get to learn about differential privacy and algorithmic fairness––I previously wanted to throw up when I thought about either topic, but Prof Dwork makes them so interesting and accessible! The class also talks about real–world stuff, perhaps even more than other practical classes. Take this class!!
Dwork is an amazing professor and worth taking a class from– she's a terrific role model. It's a wildly fascinating area of learning and if you're interested, take it!
