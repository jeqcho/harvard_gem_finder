Besides that, you do get a sense of how powerful Stat Mech is, and how its ideas can be applied to so many different fields.

This course covers important material, but in my opinion was a bit disorganized. Be prepared to do a bit of work outside of class doing the readings. You do get a lot of support in office hours which is nice.
This class is a fairly good survey of statistical mechanics. The model of teaching is essentially going over the same material twice: first, you read the notes so you can take the quiz. After, you cover the same material in less detail in lecture. I think structurally, the course is well–organized, and has lots of resources in place to help you succeed. However, the quizzes and lectures can sometimes be more confusing than helpful.
The highlight of the course was the TF, Richard. He was a fantastic teacher, and his section explanations and notes were indispensable for the class.
It takes work in this course to get a comprehensive understanding of the material and a sense of the big picture, but you learn a lot of important math/physics tools and perspectives along the way. My best advice would be to keep up with the readings, since they are helpful to frame lectures and are useful on psets! Lectures are useful to get the fundamentals down. The demos during some lectures are also really cool!
This class is probably the best physics class I've ever taken. You learn a lot of the math, statistics, and physics behind concepts like diffusion, thermodynamics, free energy, phase transitions, etc. that are hand–wavily taught in high school AP chem and physics courses. There are some minor weaknesses: the questions on reading quizzes often had confusing wording (though they were graded based on completion so it didn't really matter), the PSETs tend to have a lot of tedious math, and the last few weeks tend to be a bit disorganized since each lecture covers a different application of the material. I'd say it's really helpful to have taken quantum mechanics and probability (like stat110). You should also be comfortable with Taylor expansions, though the TFs and prof. Yelin help you a lot with this and other weird math tricks like geometric series during office hours. Overall, the course is difficult but not overwhelming (for comparison, if you've taken 143a with Doyle this course is definitely more doable, and I'd say it's just a bit harder than the hardest of the 15abc series). If deciding between this and chem161, I'd say that you'll be challenged more but also learn more and have more fun in this course. Lastly, the demonstrations are a blast (sometimes literally).
Pretty standard physics course in terms of workload, exams, etc., but the material is super cool! The first few lectures are slow, but by the end of the course things start to get really interesting. Statistical mechanics explains a huge number of observable phenomena (Bose–Einstein condensate, semiconductors, stars, to name a few), so if you want to learn about real world systems this class is a must. The lecture notes are great, but the lectures ended up following them very closely, and at times it felt redundant.
Statistical mechanics is amazing! It describes a lot of phenomena!
Learned a lot of fascinating statistical mechanics connected to crucial modern technologies. Got strong grasp of entropy from both statistical mechanics and information angles; then came to understand various ensembles and free energies, leading to quasiparticles, bosons, and fermions especially and how electron behavior gives rise to semiconductors, etc; but also BEC's and more. Thermodynamics also very important. Overall, covered a lot of ground and probably my most challenging course this semester as well as the one I covered objectively the most ground in. I'd strongly recommend for anyone seeking to dive deeper into such areas of physics especially for physics research!
