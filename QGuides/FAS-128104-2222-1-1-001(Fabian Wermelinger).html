Honestly I think this is the CS course I have learned the most from (competing with CS61). The lectures are packed with material but presented in a very digestable fashion. Fabian is fantastic and I think all the topics were well motivated and flowed clearly. This is definitely a class where I will save the materials and refer back to them in the future. Problem sets are very fair and project criteria is fair. Would definitely recommend taking this class as you get a lot out of it.
If you're not so familiar with C++, don't take this course. If you can't spare three days every week to do the homework, don't take this course. The course itself is not well organized. All the unfamiliar concepts are thrown at you in a row during the class without detailed explanation. And the workload is super heavy for a grad–level course: we have 6 labs, 5 homework, 4 quiz, a final project with 3 presentations and a ~10page report.... Also the homework is not fairly graded –– they always focus on the tiny details of your typo, and won't let go any chance of deducting your scores.
Fabian is great and he lays out a well organized course. If it is not a requirement probably review material as to whether or not it will be of use.
It's a great class. Start the project early and scope it pragmatically.
Make sure you allocate at least 13–15 hours each week for this course if you are not a CS major. The homework is very time consuming, and you also need to work on the group project at the same time
I really loved this class. it's hard, but not overwhelming and you learn a large breadth of topics. Definitely take it
Knowing C++ fundamentals will give you a huge edge in understanding some examples. Classes, constructors/destructors, methods. Get a little comfortable with the shell on Unix and you'll be golden. There were only five homeworks total, but each one recommends you spread it out over three weeks. If you're a naughty procrastinator, then you can get them done in an afternoon if you know what you're doing. There is not much to debug, but as always with debugging, it can eat up a LOT of your time.
Don't underestimate the workload. If you don't have a relevant background or a strong interest in computer architecture and systems, you are likely to have a challenging time.
This is a truly excellent class. It has great organization and structure, and the lecture slides are high quality. The content is very interesting and the class will definitely make you a better programmer and give you a much better understanding of the inner workings of computers and computing clusters. I would recommend either having some C++ experience before taking the class, or at least having a good amount of plain C experience to make learning C++ easier.
This class is a lot more difficult than you may think and not for the reasons you may first think. The content itself is not bad. However, the homeworks are designed in such a way that without office hours, you can often not do them correctly even if you understand the presentations. Furthermore, there is a lack in office hours because even when it is offered it is only offered for very short periods of time and half the time, the TFs do not show up. There is no flexibility regarding deadlines besides your late days so make sure to use your late days well. Moreover, there are quizzes in this class that are quite intimidating. You will have 10 minutes to complete about 10 questions, and some of those questions will be very specific and hard to find answers to within the short time frame. If you take this course, be prepared to do most of the work without much support due to the limited OH. The content is very interesting however and may be very useful. In place of OH, however, you can make sure to make use of the edstem to get help.
The course is kind of like a shotgun blast over a wide range of topics in high–performance computing. You'll be confused some of the time, but you will definitely come away with a set of concrete skills and at least a passing understanding of complex topics relating to computer architecture and really specialized computing techniques.
Parallel processing was useful and interesting content to learn, and the workload was lighter than I was expecting. It's a solid and well–structured class (way better than CS242 which is on a similar topic).
Fast code run fast, slow code run slow. Sound simple? It's really, really not. This is a very cool course and one that pretty much anyone who works with computers will find useful. It teaches you what's actually going on in the bowels of the hardware, but rather than teaching that material for its own sake, it teaches it so that you can learn every trick in the book to squeeze another FLOP out of your already–overworked processor. If you like writing fast code that runs fast, this is the course for you.
This class is a moderate–work, high–value type of class. You get more out of it than you might think, especially given it's not nearly as much work as some other high–value courses at Harvard. You really understand what your CPU is and how it works along the way to writing parallel code that utilizes the CPU effectively. Would recommend to any CS concentrator no matter the specialization if Fabian is still teaching it.
This course is a put work in, get work out type of course where your results will be proportional to the effort you inputted. Be prepared to dedicate long nights working with others and debugging over a public server.
If you are interested in this course, note that most of the value comes out of the projects and assignments. You will get familiar with OpenMP and other parallelization techniques, but definitely would recommend you first have experience with the low–level architecture of the computer. A lot of this class overlaps with 141, since we spent a lot of time talking about architecture in order to understand how parallelization works. Otherwise, pretty interesting course, and you get the chance to do a cool final project!
This class was about all the different ways people have designed algorithms/architectures to make your code run fast. We mainly focus parallel designs in the context of CPU architectures – this includes thread–level parallelism (OpenMPI), data–level parallelism (MPI), and instruction–level parallelism (pipelining/vectorization). It's a fairly comprehensive overview of parallel computing, and you get a good mix of understanding the software and hardware that goes into running high–performance code.

This class isn't too difficult conceptually, you write <100 lines of code for each of the 5 assignments. However, there are alot of details to get right, and Fabian is quite detail–oriented, so that can make it tricky to distinguish between them. The project varies depending on your team/project difficulty, but just make sure you're clearing all the deliverables/presentations on time.
This class is not crazy hard, but it's definitely not easy, so expect to do a good amount of work.
