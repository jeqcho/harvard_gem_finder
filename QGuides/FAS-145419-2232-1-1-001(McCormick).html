I really enjoyed this course. It gave me a preview of the different subject areas and professors in this concentration, and motivated me to consider ways in which I could further my interests in the field. While the topics are broad and lecturers only have 2.5 hours to go through course content, it's fun to have changing professors and also a nice way to get a glimpse of how diverse EAS is!
For a required course, it is very manageable and interesting. The assignments, while not the most effective, are fun to do and make you engage with the content firsthand. Section is probably the best part of class if you get a good TF. Overall, an easy, introductory course to EAS concentration.
This class is incredible– although there are response papers almost every week, they're very helpful in that they get you prepared for the midterm/final. Furthermore, all the content discussed in class is very engaging and interesting. This class really helped me start thinking about what direction I want to take my thesis as well. Highly recommend.
LECTURES: Mostly excellent. Each week is crash course into a different professor's work. You don't have to take notes; just sit back and take it in. They taper off a bit in the second half of the course, but there's still something to learn each week.

SECTIONS: Very informal discussions, sometimes about the course material. I remember laughing a lot. But I did learn about other students' perspectives and interests.

ASSIGNMENTS: Weekly response assignments that can be pretty–open ended. Some weeks can be annoying, but some weeks you can really have fun with them. One student transliterated Shakira's "Waka Waka" into Japanese Katakana for an assignment. It's really up to you.
A solid intro class to the EAS department. The course covers a wide array of topics and different professors are brought in each week to discuss their field of expertise. A lot of the content (not all) is historical though, so this may not be the best course if you want exposure to topics on modern East Asia.
Take this class — seriously. The lectures are so interesting and multivalent, covering so many different themes and topics in under 2 hours. You’ll learn about the different practices within EAS and how to analyze East Asia responsibly.
This is an absolutely amazing course. If you are even remotely considering studying EAS, take this course and you'll be sold. It's quite a bit of work at times, as the readings aren't the shortest some weeks (some of the guest lecturers have more dense material to discuss), but it's super rewarding and a great experience. Take it take it take it!
– Find a topic that you really care about your final paper since that will make the writing process much easier.
– As a non–Arts and Humanities person, this class felt somewhat weird at first, but it was one of the most unique classes I've taken at Harvard. Don't know any other class where the professors rotate every week and the topic is different.
– Highly encourage although there will be some classes that you just don't like as much since the topic won't be as engaging as you may have originally expected.
If you are an East Asian Studies concentrator or secondary, this course is a requirement. If you are interested in East Asian Studies but are not looking to concentrate in it or are on the fence about concentrating, then I recommend you take a different EAS course related to your interests. It's not that this course isn't good, it's just that there are better EAS courses out there. And, since this course is an assemblage of different topics presented by guest lecturers, the curriculum as a whole isn't cohesive – you get to familiarize yourself with the EAS department and what faculty you can study with to pursue your interests, but the topics presented for each week don't necessarily build off of one another. Some professors are really engaging lecturers (Professor Atherton and Professor Robson are fire), and some lectures are less interesting/engaging. You don't need any background to take this course, and the grading scheme is fairly generous.
Great class!
Super good introductory course! Really thorough in showing the ropes of the EALC department and giving examples of many veins of research. Could be fun no matter your major, but definitely catered to EAS majors.
There will be lectures that you do and do not like, but stay on top of the weekly assignments, start working on the midterm and final early and you will be okay
