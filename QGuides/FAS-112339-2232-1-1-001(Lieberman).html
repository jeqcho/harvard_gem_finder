I've already written enough about this course elsewhere in my evaluation, so I'm simply going to copy and paste my pro and con sections.

PROS: Interesting content, and a lot of it. This course goes to great lengths to conclusively prove its points, incorporating scientific studies wherever possible. Sections deployed a variety of different activities, most of which were hands–on, and the reading load was very reasonable. Readings integrated well with lecture, which tends to happen when a professor assigns their own book.

CONS: This class provided an incredible amount of concrete, quantitative information, and then (as if realizing that not all of the arguments it presented are as uncontroversial as Dan Lieberman would like you to believe) evaluated students' comprehension of said information in the most opaque and difficult ways possible. The questions on the midterm and final were rarely clear enough to discern what specific phrase or piece of information one was being asked to present, and even the lab assignments that weren't poorly–designed hunter–gatherer role–playing games were often difficult to understand. Lectures felt repetitive, with the first half of the class being a continual retread of the fact that early hominins developed adaptations to use different foods, and the latter half beating into our skulls that wanting to take the escalator is perfectly natural, but if you do you're putting one foot in the grave. Labs 2–5 were essentially indistinguishable. This class also falls victim to an outdated pedagogical mentality in which rigor is developed by forcing students to fight tooth and nail for every single point on every single assignment and assessment. There is no reason for a final to be worth 45% of the overall grade; if exams must be 65% (and participation only 5%), make the midterm 30% and the final 35%. Sure, the midterm is open–book and you're given 24 hours, but the fact that the average grade was still in the mid–80s tells you something about the way the questions were asked and graded. The Lab 6 assignment should have been separate from and worth more than the other labs, since it required outside research and synthesis in addition to just divination of what the grader was being told to look for. The Lab 6 assignment also brings up the fact that this class clearly isn't preparing its TFs well to do things that are way outside of their domain: these are HEB grad students, and it's not really fair to expect them to give a lesson on art history or run a game that can't be won by playing the way it's meant to be played (and it's definitely not fair to expect students to learn anything from these activities).

Overall, I wouldn't recommend taking this class unless you're an HEB affiliate and have no choice, it's the only gened that fits your schedule (as it was for me), or you really like being talked down to. Bridget Alex is a gem, and her lectures (all of the fact–based, solely informational ones) are great, but this class is ultimately the Dan Lieberman Show, and Dan is more interested in proselytizing his own opinions and making you feel bad for not sharing them than actually teaching you about human evolution. If you're interested in the subject, there are probably other HEB classes you could take, and the Deep History gened (Dan Smail and Matt Liebmann) does a much better job of introducing a lot of the same content and ideas. Also, most of the readings are from Lieberman's books, so you could just read those and get the same experience. The content of this class is important and definitely worth learning about, but there are way less frustrating ways to do so.

I recommend taking this course as an introduction to Human Evolutionary Biology. The course material was interesting and digestible and labs were engaging.
FASCINATING LECTURES: The lecture content is really interesting and well crafted. The first 2/3rds of this class is mostly evolutionary history (learning about many different ancestors to humans) and the second third takes a very rigorous approach to human health. I found the lectures to be very engaging, and Lieberman's lecture style was funny, lighthearted, rigorous and compelling. He's super knowledgable and made the lectures highly enjoyable.

HOWEVER, the grading is extremely nit–picky and you will be expected to remember many specific details, like definitions of terms, that have only shown up in lecture once. Be prepared to spend time memorizing names and dates! The class doesn't offer study guides or release solutions to the lab assignments, so it's hard to prepare appropriately. The lab assignments and midterms were also graded very harshly. (I assume the final was too). You can lose points for a correct answer which doesn't include the specific examples included in the solution guide. In addition, some TFs are far stricter than others.

TOUGH GRADING: The average grade for the class falls half–way between a B and a B+ (the same curve as CS 124).

OVERALL: The lectures are fascinating and highly enjoyable. I'm really glad I took the class, but if you you should take it pass/fail if you are concerned about grades.
This class is VERY interesting and VERY relevant to life. The first half of the course you learn all about how humans evolved starting from the last common ancestor with chimps, all the way through 5 major transitions that result in modern Homo sapiens. The second half of the course looks at an evolutionary perspective to diseases and healthcare. I found the second half a lot more interesting, and it truly changed the way I view physical activity, diet, cancer, and diseases.
