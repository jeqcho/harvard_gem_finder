One of the fundamental messages this class illustrates is that we've been accustomed as a society to treat the symptoms of diseases that arise from mismatches between what humans have adapted to historically versus the starkly different environment most of us now live in (very sedentary lifestyles, extreme abundance of food, etc.). Crucially it has taught me how effective and vital disease prevention is, and has motivated me to stay active and maintain a healthy, balanced diet even more so than I already had. Lastly, if you're interested in archaic human species, or the evolution of humans in general, as well as how humans differ from our ape relatives (and why these traits were selected for), this class is certainly a good overview of that.

If you're looking for a broad introduction to human evolutionary biology as a chill GENED, this class is for you. This class guides you through an overview of the entirety of human evolution, starting with the humans' last common ancestor with the Great Apes (Chimpanzees, Gorillas, etc.) and ending with the rise of Homo sapiens. The workload is very light: outside of lectures, the assignments are 1) weekly labs with a post–lab assignment, and 2) the midterm and final. The post lab assignments are usually ~10 short free response questions to check your understanding of the course. Some examples of labs are: touring the Great Apes section of the Harvard Natural History Museum, and using plastic models of human skulls to talk about unique human evolutionary features. The labs aren't really hardcore, and we didn't do things like using chemicals or running fancy machines (unlike LS labs, for instance).

One warning: the class focuses on human evolution for the majority of the class, and talks about human health for around the last 1/3 of the semester – although human health is somewhat dispersed throughout the year. So this class is more like a human evolution class, rather than a human health class. But if you are looking for this type of stuff, it will be a great class for you. Also, this can be said for many GENEDs, but the curve for the midterms and finals are pretty tough.

This is an interesting course which will teach you a solid amount about the evolutionary history of humans and our ancestors, and the ways that this evolutionary past informs some of the major health threats we face today. The workload is very light, and anyone should be able to succeed (read: get an A) so long as they attend and pay attention in lecture, and plan to read over lecture slides or notes. The work for the course is 2 exams (both manageable, but studying is necessary), weekly labs (~1 hr in class, and the same amount of time out of class to do assignments), and a single mini paper of about 1 page. Generally, the material will be interesting to anyone with a curiosity about human evolution or health, as the name would suggest. I thought there was a bit too much emphasis on early human ancestors and not enough on modern diseases, but it was still a diverse curriculum and I thought it at least covered all the bases that I'd hoped for. There also isn't quite as much prescriptive advice as I'd hoped––it might be useful to hear more specific behaviors we should take to improve our own health, though there are a few big ones presented in lectures.
