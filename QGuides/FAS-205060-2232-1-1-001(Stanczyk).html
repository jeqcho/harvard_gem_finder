In this class, there is a focus on John Rawls' liberal theory of justice, its applications, and its critiques. At its core, this class asks you to examine the inequalities we see in the world and envision what justice would mean. To that end, the arguments in this class are not about policy, but exist at a higher level of philosophical abstraction that allows us to asks what the goals of our policies should be. This is a perfect course for anyone trying to better understand how society should function and how we are currently failing to meet that standard.
This course is well–structured and dives deeply into philosophical questions and applications of inequality. I appreciated how we started with Rawls's theory of justice, then considered other philosophers' critiques and extensions of Rawls. The assignments (two 3–4–page papers, two 7–8–page papers) were reasonable and challenged me to think deeper and really understand and critique the readings. Section and lecture were dynamic and really helpful for strengthening and extending my understanding. Readings were very interesting and a reasonable length.

This was my first course in philosophy, and I loved it.

Also, I really liked how this course used empirical (quantitative) evidence when it was relevant and did not use it when it was not relevant (i.e., data from our unideal world may not be generalizable to an ideal–world philosophical argument). I had been wondering how to relate empirical evidence with philosophy, and I think this course handled it well.

I learned how to build a rigorous framework and theory for social justice, how to consider objections to a theory of justice, and how to extend and challenge a theory of justice. In essence, I learned how to think holistically about inequalities in our society and whether they are just or unjust. This was exactly what I hoped to learn from this class, and I am so grateful. In the past, I had felt unequipped to address arguments for or against inequalities in our society, which I feel strongly about. Now, I feel equipped to engage with others' ideas about inequality in a critical, understandable way by pointing out their implicit assumptions, implications, and alternatives.
There is no class I'd recommend more – Professor Stanczyk renders in incredible, at times troubling, clarity, the deepest workings of our society. The class has become a way of looking at the world for me, one that I'm grateful for as a person and a citizen

This is overall a great course! The lectures are great (the lecture notes were wonderfully comprehensive and so clear that you could get away with not doing the readings, which themselves are quite interesting and carefully chosen, so don't skip them). Professor Lucas is remarkably eloquent and each lecture makes you think hard. It also goes without saying that the material is very relevant.

As a STEM major taking my first course in the Philosophy department, I was reasonably stimulated and happy with my choice to branch out. The workload was very reasonable and well spaced out throughout the semester (2 short papers ~ 3 pages each, 2 long papers ~ 7 pages each, and that's it). Only thing I would warn about, particularly to STEM/non–humanities concentrators, is: you can't BS your way through the papers. It's listed as an intermediate/ upper–level Phil course so expect the grading of papers to be at least as tough as the average humanities class.
