I will not be concentrating in the humanities, so take that as you will. The following is a little bit (too?) unfiltered, but I hope it doesn't get filtered out of the displayed comments (sorry in advance for the length; i'm still recovering from the final; for a tl;dr read the end i think).

I have a lot of mixed opinions about Hum 10 — and a lot of my friends are tired of hearing me talk about my mixed feelings for Hum 10, so I'm excited to put some of them to words here. I think that Hum 10 is a really interesting class in general, and the vision for Hum 10 that is projected during info sessions and in group chats is certainly appealing; after all, a yearlong transformative experience through great texts seems to be the quintessential Harvard course, and the hype that is built up around it (not to mention the prestige it holds) makes it seem like a great and distinguished privilege to take the course.

The course itself is not bad by any means, but I think that it is slightly different than the experience that I had originally expected. The work it takes to keep up with the breakneck pace at which you are expected to read books is not inherently a good reason to dislike the course; after all, there are giant warnings throughout the application process and in the general culture/buzz surrounding Hum 10 that it is "a book a week" and "only for humanities kids who would like to marry a book one day." But, the pace at which the course runs through texts can make the texts (and conversations about them) far less meaningful. I often found myself reading these great texts with the express goal of finishing them before lecture, even though I had looked forward to reading many of them for a long time; in the end, I feel like I never got to really enjoy or savor what makes these texts so great: their cultural background, their language, their stories, their influence. A better and richer understanding of the texts felt restricted by time, and I often found myself wishing that we had another week to discuss them, which sounds cheesy and corny and cringy but is ultimately true. All that being said, Hum 10 does expose you to a ginormous selection of texts, and for someone who came into Hum 10 without reading most of the texts on the syllabus, I do appreciate being familiar with these texts that are referenced so often.

Seminar can be a little hit or miss I think. I liked my seminar both semesters, and the people in them are some of my close friends on campus, but I also think that Hum 10 is a class that encourages speaking in seminar for the sake of speaking and that punishes just listening to the insights that others have on texts. There's a broader problem, I feel, with the participation grade that is worth 25% of your grade each semester: we are never told our participation grade and are only given a vague overview of what goes into it ("eh it's like if you participate in seminar and writing lab and turn your stuff in on time and attend lecture"), which leads a lot of people to perhaps over–participate? I think that I sometimes found myself making a comment in seminar not because I was really dying to make the point but because I didn't want to say nothing for the entire seminar, and I was slightly concerned that someone would make the point before me.

Writing lab does not teach you writing. I think this is one of the reasons that I still feel a little iffy about my decision to take Hum 10 over Expos 20; I am far from being a good writer, and I've heard that Expos (at least certain sections) are really good at teaching you how to write. I can say confidently that Hum 10 did not really teach me how to write, and I'm not sure if my writing has improved much over the course of this year. Maybe that's just me though. On a related note, essay grading can feel a little arbitrary; I'll say that I never received a grade that was wildly out of my expectation, but there was always a 3 grade range in which I had no idea where I would land. Feedback can also feel a little arbitrary and detached from the grade you receive. I'm also not sure if writing labs are standardized, but there definitely are some concerns with some writing labs having a TON of really great writers and the effect that might have on the grades you receive. This is complete hearsay though.

The finals are brutal, mentally. They are eight hour finals during which you write two 1000–word essays, each of which must discuss two texts from the semester. We don't get written feedback on them, and, like above, the grading can feel a little arbitrary.

I think this is my second–to–last but main substantive paragraph. The people (yes, cheesy) are honestly what makes me feel better about taking Hum 10. As someone who is not likely to take many humanities classes in the future, I'm really, really glad that I had the opportunity to meet all of the incredible people in Hum 10: professors are all incredible people and academics, TFs are wonderful and smart and insightful, and the students are super helpful and genuine and sweet. If you do take this class, don't be the person who doesn't talk to other people; suffer in company and talk to your fellow Hum 10ers. Truly some of the smartest and most genuine and deepest people I've had the privilege of meeting.

If you're still deciding whether or not to take Hum 10, ask yourself why you want to take it, try reading one of the texts in a week, and think about whether you really have the time to devote to Hum 10. From personal experience and from comments I've heard, do not take the course a) if you don't feel super confident already in your writing ability, b) if you don't think you'll have the time/stamina to read a book a week earnestly, or c) if you are only/mostly taking the course for the prestige. Also consider whether a) you have read a lot in the past and b) your experience before college has prepared you to talk with lots of kids who went to prep schools (something I genuinely wish someone had talked to me about). Talk to lots (lots!) of people who have taken the course because almost everyone I know has a slightly different take. Best of luck if you do take the course. <3
