Approach this class with caution. If you're looking for an easy gened, this isn't it. I did more work for this class that I did for basically any other class this semester, which is saying something. There is an incredible amount of busywork, and your grade will quickly nosedive if you don't do a single reading or slack on an assignment. The grading system they use is fairly unforgiving –– if you are not actively participating every lecture, your grade will suffer. If you do not go above and beyond on every single assignment, your grade will suffer. Feedback can be vague and once you implement said feedback, it won't actually improve the grade on your final output unless you're willing to put in thesis–levels of research into your projects because that is what they ask of you.

The class often felt jumpy and disorganized. You'll be told that this is on purpose, and if you're confused then the super secret teaching method is actually working, but it can often feel frustrating. If you're looking for a linear progression though the middle ages touching on various topics of the middle ages, this class is not for you. If you're looking for a gem, this class is not for you.
THIS IS A REALLY COOL CLASS! Dan and the TF's are great, it's so clear that they care about the students as people, and the material is presented in such an engaging way that even if it's something you never thought you were capable of caring about, you'll find yourself engrossed before long. The best way to experience this class is to let yourself be fascinated by every little thing and just enjoy the ride. He literally said in class that he didn't give a final because he wanted us to come away from this class having learned different things, things we care about, and every project has tons of freedom to let you pursue your own interests. I recommend it wholeheartedly.
The course staff for this class is great and so supportive. The class itself is not structured as a traditional history course, so if you are looking to get a very clear understanding of Medieval history, this is probably not the class for you. However, if you are interested in learning broadly about a bunch of different themes within the history of the Middle Ages and learning how to look at objects, this is a great course. I feel like I learned a lot about material history and what its like to interact with objects as a historian, and I think that the course had a really interesting perspective on history.
This is a fantastic class to fulfill both History departmental requirements and GenEd requirements! I would highly recommend taking it. While you should expect to have several projects over the course of the semester that will require a lot of reading and writing, the workload in an average week is manageable. Lectures are enjoyable, interesting, and go by quickly, largely because of Professor Smail's skill as a lecturer and a teacher. He and the course staff care deeply about the content of this course, and they are incredibly accessible for student conversations and questions. The amount of time, care, and thought that they have put into this course is incredible. You will not regret taking this course, whether you're a history concentrator or just trying to get a GenEd out of the way.
You should take the class! It was so fun, meaningful, and made me feel connected to Harvard.
This is a fantastic GENED. Dan Smail's non–chronological teaching of history is refreshing. He takes the time to get to know each person individually, and has everyone's name committed to memory only several weeks into the class. The course also allows you to engage meaningfully with rare objects in the Harvard Art Museums and Houghton Library, which was an awesome and hands–on experience. Course field trips were engaging, I particularly enjoyed singing Medieval chants in the rythmic mode at Busch Hall at the CES.

The only downside is that some of the larger assignments felt overly tautological, especially for upperclassmen familiar with the research process (writing a five–page single spaced annotated bibliography, for example, just feels like writing down repetitive thoughts at a certain point). Overall, however, this class affords students to get an overall understanding of the medieval period while still doing deep dives into specific objects, which I felt was a really effective approach.

This class is on the higher–effort end of Gen Ed's. If you're expecting a walk in the park, don't take it. If you want to learn a ton and engage with Harvard's archives I couldn't recommend it more.
Take this class! Dan is an incredibly kind and generous teacher, and his passion and excitement for the Middle Ages are infectious. I entered the class with little background and virtually no interest in the Middle Ages –– I had just been told that Dan was an amazing teacher, and I wanted to fulfill the Gen Ed requirement. Looking back, I am so grateful that I made this decision. Not only has this class ignited an interest in the pre–modern that I never had before, but it's also opened my eyes to new ways of thinking about history that I will take with me in all of my future classes. Apart from the teaching itself, some of the biggest strengths of this course are its hands–on components and object/topic–based structure. By visiting the Art Museum regularly (as well as the Houghton Library and Adolfus Busch Hall), the time period came alive, and I learned valuable skills in interpreting material culture and history. The non–linear structure of the course appears disorienting at first, but Dan ties it all together such that it is the only way I can imagine the course making sense. You may have noticed there is no final exam in the course. That is because it would simply be impossible to have one. Everyone leaves with different understandings and learnings, and to me, that's the mark of an incredible course. There is a fair amount of work over the semester, but none of it is tedious, and it is all relevant and engaging as long as you find the time for it. Take this class!
This class is really interesting and if you like going to the Harvard Art Museum and other spots on campus with the class, this course is for you! It does require you to put in effort into researching your objects for the assignments, but this can be a mind opening experience.
If you're slightly interested in history but aren't too well versed in it, this is a great intro GENED! Personally, I had a very basic a/o non–existent background in history and humanities, and I couldn't recommend this class enough. Class meetings are small and very lax, and each time we meet we're guaranteed to do something other than lecture, which meant that I was always learning and doing something different. We had a good variety of topics and dabbled in econ, fashion, science, sociology, and politics. We also go on field trips and do different types of projects often (including the final) which made this class really simple to combine with the tougher classes I'm taking. This class is the embodiment of the word "holistic" and, again, is. a great class. I couldn't recommend it enough!
I would highly recommend taking this class! If you're interested in history, art, archaeology, or any related field, this is a great course to take to better understand material culture and objects. Be prepared to spend some time doing the readings and working on writing assignments or projects, but its all manageable.
This class is great for anyone interested in history, and it's also useful for anyone who plans to do research in college. We used so many different techniques to look at data and organize it so that we could ask good questions that could have all been research projects. I thought the way Prof. Smail taught us to look at data in history was inspiring.
This class is AMAZING. If you are looking for a great GenEd that you can be more personal with the content – ie. going to museums for section, handling artifacts yourself, singing the way they did in France, etc. – this is the class for you.
I highly recommend taking this GenEd!!! In my opinion, you will get so much out of this class and leave the class with new insights. You will be taught how to think like a historian and also how to approach thinking in new ways. The teaching staff takes time to debunk any myths you might have heard about medieval times. Dan, Mathilde, and Sama make up an amazing teaching staff! Make sure to attend office hours if you can and ask questions. In terms of coursework, this course is pretty reading–heavy and writing–heavy. The readings are very enjoyable and relate to the materials covered in the following lecture. Compared to my other courses, when it comes to larger assignments, whether that be papers or projects, I spent more time on assignments in this course than in some of my other courses. In brief, take this course if you want to gain new insights into medieval history, debunk myths, and learn awesome new skills like paleography; note that this is not an easy course in that there is minimum work (do all your assignments)! In terms of feedback, the teaching staff provides very detailed comments on your assignments and are very open to talk about how you can improve. One thing I loved about this course was the collaboration and group work involved. When I took it, this GenEd was pretty small (less than 30 or so students).
Engage proactively with all the objects and material, get started on the big papers and projects early so you have time to interact with as much material as possible, contact curators/experts, etc. Take advantage of how helpful and accessible Prof. Smail is!
