What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

This course was interesting and a good introduction to game theory.
This is, most often, a frustrating course. The topics – Nash equilibria, auctions, monopolist–price–setting – are naturally interesting and engaging, albeit not broadly applicable outside of a narrow research focus. However, there is massive variety in technical background among students in the course and, put simply, it slows the pace of the race significantly.
Lectures were often completely highjacked by a couple of students asking non–stop questions about unnecessary, mechanical details. We would spend an hour reviewing the basic technical set–up of a proof and then be forced to rush over the actually interesting part of the derivation because we ran out of time.
It is, of course, completely normal and ok to have different backgrounds in a graduate school. However, we should not all be forced to take a class together, since the result makes everyone worse off, both the students that are having a hard time keeping up and those who are looking to dive a bit deeper. I also thought that Pino could have done a better job saying "let's talk about this after" or perhaps "maybe this is a question for section" (most of them were).
The other main drawbacks were general organization: lecture notes were often sloppy, homeworks required multiple revisions, and exams were multiple choice, probably because the staff didn't want to grade. This is understandable given that it was the first time the course was being taught at HKS.
Perhaps most saliently, Pino is a very, very kind professor, and it was a pleasure to spend Monday and Wednesday mornings with him. This class would have been much worse if not for Pino.
Don't spend too much time studying for this class unless you find it intrinsically interesting for some reason.
I think this class will depend on who the professor is. I had a visiting professor who was very accessible and introduced us to the material in a straightforward, cliff–notes way (no required readings, less axiomatic and more example–based teaching). I walked away having a better understanding of micro topics like auctions, and the class is good to get an exposure of microeconomic theory, but I would not say I have mastered these subjects through the class although I did well in the class.
I highly recommend this class if you want to be exposed to many topics in microeconomics.
Don't take if you can help it.
Try to have a study group to answer the assignments and study for the exams. My friends were brilliant, and they helped me understand much of the material we saw in class. The students have different backgrounds, and it's hard to have a class that makes everyone happy. Some students will feel it's too complicated, and others might feel the course was too easy. If you don't have a strong quantitative and economics background, reach out to the professor, and they will guide you to make the best of this course. If you have a strong quantitative and economics background, you should also reach out to the professor, and I'm sure they will provide you with more resources to learn more about the course topics.
