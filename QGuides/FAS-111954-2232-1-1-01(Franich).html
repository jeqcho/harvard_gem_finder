A very good, clean class about phonology. Does what it says on the tin (which is a lot more than you can say about many classes at this college): the course material is tightly–focused around getting you the tools to understand and further inquire about phonology and phonetics. Dr. Franich is a gifted lecturer, and section is interesting (but maybe not the most useful).

The material isn't hard, the workload is moderate (~5h/wk) and you definitely don't need to go to office hours to complete the PSETs or anything. Exams were okay: the midterm was a bit tight timewise but questions were fair, while the exam was a bit rougher than probably intended (Dr. Franich said we would all leave by 2hrs in, we all stayed for the full 3).

Not much math at all, so also a great way to fulfill QRD (although that's not why I took it). Good mix of all four years and good opportunity to make friends. Definitely recommend!!
I gained a lot of incredibly valuable skills from the content of this course!!! Having a trained awareness of how people speak and what distinguishes people's accents is useful and a skill I will keep for the rest of my life. This course has absolutely made learning Spanish this semester easier, and generally improved my pronunciation skills.

You need to do practice and studying on your own to really learn the skills that they teach; unfortunately the textbook readings and psets, plus any other resources they provide, didn't typically cut it for me. Don't take this to fulfill the QRD requirement if you're expecting something to breeze through–– it definitely requires effort.
Highly recommend. Awesome teaching staff, not too challenging/well–planned and spaced work, doesn't take up too much time.
This is a very interesting class! You will leave with an understanding of the basics of how languages function & develop, which is very cool to apply to English or any language you are learning!
I really enjoyed the material of this course and found it really interesting. Being able to apply what I've learned from it to language in general, as well as specific languages, was really fascinating. I feel like I now have a greater understanding of language and that I have words to describe a bunch of rules/concepts that were previously subconscious. If you're interested in linguistics and want a more general background in it, this is a good course! However, it does move a bit fast and it isn't necessarily designed for people who haven't taken linguistics before, so you will have to put in more effort to understand and learn.

Prof. Franich is awesome though, really knowledgable and helpful, always willing to ask questions and helps you feel welcome in class so that helps
while this is an introductory course, it is a lot of material that does not feel introductory. prior knowledge of linguistics is pretty much necessary in order not to fall behind.
I never knew anything about phonetics or phonology before taking the class, but it was extremely fun to learn! The course staff are extremely passionate about sounds of language. It's really cool. My roommates don't really like how I made a bunch of weird sounds with my mouth while doing the problem sets, but it was super cool. What are the ways in which people articulate language through their mouths? How can I read IPA for pronunciation of words? You start noticing sounds in your daily life.
Interesting class, pretty well run. Assignments + exams are moderately tough but fair. A little advanced for someone with no linguistics background (they do spend time on the absolute basics but go through it pretty fast), easier if you've taken LING 83 or LING 101. If you take it for QRD requirement it'll be harder if you're not also interested in the material!
While it is an unconventional way to complete your QRD, which can lead to some unexpected benefits, it is by no means easy! Prepare to learn an entire new paradigm of understanding and data processing. It can be interesting and rewarding but as someone with no previous understanding of linguistics, I really struggled, especially in the second half of the course. Ask your professors and TFs for help, and take detailed notes in lecture.
