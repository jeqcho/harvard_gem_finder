Jun is such an amazing lecturer, and the material consists of fundamentally some of the most interesting results you'll ever see in statistics. Take the class to learn cool concepts like Gaussian Process regression, Dirichlet Processes (including the famous Chinese–Restaurant process!), and Mixture and Hierarchical Models! The homeworks and other assessment components were very challenging, but they do their job well in making sure you really understand what is going on in the class (with regard to the theory and also implementing these methods in a programming language of your choice).
Professor Liu is a strict but amazing teacher. I will highly recommend everyone to take the class; I have learnt so much from it! I think the biggest takeaway to succeed in the class is to actually go to office hours and not be afraid to raise questions or suggestions. Professor Liu is one of the most open–minded professors I have met in the school, and I am extremely grateful to get to learn more from him. The course materials are not extremely difficult, but it requires dedication and effort to understand. I realize some students might complain about the difficulties etc, but most of the time, these students never attend lectures, nor do they go to office hours, and come on, this is a graduate class. All in all, I think Professor Liu is an amazing lecturer, and I have personally learnt a lot from this course, and sincerely think the materials taught in the class is extremely useful.

(Probability/Statistical Inference background at the level of STAT 210/211 will make life way easier, although not necessary.)

The class goes fast. Be prepared to get your thoughts around quickly in class and follow the lectures, both on the chalkboard and the pre–published lecture note.

This isn't a good course to take until there are some considerable revisions.

The lectures were almost incoherent. Jun's a well–meaning person, but he just scrawled equations and went on tangents with no apparent motivation behind many of the models. The first 5 weeks of the course started off way too slow with us basically just repeating Stat 111 material, so by the time we actually got to new material, everyone had stopped paying attention. Then, by the time people realized we had started new material, we were far enough into the weeds that people didn't know what was going on.

All the homework assignments were loosely related problems from BDA which mostly required finding results from the BDA textbook rather than the lecture material. Furthermore, many of these problems were broken in little ways (vague wording, posteriors with too–heavy tails, etc.) that required lots of wasted time in office hours making sure your interpretation was the same as the teaching staff's. Furthermore, there was (as far as I could tell) no standardized set of solutions, so the teaching staff was trying to solve the problems at the same time as us (often to limited degrees of success). The midterm was pretty easy (probably an average in the mid–90s), and the final exam basically involved reproducing a paper Jun wrote a few years ago.

After all of this, I'm also not very sold on using Bayesian models in general. It seems like for anything more complicated than a simple conjugacy, the thing to do is to write a reasonable model and then throw it all into STAN to fit much faster than an individually implemented script could.
As someone who has taken many stat courses and enjoyed almost every one of them, Harvard truly has an amazing stat department. Jun Liu is really nice, but unfortunately, this course is the exception. If you need a Bayesian course, this is perhaps the only statistics topic where I would highly encourage you to look down the road to MIT. Note that my response may be somewhat biased to discovering I do not like Bayesian inference (though I did believe I would enjoy Bayesian inference before this course!).

4. Weekly problem sets with varying length

tldr; this class is a lot of pain for not much gain. Take at your own risk.
