Here are some results from complex analysis:

– The trig function sin(z) can exceed 1, if z is a complex number
– If a function f : C –> C is differentiable once, then it must be differentiable infinitely many times
– Constant functions are the only differentiable functions f : C –> C that are bounded (i.e. |f(z)| < M for some positive real M). In other words, all non–constant differentiable functions are unbounded.
– The sum of the reciprocal of perfect squares, 1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 + 1/25 + … converges to pi^2/6

I chose these examples because I can easily write them on the q guide (no integrals are featured in the list, for example), but this is just a small list out of the many beautiful and surprising theorems that are proven with complex analysis. Math 113 is an epic voyage through these exciting theorems––I learned something interesting in literally every lecture––which will broaden your mathematical perspective and leave you with a deep appreciation for math.

I recommend having taken real analysis at the level of Math 22b, Math 25b, or Math 112 before taking this course, because complex analysis borrows many techniques (epsilon–delta) and theorems from real analysis, and these techniques will not be covered as deeply in 113 as in these other courses. Real analysis is also important to appreciate the subject––complex functions behave in very surprising ways, but this won’t come as much of a surprise without learning how they “normally” behave on the real numbers.

This is definitely not necessary, but I also found Math 131 as a good course to take before 113 because we rely on some topological techniques in 131 (mainly homotopies of paths). Since 131 is not assumed as a prereq, these topological concepts will be covered during lecture, but having a background in topology will let you see the “big–picture” connection between complex analysis and topology, giving you a deeper understanding and appreciation for the subject. You’ll also gain this insight by taking 113 –> 131, since 131 also references some complex analysis to illustrate certain topics (Riemann sphere as one point compactification of C, for example).

Complex analysis is a super interesting topic, and I highly recommend it. Although I have not studied real analysis extensively, I find this material to be much more interesting. There are many unexpected results that keep you on your toes, and it is very rewarding to derive these counterintuitive results from scratch. For instance, [SPOILER ALERT :) ] if you have a function that is differentiable everywhere on the complex plane, then if it is bounded (has a maximum), then it is constant! In other words, every function that is differentiable everywhere has no maximum. That's crazy! You get to learn about such cool things, and we wrap up with the zeta & gamma function / the Riemann Hypothesis.

Complex Analysis is very valuable if you are doing physics or math, and while you could get away with doing an applied version of the course, I'd highly recommend this course. Frankly, as Professor Balibanu put it, because Mathematica exists, there is not a huge need to learn a million methods for solving contour integrals. I think it's a much better use of time to focus on the conceptual basis for complex analysis.
