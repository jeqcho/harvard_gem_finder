Hopkins went above and beyond the typical material that is covered in a 132. Near the end, we did a lot of cobordism and bordism homology (if you don't know what this is, don't worry: I don't either). The lectures were very hard to follow, as Hopkins would make a lot of mistakes with his proofs and manages to confuse everyone in most lectures. But he is very very funny and a very entertaining lecturer, so do go to his lectures for the experience, just don't really expect to learn much.
This course provides a scope of perspective and motivation that I have not been able to experience in many other math courses. Prof. Hopkins' teaching is personalized to his audience and conveys very deeply why the topics we study are interesting and matter. There is a story element or aspect of historical–context I find here that is not found in many other math courses. Moreover, this is an opportunity to engage with more "modern" mathematics building off of analysis and topology (some linear algebra and group theory too!).

Some feedback on feedback; the timeline for grading was highly delayed, to the point that it was not useful for learning the material. Our Problem Set 4 was fully graded around 2 months after we turned it in. Moreover, some of the feedback we did receive exclusively engaged with technical details which are were not the focus of the material and did not do much to further our understanding of the content.
But, it was a very rewarding course to take all things considered.
Prof. Hopkins's lectures were some of the most entertaining lectures I've heard – he often goes on long tangents that make the class more entertaining and personal. However, it sometimes seemed that he didn't prepare much for the class; proofs and definitions presented in lecture were often disorganized, and problem sets were released sporadically toward the end of the course. In total, the course was fairly light (short problem sets + no exams), though I'm hesitant to say that I learned much through the course of the semester.

Part of this is also driven by the extremely wide range of backgrounds that students have heading into the course. There were some students who had no experience with analysis, abstract algebra, or topology, while others had just finished Math 231a. I think that trying to accommodate such a wide range of backgrounds occasionally got in the way of learning. Some of the topics in differential topology seem difficult to understand without at least a rudimentary understanding of some math topics, and sometimes the course awkwardly balanced between explaining content without topics with other math courses and incorporating topics from other math courses.

Overall, while this course was an easy way to fulfill the topology requirement, I think that it had the potential to teach much more than it did, and I'm not sure I would have taken it knowing what I know now.
Be prepared to ask lots of questions to CA's and be very proactive about doing the homework assignments.
Professor Hopkins is amazing and is very funny. The course was solely graded based on homeworks, which is great if you want to avoid the stress of midterms and finals. However, the homeworks are very difficult, so you might spend a lot of time on it. Going to office hours was the most helpful to get unstuck. The CAs this year were amazing. Don't take this course if you have no topology background unless you are sure you can attend all of the office hours.

I highly recommend this course to first–year math concentrators in their second semester. If you are already advanced, you would probably be better served self–studying this material over the course of a week or so. On another note, Professor Hopkins is very charismatic and approachable.
I entered the class only having taken the 22 series my freshman year, where I didn't feel like I had gotten a good understanding of the materials, and having self–studied some real analysis over the winter break. The beginning was definitely tough, especially since there were homework questions where I simply didn't have enough background knowledge to even come up with an answer. But: Hopkins is amazing, funny and very helpful, and the TFs are your best friends (special shoutout to Tyler for being an amazing TF and human being). If you go to office hours, ask questions, and read the textbook carefully, you'll do great. The fact that grades were only based on homework and Hopkins was willing to adjust the homework schedule definitely made the class a lot more relaxed and enjoyable.
This course presents essential material for advanced coursework in either topology or analysis, following Guillemin and Pollack's Differential Topology pretty consistently. The problem sets for this course are both reasonable in length and effective in synthesizing important material, but the lectures definitely leave something to be desired in their structure and cohesion. However, if you are somebody like me who learns the best out of a textbook, this course is essential and presents a lot of fascinating ideas!
This is a great course. Professor Hopkins does a fantastic job telling the story of differential topology.
i would liken this course to an abusive sugardaddy that keeps you coming back for more. hopkins is an absolute mess every class and never fails to confuse everyone in the room, including himself. he stumbles over seemingly minor details in his proofs, an issue which I think is easily solved if only he bothered to look at his course notes whenever he is confused during lecture. most of the time, he turns around in the midst of his rambling and asks the class if we're okay, to which he is always answered, without fail, with deafening silence. he knows that the majority of the class stopped following everything since 5 minutes into the lesson, but he decides to move on anyways. some of us are pretty sure that he has not presented a single, completely correct proof in class since the start of the semester. im pretty sure that if I had not seen half the material before in some other math class, I would have come out of the class understanding completely nothing. personally, I came out of the class knowing buzzwords and some vague idea of how things clicked together, but its the kind of unsettling feeling where you dont really know the details well enough to be satisfied with yourself. because he never prepares for lecture, he is a mess on the blackboard, and he constantly likes to switch notation at the drop of a hat in the middle of a proof. he himself is aware of this, but he jokes about it and tells us to go figure it out for ourselves, which is pretty hilarious in some weird way.

despite all this chaos, hopkins' personality combined with his style of teaching makes for strangely addictive lectures. despite the fact that I never learn annything from class, I am extremely proud to announce that I have 100% attendance for math 132 lectures (lets just forget for a moment that this class is at 1.30 pm on mon and wed so its p hard to miss on accident). hopkins likes to present the material in a way that fits into a larger picture that was the development of modern math, and the way he briefly mentions the people and their stories behind some of the major theorems breathes a touch of humanity into an otherwise dry and technical subject. hopkins also taught cobordism, something which is unique to his take on math 132 and definitely not something you learn in a normal rendition of math 132. I barely understood the details on surgery, especially the interplay between global and local properties in cobordism and surgery, but it was pretty cool nonetheless. he also compensates for his horrible blackboard work with absolutely amazing pictures, which surely is core in any class on topology/geometry. you can always tell a good geometer/topologist by the way he draws donuts, especially 2 donuts stacked on top of each other and intertwined, which eventually turned into a picture of a meditating buddha by the time hopkins was done using the picture. finally and most importantly, quirky is an understatement for hopkins' personality. i dont think any other professor here is as cool and in touch with young students as he is. the antics that he pulls during class are so weird that they become hilarious and endearing. highlights include his habit of trying to spin the blackboard duster while throwing it behind his back onto the blackboard at the start of every lesson, claiming that his lecture quality will drop because his thumb hurts due to playing too many console games with his daughter last night, and also his profound insight of using the famous lemma stating that 1+1 = 2 in the middle of a proof (this eventually made it onto sidechat).

overall, a solid class for people who come to class to be entertained but are prepared to put in the work outside of class, because you wont learn much during lectures. his notes are amazing (full of typos though, but he likes to put his inner ramblings inside his notes so you can occasionally see him complaining some details he has to straighten out in his notes). in addition to gaining a deeper appreciation for how cool and beautiful diff top is, even though i understand close to zero of it still, i also gained an appreciation for how cool some mathematicians are. definitely a recommendation if you are insane like me. oh btw, he likes steifel manifolds and grassmanians alot for some strange reason. the psets are full of them
