Take this course! You will learn a lot that is evidently going to be useful.
For the first half of the semester, I rated this as an excellent class: I was learning about topics (IV, Matching, LATE, RD, etc.) that were frequently popping up in other areas of coursework and research. Time and reflection, though, cooled my stance. While this class certainly *covers* relevant topics, I don't really feel that I deeply *learned* them. I am going to have to supplement this course with significant amounts of outside reading to really feel qualified to engage with these topics as a researcher.

The root cause of this, in my opinion, is the extreme heterogeneity of technical background in the course: some students have significant quantitative background (multivariable calculus, coding, probability and inference), some have a mix, and some have little to none. This is completely fine, and natural, to expect in a graduate school; the issue arises when everyone is required to take the same course. The result is a class that iterates through the material in fits and starts, never really diving deep enough into the concepts to develop a strong understanding. I thought that Econometrics I in the fall did a better job of applying graduate–level quantitative skills, perhaps to the ire of some participants, but it allowed me to walk away with a better understanding. This course often felt like an undergraduate course a la Econ 101: lots of handwaving, statements like 'trust that this works' and qualitative questions meant to trick you instead of truly testing your understanding. I know that the natural fix – splitting this into two tracks – is complicated, but I'm not sure how to improve the course otherwise.

Ultimately, I would not take this class unless you are absolutely required to, which, unfortunately, you might be.
