Professor Edwards has put so much care into this course and he truly cares about every student. Take this course if you are interested in the subject matter or if you want to explore evolutionary biology for fun!
Professor Edwards is such a cool guy. The guest lectures are very cool and you really learn so much about the MCZ and some of the work/items that you never knew about on campus. This class is all about underground tours of the MCZ and I loved that part. If you're like me, you definitely will not understand everything and that's completely ok; Scott really is excited that students will learn what they can from the course. Paleontology isn't for me, but I'm glad to have taken this course; I fulfilled some of toddler me's dreams. Also go to sleep early if you have a field trip with this class!!!
Take this seminar! It will change your view of birds, of paleontology, of dinosaurs, of evolution – it's a great seminar that is very reasonable in terms of its work and with a very passionate and enthusiastic professor. If you have any vague interest in natural history or biology you'll find it a blast.
This freshman seminar was not worth it. I took it since my first freshman seminar last semester was a wonderful class, but this seminar was not enjoyable in the slightest.

The pros include the fact that the material itself is fascinating (although very poorly taught) and there are guest speakers and class visits that help with conceptualizing information and are good experiences.

The cons are many. The course would benefit greatly if the professor actually taught and lectured regularly, and broke down the information as an instructor should. Professor Edwards neglects to understand that although many of the students in the course may have had experience with the material already, this is still a freshman seminar and there are people with varying degrees of familiarity and experience with the field. Furthermore, the class was dull and badly taught. Frankly, I don't even know why the professor was there. All the class consisted of for most of the weeks was reading several dense scientific papers without any prior contextualizing or introduction from the professor (the papers were generally interesting). Then, we would just spend much of the next class period spitting up the papers' information in completely student–led presentations, again without any feedback or guidance from the professor. The class was lazily structured and badly organized. The class would have been exactly the same if he was not there and we just read the papers on our own without coming to class.

The professor barely lectured. He did so perhaps a handful of times over the semester, and even then the lectures were short and not comprehensive. They did not help in understanding or explaining the material. The assignments were useless and were basically just busy–work that wasted time. Most of them required software downloads that we never went back to after one use, so it's not even like the assignments helped with training us how to use the new software for future reference.

I came into this class with an interest in evolutionary biology I was hoping to grow. I fortunately left with the same interest, but with a hesitancy to continue studying the field in any capacity while at Harvard.
This is a fun class to learn about dinosaurs, birds, and the connections between them. I especially enjoyed learning about the development of feathers. We read a decent amount of scientific papers each week, some of which I had trouble following, but Professor Edwards helps clear up the important bits, so it's ok if they're confusing. We got to view the bird collections and handle preserved birds (so keep that in mind if that's not something that appeals to you). We also took field trips to the Boston Museum of Science and the American Museum of Natural History in New York, which were fun with our newfound dinosaur knowledge.
I would definitely recommend this course for anyone who is interested in birds, dinosaurs, evolution, or even just wants to learn more about science in a more relaxed environment! It was simultaneously really fun and fascinating and also helped expand my skills and knowledge regarding scientific processes. The field trips as well as the talks with paleontologists were all really immersive and helped me understand more about the topics we were learning about, the hands–on labs were really cool, and while the prep reading was a bit dense at times, it was all really fascinating!
