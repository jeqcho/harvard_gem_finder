First and foremost, if you are thinking about taking this course, you MUST have a strong interest in Korean history. This is not a class about K–pop or modern Korea, this is fundamentally a Korean history class. As with any history course, this class can be very dry at times, even for a student who is enamored by Korean history. Only a super strong love and appreciation for the class material will get you through the dry periods without hating the course and its busy–work assignments.

So, if you have a strong interest in Korean history, then I cannot recommend this class enough. The course is fantastic. The readings are informative and thoughtful, the movies are good to watch, Professor Eckert is a decently good lecturer, and Sungik (the TF) was phenomenal. This class completely changed the way I understand Korean politics and Korean culture, and it changed the way I understand the history of East Asia and the legacy of that history in the region today. As a Korean–American, the class also changed the way I understand my family and the struggles my family went through during the 20th century. It was really an amazing history course. My only critique is that a lot of the assignments felt like busy work, but hopefully they cut down on this in future years.
Take this course to be inspired, and to be challenged to think about one of the forefront issues of our modern world (the Two Koreas), one that will continue to remain a challenge for us young people in the future.
You will not only learn about history in this class, but also about how to better situate yourself in the grand course of time, and in the ever–fluctuating world with so much uncertainties, reading into the past may as well help prepare us appreciate the present and prepare for the future.
TAKE THIS GENED! It's one of the rare existing gems right now and actually has interesting material! Professor Eckert is an awesome lecturer, and the class just has a fun, engaging atmosphere that makes you wanna learn for the sake of it. The work is minimal and there are tons of cool movies to watch throughout the semester. RECOMMEND 100%
Make sure to get those response papers done ASAP! Do not wait until the end to get your 6 required papers done because it'll just conflict with other course things so it's much better to get them done and over with. Also it gives out a final paper AND a final exam (which is kind of brutal and everyone disliked that a lot because you really only should assign one or the other) so if that's going to be too much for your workload (like me with CS20 and CS51 exams/projects due the same week) then I do not recommend taking this course. Otherwise, go all out.
If you want to really know what is going on in the world right now, it is IMPERATIVE you take this course. Everyone should take this course regardless of if they even care to study history or not. Everyone needs to have an awareness of the deep history of Korea, especially in today's heated political climate.
This is a very fulfilling class, you will learn a lot, but it is a good amount of work, especially for a GENED. However, Professor Eckert is a very knowledgable person, and you will leave each class and section having learned so much.
Before anything else, this course is a very good course. It's structured in a smart way, it's very well–polished, the staff (Prof. Eckert and Sungik the TF) are incredibly professional, dedicated, supportive, and overall amazing. You will learn about what Korea was like in the late 19th century, what lead to it splitting, how the split occurred, and the aftermath of the split. You get some kpop references at the last week when you learn about South Korea today, but it's far from the focus of the course. The assignments are really manageable and you get good feedback (read the syllabus, but this year is submit a short response paper 6 weeks out of howevermany you have). Exams are very reasonable, even if they sound scary in the beginning (midterm and final). I think it's not easy to get an A, but it requires effort to get less than an A– (god bless grade inflation). In lectures, the prof (or guest speakers) present for 75 minutes, which can be draining at times. However, Professor Eckert often shows videos that can take up as much as half the class time, which makes it much more engaging. There is quite a bit of reading but you can get away without actually reading most of it. There are also quite a lot of films in the syllabus, which diversifies the reading experience. I can't see how a course in the humanities could be any better, 5/5.
