What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Table for [QTitle].
Comments
This a great class to learn about the intersection of the economy and education. We examined this connection through many different lenses which was cool. The first half of the course was a lot more difficult than the second due to all the math and econometrics we had to use. The description on my.harvard does not do enough to emphasize the need for an econometrics type course background. The final project was fun but was a little unstructured and the feedback portion of the presentations was not structured the same for everyone. The memo project presentation was the same; for some groups, Professor Carlana wanted them to know a lot of information about each article, and for other groups she went very easy on them. Overall this was a great course. Just make sure you are interested in the topic, know some econometrics, work well in groups from different schools and levels of education, and want to read and participate.
The course can be a really good way to take econometrics concepts and see how they apply in a specific domain of research. It can really make you grapple with how to interpret real–world studies.
This is a great course for getting a economic perspective on education policy. The tools and thought processes featured in the course are also widely applicable.
As an ed–secondary, this was a great dip into the world of ed–policy, and it was cool to talk to older master's students who had more experience in education and policy and cared about combining the issues. As a non–ec concentrator, this was the perfect way to learn econometrics without taking a boring econometrics class. This made me realize why people think Econ is cool and how it can actually be applied to solving real problems of inequity, instead of just corporate finance....

Cons: Some debates about inequity in education fell flat because we weren't able to dive deeply into more ethical/moral debates. (ie. are test scores even a good metric for intelligence and student growth? What are the implications of sorting immigrant student populations into high long–term oriented (ie. Asian countries) v. low long–term oriented (ie. Latin countries) ––> this seems potentially problematic/reifies existing social inequity + civil tension between minority groups but questions about it in class were dismissed by Michela.) I can see how this is not the purpose of this class, since the purpose is to learn econometrics in the context of Ed policy, but these feel like important debates that should not be dismissed as irrelevant or not important.
