This class has the potential to be incredibly interesting, but in reality is a bit of a let down. The workload is very manageable; there's not a ton of reading (and in my opinion it's all very fascinating and you don't have to read every single page) and the assignments are pretty quick and short. However, it did not have the best flow (we jumped around a lot) and if you have a solid background in history and current events, you probably will not actually learn that much new information. There was nothing about this course that was objectively "bad", and there were a lot of good parts (i.e. great guest speakers, visiting the map room, a pretty "gem–y" workload), but if you're looking for a super enlightening course about borders and deep, intellectual discussion, this class––at least in my opinion––is just not it.

This is a wonderful and eye–opening GenEd! Professor Lewis has put together engaging lectures that enable you to explore the history of and debates surrounding a diverse set of borders worldwide and across history. Although lectures aren't always very interactive (questions are usually asked at the end rather than during pauses in lectures), they are very interesting, and Professor Lewis absolutely conveys an enthusiasm for the material. Section is also a great way to engage with other students and the TAs in a smaller group. Lena was fantastic a TF, posing thought–provoking questions that guided the discussion. Course assignments are fair and unique, spanning everything from writing memos to drafting reflections on issues of contemporary and historical importance. Absolutely take this class if you seek to emerge more open–minded and informed. The final project also offers you the opportunity to choose and study a border of interest to you, and I think that the course absolutely lives up to its goal to create more globally–minded citizens.
Take this course!
