You learn a lot in this class and the material is super cool, but be careful because this class is a lot of work. I came in thinking it would be on part with classes like 143b and 175, but the work load was significantly more than anything else I had taken before. I though the problem sets were often unreasonable in length, sometimes taking 20 hours. If you're able to dedicate the time and energy, you will get a lot out of it, but do be careful. And definitely go to section and reach out to the TFs with questions—they will be your best friends in this class.
I would highly recommend this course to anyone who wants to learn more about quantum information. Although fully understanding the materials requires taking some intermediate–level physics courses (such as 143b and 181), grasping the core ideas of the course can be accomplished without taking any physics course before as Professor’s explanation is very clear and provides a lot of great intuition in physics of quantum information.
This is a great course if you want to get involved in quantum information. You cover a lot of material and get to high–level and cutting–edge topics.
I think the best way to describe this course is that it is the most glorious way to get academic trauma. This course was hard, definitely one of the top 3 hardest courses I've ever taken (this is coming from someone who's taken courses like CS124 and physics143a with Doyle, so use that for comparison). It is also somewhat disorganized. At first, PSETs were due on Mondays, with TF office hours and section being held on Wednesday and Friday, so basically the only time we could get help was during Friday section (by Wednesday we hadn't even learned all of the material needed for the PSET so barely anyone had actually started the PSET). Then PSETs became due on Wednesdays after some complaints, but that made it even worse because in order for OHs to be useful, you either had to start the PSET the minute it came out or use an extension. The midterm was take–home, but it was the hardest take–home midterm I've ever taken and I definitely spent 25+ hours on it. After the midterm, the material also starts becoming disorganized since you jump between random topics like implementation of quantum computers, error correction, quantum complexity, etc., which are very interesting but you go through it so fast that you end up understanding maybe 10% of it all. There was also one week when prof. Lukin said the PSET would be short and sweet––and then it turned out to be harder than all of the previous PSETs, which I had spent an average of 15 hrs/week on.

So yeah I definitely got traumatized by this course. But there is glory: Lukin is a great lecturer and is lowkey very humorous and cool, plus he's very big in the field of quantum info. The final project in this course can also be pretty cool: you basically get to use Qiskit and experiment with real quantum devices from IBM, which are a refreshing break from all of the tedious math and theory on most of the PSETs. You also get mentored by grad students from Lukin's group, which was super super helpful (if you get Nishad as your mentor, he will save your life in the class). As a result, I enjoyed this final project the most out of all final projects I've done in any course. Overall, if you are considering this course, you want to make sure you have quantum background and that you remember a lot of your linear algebra. And prepare for some trauma. But otherwise, this course is very rewarding.
