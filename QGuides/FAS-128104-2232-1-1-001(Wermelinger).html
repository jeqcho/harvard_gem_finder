CS 205 is very helpful for parallel programming and writing high–performant code. It focuses on relevant examples, with lots of rules of thumb and guidelines that are a summary of the current understanding of the field. You will walk away with very practical knowledge that you could immediately apply.

Having a good background in C/C++ is absolutely necessary. The class is very heavily focused on C/C++, and the expectation for a final project is hundreds of lines of C++ code. There is no intro to C++ provided, and you will not have time to catch up. Probably take CS 61 before this class. Do NOT think that the C++ prerequisite is a trivial one.

There is heavy focus on scientific coding. You will learn how to use OpenMP, MPI, and SIMD vectorization. These are incredibly useful techniques for parallelizing scientific code. In terms of conceptual knowledge (e.g. how threading works, CPU pipelining, memory hierarchy), there was not much to be said that was not already said in CS 61. If you care about how things work under the hood, this is not the right course.

Quizzes are non–trivial and you should allocate some time to prepare for them. They are tricky multiple–choice questions, and each quiz is 5% of your grade, so if you don't study, it's easy for your grade to go down.

Lab attendance is mandatory, but labs themselves are not very useful. After each lab, you get 10 days or so to submit the lab work, so for most people labs turn into short homeworks.

In terms of workload, this heavily depends on your background. If you have taken CS 61 or 161, most of the class will be somewhat familiar, and you can expect 8 hours or less in any given week. If you have no prior experience with parallel code at all, it will be significantly more difficult, and probably upwards of 12 hours. Definitely do not take if you don't know C/C++.

Do NOT procrastinate working on the project. First, it's very easy for your project plan to actually not work out, and you end up with a solution that does not work 2 days before the deadline. That is very stressful. Second, the entire class has to share one research cluster. As the deadline approaches, it becomes close to impossible to get computing time on the cluster. 2 days before the final project deadline, we once waited 12 hours for a job to execute. It becomes very hard to improvise if you end up in this situation.

Grading on homeworks can be very harsh. Penalties for minor things like unlabeled graph axes are significant, and can easily add up. It's worth spending a little extra time to make sure your homework is polished.

Fabian is a good teacher, but is very strict with the rules. Do NOT go over your late day budget, do NOT go above your project presentation time limit, and do NOT forget to interact on the Ed forum (class participation grade is determined by how much time you spend on Ed).
