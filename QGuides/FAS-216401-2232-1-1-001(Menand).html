What would you like to tell future students about this class? (Your response to this question may be published anonymously.)

Comments
This class was extremely beneficial to me both as a citizen and as a student of the humanities and the law. This course is grounded in analysis of the world and our society in the past, present, and future. We were able to learn so much about Supreme Court precedent, the Reconstruction Amendments, and contemporary civil rights litigation surrounding affirmative action. As someone who has spent a lot of time thinking about affirmative action, I especially enjoyed the opportunity to learn from William Lee in class when he watched us perform oral arguments for the UNC and Harvard cases. The application of this course in real life is what makes it so magnificent. This has been the most rewarding class I have taken at Harvard with the most phenomenal group of students and a very dedicated teacher.
This is a great class if you are interested in going to law school and civil rights law. If you want to know more about the Supreme Court, constitutional law, and read landmark Supreme Court cases, this is the class for you. It will challenge you to explore things you briefly learned before and will excite you as you dive deeper into the law.
This is an excellent course to take, and I'd recommend others apply for it when they have a chance. Professor Menand is both an excellent lecturer and an excellent discussion leader: he knows what questions to ask, whose comments to push further on, how to rephrase an assertion to draw out a challenge. The right quantity and length of papers for a seminar like this. The course material, obviously relevant now, is worth thinking carefully about. The discussions we had in this seminar were some of the better ones of my undergraduate years.
This has been one of my favorite classes at Harvard. I would recommend it to anyone with any interest in law and race relations in the US. Professor Menand is an expert in the field and an outstanding discussion facilitator. If you are lucky enough to be accepted into the course, TAKE IT!
Do the readings rigorously — and do them more than once, given the legal jargon can be dense. Do not be afraid to ask basic questions about the law or the Constitution that you think you “should” know; chances are, if you’re unclear about something, so is at least one other person in the room. Frequent Office Hours because they are instructive and enjoyable.
Great class, helpful if you have background in legal theory. Make sure papers are written to Menand's guidelines — they are legal papers, not English or History papers.
This is an excellent seminar class that gets at very difficult constitutional questions and allows for ample discussion with your classmates and the instructor (who asks the perfect, incisive questions).
there will be a lot of students in the class who know an insane amount about the law. don't be intimidated by them. ask questions or wait for clarifications and you will get just as much out of the course as anyone else.
