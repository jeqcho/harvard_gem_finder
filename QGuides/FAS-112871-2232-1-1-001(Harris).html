Math 55b was an interesting experience. If you took Math 55a you are most definitely prepared to take on this class. We also had I think about 4 students from Math 25a and 2 students from Math 22.

Based on my experience and other friends’ feedback it feels that Math 55b was a decent bit easier than Math 55a and that in some sense last semester was the “real Math 55 experience.” Last semester we were forced to work together on hard problems for a long time and go to office hours for help, and as a result, we formed a close community and had a deeper understanding of the materials. To some extent this semester we had the continuation of that community but personally, I felt like I understood last semester’s material better and had more interactions with the CAs and just everyone in the class.

This course is titled "Studies in Real and Complex Analysis," but on the first day of class, the professor explains to you that in reality, it is more like Math 131+Math113. We roughly had three units: 3.5 weeks on point set topology, 3.5 weeks on algebraic topology, and 5 weeks on complex analysis. As a result, we don’t really dive into real analysis and students should learn this important area of mathematics on their own at some point.

We followed Munkres’ Topology through the first two units. In point set topology we learned about fundamental concepts such as product and quotient topology, connectedness, compactness, and separation axioms, which is Munkres chapter 12–34. So I would recommend future students who want to do some preparation during winter break to read this portion of Munkres. This part of the course was comparably more intuitive and chill (admittedly compactness is not very intuitive).

In algebraic topology, we learned about homotopy equivalences, fundamental groups, Seifert–Van Kampen, covering spaces, and briefly introduced topics like higher homotopy groups and homology. This is roughly Munkres chapters 51–57 and 79–81. This part of the course was quite difficult for me as it is harder to visualize and see the significance of these constructions. Later on, after learning complex analysis, I was finally able to see some of the motivations and applications of these concepts. Definitely try to go to office hours here, for me at least the problem sets involve a degree of hand waving and many drawings.

About a week after spring break we started Complex Analysis, which according to Professor Harris is his favorite and most beautiful part of the course (as usual Joe is an absolutely fantastic and supportive lecturer). Here we followed Ahlfors’ Complex Analysis, which honestly is quite terse and hard to understand (reading lecture notes beforehand helps). We started with holomorphic functions and with the introduction of the Cauchy Integral Formula we were equipped to take on harder problems. After learning about poles, singularities, and Laurent series, we did a good amount of Residue calculus which gave us important results about holomorphic and meromorphic functions.

I have to admit, I personally did not find complex analysis to be particularly enlightening as it felt like learning a ton of cool formulas and then just applying them to solve some seemingly arbitrary problems. In retrospect, I actually liked the topology portion of the course better as they introduced new ideas I have never considered before. I will likely take Math 231, a course Professor Harris highly recommended.

From my perspective my classmates took the following courses alongside 55b: quite a few took CS 124, a decent amount took Stat 111, some took Math 123 or 129 or 132, and a few took CS 181.

Throughout this semester I have been thinking hard on what the point of taking Math 55 is. If a student took courses that taught the materials in 55 separately they may get a deeper understanding by virtue of spending more time on them. I have come to a few humble conclusions: I think by compressing a large amount of abstract mathematics into each semester we do get a slightly more surface–level understanding of individual topics. However, at the same time, we got to see some connections between different areas of mathematics that would be otherwise hard to see, for example, algebra with topology, complex analysis with algebraic topology, abstract algebra with linear algebra; and I’m sure some of the smaller topics we did like the exposure to basic category theory will serve us well in the future. I think I got a very solid overall “feel” and overview of higher–level mathematics.

Moreover, 55 is really at its core about the community. I have met some of my closest friends and some of the most interesting people through this class. The late nights at Math Lounge and Math Library will forever be a sweet piece of memory lying in the foundation of my Harvard journey and I’m sure years later my classmates and I will look back fondly on those days and laugh and shed some tears. I highly recommend anyone reading this to reach out to your classmates, schedule a lunch or a walk together and get to know them better. You will not regret this.

Overall I think continuing with 55b was the right choice for me and most of my classmates from 55a. If you are considering this course, sit in on a few lectures and check it out! I wish you the best of luck!

This was an excellent course and I highly recommend it. Material–wise, we covered topology (point–set and then algebraic topology), followed by complex analysis (we briefly encountered real analysis).
Easily the best course I have ever taken, 55B is a true crowning jewel of the Harvard maths department catalogue. Topology and complex analysis are easily the two most opulent and fascinatingly beautiful fields of maths that I have encountered thus far: an absolute joy for visual learners of mathematics like myself yet full of unexpected and powerful results. Professor Harris' passion for the material, already evident even before the very first lecture, was showcased in its full glory throughout the entire term: the course at times felt like we were being led on a trip by a deeply enthusiastic tour guide through a vast and mysterious landscape with treasures hidden at every turn.

Onto more practical reflections. With regards to what background one needs to embark on this great journey, I would argue that any student who finds themselves to have completed and enjoyed any of Math 22A, 25A or 55A is a potential student of 55B: I have friends in the class who have fallen in each of these categories and they all seem to have had a good experience in the course. What I would suggest for anyone coming into 55B without having been exposed to abstract algebra, however, is to familiarise oneself briefly with the objects discussed in the 55A Group Theory units in order that the lack of this background does not present an additional obstacle for the already difficult task of fully understanding Covering Space Theory, which I found to definitely be the most demanding subunit of the course in terms of the requisite level of mathematical maturity. Another factor I would take into consideration is that the subject of Real Analysis is left virtually entirely undiscussed in the course, which presents the prospective course taker with the motivation to self–study this by themselves during the winter break through independent reading of Baby Rudin. To this I would say certainly that a familiarity with the different types of convergence could be beneficial, but as Professor Harris correctly emphasises, one must resist the temptation to use Real Analysis as a foundation for understanding Complex Analysis as the latter has a far stronger connection with Topology. As for the problem sets, I have found that a greater proportion of the problems in 55B are written to serve a rigorous pedagogical purpose than those in 55A, which I have found to be very helpful as the problems (and the very–sometimes–overly meticulous texts from which they are chosen, namely Munkres and Ahlfors) provide a nice contrast to Professor Harris' emphasis in his lectures on conceptual intuition and aesthetic appeal of the mathematics. Depending on the specific problem set and one's level of comfort with the material discussed, I would say that 14 hours per week spent on 55B is what is to–be–expected of the student.

As for the community in the class this year, which from what I have seen has only gotten stronger since last term with the addition of newcomers, it is absolutely stellar. In my experience I have met many friends in 55 who are incredibly fun, caring, inspiring and passionate about maths and other sciences such as physics, statistics and computer science. What I thought was crucial in maintaining this was that all of my friends in 55 (i. e. most of the class) were respectful of each other's mathematical backgrounds in high school and recognised that helping each other understand mathematical ideas and collaborating on problems together were way more constructive than gatekeeping knowledge and fueling one's own ego through the derision of others. With that said I have great faith that the 2023–2024 class of 55ers will have nothing to worry about as they will have a fantastic set of CAs who will ensure that this is to–be–carried–forward.

Looking back on my year in Math 55, I am both proud of how much I have learnt this year and grateful for the opportunity to have had this experience. This course has for sure been one of the coolest most exciting things that I have done at Harvard and I hope that you will give it a try and enjoy it as much as I have.
Wonderful class! Comments:

– As with 55A, the course community is very close. In 55B many problem–setting groups have formed; there are also students who join the class having taken other math courses in the fall and will join others in psetting, so 'integration' is done well (irl or complex). My main comment here is along the lines of that for 55A (I will give the short version here): the community and people in the class are a big part of why the class is amazing!

– Material is very fun. Topology as a subject is simultaneously surprising and intuitive: for instance, the fundamental group of the circle, which we spend serious effort deriving, is a seemingly obvious but beautiful result. Similarly, complex integration techniques are quite nice: we learn Cauchy's integral formula, this time very surprising, yet the derivation is clean. I particularly enjoyed the applications of this theorem, e.g. in solving complex integration (e.g., the 'keyhole' integral) — the methods of integrating around a circle but altering the path slightly for ill–defined functions was wonderful and enjoyable! Lastly, the class concludes with the connection between topology and analysis, which is a particularly lovely connection and brings the course material together. The 55B topics are probably more intriguing than that of its predecessor (55A): it is often surprising how all of the definitions and foundations leading up to results were conceived.

– Professor Harris is a wonderful lecturer, as always: he is clear with the main ideas and the course concepts that matter (for instance, 'point–set topology is useless' :)). His office hours (very much recommend attending!) are always insightful, in respect to both clarifying course material and going beyond, e.g. in describing the efforts of the remainder of Ahlfors which we do not cover, or in tangents/glimpses of category theory, algebraic geometry, etc. — it is quite eye–opening seeing all of the mathematics ahead.

– Rigor is occasionally sacrificed in lecture for the sake of understanding. I think the lectures are well–done in that they make it clear what the main idea is (rather than being jumbled in an analytical mess). However, it I did find it helpful to sometimes look into Ahlfors or work out certain examples myself, in particular regarding issues with continuity, convergence, etc. I found this a good way to complement lecture in that it helps with a complete rigorous understanding of concepts that lecture intuits and explains well.

– The consensus seems to be that 55B is easier than 55A. Probably, this is true, in that most problem sets can be done by considering the theorems learned in class and applying them roughly directly. In this sense, the 55A problem sets had more engaging problems on average. Nonetheless, there are always challenging problems which are quite fun to do; also, topology is enjoyable to visualize.
